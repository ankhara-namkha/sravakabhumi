Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Second Yoga Place, Part Three

本地分中聲聞地第十三第二瑜伽處之三

28.1 The Three Superior Trainings

[T30.0435c26]

What are the trainings? They are the three superior trainings (tri-shiksha):[1] (1) the superior training in ethical discipline (adhishila-shiksha), (2) the superior training in mind (adhicitta-shiksha), and (3) the superior training in prajna (wisdom, adhiprajna-shiksha).

What is the superior training in ethical discipline? It is dwelling in the possession of ethical discipline and so forth, as explained in detail above. This is called the superior training in ethical discipline.

What is the superior training in mind? It is being separated from desires and from unwholesome dharmas, possessing examination (vitarka) and analysis (vicara), entering and fully abiding in the first dhyana (meditative absorption) with its joy and bliss born of separation — up through entering and fully abiding in the fourth dhyana. This is called the superior training in mind.

Furthermore, all formless attainments and whatever other meditative attainments and absorptions (samapatti) there may be are also called the superior training in mind. However, it is by relying on the dhyanas that one can first enter the direct realization of the noble truths (satya-abhisamaya) and achieve the certainty of correctness — definitive entry onto the noble path (samyaktva-niyama).[2] One cannot accomplish this by entirely forsaking all dhyanas.[3] Therefore, because the dhyanas are supreme, they are specifically designated as the superior training in mind.

What is the superior training in prajna? It is all the knowledge and vision (jnana-darshana) that accords with reality regarding the four noble truths and related matters. This is called the superior training in prajna.

Question: For what reason are there exactly three trainings — neither fewer nor more?

Answer: Because of three functions: establishing samadhi, providing the basis for knowledge, and accomplishing the purpose.

(1) Establishing samadhi refers to the superior training in ethical discipline. Why? Because through ethical discipline one establishes mental one-pointedness (ekagrata), enabling the mind to attain samadhi (meditative concentration).

(2) Providing the basis for knowledge refers to the superior training in mind. Why? Because through a rightly concentrated mind and mental one-pointedness, knowledge and vision in accordance with reality arise regarding knowable matters.

(3) Accomplishing the purpose refers to the superior training in prajna. Why? Because through fully purified knowledge and vision, one realizes the ultimate severance of all afflictions. The severance of afflictions is one's own great benefit and the supreme accomplishment — there is no accomplishment beyond this. For these reasons, there are exactly three trainings.

Question: For what reason are the three trainings in this particular sequence?

Answer: First, because ethical discipline (shila) is well purified, one is free from worry and remorse. Being free from worry and remorse, one experiences gladness and ease. Because there is ease, the mind attains right concentration. Because the mind is concentrated, one is able to know and see in accordance with reality. Because one knows and sees in accordance with reality, one gives rise to disenchantment. From disenchantment comes dispassion. From dispassion one attains liberation. Having attained liberation, one realizes the ultimate nirvana where there is nothing more to be done.[4]

In this way, beginning with the cultivation of pure ethical discipline and gradually progressing, one finally realizes the ultimate nirvana where there is nothing more to be done. This is why the three trainings are in this sequence.

Question: For what reason are the three trainings called "superior" — that is, the superior training in discipline, the superior training in mind, and the superior training in prajna?

Answer: They are called "superior" (adhika) because of two meanings: the meaning of what they lead to, and the meaning of their supremacy.

What is the meaning of what they lead to? One cultivates pure ethical discipline in order to progress toward the superior mind — this is the superior training in discipline. One cultivates the concentrated mind in order to progress toward superior prajna — this is the superior training in mind. One cultivates knowledge and vision in order to progress toward the severance of afflictions — this is the superior training in prajna. This is called "superior" in terms of the meaning of what they lead to.

What is the meaning of their supremacy? The superior training in discipline, the superior training in mind, and the superior training in prajna — these three exist exclusively within the noble teaching and are not shared with non-Buddhist paths.[5] This is called "superior" in terms of the meaning of their supremacy.

Furthermore, sometimes the superior training in mind leads to the development of the superior training in prajna, and sometimes the superior training in prajna leads to the development of the superior training in mind.

A noble disciple who has not yet attained the root dhyanas (maula-dhyana) may first enter the path through seeing.[6] Afterward, to sever the remaining afflictions progressively, they engage in diligent preparatory practice — cultivating each of the seven factors of awakening, from mindfulness (smriti-sambodhyanga) through to equanimity (upeksha-sambodhyanga). This is an instance where the superior training in prajna leads to the development of the superior training in mind.

As for the superior training in mind leading to the development of the superior training in prajna — this has been explained above.

Furthermore, one may possess the superior training in discipline without the superior training in mind or prajna. Or one may possess the superior training in discipline together with the superior training in mind, yet lack the superior training in prajna. However, it is impossible to possess the superior training in prajna without also possessing the superior training in discipline and the superior training in mind. Therefore, when the superior training in prajna is present, one should know that all three trainings are necessarily complete.

Within this establishment of the three trainings, yogins should diligently apply themselves to training.

Furthermore, there are three types of persons (pudgala) who, relying on these three trainings, enter the direct realization of the truths (satya-abhisamaya). What are the three?[7]

  1. One who has not yet abandoned desire
  2. One who has partially abandoned desire
  3. One who has fully abandoned desire

In this regard, one should know: A person who has not yet abandoned any desire at all, through diligent preparatory practice, enters the direct realization of the truths. Having attained the direct realization of the truths, they realize the fruit of stream-entry (srota-apanna-phala). One who has partially abandoned desire realizes the fruit of once-returning (sakridagamin-phala) at that same time. One who has fully abandoned desire realizes the fruit of non-returning (anagamin-phala) at that same time.

28.2 The Three Faculties

[T30.0436b19]

Furthermore, there are three faculties (indriya).[8]

  1. The faculty of "I shall come to know the unknown" (anajnatam-ajnasyami-indriya)
  2. The faculty of full knowledge (ajna-indriya)
  3. The faculty of one who possesses full knowledge (ajnatavi-indriya)

How are these three faculties established?

The faculty of "I shall come to know the unknown" belongs to one who has not yet directly realized the truths but is diligently preparing to do so. The faculty of full knowledge belongs to one who has already realized the truths and abides as a learner (shaiksha) still traversing the path. The faculty of one who possesses full knowledge belongs to the arhat whose task is complete, abiding as a non-learner (ashaiksha).

28.3 The Three Gates of Liberation

[T30.0436b25]

Furthermore, there are three gates of liberation (vimoksha-mukha).[9]

  1. The emptiness liberation gate (shunyata-vimoksha-mukha)
  2. The wishlessness liberation gate (apranihita-vimoksha-mukha)
  3. The signlessness liberation gate (animitta-vimoksha-mukha)

How are the three gates of liberation established?

Knowable objects (jneya) are, in brief, of two kinds: the existent and the non-existent. The existent is itself of two kinds: (1) the conditioned (samskrita) and (2) the unconditioned (asamskrita). Among conditioned things, this refers to the five skandhas (aggregates) bound to the three realms. Among unconditioned things, this refers to nirvana. These two — conditioned and unconditioned — taken together are called "the existent." As for what is spoken of as a self, or as a sentient being, a life-force, a being born, and so forth — this is called "the non-existent."

Because one perceives the faults and dangers in conditioned things, one has no yearning for them. Because of this absence of yearning, the wishlessness liberation gate is established.

Because one has no yearning for conditioned things, one gives rise to deep yearning for nirvana — perceiving it as supremely peaceful, supremely subtle, and as permanent emergence from conditioned existence. Because one perceives within it permanent emergence, the signlessness liberation gate is established.[10]

Regarding the non-existent — within that which does not exist — there is neither yearning nor absence of yearning. Just as it is non-existent, so it is known as non-existent and seen as non-existent. Based on this, the emptiness liberation gate is established.

This is how the three gates of liberation are established.

28.4 Factors Opposing and According with Training

[T30.0436c11]

What are the factors according with training? There are ten factors opposing training. To counteract them, one should know the ten factors according with training.

What are the ten factors opposing training?

(1) The youthful, flourishing, and attractive forms and appearances of women (matrigrama)[11] — these are the supreme obstruction for good men who are properly engaged in training.

(2) Generating attachment to the conditioned processes comprised within the transitory collection (satkaya).

(3) Laziness and indolence.

(4) The view of the transitory collection (satkaya-drishti).

(5) Attachment to fine flavors, relying on material nourishment (kavadikara-ahara).

(6) Generating desire, attachment, and craving for the many varieties of worldly idle elaboration (prapanca) on which the mind dwells.

(7) The drawbacks of yogic attention. What are these? They are of eleven kinds:[12]

  1. Vacillating doubt regarding the truths, the [three] treasures, the skandhas, and the fruits of karma.
  2. Physical heaviness (dausthulya) in one who delights in cultivating abandonment.
  3. For one who is lax and slack in cultivating shamatha (calm abiding) and vipashyana (insight): attending to drawbacks, torpor and drowsiness overwhelming the mind, causing extreme mental contraction.
  4. For one whose effort is too intense: physical fatigue and mental distress.
  5. For one whose effort is too weak: failure to advance and decline of wholesome qualities.
  6. Deep rejoicing in even small gains, fame, praise, or any pleasure.
  7. Restlessness and agitation — leaping about and fidgeting.
  8. Generating terror at the prospect of nirvana, which is the permanent extinction of the transitory collection (satkaya).
  9. Excessive argumentation in verbal exchanges and non-standard approaches to practice. Even when discussing dharma, delighting in refuting others and instigating disputes.
  10. The mind racing and scattering among the many and various objects previously seen, heard, and experienced.
  11. Forcibly brooding on matters that should not be pondered.

These are the drawbacks of yogic attention.

(8) Deep attachment to and relishing of the pleasure found in the dhyanas and meditative attainments.

(9) For one who desires to realize and enter the signless concentration (animitta-samadhi): being swept along and scattered among conditioned processes.

(10) When experiencing painful bodily feelings, up to life-threatening painful feelings: craving for life and hoping to survive, and following this hope, grieving, lamenting, and becoming bewildered.

These are the ten factors opposing training.

What are the factors according with training that counteract these ten factors opposing training? They are ten perceptions (samjna):[13]

  1. The perception of impurity (ashubha-samjna)
  2. The perception of impermanence (anitya-samjna)
  3. The perception of impermanence as suffering
  4. The perception of suffering as non-self
  5. The perception of revulsion toward food
  6. The perception that nothing in the entire world is delightful
  7. The perception of light (aloka-samjna)
  8. The perception of dispassion
  9. The perception of cessation
  10. The perception of death

When these ten perceptions are well cultivated, well practiced, and extensively developed, they can sever the ten obstructive and opposing factors of training.

In this regard, one should know there are four kinds of light: (1) the light of dharma, (2) the light of meaning, (3) the light of shamatha, and (4) the light of vipashyana. The perception of light is established based on these four kinds of light. Here, the intended meaning refers specifically to the perception that can sever the obstruction of yogic attention.

[T30.0437a15]

Furthermore, one should know there are ten additional factors according with training. What are the ten?

  1. Prior causes
  2. According with the teaching
  3. Practice in accord with principle
  4. Uninterrupted and earnest effort
  5. Keen aspiration
  6. Sustained yoga-power
  7. Stilling physical and mental heaviness
  8. Repeated observation
  9. Freedom from timidity
  10. Freedom from overestimation (adhimana)

What are prior causes? They are the maturation and accumulation of faculties from prior cultivation.

What is according with the teaching? It is teaching that is given correctly and in proper sequence.

What is practice in accord with principle? It is practicing correctly in accordance with the teaching. Such practice gives rise to right view.

What is uninterrupted and earnest effort? Through correct practice, one does not squander one's life regarding wholesome qualities but quickly accumulates them all.

What is keen aspiration? It is when a person generates yearning for the higher liberation, thinking: "When shall I be able to fully abide in that state in which the noble ones fully abide?"

What is sustained yoga-power? Two causes enable one to obtain sustained yoga-power: (1) being naturally endowed with sharp faculties, and (2) long-term continuous cultivation.

What is stilling physical and mental heaviness? There are four remedies:

(1) When someone, due to physical toil or exhaustion, generates physical and mental heaviness (dausthulya), this can be stilled by changing to a relaxed posture.

(2) When excessive examination (vitarka) and analysis (vicara) generate physical and mental heaviness, this can be stilled through the method of internal mental calming.

(3) When the mind becomes contracted and feeble, entangled by torpor and drowsiness, generating physical and mental heaviness, this can be stilled through the superior wisdom-dharma, vipashyana, and purifying attention.

(4) When physical and mental heaviness cannot yet be abandoned because afflictions have not been severed, this can be stilled through continuous diligent cultivation of the correct path.

What is repeated observation? Based on ethical discipline, one repeatedly examines one's conduct. Wrongdoing not yet committed, one does not let arise. Wrongdoing already committed, one abandons. Wholesome deeds not yet done, one does not shrink from. Wholesome deeds already established, one does not relinquish.

Furthermore, through the power of attention that observes whether afflictions have been severed or not, one repeatedly examines. If one knows they have been severed, one generates gladness. If one knows they have not been severed, one repeatedly and diligently cultivates the correct path.

What is freedom from timidity? Regarding what should later be known, seen, and realized — because of not yet having known, seen, or realized, timidity arises, and the mind becomes weary and depleted. When such timidity has arisen, one does not cling to it firmly but quickly eradicates it.

What is freedom from overestimation? It is being free from overestimation (adhimana) and distorted grasping regarding what one has obtained, contacted, and realized.[14] One generates the thought of having obtained only regarding what is truly obtained, the thought of having contacted only regarding what is truly contacted, and the thought of having realized only regarding what is truly realized.

These ten dharmas are what yogins who delight in training should study. At the beginning, middle, and end, they constantly accord with training without opposition. Therefore they are called the factors according with training.

28.5 Four Types of Yoga Failure

[T30.0437b23]

What is the destruction of yoga? The destruction of yoga is, in brief, of four kinds. What are the four?

  1. Permanent destruction of yoga
  2. Temporary destruction of yoga
  3. Destruction of yoga through loss of attainment
  4. Destruction of yoga through wrong conduct

[T30.0437b26]

Permanent destruction of yoga refers to a person without lineage (gotra).[15] Why? Because within their being there is no dharma capable of progressing toward nirvana; their supramundane yoga is permanently destroyed.

Temporary destruction of yoga refers to a person who possesses lineage. Why? Because within their being there exists a dharma capable of progressing toward nirvana. Although they may lack external conditions and even if a long time passes, they will eventually encounter the conditions, cultivate yoga, and bring it to manifestation. Having well cultivated it, they will attain parinirvana. Therefore their yoga is said to be only temporarily destroyed.

Destruction of yoga through loss of attainment refers to someone who loses what was obtained, contacted, or realized — whether knowledge, vision, or pleasant abiding in the present life (drishta-dharma-sukha-vihara).

Destruction of yoga through wrong conduct is twofold.

First, it refers to someone who does not practice diligently in accord with correct principle. Although they expend much effort, they accomplish nothing — they cannot accomplish any yoga or wholesome dharma.

Second, it refers to someone who has many afflictions and is by nature heavily polluted, yet is intellectually sharp with keen and powerful discernment, possessing innate acumen. They are skilled at absorbing what they have heard and are thorough in their learning. Whether they have studied a little or much, they may dwell in solitude. Some lay practitioners and monastics, being straightforward by nature, come to them. The person teaches them dharma, delighting their minds. Yet they practice deception, making false displays through various physical and verbal acts of feigned restraint.

Through these means, they attract offerings, respect, praise, and a reputation for great merit. They obtain various robes, food, bedding, medicines for illness, and material requisites. Kings, ministers, householders, and even merchants show them respect and reverence. All consider them to be an arhat.

Furthermore, regarding their devoted disciples — whether monastic or lay followers — they become attached, nurturing affection and following them along in order to attract more. They then reflect: "These monastic and lay disciples have faith in me and all consider me an arhat. Should they, relying on yogic attention and shamatha-vipashyana practice, come to question me, and I am unable to respond, they will lose their faith in me and no longer consider me an arhat. This will result in the loss of offerings and respect."

They further think: "I should now deliberate, examine, and establish a yoga for myself." Driven by these considerations — clinging to offerings, respect, and fame — they retreat alone to a secluded place and carefully deliberate, examine, and establish a yoga. However, this yoga does not accord with the sutras, does not reflect the vinaya, and contradicts the nature of dharma.

If there are bhikshus well-versed in the three baskets (tripitaka), the person conceals their yoga and does not wish to reveal it to them. But to their own lay and monastic disciples, they privately and secretly impart this yoga, keeping it from becoming public. Why? They fear that should someone well-versed in the three baskets hear of their yoga, they would examine it against the sutras and find it does not accord, illuminate it with the vinaya and find it does not reflect the vinaya, and investigate it through dharma and find it contradicts the nature of dharma. This would result in disbelief, reproach, dispute, and exposure — and kings, ministers, householders, and wealthy merchants would no longer respect and revere them, and they would no longer obtain robes, food, bedding, medicines, and material requisites.

Driven by their clinging to offerings and respect, they generate the notion that non-dharma is dharma, the notion of concealment, and the wrong aspiration to openly proclaim and teach non-dharma as dharma. Those who accept their views likewise generate the notion that non-dharma is dharma. Being deluded and dull, because they hold non-dharma to be dharma, although they practice diligently according to such teaching, all of it is wrong conduct.

This is called the destruction of yoga through wrong conduct. Counterfeit dharma (saddharma-pratirupa)[16] — which is not true dharma — obstructs true dharma. Bhikshus who diligently cultivate dhyana — these yogins — should correctly and fully understand these four kinds of yoga destruction and should abandon them.

28.6 The Four Yogas

[T30.0438a16]

What is yoga? It is four yogas. What are the four? (1) Faith (shraddha), (2) aspiration (chanda), (3) diligence (virya), and (4) method (upaya).

[T30.0438a17]

One should know that faith has two aspects and two bases.[17] The two aspects are: (1) the aspect of trusting accord, and (2) the aspect of purity. The two bases are: (1) the basis of examining the principles of dharmas, and (2) the basis of placing faith in the supernormal powers of [holy] persons.

Aspiration (chanda) is of four kinds. What are the four?

(1) Aspiration for attainment: It is when a person generates yearning for the higher liberation, as explained in detail above.

(2) Aspiration for inquiry: Having generated this yearning, one goes to the sangha-arama and approaches those among one's fellow practitioners of the holy life who possess knowledge and have accomplished the excellent wisdom of yoga — in order to hear what has not been heard and to bring what has been heard to completion.

(3) Aspiration for accumulating provisions: One generates yearning for the purity of the discipline of ethical restraint, the purity of sense-faculty restraint, moderation in eating, reduction of sleep, and correct dwelling in alertness (samprajanya) — each progressively surpassing the last.

(4) Aspiration according with yoga: One generates yearning and delight for practicing the path through uninterrupted application and earnest application.

Diligence (virya) — the effort that aims to accomplish what aspiration has set in motion — is of four kinds. What are the four?

(1) Diligence in learning: the courageous and resolute application of effort to hear what has not been heard and to bring what has been heard to completion.

(2) Diligence in reflection: taking what has been heard and, dwelling alone in a secluded place, reflecting on its meaning — deliberating, examining, and contemplating.

(3) Diligence in meditation: entering seclusion and from time to time diligently cultivating shamatha and vipashyana.

(4) Diligence in purifying obstructions: day and night, encouraging oneself to practice walking meditation and sitting meditation, purifying the mind from all obstructive dharmas through courageous and resolute application of effort.

Diligent method (upaya) is of four kinds. Through the power of the restraint of ethical discipline, one well guards one's mindfulness. Because one well guards mindfulness, one is able to be non-negligent — protecting the mind and cultivating wholesome dharmas. Because of non-negligence, the mind is directed inward to cultivate shamatha and the superior wisdom-dharma of vipashyana.

These four yogas have sixteen types in total.

[T30.0438b13]

In this regard, one should know: First, through faith, one develops deep conviction regarding what should be attained. Having developed conviction, one generates aspiration for wholesome dharmas. Because of aspiration, one encourages oneself day and night, abiding in diligence with firm courage. Having generated diligence, one takes up the method and can attain what has not been attained, contact what has not been contacted, and realize what has not been realized. Therefore these four dharmas are called yoga.

28.7 Types of Attention

[T30.0438b19]

What is attention (manaskara)? It is four types of attention. What are the four?

  1. Effortful operating attention
  2. Intermittent operating attention
  3. Uninterrupted operating attention
  4. Effortless operating attention

What is effortful operating attention? When a beginning practitioner is causing the mind to dwell internally and rest evenly, or is engaging in correct investigation of dharmas — so long as they have not yet attained the attention they are cultivating — at that time their attention operates with effort. Because one must exert extra force, bending and subduing the mind to make it rest on a single object, it is called effortful operating attention.

What is intermittent operating attention? After having attained the attention being cultivated, as one gradually advances through the worldly and supramundane paths, the attention to characteristics (lakshana-pratisanvedana-manaskara)[18] is interspersed with samadhi and with reflection — one is not yet able to practice exclusively and purely. Therefore it is called intermittent operating attention.

What is uninterrupted operating attention? It spans from after the attention to characteristics through to the attention of completed application (prayoga-nishtha-manaskara). This is called uninterrupted operating attention.

What is effortless operating attention? It is the attention to the fruit of completed application (prayoga-nishtha-phala-manaskara). This is called effortless operating attention.

Furthermore, there are four other kinds of attention:

  1. Conforming attention
  2. Antidote attention
  3. Purifying attention
  4. Observing attention

What is conforming attention? It is generating deep disenchantment regarding the object and engaging in correct practice, but not yet having severed the afflictions.

What is antidote attention? It is the attention that severs the afflictions.

What is purifying attention? When the mind is sunken and constricted, one takes up a pure and pleasing sign to encourage gladness.

What is observing attention? It is the attention of observation. Through the power of this attention, one sequentially observes whether afflictions have been severed or not.

[T30.0438c10]

Question: When attending properly to an object, how many aspects does one contemplate?

Answer: Four. What are the four? (1) The object aspect, (2) the condition aspect, (3) the aspect to be avoided, and (4) the aspect to be cultivated.

The object aspect: The image (pratibimba) that is similar to the knowable matter appears clearly and vividly.

The condition aspect concerns the conditions that support samadhi. Six prerequisites must be accumulated: (1) being guided by the teaching; (2) having a consistent practice; (3) having keen aspiration; (4) generating deep disenchantment regarding what should be abandoned; (5) being able to distinguish mental disturbance from mental stillness; and (6) being free from disruption by others — whether from people, non-humans, sounds, or activity. When vipashyana leads, one turns the mind inward and applies alert investigation to examine the conditions that support samadhi. When shamatha leads, one first cultivates discernment and then settles the mind with equal care — directing attention to the same basis of conditioned formations.

The aspect to be avoided is further of four kinds: (1) the sign of sinking, (2) the sign of agitation, (3) the sign of attachment, and (4) the sign of distraction. The sign of sinking: due to the object aspect and condition aspect, the mind becomes depressed. The sign of agitation: due to the object aspect and condition aspect, the mind becomes elated. The sign of attachment: due to the object aspect and condition aspect, the mind generates craving and attachment toward the object, creating various disturbances. The sign of distraction: due to the object aspect and condition aspect, the mind races and scatters outward. These various aspects have been explained above in the Ground of Meditative Equipoise (samahita-bhumi).

[T30.0438c26]

Question: When attention gives rise to resolute confidence (adhimoksha) regarding the object, how many types of resolute confidence are there?

Answer: Nine. What are the nine?[19]

  1. Resolute confidence with luminosity
  2. Resolute confidence without luminosity
  3. Dull resolute confidence
  4. Quick resolute confidence
  5. Narrow resolute confidence
  6. Vast resolute confidence
  7. Immeasurable resolute confidence
  8. Pure resolute confidence
  9. Impure resolute confidence

Nine types of resolute confidence are distinguished along four axes:

Luminosity: Resolute confidence with luminosity — clear apprehension of the sign of light, the mind vivid and bright. Resolute confidence without luminosity — the sign only dimly apprehended, the mind accompanied by obscurity.

Faculty sharpness: Dull resolute confidence, found in those of slower faculties. Quick resolute confidence, found in those of sharper faculties.

Scope: Narrow resolute confidence accompanies faith and aspiration that are themselves narrow, directed at a correspondingly narrow object. Vast accompanies broad faith and aspiration directed at a broad object. Immeasurable accompanies faith and aspiration that are boundless, directed at a limitless object.

Maturity: Pure resolute confidence is well cultivated, fully matured, brought to completion. Impure is the reverse — not yet cultivated through or brought to completion.

28.8 Yogic Activities and Three Types of Yogin

[T30.0439a16]

Question: How many kinds of yogic activities does a practitioner of yoga have?

Answer: Four. What are the four? (1) Cessation of the basis, (2) transformation of the basis, (3) thorough knowledge of the object, and (4) delight in the object. Here "the basis" (ashraya) refers to the practitioner's psychophysical constitution — the underlying body-mind foundation that either supports or hinders practice.

Cessation of the basis and transformation of the basis: Through diligent cultivation of yogic attention, the basis accompanied by heaviness (dausthulya) gradually ceases, and the basis accompanied by pliancy (prashrabdhi) gradually transforms. This is the yogic activity of cessation and transformation of the basis.

Thorough knowledge of the object and delight in the object: Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object are primary to the cessation and transformation of the basis — through the power of thorough knowledge and delight, the basis ceases and transforms. Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object take the purification of the basis as primary — through the power of the purified basis, thorough knowledge of the object becomes well purified, and delight in the object becomes well purified, operating at the time of accomplishing the purpose (krtyanushthana). These are the four yogic activities of the practitioner of yoga.

[T30.0439b01]

Question: How many types of yogin-practitioners are there?

Answer: Three. What are the three?[20]

  1. The beginning yogin (adikarmika)
  2. The practiced yogin (krita-parikarmaka)
  3. The yogin who has transcended attention (atikranta-manaskara)

What is the beginning yogin? There are two types of beginner: (1) the beginner in attention, and (2) the beginner in purifying afflictions.

The beginner in attention is a beginning person who rests on a single object and diligently cultivates attention, but has not yet attained the attention being cultivated and has not yet touched and realized mental one-pointedness (ekagrata).

The beginner in purifying afflictions has already attained the attention being cultivated. Wishing to purify the mind from afflictions, they undertake to adopt and diligently cultivate the attention to characteristics. This is the beginner in purifying afflictions.

What is the practiced yogin? Excluding the attention to characteristics, they have well practiced the remaining five attentions up through the attention of completed application.

What is the yogin who has transcended attention? They abide in the position of the attention to the fruit of completed application. Because they have transcended the attention cultivated through preparatory methods and abide in the fruit of cultivation, they are called one who has transcended attention.

[T30.0439b15]

Furthermore, beginning from the time one first generates the aspiration to cultivate wholesome dharmas until one has not yet given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration (nirvedha-bhagiya-kushala-mula)[21] — during that entire period, one is called a beginner. If one has already given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration — namely warmth (ushmanas), summit (murdhan), patience concordant with the truths (satya-anulomika-kshanti), and the supreme worldly dharma (laukikagra-dharma) — one is called practiced. If one has already entered the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama) and attained the direct realization of the truths, and is no longer dependent on others regarding the Buddha's noble teaching, nor can be led astray by other conditions — at that point, one is called one who has transcended attention. Because they have transcended attention dependent on others and abide in attention that does not depend on others, they are called one who has transcended attention.

28.9 Two Types of Cultivation

[T30.0439b22]

What is yogic cultivation? It is of two kinds: (1) cultivation through perceptions (samjna), and (2) cultivation through the factors conducive to awakening (bodhipakshya-dharma).

[T30.0439b24]

What is cultivation through perceptions?

When cultivating the worldly path, one cultivates the perception of the dangers of lower realms. When cultivating the path to nirvana, regarding the realm of abandonment (prahana-dhatu), the realm of dispassion (viraga-dhatu), and the realm of cessation (nirodha-dhatu), one perceives the supreme benefit of peacefulness and cultivates the perception of abandonment, the perception of dispassion, and the perception of cessation. When cultivating shamatha, one cultivates the perception of upper and lower in the category of calming. When cultivating vipashyana, one cultivates the perception of before and after in the category of insight.

The perception of upper and lower: One observes this body as it abides, as one wishes — from the top of the head down to the soles of the feet, filled with various types of impurities: the various hairs, bodily hair, nails, teeth, and so forth within this body, as explained in detail above.[22]

The perception of before and after: When a practitioner earnestly and sincerely attends to the observed aspects — skillfully apprehending, skillfully reflecting, skillfully understanding, and skillfully penetrating — this means: standing observes sitting; sitting observes lying down; or the later phase of action observes the earlier phase of action.[23]

This indicates observing through the practice of vipashyana the conditioned processes produced through dependent origination across the three times.

When the text says "standing observes sitting," this indicates that present attention observes future knowable conditioned processes. Why? The present attention has already arisen, hence it is called "standing." The future knowable processes have not yet arisen but are about to arise, hence they are called "sitting."

When the text says "sitting observes lying down," this indicates that present attention observes past knowable conditioned processes. Why? The present attention is about to cease, hence it is called "sitting." The past knowable processes have already ceased, hence they are called "lying down."

When the text says "the later phase of action observes the earlier phase," this indicates that present attention observes the immediately preceding attention that has ceased. Why? The apprehended attention that has already arisen and immediately ceased is called "the earlier phase." The apprehending attention that newly arises moment by moment, apprehending what has immediately ceased before it, is called "the later phase."

One should know that in this context, for the cultivation of shamatha and vipashyana, one cultivates the excellent perception of light in these two categories. This is called cultivation through perceptions.

28.10 The Thirty-Seven Factors of Awakening

[T30.0439c18]

What is cultivation through the factors conducive to awakening (bodhipakshya-dharma)? It is approaching and accumulating the thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening.[24] Cultivating them, practicing them, and extensively developing them — this is called cultivation through the factors conducive to awakening.

What are the thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening? They are: the four foundations of mindfulness (smrityupasthana), the four right efforts (samyak-pradhana), the four bases of supernatural power (riddhipada), the five faculties (indriya), the five powers (bala), the seven factors of awakening (bodhyanga), and the eightfold noble path (arya-ashtangika-marga). These seven groups represent a graduated path — each building on what precedes it — and they overlap intentionally, with qualities like mindfulness and wisdom appearing at every level in progressively purified form.

The four foundations of mindfulness: (1) the foundation of mindfulness of the body, (2) the foundation of mindfulness of feeling, (3) the foundation of mindfulness of the mind, and (4) the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas.

The four right efforts direct a single pattern of energy — generating aspiration, applying oneself, arousing energetic effort, urging and sustaining the mind — toward four aims: (1) abandoning evil and unwholesome dharmas already arisen; (2) preventing unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas from arising; (3) giving rise to wholesome dharmas not yet present; and (4) sustaining wholesome dharmas already established — maintaining them, cultivating them to perfection, and allowing them to increase and expand. Each of these, carried out with that full exertion, is right effort.

The four bases of supernatural power (riddhipada) take their names from the force that predominates in each: (1) the samadhi of aspiration (chanda), (2) the samadhi of diligence (virya), (3) the samadhi of mind (citta), and (4) the samadhi of investigation (mimamsa) — each endowed with the volitional formations of sustained effort.

The five faculties: (1) the faculty of faith, (2) the faculty of diligence, (3) the faculty of mindfulness, (4) the faculty of concentration, and (5) the faculty of prajna.

The five powers: (1) the power of faith, (2) the power of diligence, (3) the power of mindfulness, (4) the power of concentration, and (5) the power of prajna.

The seven factors of awakening: (1) the mindfulness factor of awakening, (2) the investigation-of-dharmas factor of awakening, (3) the diligence factor of awakening, (4) the joy factor of awakening, (5) the ease factor of awakening, (6) the concentration factor of awakening, and (7) the equanimity factor of awakening.

The eightfold noble path: (1) right view, (2) right thought, (3) right speech, (4) right action, (5) right livelihood, (6) right effort, (7) right mindfulness, and (8) right concentration.

28.11 The Four Foundations of Mindfulness

[T30.0440a13]

Now within this context, what is body? What is dwelling in contemplation of the body? What is mindfulness? What is the establishment of mindfulness (smrityupasthana)?[25]

The thirty-five aspects of body. In brief, there are thirty-five aspects of body. They are: (1) the internal body and (2) the external body; (3) the body belonging to the sense faculties and (4) the body not belonging to the sense faculties; (5) the body of the sentient-being class and (6) the body not of the sentient-being class; (7) the body accompanied by coarseness and (8) the body accompanied by pliancy; (9) the creating body [i.e., the great elements] and (10) the created body [i.e., derived form]; (11) the name-body [i.e., the mental skandhas] and (12) the form-body; (13) the hell-being body, (14) the animal body, (15) the ancestral-realm body,[26] (16) the human body, and (17) the deva body; (18) the body with consciousness and (19) the body without consciousness; (20) the core body and (21) the surface body; (22) the body subject to change and (23) the body not subject to change; (24) the female body, (25) the male body, and (26) the pandaka body; (27) the body of friends and (28) the body of non-friends; (29) the ordinary body and (30) the inferior body; (31) the middling body and (32) the excellent body; (33) the infant body, (34) the youthful body, and (35) the aged body. These are called the distinctions among the aspects of body.[27]

Dwelling in contemplation of the body is, in brief, of three kinds: the wisdom born of hearing, thinking, and meditation (shrutamayi, cintamayi, bhavanamayi) that takes the body as its basis. Through this wisdom, regarding all bodies and all aspects, one correctly examines and correctly investigates, observing and being aware in each instance.

Mindfulness means: taking the body as one's basis, one retains the correct dharma, reflects on the meaning of the dharma, and cultivates realization. Within the texts, within their meanings, and within the practice of realization, the mind does not forget. If one carefully reflects — "Have I correctly retained the true dharma, or not? Have I through wisdom properly understood such-and-such meanings, or not? Have I been able to directly realize such-and-such liberations, or not?" — to thus carefully and truly settle in one's mindfulness is called the establishment of mindfulness.

Furthermore, the establishment of mindfulness has three functions: guarding mindfulness, maintaining non-attachment toward objects, and settling on the meditative object.

"Guarding mindfulness" means, as stated: first guarding mindfulness, always maintaining attentive mindfulness.

"Non-attachment toward objects" means, as stated: mindfulness guards the mind in a state of equanimity — not grasping at signs, not grasping at secondary features — and so on broadly, including guarding the mental faculty and cultivating the discipline of the mental faculty.

"Settling on the meditative object" means, as stated: one settles mindfulness upon the four objects of meditation — the pervasive object, the purifying-conduct object, the skillful object, and the affliction-purifying object. Through these three aspects, properly settling in one's mindfulness, it is called the establishment of mindfulness.

What is feeling (vedana)?[28] Feeling is classified along three axes. By hedonic tone: pleasant, painful, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant. By associated consciousness: all three tones arise as bodily feeling (with the five sense-consciousnesses) and as mental feeling (with the mental consciousness). By orientation: feeling with relish (pleasant, painful, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant), feeling without relish (likewise threefold), feeling based on indulgence (likewise threefold), and feeling based on renunciation (pleasant, painful, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant). Thus there are altogether twenty-one kinds of feeling, or nine kinds of feeling.

What is mind (citta)?[29] Mind appears in twenty forms, organized in three tiers. During ordinary activity, six kinds arise: the mind with attachment and the mind free from it; the mind with aversion and the mind free from it; the mind with delusion and the mind free from it. During meditation, at the stage of purifying the hindrances, eight kinds arise: the contracted mind and the scattered mind; the sunken mind and the uplifted mind; the agitated mind and the non-agitated mind; the tranquil mind and the non-tranquil mind. At the stage of purifying the afflictions, six further kinds arise: the concentrated mind and the unconcentrated mind; the well-cultivated mind and the uncultivated mind; the well-liberated mind and the not-well-liberated mind. Thus there are altogether twenty kinds of mind.

What are dharmas? They are: attachment and the dharma that disciplines attachment; aversion and the dharma that disciplines aversion; delusion and the dharma that disciplines delusion; contraction and scattering; sinking and uplifting; agitation and non-agitation; tranquility and non-tranquility; concentration and non-concentration; good cultivation and non-cultivation; good liberation and not-good liberation. Thus one should know that these establish twenty kinds of dharmas in the categories of black and white, defiled and pure.

Furthermore, pleasant feeling is feeling arising from contact conducive to pleasant feeling, included in the category of balanced feeling. This is called pleasant feeling. When associated with the five sense-consciousnesses, it is called bodily feeling. When associated with the mental consciousness, it is called mental feeling. As with contact conducive to pleasant feeling, so too contact conducive to painful feeling and contact conducive to neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling produce, respectively, feeling included in the category of unbalanced feeling and feeling included in the category of neither-balanced-nor-unbalanced feeling. These are called painful feeling and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. When associated with the five sense-consciousnesses, they are called bodily feeling; when associated with the mental consciousness, they are called mental feeling.

Among these feelings, those that accord with nirvana, accord with discernment, lead to ultimate emergence, ultimate freedom from affliction, and the ultimate perfection of the holy life — these are called feeling without relish. Those that fall within the realms are called feeling with relish. Those bound to the form and formless realms, or that accord with the abandonment of desire, are called feeling based on renunciation. Those bound to the desire realm, or that do not accord with the abandonment of desire, are called feeling based on indulgence.

Furthermore, the mind with attachment: when, regarding a lovable object, one is ensnared by the entanglement of attachment. The mind free from attachment: when one is free from such entanglement. The mind with aversion: when, regarding a hateful object, one is ensnared by the entanglement of aversion. The mind free from aversion: when one is free from such entanglement. The mind with delusion: when, regarding an object that occasions confusion, one is ensnared by the entanglement of delusion. The mind free from delusion: when one is free from such entanglement. These six kinds of mind should all be understood as the three affliction-categories and their three antidote-categories that arise during the active state.

The contracted mind: when through right practice one binds the mind to an internal object. The scattered mind: when the mind flows and disperses outward toward the five strands of sensual pleasure. The sunken mind: accompanied by torpor and drowsiness. The uplifted mind: when the object appears with clear vividness. The agitated mind: when, through excessive uplift, one is tossed about by the entanglement of restlessness. The non-agitated mind: when, in both the uplifted state and the contracted state, one attains balanced equanimity. The tranquil mind: when one has gained freedom from the hindrances. The non-tranquil mind: when one has not yet gained freedom from the hindrances.

The concentrated mind: when, having gained freedom from the hindrances, one is further able to enter the root dhyanas. The unconcentrated mind: when one has not yet been able to enter them. The well-cultivated mind: when one has practiced this samadhi for a long time through sustained habituation, such that one can enter at will, without difficulty, without obstruction, and with speed. The uncultivated mind: its characteristics are the opposite of this, as should be understood. The well-liberated mind: when one has been utterly and completely liberated from everything. The not-well-liberated mind: when one has not been liberated from everything, or one's liberation is not complete.

These fourteen kinds of mind should all be understood as arising during the meditative state. Among these, eight kinds arise during the meditative state at the stage of purifying the hindrances — from the contracted and scattered mind through to the tranquil and non-tranquil mind. Six kinds arise during the meditative state at the stage of purifying the afflictions — from the concentrated and unconcentrated mind through to the well-liberated and not-well-liberated mind.

Furthermore, when hindrances are present internally, the practitioner knows: "I have hindrances." When absent: "I am free from hindrances." And when they arise or cease, one knows this too.

One applies this same fourfold awareness to fetters — of the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mental faculty. In each case one knows: whether a fetter is present or absent, whether an unarisen fetter has come to arise, or whether an arisen fetter has been dispelled.

One applies it also to each of the seven factors of awakening. When the mindfulness factor of awakening is present internally, one knows it; when absent, one knows it; when it arises for the first time, one knows it; and when it has arisen and abides — not forgotten, cultivated to perfection, further practiced, increased, and expanded — one knows this too. The same applies to the investigation-of-dharmas, diligence, joy, ease, concentration, and equanimity factors of awakening.

If one is thus able to truly and comprehensively know the intrinsic nature, causes and conditions, faults, and antidotes of all defiled dharmas — this constitutes the essence of the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas.

As explained for dwelling in contemplation of the body regarding mindfulness and the establishment of mindfulness, so too for feeling, mind, and dharmas one should understand accordingly.

What is dwelling in contemplation of the internal body? What is dwelling in contemplation of the external body? What is dwelling in contemplation of the internal-and-external body?[30]

Six distinct frameworks are offered for understanding what "internal," "external," and "internal-and-external" mean — each highlighting a different principle of classification. The first distinguishes by the class of beings whose bodies are observed; the second by whether form belongs to the sense faculties; the third by the quality of the meditator's concentration; the fourth by whether form is creating (the great elements) or created (derived form); the fifth by the presence or absence of consciousness; the sixth by the distinction between the core body and the surface body. All six extend from body-contemplation to feeling, mind, and dharmas.

First interpretation: When one takes as object the bodily form belonging to one's own sentient-being class and dwells in contemplation of the body — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object form not belonging to the sentient-being class — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the bodily form of other sentient beings — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. When one takes as object the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on one's own sentient-being bodily form and dwells in the three contemplations — this is the contemplation of internal feeling, mind, and dharmas. When one takes as object the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on form not belonging to the sentient-being class — this is the contemplation of external feeling, mind, and dharmas. When one takes as object the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on other sentient beings' bodily form — this is the contemplation of internal-and-external feeling, mind, and dharmas.

Second interpretation: When one takes as object form that belongs to the sense faculties, that is grasped and sentient — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object form that does not belong to the sense faculties, that is ungrasped and insentient — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object form that does not belong to the sense faculties yet is grasped and sentient — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. Similarly, when one takes as object the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly.

Third interpretation: When one takes as object one's own internal form in the concentrated state, accompanied by pliancy — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object one's own internal form in the unconcentrated state, accompanied by coarseness — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the form of others, whether accompanied by pliancy or coarseness — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. Similarly, for the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly.

Fourth interpretation: When one takes as object the internal creating great elements — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object the external creating great elements — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the derived form belonging to the sense faculties and their objects, produced from the creating great elements — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. Similarly, for the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly.

Fifth interpretation: When one takes as object the internal form of the body with consciousness — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object the form of a body without consciousness belonging to the sentient-being class — at the stage of lividity and so forth [i.e., a corpse] — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the fact that the form of a body presently without consciousness was possessed of consciousness in the past, and that the form of a body presently with consciousness will be without consciousness in the future — contemplating their similarity of nature and equality of nature — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. Similarly, for the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly.

Sixth interpretation: When one takes as object the characteristics of one's own core body — hair, nails, teeth, and so forth — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object the characteristics of another's core body — hair, nails, teeth, and so forth — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the characteristics of one's own surface body in its states of change and non-change, lividity and so forth, together with the characteristics of another's surface body in its states of change and non-change, lividity and so forth — contemplating their similarity of nature and equality of nature — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body. Similarly, for the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly.

Such and similar distinctions regarding body, feeling, mind, and dharmas should be known as manifold. Here we have displayed but a few of the many distinctions.

The four inversions.[31] Furthermore, in order to counteract the four inversions (catur-viparyasa), the Blessed One established the four foundations of mindfulness.

In order to counteract the inversion of perceiving purity in what is impure, the foundation of mindfulness of the body was established. The Buddha, the Blessed One, within the contemplation of the body taught the characteristics of impurity, in accordance with the four charnel ground paths. If one reflects extensively upon these, one eliminates the inversion of purity regarding what is impure.

In order to counteract the inversion of perceiving pleasure in what is suffering, the foundation of mindfulness of feeling was established. Through dwelling in contemplation of feelings, one truly knows that all feelings whatsoever are entirely suffering. Thus one eliminates the inversion of pleasure regarding what is suffering.

In order to counteract the inversion of perceiving permanence in what is impermanent, the foundation of mindfulness of the mind was established. Through being able to know the various distinctions of the mind with attachment and so forth — passing through days and nights, moments and instants, through manifold and numerous varieties — one sees the nature of the mind's arising and ceasing. Thus one eliminates the inversion of permanence regarding what is impermanent.

In order to counteract the inversion of perceiving self in what is non-self, the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas was established. Because previously one has had the affliction of the view of self and has lacked the wholesome dharma of the view of non-self, one has generated the view of self regarding the skandhas. Through dwelling in contemplation of dharmas, truly knowing the specific and shared characteristics of the skandhas that are falsely conceived, one eliminates the inversion of self regarding what is non-self.

The four types of ignorance regarding the self. There is another rationale. Worldly beings, not knowing the skandhas as they truly are — as merely skandha-nature, merely dharma-nature — construct a false self in four ways: they imagine the self depends on the body, that it experiences suffering and pleasure, that it is itself mind or consciousness, and that it becomes defiled or pure through dharmas and non-dharmas. Against each of these four delusions, one foundation of mindfulness was established: body-mindfulness to remove delusion regarding what the self supposedly depends upon; feeling-mindfulness to remove delusion regarding what it supposedly experiences; mind-mindfulness to remove the grasping at mind, mental faculty, and consciousness as a self; and dharma-mindfulness to remove delusion regarding that by which the supposed self becomes defiled or pure.

The four dimensions of karma. There is another rationale. That upon which one depends when creating karma; that for the sake of which one creates karma; the agent that creates karma; and that by means of which one creates karma — to comprehensively reveal all of these, the four foundations of mindfulness were established. One should know: one depends upon the body when creating karma; one creates karma for the sake of feeling; the mind is the agent that creates karma; and one creates karma by means of wholesome and unwholesome dharmas.

The four dimensions of contamination and purification. There is another rationale. That upon which one depends for contamination and purification; that for the sake of which one generates contamination and purification; the one who becomes contaminated or pure; and that by means of which one becomes contaminated or pure — to comprehensively reveal all of these, the four foundations of mindfulness were established. One should know: one depends upon the body for contamination and purification; one generates contamination and purification for the sake of feeling; the mind is the one who becomes contaminated or pure; and one becomes contaminated or pure by means of dharmas.

The meaning of "establishment of mindfulness."[32]

Question: What is the meaning of "establishment of mindfulness" (smrityupasthana)?

Answer: Both "that in which mindfulness is established" and "that by which mindfulness is established" are called "establishment of mindfulness."

"That in which mindfulness is established" refers to the object-based establishment of mindfulness (alambana-smrityupasthana) — that is, the four objects: body, feeling, mind, and dharmas.

"That by which mindfulness is established" refers to prajna and mindfulness holding concentration. This is the intrinsic-nature establishment of mindfulness (svabhava-smrityupasthana). The remaining associated mental factors constitute the associated establishment of mindfulness (samprayukta-smrityupasthana).

Furthermore, the wholesome paths — with outflows and without outflows — that arise based on body, feeling, mind, and dharmas are all called "establishment of mindfulness." These are of three kinds: (1) born of hearing (shrutamayi), (2) born of thinking (cintamayi), and (3) born of meditation (bhavanamayi).[33] Those born of hearing and thinking are with outflows only. Those born of meditation may be either with or without outflows.

End of Fascicle Twenty-Eight of the Yogacarabhumi-sastra

  1. The three superior trainings (tri-shiksha) — discipline, mind, and prajna — are one of the most fundamental frameworks of the Buddhist path, found across all schools and traditions. They organize the entire practice from ethical conduct through meditative development to liberating insight. The Sravakabhumi's treatment here establishes the logical necessity, sequence, mutual dependence, and unique character of these three trainings within the context of the shravaka path. ↩︎

  2. "The certainty of correctness" (samyaktva-niyama, 正性離生) — literally "correct nature, separated from birth" — is a Sarvastivada/Yogacara term for definitive entry onto the noble path. At this point, the practitioner has attained such certainty regarding the four noble truths that regression to an ordinary person's status is impossible. This is equivalent to entering the "stream" (srota) at stream-entry. The text emphasizes that this decisive breakthrough requires the support of dhyana — not merely conceptual understanding. ↩︎

  3. The claim that direct realization of the truths (satya-abhisamaya) requires dhyana as its basis is the normative Yogacara position. Other Buddhist schools, however, allow for stream-entry without prior dhyana attainment. The Theravada commentarial tradition recognizes the shushka-vipashyanaka (dry-insight practitioner) who gains noble attainment through pure insight. The Sravakabhumi itself acknowledges this possibility implicitly below, where it notes that prajna can sometimes lead to the development of the mind-training. ↩︎

  4. This graduated chain — from ethical purity through freedom from remorse, gladness, ease, right concentration, knowledge-and-vision, disenchantment, dispassion, liberation, to final nirvana — is one of the most widely attested path-formulations in early Buddhist literature (cf. AN 11.1–2, Kimatthiya Sutta; DN 34; MN 70; Visuddhimagga I.1-8). Each factor arises naturally from the preceding one, demonstrating that the three trainings are not three independent practices but form a single, causally interconnected progression. ↩︎

  5. The claim that the three superior trainings are unique to the Buddhist teaching and not shared with non-Buddhist paths (tirthika) is a standard Yogacara position. While non-Buddhist practitioners may cultivate forms of ethical discipline and meditative concentration, only within the Buddha's teaching are these directed toward the realization of the four noble truths and the complete severance of afflictions. The qualifier "superior" (adhika) marks these trainings as oriented toward liberation, distinguishing them from their non-Buddhist counterparts. ↩︎

  6. This describes what Sarvastivada and Yogacara scholastic literature calls the "dry-insight" (shushka-vipashyanaka) practitioner — a noble disciple who attains the path of seeing (darshana-marga) and direct realization of the truths without first developing the root dhyanas, relying instead on access concentration (anagamya-samapatti). Having entered the noble path through insight, they then develop the seven factors of awakening — which include the concentration factor — to proceed through the path of cultivation. This is the reverse of the more common sequence where the training in mind precedes the training in prajna. ↩︎

  7. These three types of persons differentiate the level of prior meditative attainment at the time of entering the path of seeing. A person with no prior dispassion has not yet attenuated the desire-realm afflictions and attains only stream-entry upon direct realization. One who has "partially abandoned desire" (倍離欲) has already weakened some of the nine grades of desire-realm afflictions through preliminary meditation; upon direct realization, they simultaneously attain once-returning. One who has "fully abandoned desire" (已離欲) has completely eliminated the desire-realm afflictions through dhyana; upon direct realization, they immediately attain non-returning. This scheme is standard Sarvastivada Abhidharma (cf. Abhidharmakosha VI). ↩︎

  8. The three faculties (tri-indriya), also called the three supramundane or undefiled faculties, span the entire arc of the noble path: (1) the faculty of "I shall come to know the unknown" is active during entry into the path of seeing, when the truths are being realized for the first time; (2) the faculty of full knowledge operates throughout the path of cultivation, as the learner progressively eliminates remaining afflictions; (3) the faculty of one who possesses full knowledge is present at the arhat stage, when the task is complete. These three correspond to the three stages of the noble path: seeing, cultivation, and beyond-training. ↩︎

  9. The three gates of liberation (tri-vimoksha-mukha) are three complementary contemplative approaches through which practitioners enter nirvana. Rather than three separate methods, they represent three aspects of a single liberating insight, each oriented toward a different facet of reality: emptiness contemplates the non-existence of what is falsely imputed (self, being); wishlessness contemplates the faults of what exists in conditioned form; signlessness contemplates the unconditioned — nirvana — as free from the marks of conditioned existence. This threefold framework is widely attested (cf. Visuddhimagga XXI; Abhidharmakosha VII). ↩︎

  10. The connection between "permanent emergence" (永出離) and the "signlessness" (animitta) liberation gate: the "signs" (nimitta) in question are the marks or characteristics of conditioned existence — arising, persisting, and ceasing. Nirvana, as permanent emergence from conditioned existence, is free from these signs. When one contemplates nirvana as utterly devoid of the marks that characterize the conditioned — and deeply yearns for this signless state — one enters the signlessness liberation gate. The term 無相 (literally "without marks") thus refers not to featurelessness in a vacuous sense, but to freedom from the defining characteristics of samsaric existence. ↩︎

  11. The Chinese 母邑 (literally "mother township") is Xuanzang's literary rendering of Sanskrit matrigrama, the standard Buddhist canonical term for women as a class. The text identifies this as the "supreme obstruction" specifically for male renunciants engaged in the training. This reflects the monastic context of the Sravakabhumi, where the audience is primarily bhikshus. ↩︎

  12. These eleven drawbacks of yogic attention enumerate the specific psychological obstacles that arise during meditation practice. They range from foundational doubt (i) through physical discomfort (ii–v), emotional disturbances (vi–viii), interpersonal difficulties (ix), and attentional failures (x–xi). The inclusion of both "too intense effort" (iv) and "too weak effort" (v) reflects the Buddhist emphasis on balanced effort. The terror of nirvana (viii) is a distinctive obstacle: the practitioner's deeply rooted attachment to the transitory collection (satkaya) produces involuntary dread at the prospect of its permanent cessation. ↩︎

  13. The ten perceptions (dasha-samjna) function as antidotes corresponding to the ten opposing factors. Their order mirrors the obstacles: (1) impurity counters attraction to women, (2) impermanence counters attachment to the transitory collection, (3–4) impermanence-as-suffering and suffering-as-non-self counter laziness and identity-view, (5) revulsion toward food counters food attachment, (6) nothing delightful counters fascination with idle elaboration, (7) light counters the eleven drawbacks of yogic attention, (8) dispassion counters attachment to dhyana pleasure, (9) cessation counters scattering during signless concentration, and (10) death counters clinging to life under duress. ↩︎

  14. Overestimation (adhimana, 增上慢) is a specific form of pride in which a practitioner falsely believes they have attained a realization or state that they have not in fact attained. It differs from ordinary pride (mana) in that it specifically concerns spiritual attainments. The Sravakabhumi emphasizes the importance of accurately assessing one's progress — claiming genuine attainment only for what is genuinely attained — as one of the ten factors essential for successful training. ↩︎

  15. The concept of a "person without lineage" (agotra-pudgala) — one who permanently lacks the potential for nirvana — is among the most controversial claims in Yogacara thought. The Lankavatara Sutra and Lotus Sutra explicitly reject this position, asserting that all sentient beings possess buddha-nature (tathagatagarbha). The Madhyamaka tradition and virtually all of East Asian Buddhist thought likewise reject it. Later Yogacara thinkers, including Paramartha, sought to harmonize the two positions. The vast majority of Buddhist traditions today — including all Tibetan schools, all East Asian schools, and the Theravada tradition — affirm that all beings possess the capacity for liberation. Asanga's presentation here represents the early, unmodified Yogacara stance, not the consensus of the Buddhist tradition. ↩︎

  16. "Counterfeit dharma" (saddharma-pratirupa, 像似正法) — dharma that resembles the true teaching but is not genuine — is one of the most important concepts in Buddhist institutional life. The text identifies three diagnostic criteria: (1) examine it against the sutras, (2) illuminate it with the vinaya, (3) investigate it through dharma. What fails all three is counterfeit. A parallel warning appears in SN 16.13 (Saddhamma-patirupa Sutta), where the Buddha predicts that the true Dharma will decline when counterfeit teachings proliferate. ↩︎

  17. Buddhist faith (shraddha) differs from theistic faith. It has two aspects: "trusting accord" (prasada) — the heart's natural confidence in what is true and good — and "purity" — the mind's clarity that arises through confidence. Its two bases show that faith in Buddhism is not blind: it rests on rational examination of dharma principles as well as on trust in the verified attainments of realized practitioners. The Sravakabhumi places faith first among the four yogas because it provides the motivational foundation for the entire practice. ↩︎

  18. The "attention to characteristics" (lakshana-pratisanvedana-manaskara, 了相作意) is the first of seven types of attention that structure the entire path of purifying afflictions. These seven attentions — detailed in fascicle 33 — progress from initial analysis of the coarse and peaceful characteristics of the realms through to the final effortless attention of the arhat. Here in section 28.7, the text introduces the attention to characteristics as a reference point for the four effort-based attention types. ↩︎

  19. The nine types of resolute confidence (adhimoksha) classify the quality of the practitioner's meditation experience along three dimensions: luminosity vs. dimness (whether the mind's clarity is strong or weak), faculty sharpness (dull vs. quick), and scope (narrow, vast, immeasurable). The final pair — pure vs. impure — assesses whether the meditation has reached full maturation. This nine-fold classification provides a diagnostic framework for both the practitioner and the teacher to assess the quality and direction of practice. ↩︎

  20. The three types of yogin are presented in two complementary frameworks. The first framework (beginner/practiced/transcended) is based on progress through the seven attentions. The second framework maps the same three stages onto the path of accumulation, the four preparatory stages (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma), and entry onto the noble path. Together they show that "beginner" refers not just to someone who has recently started but to anyone who has not yet achieved the decisive stages of the path. ↩︎

  21. The "wholesome roots concordant with penetration" (nirvedha-bhagiya-kushala-mula, 順決擇分善根) are four stages immediately preceding entry onto the noble path: (1) warmth (ushmanas) — first signs of the fire of wisdom, (2) summit (murdhan) — the peak from which one may still fall back, (3) patience (kshanti) — acceptance of the four noble truths, and (4) supreme worldly dharma (laukikagra-dharma) — the highest mundane attainment, immediately preceding the supramundane path of seeing. These four form the bridge between ordinary and noble status. ↩︎

  22. The "perception of upper and lower" is a contemplation of the body's impurity from the crown of the head to the soles of the feet. This practice — enumerating the body's constituents (hair, nails, teeth, etc.) — is the standard body-contemplation found across all Buddhist meditation traditions (cf. MN 10, Satipatthana Sutta; Visuddhimagga VIII). Here it is classified under shamatha cultivation through perceptions. ↩︎

  23. The "perception of before and after" uses a striking metaphor: the four postures (standing, sitting, lying down, and walking) are reinterpreted as temporal positions. "Standing" = the present (already arisen); "sitting" = the future (about to arise); "lying down" = the past (already ceased). This metaphorical reading transforms a seemingly simple mindfulness instruction into a profound contemplation of momentary arising and ceasing — the practitioner uses present awareness to observe the flow of conditioned processes across the three times. This is classified under vipashyana cultivation through perceptions. ↩︎

  24. The thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening (saptatrimshad-bodhipakshya-dharma, 三十七菩提分法) constitute the most comprehensive and widely attested classification of the Buddhist path in early and Abhidharma literature. The seven groups — four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, and the eightfold noble path — overlap intentionally: mindfulness appears in the foundations, as a faculty, as a power, as a factor of awakening, and as right mindfulness in the eightfold path. This repetition reflects not redundancy but the progressive deepening of the same qualities across stages of the path. ↩︎

  25. The four foundations of mindfulness (catvari smrityupasthanani, 四念住) — body, feeling, mind, and dharmas — are the most widely practiced meditation framework in Buddhist tradition (cf. MN 10, Satipatthana Sutta; DN 22). The Sravakabhumi's treatment is distinctively Yogacara-Abhidharma: rather than giving meditation instructions, it provides scholastic analysis — classifying body into thirty-five aspects, unpacking the meaning of contemplation and mindfulness, and offering six distinct interpretive frameworks for understanding "internal," "external," and "internal-and-external." ↩︎

  26. The Chinese 祖父國 (literally "grandfather's country" or "ancestral land") is Xuanzang's rendering for the preta realm — the domain of hungry ghosts (preta, pitri). The term derives from the Vedic/Hindu concept of the pitriloka (world of the ancestors/fathers), which in Buddhist cosmology became the realm of beings who, through attachment and miserliness, are reborn as pretas. ↩︎

  27. The Sravakabhumi's 35-fold classification of the body is considerably more elaborate than the Pali Satipatthana Sutta's enumeration of 31 anatomical parts (MN 10, DN 22). The Yogacara scheme includes not only physical parts but also categories such as "body with consciousness" vs. "body without consciousness," "male body," "female body," and "body of an indeterminate sex" — reflecting the Abhidharma tendency to exhaustive taxonomic analysis. Analayo (Satipatthana, ch. VI) discusses the Pali body-contemplation in detail. ↩︎

  28. The twenty-one kinds arise from seven overlapping threefold classifications — each applying the three basic hedonic tones (pleasant, painful, neither-painful-nor-pleasant) along a different axis: associated consciousness (bodily vs. mental), relish or its absence (conditioned realms vs. accord with nirvana), and basis (indulgence vs. renunciation). The "nine kinds" collapses this to one selected threefold application (cf. MN 137). ↩︎

  29. The twenty kinds of mind divide into two groups. The first six (with/without attachment, aversion, delusion) arise during the active state — ordinary daily activity — representing the three root afflictions and their antidotes. The remaining fourteen arise during the meditative state and divide between those purifying the hindrances (eight kinds, from contracted/scattered through tranquil/non-tranquil) and those purifying the afflictions (six kinds, from concentrated/unconcentrated through well-liberated/not-well-liberated). This parallels the Satipatthana Sutta's sixteen types of mind (cf. MN 10), with Yogacara additions. ↩︎

  30. The six interpretive frameworks reveal the Yogacara's characteristic interest in exhaustive classification — each offering a different principle for distinguishing "internal" from "external." For instance, the first distinguishes by class of beings (self vs. other sentient beings vs. non-sentient), while the fifth contrasts bodies with and without consciousness (living vs. corpse) and contemplates their temporal interchange. Each framework extends from body through feeling, mind, and dharmas. The text itself notes these are "but a few of the many distinctions." ↩︎

  31. The four inversions (catur-viparyasa, 四顛倒) — perceiving purity in the impure, pleasure in suffering, permanence in the impermanent, and self in the non-self — are among the most fundamental diagnostic concepts in Buddhist soteriology. They explain why beings remain in samsara: the ordinary mind systematically misperceives reality along these four axes. This fourfold correspondence is found across traditions (cf. Visuddhimagga XXII; Abhidharmakosha VI). ↩︎

  32. The threefold analysis of "establishment of mindfulness" (smrityupasthana) as (1) object-based (alambana), (2) intrinsic-nature (svabhava), and (3) associated (samprayukta) is a distinctively Abhidharma contribution, found in the Abhidharmakosha (VI.14–15) and Yogacarabhumi. The object-based type identifies what is contemplated (body, feeling, mind, dharmas). The intrinsic-nature type identifies the essential mental factors that constitute the practice — prajna and mindfulness holding samadhi — distinguishing the practice from mere attention or concentration. The associated type accounts for all other mental factors present during the practice. ↩︎

  33. The threefold classification of wisdom — born of hearing (shrutamayi-prajna), born of reflection (cintamayi-prajna), and born of cultivation (bhavanamayi-prajna) — is fundamental to Buddhist epistemology. The first two are necessarily mundane (sasrava); the third can be either mundane or supramundane (anasrava). This classification governs the entire structure of the Sravakabhumi's training path: one hears the teaching, reflects on its meaning, then cultivates it in meditation. ↩︎
