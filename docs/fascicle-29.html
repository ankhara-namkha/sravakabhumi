Composed by Asanga Translated into Chinese by Tripitaka Master Xuanzang, by imperial decree English translation from the Chinese (Taisho Tripitaka, Vol. 30, No. 1579)

Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Second Yoga Place, Part Four

本地分中聲聞地第十三第二瑜伽處之四

29.1 The Four Right Efforts

[T30.0442a26]

Having thus continuously practiced the four foundations of mindfulness (smrityupasthana), one has removed the coarser inversions and gained comprehension of wholesome and unwholesome dharmas. From this point, without interruption:[1]

One strives to prevent unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas from arising, abandon those already arisen, generate wholesome dharmas not yet present, and sustain those already established so they are not forgotten — as broadly explained above — up through uplifting the mind and holding the mind.

What are evil and unwholesome dharmas? They are the defiled bodily, verbal, and mental karma associated with desire-entanglements. These belong to the category of evil conduct of body, speech, and mind, together with all the afflictions that give rise to them.[2] When they have not yet combined and have not yet manifested, they are called "unarisen." When they have already combined and have already manifested, they are called "already arisen."

What are all wholesome dharmas? They are the antidotes to those unwholesome dharmas, the antidotes to the hindrances (nivarana), and the antidotes to the fetters (samyojana).[3] "Unarisen" and "already arisen" should be understood as above.

Aspiration regarding unwholesome dharmas. When evil and unwholesome dharmas have not yet arisen — when they have not yet combined — for the purpose of preventing their arising, one generates the aspiration: "I shall cause each and every one of them never to arise again." This is called generating aspiration for preventing unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas from arising.

When evil and unwholesome dharmas have already arisen — when they have already combined — for the purpose of abandoning them, one generates the aspiration: "I shall not tolerate any of them; I shall sever, destroy, and eliminate them all." This is called generating aspiration for the abandonment of already-arisen evil and unwholesome dharmas.

Unwholesome dharmas classified by temporal object. Furthermore, all evil and unwholesome dharmas arise either in relation to past events, future events, or present events. Accordingly, these dharmas either relate to non-visible objects or to visible objects. Those relating to past and future events are called "relating to non-visible objects." Those relating to present events are called "relating to visible objects."

Self-urging and vigorous effort distinguished by object. Self-urging addresses evil and unwholesome dharmas that relate to non-visible objects — past and future events held in thought. One prevents the unarisen from arising and permanently severs those already present. For dharmas relating to visible objects — present events encountered directly — the same preventive and severing purpose applies, but one exerts vigorous right effort. This is called vigorous effort.

Why? Only through firm self-urging and vigorous right effort can one either prevent them from arising again or permanently sever them.

Self-urging and vigorous effort by grade of affliction. Furthermore, for lower and middle-grade entanglements, one applies self-urging; for upper-grade entanglements, vigorous effort — in each case preventing unarisen afflictions from arising and permanently severing those already arisen.

Self-urging and vigorous effort by temporal domain. Furthermore, when engaging with past objects: at such times, one does not allow afflictions to arise in relation to them. If through a lapse of mindfulness they briefly arise, one does not tolerate them but swiftly severs, destroys, eliminates, and expels them. As with past objects, so too with future objects one should understand likewise. Thus, unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas are prevented from arising, and those already arisen are severed. This is called self-urging.

When engaging with present objects of attention: at such times, one does not allow afflictions to arise in relation to them. If through a lapse of mindfulness they briefly arise, one does not tolerate them but swiftly severs, destroys, eliminates, and expels them. Thus, unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas are prevented from arising, and those already arisen are severed. This is called vigorous effort.

Self-urging and vigorous effort by cause of arising. Unwholesome dharmas also differ in how they arise. Some arise solely through the power of conceptual discrimination, not through the power of the object — these occur while one is at rest, when one thinks about past or future objects and the unwholesome dharmas arise from that thinking. Others arise through the combined power of conceptual discrimination and the object — these occur during the active state, when one relates to present objects and the unwholesome dharmas arise from that encounter. At such times, there is certainly also improper mental attention (ayonisho-manaskara).[4]

One should know: regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas arising solely through the power of conceptual discrimination and not through the power of the object — one prevents the unarisen from arising and severs those already arisen. This is called self-urging. For those arising through both the power of conceptual discrimination and the power of the object, the same preventive and severing effort applies. This is called vigorous effort.

Aspiration regarding wholesome dharmas. Generating aspiration for giving rise to unarisen wholesome dharmas means: regarding all wholesome dharmas not yet obtained and not yet manifest, wishing to obtain them and make them manifest, one generates the aspiration, arouses keen intensity, and with the desire to obtain and the desire to make them present, brings them into being.

Generating aspiration for causing already-arisen wholesome dharmas to abide, not to be forgotten, and to be cultivated to perfection means: all wholesome dharmas already obtained and already manifest are called "already-arisen wholesome dharmas." Regarding these wholesome dharmas:

  • "That what has been obtained be neither lost nor diminished" — this is the meaning of causing to abide.
  • "That they remain clearly manifest, without dullness or dimness" — this is the meaning of not to be forgotten.
  • "That what has been obtained and made manifest be repeatedly practiced to completion and perfection" — this is the meaning of cultivated to perfection.

Regarding these wholesome dharmas, one generates the aspiration, arouses keen intensity, and with the desire for firm abiding, the desire for non-forgetfulness, and the desire for complete cultivation, brings them into being.

Self-urging and vigorous effort for wholesome dharmas. Self-urging is for causing what has already been obtained to become manifest. Vigorous effort is for causing what has not yet been obtained to be obtained.

More precisely: self-urging is for causing already-arisen wholesome dharmas to abide and not be forgotten. Vigorous effort is for causing them to be cultivated to perfection.

Considered by grade: for lower and middle-grade wholesome dharmas — generating those not yet arisen, and causing those already arisen to abide and not be forgotten — this is called self-urging. For upper-grade wholesome dharmas — generating those not yet arisen, and causing those already arisen to be cultivated to perfection — this is called vigorous effort.

Uplifting the mind. During diligent cultivation of shamatha one-pointedness — pursuing the four right efforts as broadly explained above — the mind may become extremely contracted and depressed internally, or come close to that state. Observing this, one takes up a particular pure, sublime, uplifting object, earnestly urging and gladdening the mind. This is called uplifting the mind.

Holding the mind. What is holding the mind? When during the practice of uplifting, the mind becomes agitated or is in danger of becoming agitated: having observed this, one then returns to internally gathering and concentrating the mind, cultivating shamatha. This is called holding the mind.

The four right strivings. These four are also called the four right strivings. Regarding dharmas of the dark category — for unarisen ones, to prevent their arising; for already-arisen ones, to cause their destruction — one generates aspiration, applies self-urging and vigorous effort, and uplifts and holds the mind. These are two right strivings. Regarding dharmas of the bright category — generating those not yet arisen and sustaining those already present — one should know these are also two right strivings. These four are also called the four right abandonments (samyak-pradhana).[5]

The four types of right abandonment.

(1) Discipline-abandonment (samvara-pradhana):[6] regarding already-arisen evil and unwholesome dharmas, for the purpose of their abandonment, one generates aspiration, applies self-urging, and so on broadly.

(2) Abandonment-abandonment (pradhana-pradhana): regarding unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas, for the purpose of preventing their arising, one generates aspiration, applies self-urging, and so on broadly.

Already-arisen unwholesome matters must be destroyed without tolerance. The primary instrument for this destruction is discipline — restraint imposed on the mind's tendency to dwell in or repeat the unwholesome. Because discipline is deployed as the means of abandonment, this effort is called discipline-abandonment. For unarisen unwholesome matters, one wishes to sever both their active manifestation and their becoming present — abandoning for the direct purpose of abandonment itself. For this reason it is called abandonment-abandonment.

(3) Cultivation-abandonment (bhavana-pradhana): regarding unarisen wholesome dharmas, for the purpose of their generation — as broadly explained through uplifting and holding the mind. Because through repeatedly cultivating wholesome dharmas, what was previously unobtained can be made manifest, and this has the power to sever afflictions — therefore it is called cultivation-abandonment.

(4) Protection-abandonment (anuraksana-pradhana): regarding already-arisen wholesome dharmas, for the purpose of causing them to abide — as broadly explained through uplifting and holding the mind. Because within wholesome dharmas already obtained and already manifest, one abandons negligence and cultivates non-negligence, enabling wholesome dharmas to abide without being forgotten and to be cultivated to perfection — because one protects already-arisen wholesome dharmas and this has the power to sever afflictions — therefore it is called protection-abandonment.

Summary meaning of the four right efforts. Having thus extensively analyzed the four right abandonments, how should one understand their summary meaning? In order to reveal the perfection of superior aspiration (adhyashaya)[7] and the perfection of application (prayoga) in the matter of discarding the dark category and adopting the bright category, the four right abandonments are taught. One should know: through generating aspiration, the perfection of superior aspiration is accomplished. Through self-urging, vigorous effort, uplifting the mind, and holding the mind, the perfection of application is accomplished.

The practitioner's essential tasks are two: to sever what must be severed and to obtain what must be obtained. Both begin with aspiration. To sever entanglements, one periodically cultivates calming, uplifting, and equanimity. To cut both entanglements and their latent roots, one further develops and accumulates the antidote wholesome dharmas. The four right strivings and four right abandonments are taught precisely to illuminate these tasks in their full scope and sequence.

29.2 The Four Samadhis

From this point, one further cultivates four samadhis (samadhi, meditative concentration): desire-samadhi, effort-samadhi, mind-samadhi, and investigation-samadhi. These four samadhis take their names from the dominant force that produces each: desire-samadhi (chanda-samadhi), effort-samadhi (virya-samadhi), mind-samadhi (citta-samadhi), and investigation-samadhi (mimamsa-samadhi).[8]

Desire-samadhi. When at that time one generates pure desire — having generated this desire, regarding all evil and unwholesome dharmas, one correctly examines and investigates their nature, causes, faults, and antidotes,[9] and establishes one-pointed mindfulness. Regarding all wholesome dharmas, one correctly examines and investigates their nature, causes, virtues, and emergence, and abides in one-pointed mindfulness. Through thus repeatedly cultivating, one attains mental one-pointedness. Regarding all evil and unwholesome dharmas, one is able to remove the actively manifesting entanglements, but has not yet permanently destroyed the latent tendencies of the afflictions. This is called the samadhi obtained through the dominant force of desire.

Effort-samadhi. One applies self-urging and vigorous effort to objects of past, future, and present that give rise to evil and unwholesome dharmas and afflictive entanglements of every grade. For those not yet arisen, one prevents their arising; for those already arisen, one brings about their cessation. Regarding the nature, causes, faults, and antidotes of those objects, one correctly examines and investigates, abiding in one-pointed mindfulness. Through thus repeatedly abiding, one is able to properly generate mental one-pointedness. As with desire-samadhi, one removes active entanglements without yet destroying latent tendencies. This is called the samadhi obtained through the dominant force of effort.

Mind-samadhi. When the mind becomes dull or sunken, one uplifts it; when agitated, one restrains and steadies it; from time to time one cultivates superior equanimity. Throughout, one correctly examines and investigates — regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas and what gives rise to them, and regarding all wholesome dharmas and what gives rise to them — their nature, causes, faults, virtues, antidotes, and emergence. One abides in one-pointed mindfulness. Through thus repeatedly abiding, one is able to properly generate mental one-pointedness — as broadly explained, up to: this is called the samadhi obtained through the dominant force of mind.

Investigation-samadhi. Regarding what is conducive to evil and unwholesome dharmas, one directs attention and reflects upon it as improper. And regarding what is conducive to all wholesome dharmas, one directs attention and reflects upon it as proper. By thus removing those entanglements, and by generating wholesome dharmas headed by samadhi that serve as antidotes to the entanglements, one is able to cause all evil and unwholesome dharmas to cease manifesting. One then reflects:

"Are there evil and unwholesome dharmas actively manifesting in me right now that I have not noticed? Or have they ceased to manifest, and am I unaware of that cessation? I should examine carefully and completely."

Through the dominant force of investigative attention, one correctly examines what has been cut off and what has not, correctly examines and investigates, and abides in one-pointed mindfulness. Through thus repeatedly abiding, one is able to directly realize mental one-pointedness. By these means one avoids overestimation (adhimana)[10] and knows honestly: "I have gained liberation of mind from active entanglements, but not yet from the latent tendencies. I have obtained the antidote dharmas headed by samadhi — but only those directed against entanglements, not those that address the latent tendencies." This is called the samadhi obtained through the dominant force of investigation.[11]

29.3 The Eight Abandonment Factors

Through the dominant force of these four samadhis, one has already distanced oneself from entanglements. Further, for the purpose of permanently destroying the latent tendencies of all evil and unwholesome dharmas, and for the purpose of cultivating and accumulating the wholesome dharmas that serve as their antidotes, one again generates desire and self-urging — as broadly explained above regarding the practice of the four right efforts. When one is thus properly cultivating, there are eight abandonment factors (prahananga). For the purpose of permanently destroying the latent tendencies, and for the purpose of bringing samadhi to complete perfection, they function with their respective distinctions.[12]

What are the eight abandonment factors?

(1) Desire (chanda): one generates the aspiration and desire, thinking: "When will my cultivation of samadhi reach perfection? When will I be able to destroy all the latent tendencies of evil and unwholesome dharmas?"

(2) Self-urging (virya): until the cultivation of all antidotes is complete, one does not abandon the application of effort.

(3) Faith (shraddha): because one has not abandoned the application and has properly abided therein, one generates deep conviction regarding the higher attainments.

(4) Ease (prashrabdhi): with pure faith as the foremost condition, the mind generates joy. Because the mind is joyful, one gradually eliminates the heaviness (dausthulya) belonging to the category of evil and unwholesome dharmas.

(5) Mindfulness (smriti): the quality that settles the mind on its object and holds it there — specifically the graduated stabilization described in the nine stages of mental abiding, from initial placement through to complete equanimity, which belong to the cultivation of shamatha.

(6) Alertness (samprajanya): the wisdom belonging to the category of vipashyana.

(7) Volition (cetana): mental activity — when correctly examining what has been cut off and what has not, one directs the mind and initiates bodily and verbal karma that accords with both the shamatha and vipashyana categories.

(8) Equanimity (upeksha): when engaging with past, future, and present objects that are conducive to evil and unwholesome dharmas, the mind is free from stain — mental equipoise.

Two means of discerning that latent tendencies have been cut off. Through two causes one knows that the latent tendencies have been severed: through volition regarding non-visible objects, and through equanimity regarding visible objects. These are called the eight abandonment factors. They are also called the eight superior practices.

Correlation with the four-factor yoga. These eight abandonment factors — the superior practices — constitute the yoga for destroying latent tendencies. They map directly onto the four-factor yoga taught previously (Fascicle 28): desire here corresponds to its desire; self-urging corresponds to its effort; faith corresponds to its faith; and the remaining five — ease, mindfulness, alertness, volition, and equanimity — together constitute its method.[13]

29.4 The Bases of Supernatural Power

Thus, the four samadhis of desire and so forth that were taught previously, together with the eight abandonment factors now explained — when samadhi has been completely perfected for the purpose of permanently cutting off all latent tendencies — all of these are collectively called the four bases of supernatural power (riddhipada), each named for its samadhi: the desire basis (chanda-samadhi-rddhipada), the effort basis (virya-samadhi-rddhipada), the mind basis (citta-samadhi-rddhipada), and the investigation basis (mimamsa-samadhi-rddhipada).[14]

Question: For what reason are they called "bases of supernatural power" (riddhipada)?

Answer: Just as one with healthy feet can travel anywhere — going and returning, leaping and striding with vigor — so too these samadhis can reach the most extraordinary dharmas in the world. The extraordinary is called "supernatural" (riddhi); the samadhis that reach it are called "bases of supernatural power" (riddhipada).

Result: capacity for supramundane attainment. Thus, when one who has brought samadhi to complete perfection possesses these dharmas, the mind becomes pure and bright, free from blemish and stain, separated from secondary afflictions — abiding in rectitude, capable, stable, unshakable, able to go and return with vigor. Such a mind can obtain and realize supramundane dharmas. Because supramundane dharmas are the most supreme — and because these samadhis can realize them — these samadhis are called "bases of supernatural power." Relying upon and being supported by this supreme samadhi, one advances in cultivating the yoga of the superior training in mind (adhicitta-shiksha) and the superior training in prajna (adhiprajna-shiksha).

29.5 The Five Faculties

Through advancing in the cultivation of this yoga, regarding what the disciples of the great teacher have realized, one generates deep conviction and deep pure faith. Because this pure faith has the meaning of dominance (adhipati), it is called the faith faculty (shraddha-indriya).[15]

Question: With respect to what are they dominant?

Answer: Faith exercises dominance in two directions: as the foremost condition for the arising of supramundane dharmas, and as the condition that generates effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom in sequence. Each subsequent faculty — effort, mindfulness, samadhi — likewise dominates both in giving rise to supramundane dharmas and in generating the faculties that follow it. Wisdom alone has a single scope: it is dominant solely over the arising of supramundane dharmas. For this reason they are called the five faculties.

The distinction between faculties and powers is directional: a faculty (indriya, lit. "governing force") is generative — it produces what follows. A power (bala, lit. "force capable of resisting") is the same quality made indomitable — it holds firm against opposition.

29.6 The Five Powers

Furthermore, knowing that there are differences between what has been previously realized and what has been subsequently realized, in accordance with this, regarding each successively realized supramundane dharma, one generates deep conviction and deep pure faith. Because this pure faith has the meaning of being indomitable, it is called the faith power (shraddha-bala).[16]

Question: By whom can they not be overcome?

Answer: This pure faith is indomitable — no god, mara, shramana, brahmana, or any being in the world can properly lead one astray from it, and afflictive entanglements cannot bend it. Because faith functions as the foremost and precursor for the remaining faculties, the same quality of indomitability extends to effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom as well — all five are therefore called powers. Because these powers possess great might, they crush and overcome the forces of all the armies of mara and can realize the complete and permanent exhaustion of all outflows (asrava). For this reason they are called powers.

29.7 Correlation of Faculties and Powers with Other Groups

Faith faculty and faith power = the four verified purities. The faith faculty and faith power correspond to the pure faith within the four purities of verified confidence (avetya-prasada).[17] Why? Because all the verified purities attained by those who have entered the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama) arise through this as cause, condition, and antecedent basis. Those verified purities are the dominant result (adhipati-phala) of this faith. For this reason, the Blessed One teaches that faith gives rise to those verified purities — the relationship is causal, not one of identity. Faith and the verified purities it produces differ in substance and in characteristic.[18]

Effort faculty and effort power = the four right efforts. Specifically, the effort faculty and effort power are the expedient right effort within the four right efforts — the effort capable of permanently cutting off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing (darshana-marga).[19] This effort is singled out because it can ultimately sever all evil and unwholesome dharmas.

Mindfulness faculty and mindfulness power = the four foundations of mindfulness. This is right mindfulness within the four foundations of mindfulness (smrityupasthana) — the mindfulness capable of completely eliminating all inversions without remainder.

Samadhi faculty and samadhi power = the four dhyanas. This is right samadhi within the four dhyanas, which serves as the expedient means for realizing the fruit of non-return.

Wisdom faculty and wisdom power = the four noble truths. This is right knowledge within the four noble truths — the knowledge that realizes direct realization (abhisamaya) and obtains the shramana fruits.

29.8 The Four Wholesome Roots

As the five faculties and five powers are cultivated and deepened, they give rise to the four wholesome roots concordant with penetration (nirvedha-bhagiya), in lower, middle, and upper grades. These are: (1) warmth (usmagata), (2) peak (murdhan), (3) acceptance of the truths (kshanti), and (4) supreme worldly dharma (laukikagradharma).[20]

The fire-drilling analogy. It is like a person who wishes to use fire to accomplish fire's work. Seeking fire, he places dry wood below and sets the fire-drill above it. With diligent self-urging and vigorous effort, he drills. As he thus diligently exerts himself and vigorously drills:

  • First, on the lower wood, warmth is initially generated.
  • Next, the warmth increases and heat rises upward.
  • Next, it further intensifies and smoke begins to appear.
  • Next, flameless fire suddenly bursts forth.
  • Immediately after the fire emerges, blazing flames arise.
  • Once the blazing flames have arisen, one is then able to accomplish the work of fire.

In the same way, the fire-driller's diligent exertion corresponds to the sustained cultivation of the five faculties and five powers:

  • The initial warmth on the lower wood corresponds to warmth — the precursory sign before the fire of undefiled dharmas that burns away all afflictions.
  • The rising heat corresponds to peak.
  • The appearance of smoke corresponds to acceptance of the truths.
  • The flameless fire bursting forth corresponds to supreme worldly dharma.
  • The blazing flames that follow without interruption correspond to the supramundane, undefiled, noble dharmas arising from the five faculties and five powers comprised within supreme worldly dharma.

29.9 The Seven Factors of Awakening

[T30.0444c29]

What are these further? They are the seven factors of awakening (sapta-bodhyanga). For persons who have already entered the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama) — that is, stream-entry — the wisdom of true awakening uses these as its branches — therefore they are called factors of awakening.[21]

These seven factors of true awakening are comprised within three categories: three factors of awakening belong to the category of shamatha, three belong to the category of vipashyana, and one pervades both categories. Therefore they are called the seven factors of awakening:

  • Investigation of dharmas (dharma-pravicaya), effort (virya), and joy (priti) — these three belong to the category of vipashyana.
  • Ease (prashrabdhi), samadhi, and equanimity (upeksha) — these three belong to the category of shamatha.
  • Mindfulness (smriti) alone belongs to both categories and is called "pervasive."

At that time, one first obtains the seven factors of awakening and is called an "initial learner." Having seen the traces of the noble truths — that is, having entered the stream — one has permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing. Only the afflictions abandoned by the path of cultivation (bhavana-marga) remain.

29.10 The Noble Eightfold Path

For the purpose of cutting off those remaining afflictions, one cultivates the noble eightfold path (arya-ashtangika-marga) comprised within the three trainings. In this context: right view, right thought, and right effort belong to the training of wisdom (prajna). Right speech, right action, and right livelihood belong to the training of discipline (shila). Right mindfulness and right samadhi belong to the training of mind.[22]

Question: For what reason is it called the noble eightfold path?

Answer: Noble learners who have already seen the traces of the truths, traveling this correct path of eight branches, are able to cut off all afflictions without remainder and ultimately realize liberation. Therefore it is called the noble eightfold path.

Right view (samyag-drishti): In this context, right view encompasses two things: the true awakening obtained through the awakening factors — the direct realization at stream-entry — and the wisdom that subsequently establishes understanding in accordance with that realization.

Right thought (samyak-samkalpa): Through the dominant force of this right view, the discriminative thinking that arises — of renunciation, non-ill-will, and non-harm — is called right thought.

Right speech (samyag-vac): When the mind turns to thought, one entertains only thoughts of this character. When the mind turns to speech, one gives rise — through the dominant force of right view — to wholesome thinking and engages in discourse that accords with the Dharma. This is right speech.

Right action (samyak-karmanta): One who lawfully seeks robes, food and drink, bedding and seating, medicine for illness, and bodily requisites maintains alertness (samprajanya) throughout the seeking — while going and returning, while looking, gazing, bending, and stretching, while handling the robe-bowl and sanghati (outer robe), while eating, drinking, chewing, and tasting. At rest too, regarding items already obtained, that same alertness continues: in walking, standing, sitting, lying down, and in alleviating fatigue and drowsiness as broadly explained above. This is called right action.

Right livelihood (samyag-ajiva): Lawfully seeking robes, food and drink, and so on through requisites, and distancing oneself from all practices that constitute wrong livelihood — this is called right livelihood.

Right speech, action, and livelihood obtained at the time of awakening. The right speech, right action, and right livelihood that consist in abstentions — these were already obtained at the time of realizing the undefiled attention belonging to the awakening factors.

The discipline beloved of noble ones (arya-kanta-shila).[23]

Question: Why is this called "the discipline beloved of noble ones"?

Answer: Noble ones — the wise, virtuous, and rightly arrived — have long cherished this discipline, thinking: "When will I obtain the restraint (samvara) against evil conduct of speech, evil conduct of body, and wrong livelihood?" Having yearned for it over the long course of time, it is called "beloved of noble ones" when at last obtained.

Having obtained this discipline beloved of noble ones, one will never knowingly speak falsehoods, never intentionally take the life of a sentient being, never take what is not given, never engage in sexual misconduct, and never unlawfully seek robes and the like.

Through the dominant force of this discipline beloved of noble ones, during the path of cultivation, all activities of speech, bodily action, and livelihood that arise are also called right speech, right action, and right livelihood.

Right effort (samyag-vyayama): Right effort is the desire, diligence, renunciative valor, powerful exertion, and continuous urging of the mind that arise in one who cultivates diligently on the basis of right view, right thought, right speech, right action, and right livelihood.

Right mindfulness and right samadhi (samyak-smriti, samyak-samadhi): Right effort having been established, one cultivates right mindfulness through the four foundations of mindfulness — the non-inverted mindfulness comprised within the nine aspects, which settles the mind in those same nine aspects. This is right mindfulness and right samadhi.

Two categories of path factors. Thus, all eight branches of the noble eightfold path are established in two categories: the non-active and the active. The non-active are right speech, right action, and right livelihood — abstentions received at stream-entry. The active are further divided into two: shamatha and vipashyana. Right view, right thought, and right effort belong to vipashyana. Right mindfulness and right samadhi belong to shamatha.

Path of cultivation and path of seeing distinguished. On the basis of purified right speech, right action, and right livelihood, one cultivates shamatha and vipashyana from time to time. This practice can cut off all fetters (samyojana) permanently and without remainder, attaining the supreme fruit of arhatship. The path of cultivation unfolds through sustained practice over long duration — extended habituation gradually severs afflictions. The path of seeing arises in a single flash of wisdom — momentary insight permanently severs them. Because right speech, right action, and right livelihood belong to sustained cultivation, they are first established within the path of cultivation.

29.11 Summary of the Thirty-Seven Factors

[T30.0445b27]

This sustained, graduated cultivation of all thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening (saptatrimshad-bodhipakshya-dharma) — carried out through these preparatory practices — is what the text means by "the cultivation of the factors conducive to awakening."

29.12 The Four Shramana Fruits

[T30.0445b29]

What are the fruits of cultivation? They are the four shramana fruits (catur-shramana-phala):[24] (1) the fruit of stream-entry (srota-apanna-phala), (2) the fruit of once-returning (sakridagamin-phala), (3) the fruit of non-returning (anagamin-phala), and (4) the supreme fruit of arhatship (arhat-phala).

[T30.0445c01]

What is meant by "shramana"? What is meant by "fruit"? The noble path is called "shramana." The severance of afflictions is called "fruit." Furthermore, a later-arising path — whether intermediate or superior — is the fruit produced by an earlier-arising path.

Question: For what reason are exactly four fruits established?

Answer: Because they counteract four kinds of afflictions.

The fruit of stream-entry is established because the afflictions that propel one toward evil destinies — the very causes of going to evil destinies — are severed, and because the antidote that severs them arises.[25] The Blessed One speaks of "permanently severing the three fetters" as the basis for this fruit. The three fetters correspond to three domains of obstruction, each preventing the noble path from arising:[26]

(1) The householder domain. Here, the view of the transitory collection (satkaya-drishti) predominates. Because of this view, one generates fear at the outset and does not wish to set forth toward the noble path at all.

(2) The domain of wrong teaching and discipline. Here, attachment to rules and observances (shila-vrata-paramarsha) predominates. Having set forth, one is nonetheless led astray by this attachment — and therefore cannot generate the noble path.

(3) The domain of right teaching and discipline. Here, doubt (vicikitsa) predominates. Although one has set forth and does not go astray, one has not yet become habituated to the correct path. Even where matters have been truly seen and known, doubt causes wavering and hesitation — and so the noble path cannot arise.

For these reasons, only the severing of these three is cited as the basis for the fruit of stream-entry.

A stream-enterer has at most seven further existences remaining. Because of this, many births continue in succession. When the afflictions that produce continued rebirth are severed such that births no longer repeat, the fruit of once-returning is established: one has permanently abandoned the afflictions belonging to heavenly and human existence that drive repeated rebirth beyond one further cycle, and will undergo at most one more heavenly birth and one more human birth.

When one has fully cut the afflictions that cause one to return and be born again in this world, one will take rebirth only in heavenly existence. At that point the fruit of non-returning is established.

When one has uprooted every affliction that sustains any form of existence whatsoever, the supreme fruit of arhatship is established.

The Blessed One likewise identifies the scriptural grounds for each fruit: permanently severing the three fetters and thinning[27] greed, aversion, and delusion for once-returning; severing the five lower fetters (pancha-avarabhagiya-samyojana)[28] for non-returning; and severing all afflictions without remainder for the supreme fruit of arhatship. These are called the fruits of cultivation.

29.13 Person Typology: Behavioral Types and the Thin-Dust Person

[T30.0445c28]

Among these practitioners, those of greed, aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive-thought conduct must first purify their disposition through the appropriate antidote practice — the purifying-conduct object prescribed for each type — before they can attain correct mental abiding.[29] Each practices diligently with the object suited to their particular disposition, drawing on the differentiating power of the objects. A person of balanced-conduct, by contrast, practices with whichever object is found congenial. Such practice achieves mental abiding but does not purify any one conduct in particular. The same holds for persons of thin-dust conduct.

These conduct types differ, however, in the time required. Those of greed and other specific conducts need a long period of practice before attaining mental abiding. Those of balanced-conduct attain it in a somewhat shorter time. Those of thin-dust conduct attain mental abiding with the greatest speed.

Question: The behavioral characteristics of persons of greed and other conducts were explained extensively above. What are the behavioral characteristics of persons of balanced and thin-dust conduct?

Answer: A person of balanced-conduct possesses all the characteristics found among persons of the specific conduct types, but none is dominant or outstanding. Whichever conditions one encounters, the corresponding characteristics manifest — in this respect, balanced-conduct resembles each of the specific types by turns.

The characteristics distinguishing the person of thin-dust conduct are sevenfold: (1) freedom from heavy obstructions, (2) initial purity, (3) complete accumulations, (4) abundant pure faith, (5) endowment with discernment, (6) endowment with merit, and (7) endowment with virtue.

(1) Freedom from heavy obstructions means freedom from three obstructions: karmic, afflictive, and maturation.[30]

Karmic obstruction: the five acts of immediate retribution (pancha-anantarya-karma) and all other especially grave karma committed intentionally. When the maturation fruit of such karma comes to fruition, it obstructs the noble path and prevents it from arising.

Afflictive obstruction: fierce and long-standing afflictions that, despite the application of various purifying-conduct objects, cannot be purified in the present life.

Maturation obstruction takes two forms. In the first, one is born in a state where the noble path can neither arise nor develop. In the second, one is born where the noble path could in principle arise, but the maturation of one's karma produces deafness, stupidity, dullness, blindness, muteness, or the inability to communicate except by hand — rendering one incapable of understanding what is well-taught or poorly taught in the dharma.

(2) Initial purity means possessing pure discipline and upright view. Discipline is purified through ten causes, as explained previously. Upright view possesses four qualities: it is associated with pure faith, associated with resolute conviction, free from deception and flattery, and oriented toward liberation through well-considered reflection on the meaning of dharma, without confusion or doubt.

In detail: because upright view is grounded in pure faith, one holds firm in the Buddha's dharma and vinaya and is not drawn away by other teachings. Resolute conviction deepens this: one rests in confidence regarding the inconceivable powers of the tathagatas and noble disciples, the varieties of birth, the profound dharma teachings, and the indeterminate matters — without alarm, without fear. Free from deception and flattery, one's view is honest: one practices as taught and presents oneself as one truly is. And through sustained reflection on impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self in all dharmas, confusion and doubt dissolve, the two extreme views fall away, and one advances. These four qualities together make the view upright.

(3) Complete accumulations (sambhara) are of four kinds,[31] as broadly explained previously. In brief: merit accumulations provide suitable resources and favorable conditions in this life — abundant provisions, genuine fields of merit as spiritual friends, freedom from obstructions, and the ability to practice diligently. Wisdom accumulations furnish discernment and the capacity to understand the meaning of what is well-taught and poorly taught, and to obtain suitable instruction, guidance, and admonishment. Prior-life accumulations are wholesome roots gathered in past lives, through which one attains perfect faculties in this life. Present-life accumulations include the desire for wholesome dharma, mature faculties, and the discipline of moral training and sense-faculty restraint, as explained previously.

(4) Abundant pure faith: one is without confusion or doubt toward the Teacher and generates deep pure faith and resolute conviction. As toward the Teacher, so too toward the dharma and the training.

(5) Endowment with discernment: one quickly comprehends both the dharma and its meaning, retains them without loss over long periods, and rapidly penetrates their significance.

(6) Endowment with merit: one's physical form is dignified and pleasing, inspiring pure faith in others. One enjoys good health and longevity, speaks with dignity, comes from a distinguished lineage, is widely known, possesses great blessings, obtains ample robes and other necessities of life, and is honored, respected, esteemed, and praised by rulers and officials.

(7) Endowment with virtue: one is naturally accomplished in such virtues as extreme few-wanting and the like, whose characteristics should be understood as the shramana adornments previously described.

These are the behavioral characteristics distinguishing persons of thin-dust conduct.

29.14 Six Categories of Persons

[T30.0446c06]

What are the alternative designations for persons (pudgala)? There are six:[32] (1) shramana, (2) brahmin, (3) practitioner of pure conduct, (4) bhikshu, (5) the diligent, and (6) renunciant.

[T30.0446c08]

First: The Shramana — Four Types[33]

The shramana has four subtypes: (1) the shramana who has mastered the path, (2) the shramana who teaches the path, (3) the shramana who lives by the path, and (4) the shramana who corrupts the path.

The well-gone ones (sugata) — those who have realized the complete and permanent exhaustion of greed, aversion, and delusion — are shramanas who have mastered the path. Those who expound the true dharma in order to tame greed, aversion, and delusion are shramanas who teach the path. Those who diligently practice right conduct in order to tame these same afflictions are shramanas who live by the path. Those who transgress discipline and engage in unwholesome dharmas are shramanas who corrupt the path.

Alternatively: learners (shaiksha) and those beyond learning (ashaiksha) are called shramanas who have mastered the path, because through the undefiled path they crush all afflictions abandoned by the paths of seeing and cultivation. When there are no tathagatas, and no bodhisattvas diligently practicing right conduct for the sake of awakening, the shravakas who uphold the three baskets (tripitaka) serve as shramanas who teach the path, because they sustain the conventional dharma and vinaya — preserving the lineage of transmission so that it does not cease.

A shramana who lives by the path is an ordinary being (prithagjana) of trainable nature: one who practices right conduct for self-benefit, who possesses shame and conscience, who delights in proper training, and who strives to obtain the unobtained, realize the unrealized, and witness the unwitnessed — with the genuine capacity to do so. Such a person possesses the warmth of wholesome dharmas and can generate the life-faculty of noble wisdom. Being alive to the path — not spiritually inert — one is therefore called a shramana who lives by the path.

A person who transgresses discipline and frequently engages in unwholesome dharmas — up to and including one who falsely claims pure conduct while not actually practicing it — is a shramana who corrupts the path. Having destroyed the very root of the initial noble path, such a person lacks the power and ability to generate it: even when teachers of the path and those who have realized it are present, this person cannot attain it.

The Blessed One said regarding these: "This first shramana through to the fourth — among the teachings of non-Buddhist shramanas and brahmins, they are entirely absent. Wherever the noble eightfold path can be established, there the first shramana through to the fourth exists."

Second: The Brahmin — Three Types

The brahmin (brahmana) has three subtypes: (1) brahmin by birth, (2) brahmin by designation, and (3) brahmin by right conduct. A brahmin by birth is one born into a brahmin family, issued from a mother's womb, with both parents of brahmin lineage. A brahmin by designation is one whom the world, through concept and convention, provisionally names a brahmin. A brahmin by right conduct is one whose task is certain and complete, who has driven out all evil and unwholesome dharmas — as should be known from the saying: "A brahmin has nothing more to be done; the work is complete. This is called a brahmin."

Third: Pure Conduct — Three Types

Pure conduct (brahmacarya) has three subtypes: (1) the pure conduct of accepted renunciation, (2) the pure conduct of temporary severance, and (3) the pure conduct of ultimate severance. The first is the undertaking to renounce all sexual activity. The second belongs to ordinary beings who have separated from desire-realm desire through the worldly path. The third belongs to noble ones who have attained the fruit of non-returning and the supreme fruit of arhatship.

Fourth: The Bhikshu — Five Types

The bhikshu has five subtypes:[34] (1) the bhikshu by begging, (2) the self-proclaimed bhikshu, (3) the bhikshu by designation, (4) the bhikshu who destroys afflictions, and (5) the bhikshu fully ordained through the formal monastic procedure.

Fifth: The Diligent — Three Types

The diligent practitioner has three subtypes. (1) Diligence in ceasing transgression: renouncing all unwholesome bodily and verbal karma. (2) Diligence in ceasing engagement with harmful objects: closely guarding the sense gates and cultivating protective mindfulness and continuous mindfulness, as explained previously. (3) Diligence in ceasing afflictions: permanently severing all afflictions abandoned by the paths of seeing and cultivation.

Regarding the third: whenever there arise thoughts of desire, thoughts of ill-will, or thoughts of harm; greed, aversion, or wrong views; anger, resentment, concealment, vexation, deception, flattery, and the like — states contrary to the shramana dharma that lead to the hells and other evil destinations — one does not tolerate them. One immediately severs, destroys, removes, and expels them. This ceasing of afflictions is of two kinds: ceasing the latent tendencies (anushaya) — afflictions dormant in the mind-stream — and ceasing the active entanglements (paryavasthana) — afflictions that have surfaced and seized the mind.

Sixth: The Renunciant — Two Types

The renunciant (pravrajita) has two subtypes: (1) one who goes forth in the well-taught dharma and vinaya, and (2) one who goes forth in the wrongly-taught dharma and vinaya. Those who go forth in the well-taught dharma and vinaya include bhikshus, bhikshunis, probationary nuns (shiksha-mana), novice monks (shramanera), and novice nuns (shramanerika). Furthermore, one who is able to rid oneself of all evil and unwholesome dharmas should be understood as truly gone forth. Those who go forth in the wrongly-taught dharma and vinaya are the various non-Buddhist ascetics — those who wear no clothing, those who wear discolored robes, those who smear ashes, and other similar groups.

Therefore it is said: whether shramanas, brahmins, practitioners of pure conduct, bhikshus, the diligent, or renunciants — all of these are alternative designations for the person (pudgala).

29.15 Eight Types of Persons by Capacity

[T30.0447b13]

Persons are of eight types, established through four kinds of differentiating causes.

The Eight Types

What are the eight types of persons? (1) Those with capacity and (2) those without capacity. (3) Those skilled in methods and (4) those unskilled in methods. (5) Those who practice without interruption and (6) those who do not. (7) Those with extensive training and (8) those without extensive training.

The Four Differentiating Causes

What are the four causes that establish these eight types? They are differences in faculties, yoga knowledge, practice intensity, and duration.

(1) Faculties: whether one's faculties have matured or remain unmatured — distinguishing those with capacity from those without. (2) Yoga knowledge: whether one knows the yoga skillfully or does not — distinguishing those skilled in methods from those unskilled. (3) Practice intensity: whether one practices continuously and earnestly, or does not — distinguishing those who practice without interruption from those who do not. Continuous and earnest practice is also called "constant devoted practice"; its absence is called "lacking constant devoted practice." (4) Duration: whether one has cultivated the path over a long period or has not — distinguishing those with extensive training from those without.

Thus, through differences in faculties, yoga knowledge, practice intensity, and duration, the eight types of persons are established.

Interaction of the Four Factors

If a person's faculties have not matured, then no amount of skillful method, continuous practice, or extensive training can bring accomplishment — nothing can be achieved in accordance with principle, dharma, or skill.

If the faculties have matured but the person lacks knowledge of skillful methods, accomplishment is equally impossible.

If the faculties have matured and one knows the methods but lacks continuous practice, one cannot attain swift penetrating wisdom.

If the faculties have matured, one knows the methods, and one practices continuously, yet one has not trained over a long period — one's own tasks remain unfinished.

Only when all four factors converge — matured faculties, knowledge of skillful methods, continuous practice, and extensive training — can one accomplish everything, attain swift penetrating wisdom, and bring all one's tasks to completion.

29.16 Four Maras and Mara's Activities

[T30.0447c15]

One should know that the maras (mara) are of four types, while mara's activities are beyond counting.[35] Yogins who diligently cultivate contemplative practice should thoroughly understand them and resolutely keep their distance.

The Four Maras

What are the four maras? (1) The mara of the skandhas (skandha-mara), (2) the mara of afflictions (klesha-mara), (3) the mara of death (mrtyu-mara), and (4) the celestial mara (devaputra-mara).

The mara of the skandhas is the five appropriating skandhas themselves. The mara of afflictions encompasses all afflictions throughout the three realms. The mara of death is the perishing and passing away of beings among their various kinds. The celestial mara is a being born at the highest level of the desire realm who has attained great power. He appears when those who diligently cultivate superior wholesome qualities seek to transcend the other three maras — creating obstacles and setting in motion every manner of disturbance.

The Fourfold Basis

These four maras can be understood through their relation to death: what death rests upon, what brings death about, what death itself is, and what stands in the way of transcending it.

Death rests on the five appropriating skandhas already arisen and presently existing — without them, death cannot occur. Afflictions drive future rebirth, and once reborn, perishing follows. The exhaustion and cessation of the life faculty among all classes of beings — their perishing and passing away — is the nature of death itself. And when those cultivating goodness strive to transcend this cycle, the celestial mara brings great power to bear against them. Because of this final obstacle, beings either cannot escape the law of death at all, or do so only after enormous difficulty and a very long time.

The Limits of Mara's Power

Mara's power is not absolute. When ordinary beings separate from desire through the worldly path — whether they remain in this realm or are reborn in higher ones — mara temporarily loses his command over them. But those who have not yet separated from desire remain entirely in his hands, subject to his will.

Even those who have separated from desire through the worldly path remain bound by mara's bonds and have not escaped mara's net — for they will inevitably return and be reborn in this realm.

Mara's Activities

What are mara's activities?

When the desire for wholesome dharmas leading to liberation arises, but through the dominant force of attachment to sensory pleasures one quickly retreats and abandons them — this is the work of mara.

When one is established in closely guarding the sense gates, yet regarding pleasant forms, sounds, smells, tastes, tangibles, and mental objects, one grasps at their general features and detailed signs and the mind delights in pursuing them — mara is at work here too.

When one is established in knowing the proper measure regarding food, yet through craving the mind is drawn toward delicious flavors and immoderate eating — this is mara's domain.

When one is established in diligent wakefulness through the first and last watches of the night, yet through the force of laziness the mind is drawn toward the pleasure of sleep, of reclining, of lying on one's side — here too, mara is at work.

When one is established in full awareness during the activities of daily life, mara's opportunities multiply. Upon seeing youthful, attractive women and the like, one improperly grasps at their features. Upon encountering worldly wonders, the mind is drawn in. One becomes absorbed in a multitude of tasks and activities. Or upon seeing householders and monastics gathered in entertainment, or falling in with bad companions, one generates approving delight and the mind follows after them. All of this is mara's activity.

If doubts arise regarding the Buddha, the dharma, the sangha, the truths of suffering, origination, cessation, and the path, this world, or the next — doubt is mara's instrument.

When dwelling in forest wilderness, under trees, among tombs, or in secluded rooms — if one encounters great and terrifying apparitions that cause the hair to stand on end; or if figures resembling shramanas, brahmins, humans, or non-humans appear suddenly and against all reason urge one to abandon the wholesome and adopt the unwholesome — these apparitions are mara's manufacture.

If the mind delights in pursuing material support, respect, and praise; or if one is drawn toward miserliness, excessive desires, discontentment, anger, resentment, concealment, vexation, deception, flattery, and other qualities opposed to the shramana adornments — know that all of these are the work of mara.

Thus, these and countless other varieties of mara's activities are all the work of the four maras. One should correctly understand each according to its nature.

29.17 Three Causes of Fruitless Practice

[T30.0448b04]

Through three causes, one who practices correctly and strives diligently may yet produce no fruit whatsoever. What are the three?

(1) One's faculties have not been sufficiently developed. (2) The instructions one has received are unsuitable. (3) One's power of samadhi is weak.

Even with suitable instructions and strong samadhi, if the faculties remain undeveloped, diligent striving produces nothing. Even with developed faculties and strong samadhi, without suitable instructions, effort has no direction. And even with developed faculties and suitable instructions, if the power of samadhi is weak, practice cannot ripen into fruit.

Only when all three converge — developed faculties, suitable instructions, and strong samadhi — does diligent striving produce certain fruit.

Thus, through three failures practice is fruitless; through three fulfillments it bears definite fruit.

End of Fascicle Twenty-Nine of the Yogacarabhumi-sastra

  1. The four right efforts (catvari samyak-pradhana, 四正斷) — also called the four right strivings (四正勝) — are the second of the seven groups within the thirty-seven factors of awakening. They arise directly from the four foundations of mindfulness, carrying forward the practitioner's comprehension of wholesome and unwholesome dharmas into active application. The Sravakabhumi distinguishes effort by temporal object (past/future vs. present), by grade of affliction (lower/middle vs. upper), and by cause of arising (conceptual vs. object-based). ↩︎

  2. The definition given here — defiled karma associated with entanglements (paryavasthana) plus the root afflictions that produce them — is characteristically Yogacara-Sarvastivada. It distinguishes the active manifestation (the three types of evil conduct) from the generating conditions (the afflictions). The standard Pali parallel (MN 117) defines unwholesome dharmas more simply as states rooted in greed, aversion, and delusion. ↩︎

  3. The Sravakabhumi defines wholesome dharmas functionally as antidotes operating at three levels: (1) antidotes to unwholesome dharmas (the three kinds of evil conduct), (2) antidotes to the hindrances (nivarana, 蓋) — the five mental obstructions to samadhi (sensual desire, ill-will, torpor-drowsiness, restlessness-remorse, doubt), and (3) antidotes to the fetters (samyojana, 結) — the deeper bonds that sustain samsaric existence. This three-level structure reflects the progressive depth of practice: moral restraint, meditative purification, and liberating insight. ↩︎

  4. Improper mental attention (ayonisho-manaskara, 非理作意) is identified throughout the Yogacarabhumi as the proximate condition for the arising of afflictions. The term means attending to an object in a way that does not accord with reality — e.g., perceiving the impermanent as permanent, the painful as pleasurable, the impure as pure. Here, when unwholesome dharmas arise through both conceptual and object-based forces, improper attention is invariably present as a contributing factor. Cf. MN 2 (Sabbasava Sutta), where improper attention is identified as the root condition for the arising of outflows. ↩︎

  5. The terms "right strivings" (正勝) and "right abandonments" (正斷) are two designations for the same set of four efforts. The "strivings" label emphasizes the active exertion; the "abandonments" label emphasizes the result (severing afflictions). The four types of abandonment — discipline, abandonment-of-abandonment, cultivation, and protection — map directly to the four efforts: (1) discipline-abandonment = abandoning arisen unwholesome (cultivating restraint); (2) abandonment-abandonment = preventing unarisen unwholesome (abandoning for the sake of abandonment); (3) cultivation-abandonment = generating unarisen wholesome (cultivation has power to sever); (4) protection-abandonment = maintaining arisen wholesome (protection has power to sever). This fourfold scheme is standard Sarvastivada-Yogacara Abhidharma (cf. Abhidharmakosha VI). ↩︎

  6. The term "discipline-abandonment" (samvara-pradhana, 律儀斷) may seem paradoxical: discipline (samvara) means restraint, while abandonment (pradhana) means exertion or striving. The compound means "abandonment through discipline" — one abandons already-arisen unwholesome states by cultivating the restraint that counteracts them. The nomenclature follows Abhidharmakosha VI.59, which distinguishes the four pradhanas by the mode of their operation rather than by their object. ↩︎

  7. "Superior aspiration" (adhyashaya, 增上意樂) is a key Yogacara concept denoting the deeply internalized motivation that drives practice beyond mere intellectual understanding. The Sravakabhumi distinguishes it from ordinary aspiration (chanda) by its depth and quality: while aspiration initiates effort, superior aspiration represents the mature, unwavering commitment that sustains practice through difficulties. In later Yogacara literature (Mahayanasutralankara, Bodhisattvabhumi), adhyashaya becomes a defining quality of the bodhisattva path. ↩︎

  8. The four samadhis (catur-samadhi, 四三摩地) correspond to the four bases of supernatural power (catur-rddhipada, 四神足), the third of the seven groups within the thirty-seven factors of awakening. The Sravakabhumi presents them as arising directly from the four right efforts: having established proper aspiration and effort, the practitioner now develops concentrated states. Each samadhi is named for the dominant force (adhipati) that produces it — desire, effort, mind, or investigation. A central theme throughout is the distinction between removing active entanglements (paryavasthana) and permanently destroying latent tendencies (anushaya): the four samadhis accomplish only the former. ↩︎

  9. The fourfold analytical framework — nature (svabhava), causes (hetu), faults (adinava) or virtues (anushamsa), and antidotes (pratipaksha) or emergence (nihsarana) — is applied consistently throughout the four samadhis. For unwholesome dharmas, one examines nature, causes, faults, and antidotes. For wholesome dharmas, one examines nature, causes, virtues, and emergence. This analytical structure appears throughout the Yogacarabhumi as a standard pattern for meditative investigation (cf. fascicle 30, section 30.14, on the six aspects of vipashyana objects). ↩︎

  10. Overestimation (adhimana, 增上慢) is the mistaken belief that one has attained a stage of realization not in fact reached. In this context, the practitioner might believe they have fully destroyed afflictions when they have only suppressed active entanglements while latent tendencies remain intact. The Visuddhimagga (XXI.2-3) warns extensively against this same danger. The Buddha addresses it directly in MN 105 (Sunakkhatta Sutta), where a monk who has attained samadhi wrongly claims arhatship. The investigation-samadhi's distinctive function is precisely this honest self-assessment. ↩︎

  11. The investigation-samadhi (mimamsa-samadhi, 觀三摩地) contains a distinctive passage on avoiding overestimation (adhimana, 增上慢): the practitioner must honestly assess whether active afflictions remain, recognizing that freedom from entanglements does not yet constitute freedom from latent tendencies. This self-examination is the specific function of "investigation" (mimamsa). The awareness that one has achieved only partial liberation — from entanglements but not from latent tendencies — is what motivates the subsequent cultivation of the eight abandonment factors. ↩︎

  12. The eight abandonment factors (ashta-prahananga, 八斷行) represent the specific mental qualities deployed to destroy the latent tendencies that the four samadhis alone cannot reach. They are: (1) desire, (2) self-urging, (3) faith, (4) ease, (5) mindfulness, (6) alertness, (7) volition, and (8) equanimity. Also called the "eight superior practices" (八勝行). The fifth factor, mindfulness, is defined through the "nine aspects" (九種相), referring to the nine stages of mental abiding (navakara-cittasthiti) detailed in Fascicle 30. ↩︎

  13. The eight abandonment factors are correlated with the four-factor yoga (catur-anga-yoga) taught in Fascicle 28 (section 28.6: faith, desire, effort, method). Desire maps to desire; self-urging maps to effort; faith maps to faith; and the remaining five factors (ease, mindfulness, alertness, volition, equanimity) collectively constitute method (upaya). This correlation shows that the eightfold scheme is an elaboration of the same fundamental structure governing all yogic practice. ↩︎

  14. Each "basis of supernatural power" (riddhipada, 神足) is the conjunction of a specific samadhi (e.g., desire-samadhi) together with the eight abandonment factors, brought to complete perfection for the purpose of cutting off latent tendencies. The term synthesizes two preceding sequences: the four samadhis (29.2) remove active entanglements; the eight abandonment factors (29.3) destroy the latent tendencies the samadhis alone cannot reach. Together, fully perfected, they constitute the rddhipada. ↩︎

  15. The five faculties (pancha-indriya, 五根) — faith, effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom — are the fourth of the seven groups within the thirty-seven factors of awakening. The term "faculty" (indriya) means "having dominance" (adhipati): each faculty exercises a controlling influence over the arising of supramundane dharmas and over the subsequent faculties in the series. The text defines the hierarchy of dominance: faith is dominant over all four subsequent faculties plus supramundane dharmas; each succeeding faculty has progressively narrower dominance; wisdom is dominant solely over supramundane dharmas. ↩︎

  16. The five powers (pancha-bala, 五力) share the same five members as the five faculties but are defined by the quality of being "indomitable" (durjaya, 難伏) — they cannot be overcome by gods, maras, shramanas, brahmanas, or any worldly beings, nor can afflictive entanglements bend them. The distinction between "faculties" and "powers" is one of function: faculties exercise dominance over their sphere, while powers exercise invincibility against opposing forces. ↩︎

  17. The four verified purities (catur-avetya-prasada, 四證淨) — also called the four factors of stream-entry — are unshakeable confidence in the Buddha, the Dharma, the Sangha, and the discipline beloved of noble ones. They are "verified" (avetya) because they arise from direct realization, not from hearsay or tradition. The Pali parallels (SN 55.1-74, the Sotapatti-samyutta) treat these four as the defining characteristics of the stream-enterer. ↩︎

  18. The key analytical point of the Sravakabhumi's correlation is that the relationships are causal, not one of identity: the faith faculty is the cause of the four verified purities (which are its dominant result, adhipati-phala), not identical to them. The same principle holds across all five: each faculty produces its correlated group as a soteriological outcome. For instance, the effort faculty cuts afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing; the mindfulness faculty eliminates all inversions without remainder. ↩︎

  19. The path of seeing (darshana-marga, 見道) is the first supramundane path, at which the practitioner directly realizes the four noble truths and enters the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama). Afflictions "abandoned by the path of seeing" are those eliminated through this initial direct insight — primarily the three fetters (identity-view, attachment to rules, doubt) plus certain forms of desire-realm and form-realm afflictions. The remaining afflictions require the path of cultivation (bhavana-marga, 修道), which operates through repeated practice over time. This two-path scheme is fundamental to the Sarvastivada-Yogacara soteriology; cf. Abhidharmakosha VI.26-49. ↩︎

  20. The four wholesome roots concordant with penetration (nirvedha-bhagiya-kushala-mula, 順決擇分四種善根) represent the final stages of mundane practice before entry into the supramundane path, each arising in lower, middle, and upper grades. The fire-drilling analogy captures the relationship between sustained effort and supramundane emergence: just as fire requires sustained friction before it blazes forth, the undefiled dharmas that burn away all afflictions require steady cultivation before they arise. "Supreme worldly dharma" (laukikagradharma) is the highest point of mundane realization — the moment immediately preceding stream-entry. Cf. Abhidharmakosha VI.17-25; Visuddhimagga XXI. ↩︎

  21. The seven factors of awakening (sapta-bodhyanga, 七覺支) are the fifth group within the thirty-seven factors of awakening, arising at the moment of entry into the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama) — that is, stream-entry. The Sravakabhumi classifies them into three categories: investigation of dharmas, effort, and joy belong to vipashyana; ease, samadhi, and equanimity belong to shamatha; mindfulness alone pervades both categories and is termed "pervasive" (sarvatraga). This tripartite classification reflects the text's consistent emphasis on the shamatha-vipashyana framework as the organizing principle of the path. ↩︎

  22. The noble eightfold path (arya-ashtangika-marga, 八支聖道) is the seventh and final group within the thirty-seven factors of awakening. The Sravakabhumi assigns the eight factors to the three trainings: right view, right thought, and right effort to prajna; right speech, right action, and right livelihood to shila; right mindfulness and right samadhi to samadhi. The distinctive Yogacara-Sarvastivada point is the placement of right effort under prajna — in the Pali tradition (e.g., DN 18; Visuddhimagga I.32), right effort belongs to the samadhi training. The Yogacara view is that right effort is fundamentally a function of discriminative awareness rather than of concentration. ↩︎

  23. "The discipline beloved of noble ones" (arya-kanta-shila, 聖所愛戒) is the fourth verified purity alongside confidence in the Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha. It refers to the ethical restraint that arises spontaneously from direct realization of the truths — not merely conventional discipline undertaken through vows, but an innate incapacity for certain transgressions. The stream-enterer becomes constitutionally incapable of deliberate killing, stealing, sexual misconduct, lying, and wrong livelihood. Cf. SN 55.1; AN 9.12; Abhidharmakosha VI.29. ↩︎

  24. The term "shramana" (shramana, 沙門) — literally "one who strives" — here refers not to the practitioner but to the noble path itself, while "fruit" (phala) refers to the result of that path, namely the severance of afflictions. This identification of path-as-shramana and severance-as-fruit is a characteristic Sarvastivada-Yogacara formulation (cf. Abhidharmakosha VI.50; cf. DN 2, Samannaphala Sutta, for the broader treatment of shramana fruits). ↩︎

  25. Stream-entry guarantees that one will never again be reborn in the lower realms — hell (naraka), the animal realm (tiryak), or the hungry-ghost realm (preta). This is a direct consequence of severing the three fetters, which eliminates the root causes that can propel one toward evil destinies. The stream-entrant has at most seven further existences remaining, all in the human or heavenly realms. ↩︎

  26. The three fetters (tri-samyojana) — view of the transitory collection (satkaya-drishti), attachment to rules and observances (shila-vrata-paramarsha), and doubt (vicikitsa) — are standard across all Buddhist traditions (cf. SN 55.1; AN 3.86; Abhidharmakosha VI). The Sravakabhumi's distinctively Yogacara contribution is mapping each fetter to a practitioner-type: the householder not yet set forth (held back by identity-view), the seeker who has set forth toward a wrong teaching (led astray by attachment to non-Buddhist rituals), and the seeker in the right teaching but not yet habituated (stalled by doubt). These are three sequential barriers to the noble path, not merely co-existing psychological tendencies. ↩︎

  27. The Chinese 縛 at this point in the Taisho text likely represents a textual variant for 薄 ("thinned"). The standard formula in parallel Pali texts (SN 48.18; AN 9.12) describes the once-returner as one who has "thinned" (tanu) greed, aversion, and delusion — not yet eliminated them but weakened them significantly. The character 縛 (bound/fettered) would yield a less standard reading. The Shukla edition of the Sanskrit Sravakabhumi uses tanu (thinned) at this point. This translation follows the reading 薄 (thinned), consistent with the doctrinal principle that the once-returner has attenuated but not yet eliminated desire-realm afflictions. ↩︎

  28. The five lower fetters (pancha-avarabhagiya-samyojana, 五下分結) are: (1) view of the transitory collection (satkaya-drishti), (2) attachment to rules and observances (shila-vrata-paramarsha), (3) doubt (vicikitsa), (4) sensory desire (kama-raga), and (5) ill-will (vyapada). The first three are severed at stream-entry; the fourth and fifth are fully severed at non-return. "Lower" (avarabhagiya) means "pertaining to the lower realms" — these fetters bind beings specifically to the desire realm. Cf. SN 45.179-180; AN 10.13; Abhidharmakosha V.15. ↩︎

  29. The seven qualities of the thin-dust person (manda-rajas) define the ideal practitioner — one who possesses all prerequisites for rapid progress. The preceding paragraph established that persons of thin-dust conduct attain mental abiding most quickly; this passage explains why. The seven qualities represent a comprehensive inventory of favorable conditions: freedom from karmic, afflictive, and maturation obstructions; an ethical and intellectual foundation (pure discipline, upright view); accumulated merit and wisdom from both past and present lives; and innate endowments of faith, discernment, merit, and virtue. Cf. the 28 person types in fascicle 26 (sections 26.1–26.2). ↩︎

  30. The three obstructions (tri-avarana, 三障) are standard Yogacara-Sarvastivada categories (cf. Abhidharmakosha IV; Yogacarabhumi, Manobhumi). The five acts of immediate retribution (pancha-anantarya-karma, 五無間業) are: matricide, patricide, killing an arhat, wounding a buddha with hostile intent, and causing a schism in the sangha. These are called "immediate" because their karmic fruit ripens in the very next life without any intervening birth, guaranteeing rebirth in the hells. ↩︎

  31. The four types of accumulations (catur-sambhara, 四資糧) — merit, wisdom, prior-life, and present-life — are a characteristically Yogacara elaboration of the standard twofold division (merit and wisdom accumulations) found across Buddhist traditions. The inclusion of "prior-life accumulations" (wholesome roots gathered in past lives) and "present-life accumulations" (current moral and contemplative development) reflects the Yogacara emphasis on the long arc of spiritual development across multiple lifetimes. Cf. fascicle 26, sections 26.2-26.3, for the initial treatment of person types and their prerequisites. ↩︎

  32. These six categories classify persons by social and institutional role — shramana, brahmin, renunciant, etc. — and are distinct from the twenty-eight person types established in fascicle 26 (section 26.2), which classify practitioners by spiritual capacity and behavioral disposition. The two typologies are orthogonal: a person of greed-conduct (behavioral type) might be a bhikshu (institutional role), and vice versa. ↩︎

  33. The four types of shramana appear in multiple canonical sources (MN 40, AN 4.241; cf. DN 25). The Blessed One's pronouncement — that these four exist only where the noble eightfold path is found — echoes MN 11 and DN 16. The metaphor of "warmth of wholesome dharmas" (kushala-dharma-ushmagata) for the living-path shramana recalls the nirvedha-bhagiya stages (section 29.8): the practitioner possesses the first wholesome root and can generate the life-faculty of noble wisdom. ↩︎

  34. The formal monastic procedure (jnapti-caturthi-karma, 白四羯磨) is the standard Vinaya method of conferring full ordination (upasampada). It consists of one formal announcement (jnapti) of the candidate's request followed by three readings of the resolution, during which the assembled sangha may raise objections; silence constitutes consent. The five types of bhikshu range from the merely nominal (by begging, by self-proclamation, by designation) through the spiritually realized (destroying afflictions) to the legally and spiritually complete (formally ordained). ↩︎

  35. The four maras (catur-mara, 四魔) are a standard Buddhist classification (cf. SN 23.1; Visuddhimagga XVII): the skandha-mara (the five aggregates), klesha-mara (all afflictions), mrtyu-mara (death itself), and devaputra-mara (the celestial obstructer). The Sravakabhumi's catalog of mara's activities maps each obstacle to a specific practice domain — sense-gate guarding, moderation in eating, wakefulness, full awareness, seclusion — showing the yogin exactly where interference will arise. ↩︎
