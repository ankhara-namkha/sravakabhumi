Translation Notes

Scope of This Translation

This translation covers fascicles 26 through 33 of the Chinese Yogacarabhumi-sastra (Taisho Tripitaka, Vol. 30, No. 1579), encompassing three major divisions of the Sravakabhumi:

The Second Yoga Place (fascicles 26-29) establishes the foundations of practice. It opens with a taxonomy of twenty-eight types of persons and eleven criteria for their assessment, then presents a detailed treatment of the objects of meditation — pervasive objects, purifying-conduct objects (impurity, loving-kindness, dependent origination, element differentiation, and mindfulness of breathing), skillful objects, and affliction-purifying objects. This is followed by the three superior trainings, the thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening (bodhipakshya-dharma), the four shramana fruits, person typology, and the four maras.

The Third Yoga Place (fascicles 30-32) contains the heart of the meditation instruction. It begins with the protocol for approaching a teacher and the four inquiry points, then presents the five establishments for practice, including the nine stages of mental abiding (nava-akara-citta-sthiti) that became foundational for later shamatha instruction in both the Tibetan and East Asian traditions. The six powers and four types of attention that govern progress through the nine stages are detailed, followed by the four types of vipashyana and the six-aspect analytical framework applied to each of the five purifying-conduct objects. The nine practice approaches, purification of obstacles, criteria for the completion of shamatha-vipashyana, and the goldsmith simile for cultivating attention round out the theoretical framework. Fascicle 32 then provides sustained practical instruction for the beginner — the five signs, the oil-bowl simile, graduated contemplation, light-perception, the four foundations of mindfulness applied through impurity contemplation, conduct between sessions, universal shamatha practice, and the attainment of pliancy and the status "possessed of attention."

The Fourth Yoga Place (fascicle 33) describes the worldly path of ascending through the dhyanas and formless attainments. The seven attentions (sapta-manaskara) — the systematic method for abandoning attachment to each successive ground — are presented in detail, followed by term-by-term commentaries on the standard dhyana formulas, the four formless attainments, the two absorptions without mind, the five superknowledges developed through twelve perceptions, rebirth destinations mapped to attainment grades, and the characteristics of the person free from desire.

The Sravakabhumi continues beyond fascicle 33 into the supramundane path — the direct realization of the four noble truths, the stages of the noble path, and the detailed analysis of the practitioner's cultivation and culmination. These remaining portions are not included in the present translation.

Source Text

The primary source is Xuanzang's Chinese translation of the Yogacarabhumi-sastra, completed in 648 CE. The text is preserved in the Taisho Tripitaka as No. 1579, Volume 30. Xuanzang's translation is renowned for its precision and systematic terminology — his rendering of the Sravakabhumi provides a reliable witness to an Indian Sanskrit original that is only partially extant.

Where the Sanskrit text survives (notably in Shukla's 1973 critical edition), it has been consulted for terminological precision. The Tibetan translation has also been referenced where relevant. However, this is a translation from the Chinese: where the Chinese and Sanskrit diverge, the Chinese reading is followed, with the divergence noted.

Taisho page, column, and line references (e.g., [T30.0424a07]) are provided throughout for scholarly traceability.

Terminology

This translation follows the terminological register of the Nalanda Translation Committee, rendering Sanskrit terms consistently throughout as governed by a master glossary. For conventions on terminology, romanization, diacritics, and formatting, see the Abbreviations and Conventions appendix.

The Translation Process

The Challenge

The Sravakabhumi has waited over seventy years since Shukla's partial Sanskrit edition for a complete English translation. The work's combination of scale, technical density, and specialized source language has made it a formidable undertaking. A traditional translation of this kind — the sort produced by the Nalanda Translation Committee for the works of Chogyam Trungpa Rinpoche, or by the Padmakara Translation Group for the Nyingma tradition — requires a convergence of rare expertise: scholars with deep doctrinal understanding of Yogacara and Abhidharma, proficiency in Classical Chinese (and ideally Sanskrit and Tibetan for cross-reference), command of English prose sufficient to render technical philosophy as living instruction, and the collaborative patience to subject every passage to multiple rounds of specialist review.

This combination of expertise has not converged on the Sravakabhumi. Meanwhile, the text's detailed meditation instruction — its nine stages of mental abiding, its framework for vipashyana, its graduated path from beginner's practice to the dhyanas — continues to be studied and taught across Asia in languages other than English. The present translation was undertaken in the conviction that an imperfect but careful English rendering, available now, serves the Dharma better than an indefinite wait for the ideal team.

The Approach

This translation was produced computationally, using a multi-stage process designed to replicate the collaborative committee model that has produced the most respected English-language Dharma translations of the past several decades.

The initial rendering of each section was produced by translating sentence by sentence from Xuanzang's Chinese, with continuous reference to a master glossary of over 530 technical terms. Where the Sanskrit survives in Shukla's edition, it was consulted for terminological precision. The glossary itself was calibrated against nine established English translations of Buddhist texts spanning the Theravada, Tibetan, and modern practice traditions (listed in the bibliography), ensuring that key terms were rendered in accordance with the conventions familiar to English-speaking practitioners and scholars.

Each section then underwent a four-fold review by specialist processes, each examining the translation from a distinct perspective:

  • A terminological reviewer cross-referenced every technical and Sanskrit term against the master glossary, flagging inconsistencies, deprecated renderings, and terms requiring first-use glosses — the kind of systematic consistency work that a dedicated terminologist performs on a translation committee.

  • A doctrinal reviewer examined the translation for accuracy against canonical sources and established commentarial literature, checking for category errors, anachronisms, and misleading omissions — the role of the scholar-practitioner who ensures that what the text says is what the tradition actually teaches.

  • A readability reviewer approached the English text with fresh eyes, as a serious practitioner with no access to the Chinese, flagging passages that read as translated rather than written — formulaic repetition, buried main verbs, unexplained jargon, prose that catalogs rather than instructs. This is the role of the English stylist who insists that a translation not merely preserve content but transmit it.

  • A source fidelity reviewer compared the English against the Chinese sentence by sentence, counting sections, verifying that nothing had been added, omitted, or structurally rearranged — the philologist's work of ensuring that every statement in the source text has a corresponding statement in the translation.

The findings of all four reviewers were then synthesized by an editorial process that resolved conflicts according to a clear priority: source fidelity first, then doctrinal accuracy, then terminological consistency, then readability. Where readability and accuracy conflicted, a footnote was added rather than the source compromised. The result was an iterative refinement: 46 prose register rewrites across the eight fascicles, 218 scholarly footnotes providing doctrinal context and cross-references, and multiple cross-fascicle consistency passes.

The Register Standard

The target for the English prose was the register exemplified by three bodies of work: Bhikkhu Nanamoli's translation of the Visuddhimagga, Bhikkhu Bodhi's anthologies of Pali suttas, and the Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee's rendering of Tsongkhapa's Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path to Enlightenment. These translations share a quality that the present work aspires to: they are precise enough to satisfy the scholar and clear enough to serve the practitioner. They read as if written in English by someone who has absorbed the material deeply, not as if assembled from a source language clause by clause.

The intended audience is the serious practitioner who also values scholarly rigor — someone who wants the text to open understanding, not merely convey information. Taxonomic sections (the thirty-seven factors, the classification of persons) aim for the systematic clarity of Nanamoli and Bodhi. Practice-instruction sections (the meditation guidance of the Third Yoga Place, the similes for cultivating attention) aim to be warmer — the reader should feel they are receiving instruction, not reading a catalog. Throughout, Chinese clause order has been rearranged for natural English sentence flow, and formulaic repetitions — a hallmark of Xuanzang's Chinese — have been varied or compressed where this can be done without loss of content.

Limitations and Hopes

This is a machine translation, and it would be dishonest to present it as anything else. It carries inherent limitations: it lacks the direct contemplative understanding that a practitioner-scholar brings to a text about meditation, the intuitive feel for register that a native bilingual translator possesses, and the decades of immersion in the living tradition that inform the best human translations. No amount of systematic review can fully compensate for these.

What this process does offer, beyond what is typical of machine translation, is a degree of deliberate care. Standard machine translation of Buddhist texts tends to produce flat, word-by-word renderings that are technically parseable but spiritually inert — useful as cribs for scholars, but not as translations a practitioner could sit with. The approach used here — context-sensitive rendering, formula variation to avoid mechanical repetition, natural English sentence flow rather than Chinese clause order, doctrinal cross-referencing against canonical sources, and iterative multi-perspective review — was designed to produce something closer to what a translation committee would produce: a text that is faithful to the source and alive in the target language.

A note on practice passages: The Sravakabhumi contains detailed meditation instructions — graduated contemplation exercises, breathing practices, and attentional training methods — that were designed to be taught by an experienced teacher to a student whose temperament, capacity, and readiness the teacher has assessed. The text itself makes this explicit: fascicle 32 frames its entire instruction as a "teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga" guiding "a beginner who relies on yogic practice." Readers interested in undertaking any of the contemplative practices described here are strongly encouraged to seek the guidance of a qualified meditation teacher within an established Buddhist lineage.

Whether it succeeds is for the reader to judge. The hope is that this translation will serve as a working resource — for practitioners who want access to Asanga's meditation instruction, for scholars studying the Yogacara tradition, and perhaps as a foundation for a future translation by a team of lineage-holder scholars who bring to the work the direct understanding of the contemplative practices it describes. The text, together with all review findings and verification reports, is published at github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi under a Creative Commons Attribution-NonCommercial-ShareAlike 4.0 International license (CC BY-NC-SA 4.0), precisely so that such future work can build upon it freely.

Acknowledgments

This translation owes its existence first to the Chinese source text preserved in the Taisho Tripitaka — the fruit of Xuanzang's extraordinary seventh-century translation mission. Shukla's Sanskrit critical edition provided an invaluable point of cross-reference where the Indian original survives. The nine reference translations listed in the bibliography did not merely supply terminology; they established the register — the standard of what a serious English-language Dharma translation sounds like and does. Finally, the broader community of Dharma translators, from the Nalanda Translation Committee to the Padmakara Translation Group to the 84,000 project, has set the standards that this project aspired to meet. Whatever merit this work may have belongs to that lineage of effort; its shortcomings are entirely its own.
