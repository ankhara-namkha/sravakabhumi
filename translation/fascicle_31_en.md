### 31.1 Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Dependent Origination

[T30.0454a16]

The following three sections apply the same six-aspect vipashyana framework — meaning, things, own-characteristic, common characteristic, categories, time, and reasoning — to the remaining purifying-conduct objects: dependent origination, element differentiation, and mindfulness of breathing.

How does one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of dependent origination (*pratityasamutpada*) practice the six-aspect vipashyana?

**Meaning.** Based on the correct teachings concerning conditioned arising — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — one comes to understand: "Because these conditions arise, these effects arise; because those conditions cease, those effects cease." In this, there is no sovereign creator, no progenitor, no transformer who produces dharmas. There is no self-nature. No intermediary person transforms dharmas from within.[^no_agent] Understanding this is investigating the meaning of dependent origination.

**Things.** One then examines the twelve links (*dvadasha-nidana*) as they appear internally and externally, coming to understand each with clarity and confidence.

**Own-Characteristic.** One further discerns the own-characteristics of each link, beginning with ignorance — ignorance regarding the past, ignorance regarding the future — as broadly explained in the previous analysis of the links of dependent origination.

**Common Characteristic.** One further discerns that all these conditioned formations (*samskara*) are without exception: previously nonexistent, presently existent, and once having existed, they disintegrate. Therefore, from first to last, they are all impermanent. Since all are subject to birth, aging, sickness, and death, their nature is suffering. Since they are not under one's control and no intermediary person can be found, they are empty by nature and without self.

**Categories.** If I become confused about the true principles of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and no-self regarding these formations, this is inversion — belonging to the dark category, as described above. If I am not confused, this is non-inversion — belonging to the bright category.

**Time.** The self-continuity obtained in past lives had no true permanence — yet it persisted. The self-continuity obtained in the present has no true permanence — yet it persists now. The self-continuity to be obtained in future lives will have no true permanence — yet it will persist still.

**Reasoning.** One investigates dependent origination through the four types of reasoning:

*Reasoning from dependence.* "There are only karmic actions and their maturation fruits. No sovereign agent can be found — no 'doer' and no 'experiencer.' These are merely conventional designations established upon dharmas. Within the chain from ignorance conditioning formations through to birth conditioning aging-and-death, we merely posit conventional labels — a 'doer' and an 'experiencer.' This being has a particular name, clan, and lineage; eats such food; experiences such happiness or suffering; lives for a certain span and reaches such a limit of lifespan."

Within this dependent process, there are two kinds of fruit and two kinds of cause.[^two_causes]

The two fruits: (1) *The fruit of self-continuity* — the maturation-born six sense-bases and related dharmas in the present life. (2) *The fruit of experiential objects* — the feelings born from the six contacts, which arise through the dominant force of lovable and unlovable karma.

The two causes: (1) *The drawing cause.* Regarding these two fruits, ignorance arises first. With ignorance as the forerunner, meritorious, non-meritorious, and imperturbable formations are generated. These formations appropriate the seed of consciousness for a future existence — and through that, they in turn appropriate the seeds of name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, and feeling. The sprout of existence arises because each of these seeds has been appropriated in advance, ready to unfold in sequence in the next life. All of this is the drawing cause.

(2) *The generating cause.* When one receives the feelings born from contacts accompanied by ignorance, craving for present objects gives rise to craving for future existence, along with every form of grasping associated with craving and delusion. Through this force and capacity, the karma-seeds are moistened, enabling them to yield their maturation fruits. All of this is the generating cause.

Through the dominant force of these two causes, one is pursued by the three sufferings and accumulates the entire mass of suffering (*duhkha-skandha*).

*Reasoning from function.* By thoroughly and extensively cultivating this contemplation of dependent origination, one can eradicate ignorance.

*Reasoning from proof.* This is confirmed by scripture, by direct inner realization, and by inference.

*Reasoning from the nature of things.* The established nature of dharmas likewise confirms it.

This is the six-aspect vipashyana for one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of dependent origination.

[^no_agent]: The Chinese text uses three terms — 自在作者 (sovereign creator, alluding to Ishvara), 生者 (progenitor), and 化者 (transformer) — to deny any form of external or internal agent behind conditioned phenomena. This denial of a creator or agent is shared across all Buddhist schools. The Yogacara distinctive contribution is the positive mechanism — seed-consciousness (*alaya-vijnana*) and karmic seeds (*bija*) — that accounts for dependent origination without an agent.


[^two_causes]: This analysis of the twelve links into two causes (drawing and generating) and two fruits (self-continuity and experiential objects) is distinctive to the Yogacara school. The drawing cause (*akshepaka-hetu*) maps to the first two-and-a-half links (ignorance, formations, and the seed-aspect of consciousness through feeling); the generating cause (*abhinirvartaka-hetu*) maps to craving, grasping, and becoming, which "moisten" the dormant seeds and activate them for manifestation. Tsongkhapa discusses this analysis extensively in the *Lamrim Chenmo*, Vol. 1, ch. 21.

### 31.2 Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Element Differentiation

How does one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of element differentiation (*dhatu-prabheda*) practice the six-aspect vipashyana?

**Meaning.** Based on the correct teachings concerning element differentiation — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — one comes to understand the meaning of "element" (*dhatu*): it signifies lineage, seed, cause, and nature.

**Things.** One discerns the six elements — earth, water, fire, wind, consciousness, and space — in their internal and external aspects.

**Own-Characteristic.** One discerns the characteristic mark of each element: earth is solidity; water is moisture; fire is warmth; wind is lightness and movement; consciousness is discernment; and the space element is characterized by pervasive openness, filling the interstices of form and offering no obstruction.

**Common Characteristic.** All these elements, without exception, are impermanent — and being impermanent, they are likewise suffering, empty, and without self.

**Categories.** If one does not recognize the differentiated nature of elements within what appears to be a unified composite, one generates pride regarding the body composed of these elements — this is inversion, belonging to the dark category, as described above. The opposite is non-inversion, belonging to the bright category.

**Time.** One discerns that in past, present, and future alike, the six elements serve as the conditions for entering the mother's womb — assembled each time anew.

**Reasoning.** One investigates element differentiation through the four types of reasoning:

*Reasoning from dependence.* "Just as grass, wood, and other materials are assembled around empty space and are conventionally called a 'house,' so too sinew, bone, blood, and flesh — the assembled conditions surrounding empty space — are by conventional designation called a 'body.' Furthermore, this body arises from past karma and afflictions together with one's own seeds as causes and conditions."

*Reasoning from function.* By thoroughly and extensively cultivating this contemplation of element differentiation, one can eradicate pride.

*Reasoning from proof.* This is confirmed by scripture, by inner direct cognition, and by inference.

*Reasoning from the nature of things.* The nature of dharmas — established, inconceivable, abiding — also confirms it.

Such is the six-aspect investigation for the practitioner of element differentiation.

### 31.3 Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Mindfulness of Breathing

How does one who diligently cultivates mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*) practice the six-aspect vipashyana?

**Meaning.** Based on the correct teachings concerning mindfulness of in-breaths and out-breaths — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — one comes to understand: binding the mind to the object of the in-and-out breaths, with clear knowledge, non-forgetfulness, and lucid recollection — this is the meaning of *anapanasmriti*.

**Things.** One discerns that in-breaths and out-breaths are internally obtained, since they belong to the body; yet they are classified under the external sense-base.[^breath_external] This internal-external distinction constitutes the investigation of things.

**Own-Characteristic.** One discerns that there are two types of in-breath and two types of out-breath: wind entering inward is the in-breath; wind passing outward is the out-breath. One further recognizes long and short in-breaths and out-breaths, and that the breath pervades all parts of the body.

**Common Characteristic.** One discerns that when an in-breath ceases, an out-breath arises; when an out-breath ceases, an in-breath arises. The movement of in-breaths and out-breaths depends upon the life-faculty (*jivitindriya*) and the sentient body. Both the breaths and the body they depend upon are impermanent.

**Categories.** If one does not maintain right mindfulness regarding in-breaths and out-breaths, allowing unwholesome discursive thoughts to disturb the mind, this is inversion — belonging to the dark category, a conflicted dharma, as described above. The opposite is non-inversion — belonging to the bright category, a non-conflicted dharma.

**Time.** One discerns that across past, present, and future, in-breaths and out-breaths depend upon body and mind, just as body and mind depend upon in-breaths and out-breaths.

**Reasoning.** One investigates mindfulness of breathing through the four types of reasoning:

*Reasoning from dependence.* "In this, there is no one who holds the in-breath, no one who holds the out-breath — no agent to whom the breaths belong. There are only formations arising from causes and conditions. Within these, purely by conventional designation, we speak of one who 'holds' the in-breath and out-breath."

*Reasoning from function.* By thoroughly and extensively cultivating mindfulness of breathing, one can eradicate discursive thought.

*Reasoning from proof.* The same three witnesses apply: scripture, direct inner experience, and inference.

*Reasoning from the nature of things.* The nature of dharmas — established, inconceivable, and abiding — confirms this as well. It should not be further analyzed or elaborated upon, but met with faith and understanding.

This completes the six-aspect vipashyana for the practitioner of mindfulness of breathing.

### 31.4 Shamatha and Vipashyana in Mutual Support

Having investigated the purifying-conduct objects through the six aspects, one alternates: stilling the mind inwardly through shamatha, then turning to investigate what has been discerned through vipashyana. Supported by shamatha, vipashyana is swiftly purified. Supported by vipashyana, shamatha grows vast and expansive.[^mutual_support]

As for the six-aspect vipashyana applied to the skillful objects and the affliction-purifying objects, these will be explained in their proper place later.

### 31.5 The Nine Practice Approaches — Introduction and Approaches 1–4

[T30.0455b16]

Nine approaches characterize practice belonging to the bright category; their nine opposites characterize the dark.

What are the nine approaches of the bright category? They are: (1) appropriate practice, (2) habituated practice, (3) non-inverted practice, (4) unslackened practice, (5) timely practice, (6) comprehending practice, (7) insatiable practice, (8) not-abandoning-the-yoke practice, and (9) correct practice.

Through these nine bright-category approaches, one can swiftly attain samadhi and cause one's samadhi to advance further. Through them, one can swiftly reach whatever ground one should reach and attain whatever one should attain, without delay. The nine dark-category approaches, by contrast, cannot bring swift samadhi. They cause great delay in reaching one's destination and attaining one's goal.

---

**(1) Appropriate practice.** The object must match the practitioner's character-type: those of the greed-conduct type settle the mind on impurity; the aversion-conduct type, on loving-kindness and compassion; the delusion-conduct type, on dependent origination; the pride-conduct type, on element differentiation; the discursive-thought-conduct type, on mindfulness of breathing. For the balanced-conduct type or the thin-dust type, one settles the mind on whatever single object one finds pleasing. In each case, one diligently practices.

**(2) Habituated practice.** One has previously cultivated shamatha and vipashyana to at least some degree — one is not a complete beginner in all respects. A complete beginner, even when diligently practicing with the appropriate object, is repeatedly beset by the hindrances; physical and mental heaviness keeps arising. For this reason, the mind cannot swiftly attain samadhi.

**(3) Non-inverted practice.** One follows the instruction of accomplished yogin teachers who have awakened to the practice, learning just as they teach. One grasps dharma and meaning without distortion, free from conceit, not clinging to one's own views, free from wrong and biased attachments, never disdaining the venerable teacher's instruction.

**(4) Unslackened practice.** One practices with uninterrupted effort and earnest application. When one leaves samadhi — whether to beg for food, respectfully serve teachers and elders, care for the sick, maintain communal harmony, or attend to other necessary tasks — the mind does not fully follow those activities, does not fully incline toward them, does not fully enter into them. One swiftly completes the task at hand and returns to diligent seated meditation in quietude.

Even when encountering various assemblies — bhikshus, bhikshunis, upasakas, kshatriyas, brahmins, and others — though among them for some time, exchanging courtesies and gestures, one does not sustain prolonged conversation. One delights only in seclusion and diligent practice.

One cultivates courageous diligence: "I will certainly attain what should be attained. I must not be slack!" Why not? Because there are many causes of sudden death. Within the body, wind, heat, or phlegm may become agitated. Food may fail to digest and lodge as illness. Externally, one may be bitten by snakes, scorpions, centipedes, or other venomous creatures, or frightened to death by humans or non-human beings. Constantly reflecting on these dangers, one cultivates the perception of impermanence and dwells in non-negligence.

Dwelling in such non-negligence, one constantly reflects: "If my life should last seven more days — six, five, four, three, two, one day — one hour, half an hour, a moment, half a moment, the time of a single meal, a single in-breath to out-breath, a single out-breath to in-breath — for however long I remain alive, I will attend to the Buddha's sacred teaching with diligent effort, cultivating yoga. In whatever time remains, I will surely accomplish much."

### 31.6 The Nine Practice Approaches — Approach 5: Timely Practice

**(5) Timely practice.** One cultivates the signs of calming, observation, uplifting, and equanimity each at the appropriate time — truly understanding each of these four: its nature, its sign, and its proper timing.

What is calming? It is the nine stages of mental abiding, through which the mind becomes signless, free from conceptual elaboration — peaceful, supremely peaceful, evenly abiding, stilled, purely unified.

What is the sign of calming? There are two kinds. The *object-sign* is the cognitive image corresponding to knowable objects of the shamatha class; through this object, the mind becomes still. The *causal sign*: based on a mind habituated to shamatha, one practices the vipashyana aspect of yoga so that one's future shamatha may be purified.

When is the time for calming? When the mind is agitated or in danger of becoming agitated. Likewise, when a mind habituated to vipashyana is disturbed by discursive thoughts or the demands of daily activities — that too is the time for calming.

What is observation? It is the contemplation that operates through four modes, three gates, and the six aspects of differentiated objects.

What is the sign of observation? Again, two kinds. The *object-sign* is the cognitive image corresponding to knowable objects of the vipashyana class; through this object, wisdom investigates. The *causal sign*: based on a mind habituated to vipashyana, one practices internal shamatha so that one's future vipashyana may be purified.

When is the time for observation? When the mind sinks or is in danger of sinking. Also, when a mind habituated to shamatha should now realize knowable objects as they truly are — that is the time for observation.

What is uplifting? Taking up a pure, excellent object to encourage, guide, and gladden the mind.

What is the sign of uplifting? It is the pure, excellent object that energizes the mind, in accord with arousing diligent effort.

When is the time for uplifting? When the mind has sunk low or is in danger of sinking low.

What is equanimity? It is the mind's freedom from affliction toward its object — the quality of mental evenness. In both the calming and observation aspects, the mind possesses pliancy, uprightness, and spontaneous functioning; it is a capable, trained mind that operates freely and naturally.

What is the sign of equanimity? Through the object, the mind lets go of striving. One does not generate excessive effort toward the object.

When is the time for equanimity? When the mind has been freed from agitation in both the shamatha and vipashyana aspects — that is the time for equanimity. This is timely practice.

### 31.7 The Nine Practice Approaches — Approaches 6–9 and Conclusion

**(6) Comprehending practice.** Having well grasped and thoroughly understood the signs described above, one can enter samadhi at will, abide in it at will, and emerge at will. At times, one sets aside the cognitive images generated in samadhi and turns attention to the ordinary objects of the non-concentrated state.

**(7) Insatiable practice.** One is never satisfied with wholesome dharmas; cultivation and abandonment proceed without lapse. One continually aspires to states ever higher and more excellent, never resting content with a small measure of samadhi and losing heart midway. There is always further aspiration toward what remains to be accomplished.

**(8) Not-abandoning-the-yoke practice.** One maintains all training rules without breach or gap. Even upon seeing a young woman of attractive appearance, one does not grasp at signs or attend to secondary features. One is moderate in eating, diligent in wakefulness, engaged in few tasks and few activities, with little distraction. One is able to recall — and help others recall — actions and words from long ago.

These practices properly support mental one-pointedness. One does not cast off the yoke: the mind is kept from scattering, prevented from rushing toward external objects, and maintained in internal pliancy.

**(9) Correct practice.** One repeatedly generates resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*) toward the object and repeatedly clears it away. For example, one who cultivates the contemplation of impurity repeatedly clears the impurity object and then, directing attention to the signs of impurity through the sign-following mode of vipashyana, causes the object to appear again — clearing and presenting it in alternation.

This practice of clearing has five types: (1) internally collecting the mind, (2) non-attending attention, (3) redirected attention, (4) antidote attention, and (5) signless-realm attention.

*Internally collecting the mind* means: through the nine stages of mental abiding, with vipashyana as the leading force, one draws the mind inward. *Non-attending attention* means: from the very beginning, one turns away from all signs and abides without disturbance. *Redirected attention* means: one takes up the objects of another concentrative level, contemplating a different ground of concentration. *Antidote attention* means: one contemplates impurity to counteract attachment to purity, mindfulness of breathing to counteract discursive thought, the space element to counteract material form — and so on for each pairing. *Signless-realm attention* means: one ceases attending to all signs and turns attention to the signless realm.

Although all five types of clearing apply across every object, the principal methods intended here are two: internally collecting the mind, and non-attending attention. These two are foundational; the remaining three arise from specific circumstances.

When a beginner first undertakes the practice, one does not initially bind the mind to any object at all — neither impurity nor anything else. One simply resolves: "How can my mind become non-scattered, signless, free from conceptual elaboration — peaceful, supremely peaceful, without movement, without agitation, without hope, free from all activity, inwardly at ease?" With this earnest resolve, one ceases attending to all external signs that arise, practicing non-attending attention.[^signless_beginner] Through this, one clears away the objects and gathers ease and comfort within the practice of yoga.

One then returns to the observed objects — impurity and the rest — which have signs and conceptual content. How does one engage with them? Through the sign-following mode, the investigation-following mode, the examination-following mode, and the vipashyana mode. Yet one does not exclusively cultivate vipashyana regarding those objects. One relinquishes the observation aspect and returns to contemplating the calming mode of the object.

For this reason, the practitioner at that time neither abandons nor grasps the object. Because calming continues to operate toward the object, it is not abandoned. Because one does not form signs or engage in conceptualization regarding the object, it is not grasped. Through this internal collection of the mind, one clears the object. The mind remains undisturbed because one does not take up the observation aspect; and because one takes up the calming aspect, the mind still engages with the knowable object.

If one only generates resolute confidence toward the object again and again without ever clearing it, that confidence will never become progressively clearer and more refined. One cannot advance to direct perception of the knowable object. It is because one alternates — generating confidence and then clearing — that each successive arising becomes progressively clearer and more refined, ultimately reaching the point of directly perceiving the object.

It is like an apprentice painter first learning the craft from a master. The apprentice studies the model, observes it carefully, and draws the form. Having drawn it, one erases it. Having erased it, one draws again. Each time one erases and redraws, the successive forms become clearer, more refined, and more fully realized. Training in this way over a long period, one eventually earns recognition as a great painter — or at least is counted among master painters. But if one never erases, instead painting layer upon layer over the same form, the image will never become clear and refined. The principle here is just the same.[^painter]

When one generates confidence regarding a particular object, one must clear that same object — not a different one. However, after clearing, one is not bound to generate confidence at the same scope again. When confidence is generated at a narrow scope, it is cleared at the same scope. After clearing, one may generate confidence again at the narrow scope, or shift to the vast or immeasurable. The same principle applies throughout.

When the images of material dharmas appear, they manifest as coarse, transformation-like forms. The images of immaterial dharmas arise led by conceptual designation, manifesting through the dominant force of prior experience. All of this constitutes correct practice.

---

These nine bright-category approaches are favorable to shamatha and vipashyana. Their opposites — the nine dark-category approaches — are adverse to shamatha and vipashyana. Distinguishing dark from bright, there are eighteen approaches in all. This constitutes the teaching on mental one-pointedness (*ekagrata*).

[^painter]: The painter simile illustrates the central insight of correct practice: resolute confidence alone — no matter how intense or repeated — cannot refine itself. Only by alternating between generating the meditation image and dissolving it does each successive arising become clearer. This dynamic of conviction and clearing is the essential rhythm of contemplative training in the Yogacara system.

### 31.8 Purification of Obstacles

[T30.0457b04]

How does one purify obstacles? Yogins who properly cultivate the approaches purify their minds of obstacles through four causes: (1) thorough knowledge of their nature, (2) thorough knowledge of their causes, (3) thorough knowledge of their dangers, and (4) cultivating antidotes.

**The nature of obstacles.** There are four types: (1) the timidity obstacle, (2) the covering obstacle, (3) the discursive-thought obstacle, and (4) the self-elevation obstacle.

The *timidity obstacle* arises when one diligently practices renunciation and seclusion: afflicted longing, displeasure, anxious hope, and distress surface. The *covering obstacle* is the five hindrances — sensual desire and the rest. The *discursive-thought obstacle* consists of afflicted discursive thoughts beginning with thoughts of sensual desire. The *self-elevation obstacle* arises from partial, inferior wisdom-views or comfortable abiding: one exalts oneself, thinking "I can attain this; others cannot" — as broadly explained above.[^four_obstacles]

**The causes of obstacles.** The timidity obstacle has six causes: (1) through the force of past karma or disturbed by illness, the body is weakened; (2) excessive effort; (3) failure to practice; (4) being a beginner in the practice; (5) intense afflictions; (6) unfamiliarity with seclusion.

The covering, discursive-thought, and self-elevation obstacles share a single root cause: improper attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*) habitually directed toward conditions that favor these obstacles. What is improper attention here? Attending to signs of purity instead of contemplating impurity; to signs of anger instead of loving-kindness; to darkness rather than the sign of brightness; to memories of relatives, homeland, immortality, past pleasures, and distracting thoughts instead of the sign of shamatha; and conceiving "I" and "mine" regarding formations across the three times instead of contemplating dependent origination.[^improper_five]

**The dangers of obstacles.** One thoroughly understands: because these obstacles exist, what has not yet been attained remains beyond reach, and whatever has been attained slips away. One's yoga practice is corrupted. One dwells with affliction and suffering, harms oneself and others, and at death is reborn in evil destinies.

**Cultivating antidotes.** For all forms of timidity, recollection (*anusmriti*) is the general antidote: one gladdens the mind through remembrance, dispelling timidity already arisen and preventing its future arising. The specific antidotes vary by cause. Bodily weakness, excessive effort, and beginner inexperience call for balanced, thorough application of effort. Failure to practice calls for respectful listening and diligent questioning. Intense afflictions call for practice with the appropriate object — impurity contemplation, for instance. And for unfamiliarity with seclusion, one reasons: "Because in the past I was unfamiliar with seclusion, timidity now arises when I practice it. If I do not train in seclusion now, the same will be true in future lives. Therefore I should abandon my displeasure toward seclusion and cultivate delight in it."

For the covering and other obstacles rooted in improper attention, the antidote is wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) — the direct opposite of the improper attention that caused them.

Furthermore, one knows that obstacles by nature are obstructing, defiling, and belong to the dark category — they should be abandoned. To abandon them, one must know their causes. To generate urgency in abandoning them, one must know their dangers. Having abandoned them, one reflects: "How can I prevent these obstacles from arising in future lives?" — and therefore seeks to cultivate antidotes. Through these four causes, one purifies the mind of obstacles.

[^four_obstacles]: These four obstacles map to the four meditation activities of approach 5 (timely practice): timidity is countered by uplifting; the covering obstacle by calming; the discursive-thought obstacle by observation; and self-elevation by equanimity. The typology is distinctive to the Yogacara meditation system.

[^improper_five]: The five examples of improper attention correspond to the five hindrances and their antidotes. This mapping partially overlaps the five behavioral types and purifying-conduct objects but is not identical: notably, brightness/light and shamatha appear here as antidotes, rather than element differentiation and mindfulness of breathing as in that system.

### 31.9 Shamatha and Vipashyana: Mutual Growth, Completion Criteria, and Paired Operation

[T30.0457c27]

One should know: because the teachings that support practice are many, vipashyana is also manifold. Because vipashyana is manifold, shamatha too is manifold. Furthermore, because the objects of knowledge are without limit, the scope of vipashyana is likewise without limit — for through three gates and six aspects, each admits boundless varieties of differentiated realization.

As vipashyana is sustained and refined, it generates increasing physical and mental pliancy — and that pliancy, in turn, deepens mental one-pointedness. Shamatha and vipashyana thus feed each other: the stillness sharpens the seeing; the seeing opens the stillness wider. Mental one-pointedness and physical-mental pliancy depend upon each other, each sustaining the other. This mutual nourishment is perfected only when transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravrtti*) is attained. Through this transformation, direct perceptual wisdom regarding the knowable objects arises.[^transformation]

**Completion criteria.** At what point can one say that the contemplation of impurity has been fully attained — and likewise, each of the remaining contemplations up to mindfulness of breathing?

In brief:[^completion_criteria] through close, extensive, and repeated cultivation of the contemplation of impurity, there comes a point where the aspect of impurity naturally predominates without deliberate effort — whether one is walking or standing, whatever objects are present and however many aspects are observed — rather than aspects of purity. Because impurity has been well cultivated, the mind does not enter into, does not delight in, and does not incline toward conditions that give rise to the entanglement of desire. One abides in equanimity with deep revulsion. At that point, the practitioner should know: "I have attained the contemplation of impurity. I have attained the fruit of my cultivation." This is full attainment. Its absence means one has not yet fully attained.

The same principle applies to the remaining four contemplations, each with its distinctive signs: for loving-kindness, the aspect of a loving mind predominates, displacing anger, and the mind does not enter conditions favoring anger. For dependent origination, impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and no-self come to the fore, dissolving the aspects of permanence, pleasure, and the delusion accompanying self-view. For element differentiation, one perceives varied, multiple element-natures and a differentiated body, no longer a unified whole. For mindfulness of breathing, inner tranquility and shamatha predominate, leaving idle elaboration behind.

**Paired operation.** At what point can one say that shamatha and vipashyana operate in balanced union together — the "path of paired operation"?

When one has attained the ninth of the nine stages of mental abiding — meditative equipoise (*samahita*) — and, using this perfected samadhi as a basis, cultivates higher wisdom through dharma-investigation: at that point, through dharma-investigation, the path flows spontaneously, operating without effort and without deliberate exertion. Vipashyana becomes pure and luminous, pliantly gathered within shamatha's embrace, flowing as if on shamatha's own path. This is the balanced union of shamatha and vipashyana in mutual operation — the path of paired operation.[^paired]

[^transformation]: The term 轉依 (*ashraya-paravrtti*, "transformation of the basis") is central to Yogacara soteriology. Here it marks the point where shamatha and vipashyana cease being separate trainings and become a single, self-sustaining process — generating direct (non-conceptual) knowledge of the objects of meditation.

[^paired]: The "path of paired operation" (*yuganaddha-marga*) represents the culmination of shamatha-vipashyana training described in this section. Tsongkhapa discusses this passage at length in the *Lamrim Chenmo* Vol. 3, noting that genuine union requires the ninth stage of mental abiding (balanced settling) as its foundation — not merely occasional alternation between calming and insight.

### 31.11 Cultivation of Attention: Four Types, Objects of Practice, and the Goldsmith Simile

[T30.0458b23]

How does one cultivate attention? When a beginner first undertakes the practice, within the universally established signs, one seeks mental one-pointedness and the joy of abandonment — leaving wrong practice behind and learning correct practice. One should first resolve: "To attain mental one-pointedness and the joy of abandonment, I shall diligently cultivate four types of attention."

What are the four? (1) *Attention that disciplines the mind* — through this attention, regarding what is disagreeable, one causes the mind to become disenchanted and turn away. (2) *Attention that moistens the mind* — through this attention, regarding what is admirable, one causes the mind to delight. (3) *Attention that generates pliancy* — one alternates: at intervals turning the mind from disagreeable dharmas, at intervals delighting it with admirable ones, then settling inwardly in signless, non-conceptual, one-pointed serenity. All physical and mental heaviness falls away; ease and pliancy pervade body and mind. (4) *Attention that purifies wisdom-vision* — at intervals, resting on inner mental serenity as a basis, one repeatedly applies dharma-investigation to cultivate higher wisdom.[^four_attentions_31]

**Objects of repulsion.** At intervals, the practitioner turns the mind from disagreeable dharmas, generating urgency and deeper urgency, disenchantment and deeper disenchantment regarding outflows and the conditions that sustain them. There are four objects of repulsion: (1) one's own decline, and (2) another's decline — when currently encountering these and reflecting with wise attention, they become objects of disenchantment; (3) one's own prosperity, and (4) another's prosperity — when these have been exhausted, destroyed, and changed in the past, reflecting with wise attention makes them too objects of disenchantment.

**Objects of delight.** At intervals, the practitioner delights the mind in admirable dharmas, so that the mind becomes thoroughly moistened, supple, and clear. There are three objects of delight:

*First, recollecting the Three Jewels.* One reflects: "I have well obtained this great benefit — the Tathagata, worthy and fully awakened, is my great teacher. I have well obtained this great benefit — I have gone forth in the well-proclaimed Dharma-Vinaya. I have well obtained this great benefit — my companions in the holy life are those who possess discipline and virtue, who are patient, gentle, and endowed with wholesome qualities. I shall attain a good death and a good destination in the next life."

*Second, recollecting pure discipline.* One reflects: "I have well obtained this great benefit — among the shravaka community who rightly practice in the Dharma-Vinaya proclaimed by the Tathagata, I share with my companions the same precepts, the same learning, the same loving-kindness in body, speech, and mind, the same views, the same enjoyments." Beginning with non-regret, joy arises.

*Third, confidence in one's own attainments.* One reflects: "I have power and capacity. My discipline is pure. I am a fit vessel for the dharma. I share pure discipline with my companions and share views with the wise and virtuous. I am capable of diligently practicing this right conduct. In this very life, I can attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized." Through this, great joy arises. Recollecting the attainments already established through courageous diligence, one generates deep confidence that future attainments too are within reach — and delight arises.

**Integration.** The practitioner disciplines the mind through repulsive objects — regarding conditions that sustain afflictive outflows, the mind turns away, rejects, and separates from them. Through delightful objects, the mind is moistened and refreshed — because one has affection for the dharmas born of renunciation and freedom, the mind inclines toward them and abides in joyful harmony. Through these two movements — repulsion and delight — the mind turns from the dark category toward the bright, flowing freely.

The mind turns from dark through the disciplining attention, and toward bright through the moistening attention. At intervals the pliancy-generating attention gathers it inward through shamatha; at intervals the wisdom-purifying attention engages it in discerning, scrutinizing, investigating, and examining dharmas. Shamatha and vipashyana thus sustain the mind in alternation, and it becomes capable of embracing all aspects and qualities as causes for further cultivation. Day and night, moment by moment, the practice deepens.

**The goldsmith simile.** It is like a skilled goldsmith — or the goldsmith's apprentice — who at intervals fires and refines gold and silver, causing them to shed all impurities, and at intervals plunges them in cool water, rendering them workable, pliant, and suited for the craft. Then, with fine expertise and well-practiced skill, the goldsmith takes up the tools and transforms the metal into whatever ornaments are desired.[^goldsmith]

Just so, the diligent yogin — to make the mind shed the impurities of desire and all afflicted distress — generates deep disenchantment with repulsive dharmas. To direct the mind toward all that is pure, wholesome, and joyful, one generates delight in admirable dharmas. Then the practitioner, wishing to settle in whatever aspect — whether shamatha or vipashyana — can readily approach each, skillfully unite with each, without wavering or disturbance. Whatever purpose one sets the mind toward, one accomplishes in accord with one's resolute confidence.

[^four_attentions_31]: These four types of cultivation-attention — disciplining, moistening, pliancy-generating, and wisdom-purifying — are distinct from the four operational attentions of section 30.12 (forceful, interrupted, uninterrupted, effortless), which describe how attention functions within the nine stages. The present set describes how the yogin cultivates the mind overall: objects of repulsion (decline and impermanence of prosperity), objects of delight (Three Jewels, pure discipline, confidence in attainments), and their integration in practice.

[^goldsmith]: The goldsmith simile maps directly to the four attentions: firing removes impurities (= disciplining attention turning the mind from afflictions); cool water renders the metal workable (= moistening attention brightening the mind with joy); the goldsmith's expertise and tools shape the ornament (= pliancy-generating and wisdom-purifying attentions enabling shamatha and vipashyana at will). The simile recurs in the Pali canon (e.g., AN 3.100) with similar structure.

[^breath_external]: Breath is "internally obtained" — experienced within one's own body — yet classified under the external tangible sense-base (*sprashtavya-ayatana*), because wind as tangible form is an object of tactile consciousness. This classification grounds the subsequent instruction to contemplate breath as simultaneously internal and external.

[^mutual_support]: This brief but pivotal passage states the principle developed at length in section 31.9 below: shamatha and vipashyana are not parallel tracks but a single spiral, each deepening the other. The Lamrim Chenmo devotes extensive discussion to this interdependence, emphasizing that neither calm abiding alone nor insight alone suffices — only their union produces transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravrtti*).

[^signless_beginner]: This instruction — that a beginner should initially settle the mind without binding it to any object whatsoever, simply resolving toward signlessness and freedom from elaboration — differs significantly from the graduated approach of the Visuddhimagga, where the beginner always starts with a specific meditation object (Visuddhimagga III.104 ff.). The Sravakabhumi's method begins with objectless settling, then introduces objects only after initial ease is established. This "clearing-first" approach may reflect a distinctively Yogacara pedagogy.

[^completion_criteria]: These completion criteria — specifying exactly when a practitioner can know that a contemplation has been "fully attained" — are among the most practically significant passages in the Sravakabhumi. Each criterion has the same structure: (1) the contemplation's characteristic aspect arises spontaneously without deliberate effort, and (2) the mind no longer enters conditions that sustain the corresponding affliction. This precision in defining mastery is distinctive to the Sravakabhumi and is not found in the Pali meditation manuals at this level of specificity.
