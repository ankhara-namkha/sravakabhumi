## Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Fourth Yoga Place, Part One

本地分中聲聞地第十三第四瑜伽處之一

### 33.1 Uddana Verse for the Fourth Yoga Place

[T30.0465a28]

> Seven attentions, abandoning desire,
> And the dhyanas explained in full;
> Two absorptions and five superknowledges,
> Birth-differences and their characteristics.
> Observation of the truths
> And their realization as they truly are;
> Detailed analysis of cultivation,
> With culmination at the end.[^uddana_33]

[^uddana_33]: The uddana verse outlines the full scope of the Fourth Yoga Place. Only the first four topics — seven attentions, dhyanas, two absorptions with superknowledges, and rebirth — are covered in fascicle 33. The remaining topics (observation of truths, realization, detailed analysis of cultivation, culmination) continue into subsequent fascicles of the Yogacarabhumi.

---

### 33.2 Two Paths After Attaining "Possessed of Attention"

[T30.0465b04]

The yogins who have attained the status of "possessed of attention" have entered this initial degree of the joy of abandonment. From this point forward, only two directions remain — no other. What are the two? The worldly and the supramundane.[^two_paths]

Through this attention, a beginner yogin may think: "I shall proceed toward the worldly path," or "I shall proceed toward the supramundane path." Through continued cultivation of this attention — the more extensively one practices, the more pliancy and mental one-pointedness expand and deepen, period by period, day and night.

When this attention becomes firm, continuous, and powerful — giving rise to clear conviction regarding the meditation object, well apprehending the signs of both the shamatha and vipashyana aspects — the yogin then undertakes the practice of either the worldly path or the supramundane path.

[^two_paths]: Having attained the initial shamatha ("possessed of attention," *samanaskara*) at the end of fascicle 32, the yogin faces a crucial fork: the worldly path (*laukika-marga*, 世間道) cultivates the dhyanas and formless attainments by suppressing afflictions, while the supramundane path (*lokottara-marga*, 出世間道) aims at their permanent eradication through insight into the four noble truths. This fascicle primarily describes the worldly path; the supramundane path continues in subsequent fascicles.

---

### 33.3 Four Types of Persons Pursuing the Worldly Path

[T30.0465b14]

**Question:** How many types of persons, in the present life, take pleasure in undertaking the practice of the worldly path rather than the supramundane?

**Answer:** There are, in brief, four types of persons.[^four_types] **(1)** All non-Buddhists (*tirthika*). **(2)** Those within the correct dharma whose faculties are weak and who first cultivate the path of shamatha. **(3)** Those whose faculties, though sharp, have wholesome roots not yet ripened. **(4)** All bodhisattvas who seek great awakening in future lives rather than in the present.

These four types of persons take pleasure in undertaking the worldly path in this present life. Among those who undertake the worldly path, there are further two sub-categories:[^two_sub_types] **(a)** the fully fettered — that is, ordinary beings; and **(b)** those not fully fettered — that is, learners (*shaiksha*).

How does this path proceed? One first perceives the desire realm as coarse and the first dhyana — whether as an attainment or as a realm of birth — as peaceful. One then undertakes the practice of abandoning desire for the desire realm. The same pattern applies in succession through the abandoning of desire for the sphere of nothingness. Additionally, relying on the dhyanas and related attainments, one can enter the attainment of non-perception and develop the five superknowledges (*abhijna*).

[^four_types]: The four types who pursue the worldly path: (1) non-Buddhists (*tirthika*, 外道), who lack the view of the four noble truths; (2) those with weak faculties (根性羸劣), who need to build samadhi through shamatha before they can sustain insight; (3) those with sharp faculties but unripened wholesome roots (善根未熟), who are not yet ready for direct realization; (4) bodhisattvas (菩薩), who deliberately choose future great awakening (*mahabodhi*) rather than present liberation. The distinction reveals the Yogacara perspective that the worldly path, while not leading to final liberation, serves crucial preparatory functions.

[^two_sub_types]: "Fully fettered" (具縛) refers to ordinary beings (*prthagjana*) still bound by all afflictions — these include non-Buddhists and practitioners with unripened roots. "Not fully fettered" (不具縛) refers to noble learners (*shaiksha*, 有學) who have already cut some fetters through supramundane insight but cultivate the worldly path for additional samadhi powers.

### 33.4 The Seven Attentions for Abandoning Desire

[T30.0465b27]

Both the births and characteristics pertaining to this worldly path shall now be explained in detail.

The yogins who diligently cultivate contemplative practice to abandon desire for the desire realm can achieve this abandonment only through seven types of attention.[^seven_attentions] What are these seven? **(1)** Attention to characteristics. **(2)** Attention of conviction. **(3)** Attention of separation. **(4)** Attention of joyful engagement. **(5)** Attention of investigation. **(6)** Attention of completed application. **(7)** Attention to the fruit of completed application.[^seven_vs_four]

[^seven_attentions]: The seven attentions (*sapta-manaskara*, 七作意) constitute the step-by-step method of the worldly path for abandoning desire for a lower realm and entering the next higher attainment. The same framework applies at each successive transition: from the desire realm to the first dhyana, from the first to the second dhyana, and so on through the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception (see section 33.13). The first attention uses conceptual investigation still mixed with hearing and reflection; the second transitions to pure meditative cultivation; the third severs upper-grade afflictions, the fourth middle-grade; the fifth verifies whether latent tendencies remain; the sixth severs lower-grade afflictions; and the seventh is the actual entry into the higher attainment.

[^seven_vs_four]: These seven attentions are a distinct framework from the four types of attention described in section 30.12 (forceful, interrupted, uninterrupted, and effortless). The four types of attention characterize stages of the shamatha path itself; the seven attentions here characterize stages of the worldly renunciation path — the sequential abandonment of desire-realm afflictions through contrast with a higher attainment.

### 33.5 Attention to Characteristics: The Six-Aspect Analysis of Desire

What is the attention to characteristics? It is the attention that correctly discerns the coarse aspects of the desire realm and the peaceful aspects of the first dhyana.[^attention_chars]

How does one discern the coarse aspects of the desire realm? Through correct investigation of six aspects of desires (for the six aspects, see section 30.14). The six are: **(1)** meaning, **(2)** substance, **(3)** characteristic, **(4)** category, **(5)** time, and **(6)** reasoning.

*How does one investigate the coarse meaning of desires?* One investigates that desires have many faults, many injuries, many pestilences, and many calamities. This quality of being full of faults — and so on through being full of calamities — is called the coarse meaning of desires.

*How does one investigate the coarse substance of desires?* One investigates that among desires there is internal craving and external craving.

*How does one investigate the own-characteristic of desires?* One investigates the two basic forms: the affliction of desire itself — the inner craving (*klesha-kama*) — and the objects that occasion it (*vastu-kama*).[^own_char]

Each class of sense-experience then becomes a breeding ground for specific afflictions. Pleasant experience sustains craving and the inversions of perception and mind. Painful experience gives rise to anger and resentment. Neutral experience, more subtly, sustains ignorance — and from that root: concealment, vexation, deceit, flattery, shamelessness, recklessness, and the inversion of views. Whatever the feeling-tone, desires are attended by the most harmful kinds of feelings and the most harmful kinds of afflictions. This is investigating the own-characteristic of desires.

*How does one investigate the shared characteristic of desires?* One investigates that all desires are attended and bound by suffering — birth, aging, sickness, death, separation from what is loved, and the failure to obtain what one seeks. Those who indulge desires are driven and oppressed even when apparently satisfied, because they remain unfreed from birth and from all that follows it. However excellent and complete desires may be, they endure only briefly.

*How does one investigate the coarse category of desires?* One investigates that all desires fall within the dark category.[^eight_similes] They are like a chain of bones, like a lump of clotted flesh, like a grass torch, like a pit of burning coals, like a great venomous serpent, like objects seen in a dream, like borrowed ornaments, and like fruit at the top of a tree.

Sentient beings who pursue desires suffer the pain of that pursuit, the pain of guarding what they possess, the pain of losing those they love, the pain of insatiability, the pain of being without autonomy, and the pain caused by misconduct. All of this should be understood as explained previously.

As the Blessed One has said, there are five faults in habitually engaging desires. First, desires yield extremely meager gratification yet bring much suffering and many faults. Second, when one habitually engages them, they breed insatiability — one can never be sated, never satisfied, never fulfilled. Third, they are constantly reproached and condemned by the Buddhas and their disciples, and by all those who are virtuous and practicing rightly, through innumerable teachings. Fourth, they cause fetters to accumulate and increase. Fifth, when one habitually engages them — I say there is no evil or unwholesome action that one will not commit.

Thus desires produce insatiability and are shared by many. They are the cause of unrighteous and evil conduct and increase craving. The wise abandon them. They tend quickly toward destruction, depend on numerous conditions, and are the ground of heedlessness and peril — impermanent, deceptive, and delusive phenomena, like magical illusions deceiving fools. Whether desires of this present life or of future lives, whether heavenly or human, all are Mara's domain and Mara's dwelling place. Within that domain arise innumerable mental evils and unwholesome dharmas — craving, anger, fury, contention, and the rest — that obstruct noble disciples in their training.

Through these and other such distinctions, desires are for the most part contained within the dark category. This is investigating the coarse category of desires.

*Regarding the coarse time-aspect of desires:* one investigates that desires — past, present, and future — are at all times and without respite full of faults, injuries, pestilences, and calamities. This is investigating the coarse time-aspect of desires.

*How does one investigate the coarse reasoning about desires?* One investigates that acquiring, sustaining, and increasing desires demands enormous resources — great pursuit, great exertion, and mastery of countless trades and skills. Even when desires are fully established, they mostly serve others — parents, spouse, children, servants, workers, friends, and relatives. Whatever remains is spent maintaining one's own body: this heavy vessel of the four great elements, requiring constant nourishment, covering, bathing, and care — a body subject by its nature to deterioration, breakage, dispersal, and destruction, and the source of endless suffering.

Food counteracts the suffering of hunger and thirst. Clothing counteracts the suffering of cold and heat and covers what should be hidden. Bedding counteracts the suffering of fatigue, sleepiness, and the strain of walking and standing. Medicine counteracts the suffering of illness.

Therefore desires serve only to counteract the various sufferings that arise. One should not enjoy them with attachment but should use them only with right mindfulness — like a person oppressed by grave illness who takes foul medicine for the sake of curing the disease.

Furthermore, the coarseness of desires is confirmed by scriptural authority (*agama-pramana*). Their coarse aspects are known through one's own direct perception and insight. They are known through inference (*anumana*). And desires have been coarse and impure by nature since beginningless time — this simply is how things are (*dharmata*), beyond conceptual elaboration or discrimination. This is investigating the coarse reasoning about desires.

Such is how one discerns the coarse aspects of desire-realm desires through six kinds of investigation.

One further discerns the peaceful aspects of the first dhyana: all the coarseness of the desire realm is entirely absent in the first dhyana. Because it is free from these coarse qualities, the first dhyana is said to possess the quality of peacefulness. This is discerning the peaceful aspects of the first dhyana.

Through this attention belonging to the ground of concentration, one discerns the desire realm as coarse and the first dhyana as peaceful. It is therefore called the attention to characteristics. This attention should be understood as still interspersed with hearing and reflection.[^hearing_reflection]

[^attention_chars]: The attention to characteristics (了相作意) is the first and most analytically detailed of the seven attentions. Its function is to establish a clear cognitive contrast between the coarseness (*audarika*) of the current level and the peacefulness (*shanta*) of the next-higher level. Here, applied to the transition from the desire realm to the first dhyana, it involves systematic analysis of desires through six perspectives. This analysis remains at the level of hearing (*shruta*) and reflection (*cinta*) rather than direct meditative cultivation (*bhavana*).

[^own_char]: Affliction-desire (*klesha-kama*, 煩惱欲) is the inner affliction of craving itself; object-desire (*vastu-kama*, 事欲) encompasses the external objects that occasion craving. The three feeling-bases correspond to standard Abhidharma vedana analysis: pleasant feeling grounds craving and the inversions of perception and mind (*samjna-citta-viparyasa*, 想心倒); painful feeling grounds anger; and neutral feeling grounds ignorance and its associated secondary afflictions (*upaklesha*) as well as the inversions of view (*drsti-viparyasa*, 見倒).

[^eight_similes]: These eight similes for the dangers of sensual pleasures derive from early Buddhist sources, particularly the Alagaddupama Sutra (MN 22) and Potaliya Sutra (MN 54). A chain of bones offers no nourishment; a lump of flesh is fought over by many; a grass torch burns whoever clings to it; a pit of coals scorches whoever falls in; a venomous serpent may strike at any moment; a dream vanishes on waking; borrowed ornaments must be returned; and fruit at a treetop costs the climber a fatal fall.

[^hearing_reflection]: The text explicitly notes that the attention to characteristics remains "interspersed with hearing and reflection" (聞思間雜) — it has not yet transcended learning (*shruta*) and analytical reflection (*cinta*) to reach meditative cultivation (*bhavana*). This transition occurs with the next attention, the attention of conviction, which "transcends hearing and reflection and employs only cultivation." The three wisdoms — hearing, reflection, and cultivation (*shruta-cinta-bhavana-prajna*) — form a fundamental progression in Buddhist epistemology and practice.

### 33.6 From Conviction to the Fruit: The Remaining Six Attentions

Having thus investigated according to reason and come to know that desires are coarse and the first dhyana peaceful, the yogin then transcends hearing and reflection and employs only cultivation. Generating conviction regarding the meditation object, one cultivates shamatha and vipashyana. Through this cultivation, one repeatedly generates conviction about the coarse and peaceful aspects as previously investigated. This is called the **attention of conviction**.

When this conviction has been well cultivated, well practiced, and extensively developed, it serves as the cause and condition for the first arising of the path that severs afflictions. The attention accompanying this newly arisen path is called the **attention of separation**. It is so named because it first cuts the desire-realm afflictions due for severance, and in doing so removes the coarseness and heaviness (*dausthulya*) that belonged to them.[^separation]

From this point on, the yogin delights in severance and delights in separation, seeing superior virtues in what has been severed, and touching a measure of the joy and bliss of separation. At times, with deep gladness, the mind rests in joyful contemplation of what has been freed; at other times, with deep weariness, it turns in revulsion toward what remains — alternating between these two to dispel torpor, drowsiness, agitation, and the like. This is called the **attention of joyful engagement**.[^joyful_engagement]

Through delighting in severance and cultivation, correctly applying practice and sustained by wholesome qualities, the afflictive entanglements bound to the desire realm no longer manifest, whether one is in motion or at rest. One then thinks: "Do I still have craving and afflictions regarding desires without being aware of it? Or have they been eliminated?"

To investigate this carefully, one deliberately attends to one type of attractive, pure object. Because the latent tendencies (*anushaya*) have not yet been permanently severed, when one contemplates such a pure and delightful object, a mind of approach arises — following toward it, inclining toward it, drawing near. One cannot remain in equanimity toward it, cannot feel revulsion, cannot condemn, suppress, or push it away.[^investigation_test]

One then thinks: "My mind is not yet freed from desires. I have not yet truly attained liberation. My mind is still constrained by formations, like water held in a vessel — not yet governed by the nature of things. I should now strive anew, dwelling in delight of severance and cultivation, to permanently sever the remaining latent tendencies." This is called the **attention of investigation**.

Redoubling one's delight in severance and cultivation, one practices shamatha and vipashyana with earnest effort, cultivating the antidote and periodically re-examining what has already been severed. Through this, the mind is freed from all desire-realm afflictions — freed by suppression, not by permanently destroying their seeds.[^temporary_suppression] Nevertheless, the preliminary path for the ground of the first dhyana is now complete: the attention that serves as antidote to all desire-realm afflictions has arisen. This is called the **attention of completed application**.

Immediately thereafter, through these causes and conditions, one enters the fundamental first dhyana. The attention that accompanies this fundamental first dhyana is called the **attention to the fruit of completed application**.

The joy and bliss born of separation progress through three stages.[^progressive_joy] During the attentions of separation and joyful engagement, this joy arises only occasionally, faint and tentative, bringing a quiet comfort to the body. During the attention of completed application, it expands and deepens. Then, during the attention to the fruit of completed application, the joy and bliss born of separation pervade every part of the body, filling it completely without remainder.

At this point, separated from desires and separated from all evil and unwholesome dharmas, with examination (*vitarka*) and analysis (*vicara*), with the joy and bliss born of separation, one abides fully equipped in the first dhyana with its five branches complete. This is called "dwelling in the fruit of cultivation as the antidote to the desire realm" and "having correspondingly attained separation from desire-realm desire."

[^separation]: The attention of separation (遠離作意) marks the first actual severance of afflictions. It severs the upper-grade (*adhimatra*) afflictions of the desire realm and removes their associated coarseness and heaviness (*dausthulya*, 麁重). This is the beginning of the experiential shift — the yogin starts to taste, however faintly, the joy and bliss of being freed from what was renounced.

[^joyful_engagement]: The attention of joyful engagement (攝樂作意) alternates between two modes: joyful attention (欣樂作意), which delights in the freedom already won, and revulsion attention (厭離作意), which reinforces weariness toward what remains of affliction. This alternation specifically counteracts torpor and drowsiness (*styana-middha*, 惛沈睡眠) on one hand and agitation (*auddhatya*, 掉舉) on the other — the two primary obstacles at this stage.

[^investigation_test]: This is the critical self-examination of the worldly path. The yogin deliberately exposes the mind to an attractive object to test whether latent tendencies (*anushaya*, 隨眠) remain active. The three progressive stages of the mind's approach — following toward (隨習近心), inclining toward (趣習近心), and drawing near (臨習近心) — describe increasingly intimate engagement with the object, revealing that desire has been suppressed but not eradicated. The water simile (如水被持) illustrates temporary containment: the mind's afflictions are held in check by effort, like water held by a vessel, rather than genuinely transformed through insight into the nature of things (*dharmata*). This honest self-assessment prevents overestimation (*adhimana*) — wrongly believing one has attained what one has not.

[^temporary_suppression]: A crucial doctrinal point: the worldly path achieves only temporary suppression (暫時伏斷方便) of afflictions, not permanent destruction of their seeds (非是畢竟永害種子). The afflictions are held in abeyance through the power of samadhi, but their latent seeds remain intact. Only the supramundane path — through insight into the four noble truths — can permanently destroy (*samuccheda-prahana*) the seeds of affliction. This distinction between suppression and eradication is fundamental to the Yogacara soteriology of the worldly versus supramundane paths.

[^progressive_joy]: The joy and bliss born of separation (*viveka-ja priti-sukha*, 離生喜樂) develops through three stages corresponding to the deepening of practice: faint and intermittent during the attentions of separation and joyful engagement, expanding during the attention of completed application, and fully pervading the body during the attention to the fruit of completed application. This progression mirrors the classical description of the first dhyana, where joy and bliss eventually "pervade every part of the body without remainder" — a formulation found in both the Nikayas/Agamas and the Visuddhimagga.

### 33.7 Functional Summary and Classification of the Seven Attentions

The seven attentions serve distinct functions. The **attention to characteristics** correctly discerns what is to be abandoned and what to be attained, generating aspiration toward both. The **attention of conviction** then initiates the actual practice of abandonment and attainment. From there, three attentions carry out progressive severance: the **attention of separation** relinquishes upper-grade afflictions, the **attention of joyful engagement** relinquishes middle-grade, and the **attention of completed application** relinquishes the lower-grade. Between these, the **attention of investigation** holds a diagnostic function: it prevents overestimation (*adhimana*) by testing whether what seems severed has truly been abandoned. Finally, the **attention to the fruit of completed application** receives the fruit of all this cultivation.[^affliction_grades]

The seven attentions can also be grouped into four functional categories.[^four_categories] The attentions to characteristics and of conviction together constitute the *conforming attention* — the preparatory phase of turning away through revulsion and dismantling the lower ground's appeal. The attentions of separation and of completed application constitute the *counteracting attention*, which actively severs afflictions. The attention of joyful engagement occupies both the *counteracting* and the *purity-conforming* roles — it severs while also consolidating what has been purified. The attention of investigation stands alone as the *investigation-conforming attention*, verifying whether liberation is genuine. These four categories fall within the broader framework of six types of attention recognized in the Yogacarabhumi, of which these four are the relevant ones here.

[^affliction_grades]: The three grades of affliction — upper (*adhimatra*, 上品), middle (*madhya*, 中品), and lower (*mrdu*, 下品) — represent decreasing intensity of the desire-realm afflictions. The third attention (separation) severs the upper grade, the fourth (joyful engagement) the middle grade, and the sixth (completed application) the lower grade. The fifth attention (investigation) does not itself sever anything; it tests whether latent tendencies remain — a safeguard against wrongly concluding that lower-grade afflictions have been eliminated when they have merely been suppressed.

[^four_categories]: The four functional categories of the seven attentions: (1) *conforming attention* (隨順作意) — preparatory, generating the right view of coarse and peaceful through revulsion and dismantling; (2) *counteracting attention* (對治作意) — actively severing afflictions; (3) *purity-conforming attention* (順清淨作意) — sustaining what has been purified; (4) *investigation-conforming attention* (順觀察作意) — verifying progress and preventing overestimation.

### 33.8 Extension to Higher Attainments and the Second Dhyana

Just as the first dhyana has seven types of attention, so too do the second, third, and fourth dhyanas, and the spheres of infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Each of these attainments should be understood as having its own seven types of attention.

For entering the second dhyana, the attention to characteristics discerns the coarse aspects of the first dhyana — which has examination and analysis — and the peaceful aspects of the second dhyana — which is free of examination and analysis.[^second_dhyana_coarse]

One who has entered and attained the first dhyana correctly knows that examination (*vitarka*) and analysis (*vicara*) are coarse. Within the ground of concentration, examination is the initial, sudden engagement with an object — a coarse mental verbalization. Analysis is the subsequent, gradual traversal of the object — a subtle mental verbalization. One further recognizes that examination and analysis are mental dharmas: they arise together with the mind, accompanying it, co-present with it, directed at the same object. Internally generated, they are nonetheless classified among external bases. They belong to all three times; they arise from conditions; they increase and decrease, never abide for long, and arise and pass in a flash.

They agitate the mind, scatter the mind, and move it without stillness. When one seeks the higher ground, they are painful companions. They belong to the dark category, attendant upon desire. However slight the joy and bliss of separation, examination and analysis are always present — and wherever they are present, the mind turns upon its object with agitation, unable to find stillness.

Through these and other such aspects, one discerns the coarse qualities of examination and analysis. One further knows that the second dhyana is free of both examination and analysis — all the coarse aspects just described are entirely absent. Therefore the second dhyana is said to possess the quality of peacefulness, being free from those coarse aspects. For entering the second dhyana, the remaining attentions should be understood as before.

In this way, through to entering the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, at each successive ground the seven types of attention should be understood to apply as appropriate.

[^second_dhyana_coarse]: The transition from the first to the second dhyana requires recognizing examination (*vitarka*, 尋) and analysis (*vicara*, 伺) as coarse. The definitions given here are important: examination is "initial, sudden, coarse mental verbalization" (最初率爾而起、怱務行境、麁意言性) — the mind's first gross engagement with an object. Analysis is "subsequent, gradual, subtle mental verbalization" (隨彼而起隨彼而行、徐歷行境、細意言性) — the mind's sustained, refined exploration. Though these are positive factors in the first dhyana (serving as antidotes to desire-realm afflictions), they become obstacles to the deeper stillness of the second dhyana. The Visuddhimagga (IV.88-94) provides a parallel analysis of how *vitakka* (Pali; Skt. *vitarka*) and *vicara* (Pali; Skt. *vicara*) must be abandoned for the second dhyana.

### 33.9 Universal Coarse Aspects Across All Lower Grounds

The coarse aspects described above are found universally in all lower grounds, from the desire realm up through the sphere of nothingness. In brief, every lower ground exhibits two kinds of coarseness compared to the ground above it: **(1)** a preponderance of painful abiding — the lower ground is not as still as the upper — and **(2)** a shorter span of life — the lower ground's lifespan is progressively shorter than the upper ground's.

These two coarse aspects should be correctly investigated through the same six perspectives as before, adapted to each ground as appropriate. Whenever one wishes to abandon desire for a particular ground, one investigates the peaceful aspects of the next-higher ground accordingly, progressing step by step until one attains the attention to the fruit of completed application for that ground.

### 33.10 Detailed Exposition of the First Dhyana Formula

[T30.0467b22]

The standard formula for the first dhyana is now explained term by term.

**"Desires"** — Desires are of two kinds: affliction-desire (*klesha-kama*) and object-desire (*vastu-kama*).

**"Separated"** — Separation is of two kinds: separation by disassociation — being freed from the mental afflictions themselves — and separation from objects — no longer being bound to the external things that occasion desire.

**"Evil and unwholesome dharmas"** — These are the various evil and unwholesome dharmas born from affliction-desire as their cause: bodily misconduct, verbal misconduct, and the like — taking up weapons and blades, fighting, quarreling, contending, and competing; deceit, fraud, trickery, and the giving rise to false speech. Because one has severed these, it is called "separated from evil and unwholesome dharmas."

**"With examination and analysis"** — Because the faults of examination (*vitarka*) and analysis (*vicara*) have not yet been perceived, and because wholesome examination and analysis still exist in this ground as antidotes to the desire realm, it is called "with examination and analysis."

**"Separation"** — This means one has attained the attention of completed application.

**"Born"** — With that attainment as cause and condition, joy and bliss are immediately produced therefrom — hence "born of separation."

**"Joy and bliss"** — One has attained what one sought. In joy, no faults are yet perceived. All coarseness and heaviness have been removed. And one has attained vast pliancy (*prashrabdhi*) — body and mind are supple, harmonious, and capable. Therefore it is called "joy and bliss."

**"First"** — This is the very first upward advance from the desire realm, the initial attainment in sequential order. Therefore it is called "first."

**"Dhyana"** — Upon a single object, binding mindfulness in stillness, correctly and precisely deliberating. Therefore it is called dhyana.[^dhyana_etymology]

**"Equipped"** — One has attained the attention to the fruit of completed application.

**"Dwelling"** — Subsequently, because one's practice has been well accomplished, one can enter at will, without difficulty, without obstruction. Day and night the mind can correctly accord with the dhyana — approaching, entering, and abiding as one wishes, for up to seven days and seven nights. Therefore it is called "dwelling."

[^dhyana_etymology]: The etymological explanation "upon a single object, binding mindfulness in stillness, correctly and precisely deliberating" (於一所緣繫念寂靜正審思慮) reflects the Chinese 靜慮, which Xuanzang chose as the translation for Sanskrit *dhyana*. The compound 靜慮 literally means "still deliberation" — combining 靜 (stillness, calm) with 慮 (thought, deliberation) — capturing the dual aspect of calm and discernment that characterizes the dhyanas. This differs from the older Chinese translations 禪 or 禪那, which are simply phonetic transcriptions.

### 33.11 Detailed Exposition of the Second Dhyana Formula

The mind relinquishes the samadhi characterized by examination and analysis, and binds mindfulness to abide in the samadhi free from examination and analysis. It properly distances itself from the realm of hurried activity and abides in the realm of what is unhurried. The mind turns single-flavored — still, supremely still. Therefore it is said: "examination and analysis are stilled" and "inner clarity (*adhyatma-samprasada*) is attained."[^inner_clarity]

Through serial practice within this samadhi free from examination and analysis, one transcends the interrupted state where examination and analysis still intrude, and correctly attains the uninterrupted state. Therefore it is said: "the mind proceeds in one direction." "Without examination, without analysis" — because all examination and analysis are entirely severed.

**"Concentration"** — One has attained the attention of completed application.

**"Born"** — With that attainment as cause and condition, joy and bliss are immediately produced therefrom — hence "born of concentration," not "born of separation" as in the first dhyana.

**"Joy and bliss"** — One has attained what one sought. In joy, no faults are yet perceived; there is gladness and joy. All the coarseness and heaviness belonging to the affliction categories of the first dhyana ground — including the agitation of examination and analysis — are entirely removed. The vast pliancy that counteracts these is attained; body and mind are supple, and the bliss of capability attends. Therefore it is called "having joy and bliss."

In sequential order this is the second. The rest — "equipped" and "dwelling" — should be understood as before.

[^inner_clarity]: Inner clarity (*adhyatma-samprasada*, 內等淨) is a distinctive factor of the second dhyana. The term describes the serene, lucid confidence that arises internally once the agitation of examination and analysis has been stilled. The Chinese 等淨 means "evenly pure" or "serenely clear." The Visuddhimagga (IV.139) similarly identifies *ajjhatta-sampasadana* (Pali; Skt. *adhyatma-samprasada*) as a key second dhyana factor: the mind becomes composed and clear because it is no longer disturbed by the coarse mental activity of *vitakka* and *vicara*. The "hurried activity" (怱務所行境界) and "unhurried" (不怱務) contrast vividly captures how examination and analysis keep the mind busy, while their absence allows it to settle into a single, unified quality.

### 33.12 Detailed Exposition of the Third Dhyana Formula

One discerns joy itself as a source of disturbance. Therefore it is said: "separated from the desire for joy." At that time one distances oneself from two kinds of disturbance that disorder the mind, and is able to collect one's mind in the third dhyana, which is free from joy.[^two_disturbances]

The second dhyana had already separated from examination and analysis; now, in addition, one separates from joy. Therefore it is said: "abides in equanimity."

These two dharmas — examination-and-analysis and joy — can disturb the mind and obstruct uninterrupted equanimity (*upeksha*). In the first dhyana, examination and analysis prevent uninterrupted equanimity from fully operating; in the second dhyana, joy does the same. Therefore this equanimity is said to be absent in the first two dhyanas. For this reason, only in the third dhyana does genuine equanimity emerge.

Because there is equanimity: as one abides in mindfulness, the perceptions and attentions accompanied by joy cease to manifest. But if one does not practice the third dhyana well, or sometimes loses mindfulness, those joy-accompanied perceptions and attentions may arise again. One should immediately, with swift wisdom, penetrate and correctly know them. Whatever arises, one should not tolerate it but should skillfully abandon, dismiss, expel, and reject it, abiding the mind in higher equanimity. Therefore it is said to have "mindfulness and alertness."

Abiding in such equanimity with mindfulness and alertness, cultivating closely and repeatedly, through extensive cultivation the elation and joy-accompanied feeling are eliminated. Separated from joy — still, supremely still — a mental feeling contrary to joy arises. At that time, the physical body and the mental body both experience the reception of bliss and the bliss of pliancy. Therefore it is said: "experiencing bodily bliss."

Below the third dhyana, neither such bliss nor uninterrupted equanimity exist. Above the third dhyana, uninterrupted equanimity is attainable, but not such bliss. The lower grounds have neither bliss nor equanimity; the upper grounds have equanimity but not bliss. For this reason, the saints — meaning the Blessed One and his disciples — declare of the third dhyana: "The person who abides here is fully equipped with equanimity, mindfulness, and alertness, and experiences bodily bliss — dwelling fully equipped in the third dhyana."

[^two_disturbances]: The "two kinds of disturbance" are (1) examination and analysis (*vitarka-vicara*) and (2) joy (*priti*). Importantly, they are called disturbances retrospectively — from the perspective of the third dhyana's equanimity, both are seen as forms of agitation that prevented genuine equanimity from manifesting. Only in the third dhyana, free from all three, can uninterrupted equanimity first emerge. This is consistent with the Visuddhimagga (IV.183-191).

### 33.13 Detailed Exposition of the Fourth Dhyana Formula

[T30.0468a26]

Because the type and force of the antidote are similar to those of the third dhyana, the text does not separately expound the joyful-severance antidote but only states the result accomplished by the antidote.

What is the antidote here? Equanimity, mindfulness, and alertness. Through repeated cultivation of this antidote, one is able to abandon the excellent bliss of the third dhyana — that which would otherwise prevent one from transcending it. Therefore it is said: "because bliss is abandoned."

The practitioner of dhyana at that time transcends all suffering and bliss. For this reason, collecting together what was previously abandoned and what is now abandoned: "bliss is abandoned, suffering is abandoned; previously, joy and grief disappeared." That is: on entering the fourth dhyana, pleasant feeling is abandoned; on entering the second dhyana, painful feeling was abandoned; on entering the third dhyana, joy disappeared; on entering the first dhyana, grief disappeared.

Here, because the two feelings of suffering and bliss are abandoned, there remains a feeling that is neither suffering nor bliss. Therefore it is said: "at that time, neither suffering nor bliss."

Starting from the first dhyana, all the calamities and disturbances of the lower grounds have been severed: examination, analysis, joy, bliss, and in-and-out breathing. Because all of these are severed, equanimity and mindfulness here are pure and radiant (*parishuddha*). For this reason, on correctly entering the fourth dhyana, the mind abides unmoving; all agitation and disturbance are entirely left behind. Therefore it is said: "purity of equanimity and mindfulness."

"Fourth" and the remaining terms — "equipped," "dwelling," and the like — should be understood as previously explained for the first dhyana.

### 33.14 The Four Formless Attainments

**The sphere of infinite space** (*akashanantya-ayatana*). Through generating conviction regarding space, one transcends all perceptions of visible form — blue, yellow, red, white, and the like. Because these color-perceptions no longer appear and because one has turned away from them in weariness, one can transcend them. Therefore it is said: "transcending form-perceptions."

Because one has transcended those color-perceptions, all resistance-perceptions — those based on the aggregation and accumulation of visible forms — are removed. Therefore it is said: "resistance-perceptions cease." Because those perceptions are left behind, all the differentiated perceptions operating within assemblies of objects — perceptions of food and drink, of vessels and clothing, of vehicles, ornaments, cities, dwellings, armies, parks, mountains, and forests — are no longer attended to. Therefore it is said: "diverse perceptions are no longer attended to."

Having removed perceptions of form, of resistance, and of diversity, one generates conviction regarding space with its aspect of boundlessness. Therefore it is said: "one enters infinite space." Having transcended the preliminary attention of completed application and entered the root attention to the fruit of completed application, it is said: "one dwells fully equipped in the sphere of infinite space."

Here one should know that the objects of cognition differ between stages. In the preliminary stages, the meditation object is space alone. However, since the practitioner has not yet fully transcended the lower ground, one also cognizes the aggregates of the lower ground. Once the root attainment is entered, one cognizes both space and one's own aggregates belonging to that ground.

**The sphere of infinite consciousness** (*vijnanananty-ayatana*). The consciousness by which one generated conviction regarding infinite space — now that very consciousness becomes the object, with conviction in its infinite aspect. If one wishes to enter the sphere of infinite consciousness, one first relinquishes the perception of infinite space and then generates conviction regarding that consciousness as infinite.

[T30.0468c05]

At that time one transcends both the preliminary and root stages of the sphere of infinite space. Therefore it is said: "transcending all of infinite space, one enters infinite consciousness." Having transcended the preliminary stages through the attention of completed application and entered the root attention to the fruit of completed application, it is said: "one dwells fully equipped in the sphere of infinite consciousness."

**The sphere of nothingness** (*akimcanya-ayatana*). Seeking to advance beyond infinite consciousness, one searches for some further object beyond that consciousness — whether of form or formless nature — and finds nothing at all. Finding no object, one transcends both the preliminary and root stages of infinite consciousness and generates conviction that there is nothing whatsoever remaining. This is called a "provisional conviction" (*parikalpita-adhimoksha*) regarding nothingness.[^provisional_conviction] Through extensive cultivation of this provisional conviction, one transcends all the preliminary stages through the attention of completed application and enters the root attainment. Therefore it is said: "transcending all of infinite consciousness — nothing whatsoever — one dwells fully equipped in the sphere of nothingness."

**The sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception** (*naiva-samjña-nasamjña-ayatana*). Seeking to advance beyond nothingness, one perceives the perception of nothingness as coarse and is able to abandon it. By this means, having previously transcended all perceptions of "having something" when entering the sphere of nothingness, one now further transcends the perception of "having nothing." Therefore it is said: "neither having perception" — meaning neither the perception of something nor the perception of nothing.

Yet this is not like the attainment of non-perception or the cessation attainment, where all perceptions cease entirely. Here, only an extremely subtle perception remains, cognizing a signless object. Therefore it is said: "neither perception nor non-perception."

Generating conviction at this point, one transcends all stages of the sphere of nothingness and the preliminary stages of neither-perception-nor-non-perception through the attention of completed application, and enters the root attainment. Therefore it is said: "transcending all of nothingness, one dwells fully equipped in the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

**The simile of room and open space.** When entering the dhyanas, the body's state is like being in a room. When entering the formless attainments, the body's state is like being in open space. In both cases, one should understand that the practice proceeds through the shamatha aspect, abiding in upper equanimity and diligently cultivating.

[^provisional_conviction]: The text's characterization of the nothingness conviction as "provisional" or "imagined" (假想勝解) is doctrinally significant. Unlike the other attainments, where conviction (*adhimoksha*) corresponds to something genuinely present (space, consciousness), the sphere of nothingness is based on conviction regarding an absence — the absence of any further object. The Yogacara tradition treats this as a conceptually constructed conviction — directed at an absence rather than at something directly perceived — which accounts for its designation as "provisional." The practitioner should understand that this conviction in "nothingness" is a meditative technique — a deliberately constructed perception serving as the object for this attainment — not a philosophical conclusion about the nature of reality. This is consistent with the Abhidharma classification that distinguishes between perceptions directed at actual objects and those directed at conceptual constructs.

### 33.15 Two Absorptions Without Mind

[T30.0469a04]

Based upon the dhyanas, one should know that it is possible to enter two absorptions without mind (*acitta-samapatti*): **(1)** the attainment of non-perception (*asamjni-samapatti*) and **(2)** the cessation attainment (*nirodha-samapatti*).[^two_absorptions]

The attainment of non-perception is entered only by ordinary beings, through the method of attention that rejects perception. The cessation attainment is entered only by noble ones, through the method of attention that temporarily halts perception and feeling. Thus these two absorptions are entered by means of two distinct types of attention.

**The attainment of non-perception.** The ordinary being thinks: "Perceptions are like an illness, like a tumor, like an arrow. Only non-perception is peaceful and subtle" (this is the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning). Embracing this attention that rejects perception, one diligently cultivates non-attending attention toward all arising perceptions. Through this cultivation, during the preparatory path — while the mind is still active — one enters the absorption, and immediately thereafter the mind ceases to function.

The prerequisite is that one must have been freed from desire for the third dhyana (*subhakrtsna*, universal purity) but not yet freed from desire for the fourth dhyana (*brhatphala*, vast fruit). When this perception-rejecting attention takes the lead and mind and mental factors cease, this is called the attainment of non-perception.

**The cessation attainment.** A noble one who has already attained the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, and who wishes to abide temporarily in peaceful abiding — from neither-perception-nor-non-perception, the mind seeks to advance upward. When the mind seeks upward, it searches for a higher object but ultimately finds nothing. Finding nothing, it ceases and no longer functions.

For a learner (*shaiksha*), this requires having been freed from desire for the sphere of nothingness. For an arhat, it requires the aspiration to abide temporarily in quiescence. When this upward-seeking attention takes the lead and mind and mental factors cease, this is called the cessation attainment.

[^two_absorptions]: The two absorptions without mind (二無心定) are radically different from the dhyanas and formless attainments: in these, mind and mental factors (*citta-caitta*) actually cease to function. The key doctrinal distinction is in who can enter each: (1) the attainment of non-perception (無想定) is available only to ordinary beings (*prthag-jana*) — those who, mistaking the cessation of perception for liberation, deliberately suppress all perceptions. The prerequisite (freed from third dhyana desire but not yet from fourth dhyana) places it within the fourth dhyana realm. (2) The cessation attainment (滅盡定, also called *samjña-vedayita-nirodha*) is available only to noble ones (*arya*) who have mastered all eight attainments — in practice, non-returners and arhats — who enter it not from mistaken views but as a deliberate temporary rest from mental activity. The cessation attainment requires mastery of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, and from that peak the mind simply ceases when it can find no higher object. The Yogacara tradition emphasizes that these are functionally identical (mind ceases) but soteriologically opposite: one is based on wrong view, the other on right realization.

### 33.16 Five Superknowledges and the Twelve Perceptions

[T30.0469a25]

How does one develop the superknowledges? A practitioner who has already attained a purified root dhyana takes that purified dhyana as a basis and then listens to, receives, retains, and thoroughly masters the correct dharma pertaining to the five superknowledges. These are: magical display (*rddhi-abhijna*), recollection of past lives (*purvanivasanusmrti*), the divine ear (*divya-shrotra*), knowledge of death and rebirth (*cyuty-upapatti-jnana*), and knowledge of others' minds (*para-citta-jnana*). One directs attention to these and, through concentration-based attention, comes to understand their meaning and dharma. Through such understanding, one trains the mind accordingly. By cultivating and extensively cultivating this, one eventually generates the five superknowledges as fruits of cultivation.[^five_superknowledges]

To develop the superknowledges, one cultivates **twelve perceptions**:

**(1) Lightness.** Through this perception one generates the conviction that the body is light — like *dutula* cotton (an especially fine, light variety), like tufts of silk, like a wind-wheel. Having generated this conviction of lightness, one employs conviction-attention to move the body from place to place — from a bed to a table, from a table back to a bed, from a bed to a grass seat, from a grass seat back to a bed.

**(2) Softness.** Through this perception one generates the conviction that the body is soft — like a cotton bag, like wool, like well-refined fabric. This perception of softness nourishes and sustains the preceding perception of lightness, causing it to grow and expand.

**(3) Space-element.** Having already established the convictions of lightness and softness, wherever one wishes to go, if material objects form obstacles along the way, one generates conviction-attention regarding those objects as empty space, and passes through them without hindrance.

**(4) Body-mind accord.** Through this perception, either the mind conforms to the body or the body conforms to the mind. Thereby the body becomes increasingly light, increasingly soft, increasingly capable, and increasingly luminous. It follows the mind, is subordinate to the mind, and depends upon the mind.

**(5) Conviction.** Through this perception, what is far is apprehended as near; what is near, as far. What is coarse is apprehended as subtle; what is subtle, as coarse. Earth is apprehended as water; water, as earth. In this way, each great element is transformed into any other, extensively, as in the transformations produced through conviction — whether transformations of form or of sound.

Through the completion of these five perceptions, one attains the various wonderful powers of magical display. Through the conviction of manifestation, one body becomes many; through concealment, many become one. The body passes through walls, fences, and city ramparts without obstruction. One emerges from the earth and submerges in it as in water. One walks upon water as upon land. Seated cross-legged, one soars through the air like a bird. With one's hand one touches the sun and moon in all their vast power and brilliance. With one's body one travels freely as far as the Brahma-world.

There are two kinds of mastery regarding the Brahma-world: **(a)** traveling there and back at will, and **(b)** transforming the great elements and derivative forms of the Brahma-world as one wishes, according to the power of conviction.

**(6) Sequential recollection of prior experience.** Through this perception, from childhood to the present, one recalls in sequence and without gap all one's actions and positions — walking, standing, sitting, lying — and all one's past experiences, coarsely and in detail, in order and without omission. Through extensive cultivation of this, one achieves as its fruit the recollection of innumerable past lives, with all their aspects and all that was spoken.

**(7) Perception of diverse assembled sounds.** Through this perception, one extends attention across villages and gatherings — assemblies of elders, townspeople, great crowds — through open halls, homes, and chambers, and takes the signs of all the diverse, clamorous sounds that arise there. One also takes the signs of the sounds of great rivers, rushing currents, and crashing waves. Then, with concentration-based attention, one listens attentively to the sounds of humans and divine beings, near and far, holy and unholy. Through extensive cultivation, one achieves the divine ear: the ability to hear all sounds in the human and heavenly realms, whether near or far.

**(8) Light and form-aspect.** One thoroughly apprehends the signs of the various kinds of luminosity as previously described, and also apprehends the signs of the various wholesome and unwholesome karmic activities of sentient beings. One directs attention to these signs. Through extensive cultivation, one achieves the knowledge of death and rebirth — the purified divine eye — whereby one sees sentient beings dying and being reborn in states fortunate and unfortunate.

**(9) Color-changes wrought by afflictions.** Through this perception, one discerns and distinguishes the changes in color, demeanor, and appearance among sentient beings whose minds are entangled by craving, anger, ignorance, resentment, concealment, vexation, deceit, flattery, miserliness, jealousy, arrogance, harmfulness, shamelessness, recklessness, and all other afflictions and secondary afflictions. Those dominated by craving show restless faculties, agitated bearing, and constant smiling. Those dominated by anger show perpetual frowning, harsh and halting speech, and frequent changes of color. Those dominated by ignorance are mostly incoherent, dark in understanding, with unclear speech and vulgar expression. In this way, through these patterns, one extends the analysis to all the remaining afflictions, including shamelessness and recklessness. Having thoroughly apprehended these signs and directed attention to them, through extensive cultivation one achieves the knowledge of others' minds — the ability to know as they truly are the minds, mental states, and consciousness of other beings and persons, according to what one investigates and examines.

**(10-12) Liberation, bases of mastery, and totality perceptions.** These three should be understood as described in the section on the ground of meditative equipoise (*samahita-bhumi*) above.[^liberation_mastery] Through their close cultivation, one can develop the supreme holy superknowledges — the power of transforming things, the power of creating things, and the power of conviction — as well as no-contention knowledge (*arana-jnana*), the power of aspiration (*pranidhi-jnana*), and the four analytical knowledges (*pratisam-vid*): analytical knowledge of dharmas, of meanings, of expressions, and of eloquence, among other virtues.

The superknowledges of noble ones differ from those of non-noble practitioners in this: whatever a noble one transforms, creates, or resolves through conviction genuinely materializes and is truly functional. The superknowledges of the non-noble are like illusions — visible but not usable.

One should know that through the close cultivation of these twelve perceptions, as appropriate to each, one can develop the five superknowledges and also those virtues exclusive to noble ones that are not shared with ordinary beings.

[^five_superknowledges]: The five superknowledges (*pañca-abhijña*, 五神通) are: (1) magical display (*rddhi-abhijna*) — supernatural physical powers; (2) recollection of past lives (*purvanivasanusmrti-abhijna*); (3) the divine ear (*divya-shrotra-abhijna*) — clairaudience; (4) knowledge of death and rebirth (*cyuty-upapatti-jnana-abhijna*); (5) knowledge of others' minds (*para-citta-jnana-abhijna*). The well-known sixth superknowledge — knowledge of the exhaustion of outflows (*asravakshaya-jnana*) — is deliberately absent: it belongs to the soteriological path rather than the concentration-based attainments, and the Sravakabhumi treats it in the context of arhat realization.

[^liberation_mastery]: The three perceptions of liberation (*vimoksha*), bases of mastery (*abhibhu-ayatana*), and totality (*krtsna-ayatana*) are treated extensively in the *samahita-bhumi* section of the Yogacarabhumi. Together they develop what the text calls the "supreme holy superknowledges" — the transformative, creative, and conviction-based powers that are exclusive to noble ones; the superknowledges of ordinary beings, however impressive, remain "like illusions — visible but not usable."

### 33.17 Rebirth Destinations According to Attainment

[T30.0470b04]

Those who cultivate each dhyana in its lower, middle, and upper grades are reborn, degree by degree, in the corresponding heavens. The first dhyana yields the Brahma-host, the Brahma-ministers, and the great Brahma deities; the second, the minor-radiance, boundless-radiance, and supreme-radiance deities; the third, the minor-purity, boundless-purity, and universal-purity deities; the fourth, the cloudless, merit-born, and vast-fruit deities.

Non-returners who intermittently infuse the contaminated fourth dhyana with the uncontaminated, cultivating it through five grades — lower, middle, upper, superior, and supreme — are reborn in the five pure abodes: the non-afflicted, the non-heated, the clearly appearing, the clearly seeing, and the highest-form deities.

Those who cultivate the spheres of infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception in their three grades are reborn accordingly among the deities of each respective formless sphere. Since those deities have no material form, there are no spatial distinctions among them, though their modes of abiding differ.

One who has cultivated the attainment of non-perception is reborn among the non-perceiving deities.

### 33.18 Characteristics of the Person Free from Desire

[T30.0470b22]

How should one recognize one who is free from desire? The person free from desire shows the following characteristics:

The body is settled: faculties unmoved, bearing undisturbed, posture sustained for a long time without alarm or anxiety, without frequent shifting. The speech is gentle, calm, and unhurried; there is no pleasure in clamor, no pleasure in crowds.

When the eyes see a form, one simply cognizes it — no craving arises on its account. The same holds for sounds heard by the ears, odors known by the nose, flavors tasted by the tongue, and sensations felt by the body: each is simply known, without craving following in its wake.

There is no fear. The discernment is deep and subtle; pliancy is vast. Body and mind are composed and self-contained — free from greed, free from eruptions of anger. The mind is patient, not disturbed by craving-related thought or by any other unwholesome movement.

Such are the characteristics by which one should know the person free from desire.
