## Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Third Yoga Place, Part Three

本地分中聲聞地第十三第三瑜伽處之三

---

### 32.1 The Five Signs: Framework for the Beginner

[T30.0459b25]

How does a beginner, when first undertaking the practice, properly establish the cultivation of attention? When practicing in accord with what has been established, what does one first touch and realize?

The joy of abandonment and mental one-pointedness — these are what the beginner first attains.

A teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga instructs the beginner who relies on yogic practice as follows:[^teacher_frame]

"Welcome, Worthy One. Drawing on three sources — what you have seen, what you have heard, and what you can infer through reflection — you should apprehend five types of signs with discerning attention:

**(1)** The sign of disenchantment. **(2)** The sign of delight. **(3)** The sign of danger. **(4)** The sign of luminosity. **(5)** The sign of discerning the object."[^five_signs]

[^teacher_frame]: From this point forward, the entire fascicle is framed as a skilled yoga teacher (善通達修瑜伽師) instructing a beginner (初修業者), addressed as "Worthy One" (賢首, *bhadanta*). The instructional voice is maintained throughout — this is the most directly practice-facing section of the Sravakabhumi.

[^five_signs]: The five signs (五種相) form the complete instructional framework for the beginner's entry into meditation. They are apprehended through three modes: direct perception (見), report (聞), and inference (心比度). The first two signs — disenchantment and delight — correspond to the disciplining and moistening attentions of section 31.11. The remaining three — danger, luminosity, and discernment — support the development of shamatha and vipashyana.

### 32.2 The Sign of Disenchantment — Instruction for the Greedy Temperament

[T30.0459c01]

**Question:** If the beginner is of the greed-conduct type (*raga-carita*) and must be tamed through the contemplation of impurity, how does one teach them to apprehend the five signs?

**Answer:** One should instruct as follows:

"Welcome, Worthy One. As you dwell among various settlements and villages, attend to the signs of decline around you. You may hear of men and women who once lived in happiness and later met with misfortune — through serious illness, through death, through the suffering of those they loved. You may hear of villages where wealth was destroyed: seized by enemies, burned by fire, swept away by flood, squandered through wrongdoing, lost through mismanaged enterprises or poor judgment, drained by the schemes of ill-disposed partners, or consumed in a household fire.

"Perhaps you have witnessed these things directly, in your own settlement, or in your own life — sharp pleasures you once knew that later faded, as explained before.

"Whatever source brings these things to your attention — hearing, seeing, or your own experience — let them give rise to deep disenchantment. Samsara is grievous suffering. This body is won at great cost, and within it one encounters such various forms of decline in oneself and others: decline of health, decline of life, decline of kin, decline of wealth. The nature of the ill is to fall ill; the nature of the mortal is to die. Some also suffer the decline of pure discipline and the decline of right view.[^decline_types] Through these, beings dwell in suffering in the present and journey to evil destinies hereafter.

"Those who prosper — though they may enjoy happiness now and journey to good destinations — are subject to impermanence, and this impermanence is directly perceivable. For whoever experiences prosperity, decline will surely come. For whoever experiences decline, renewed prosperity is hard to find. All prosperity is hard to gain, easy to lose, subject to destruction. Thus you should be deeply disenchanted.

[T30.0460a01]

"Attend carefully and receive this well: such conditions are unreliable. While I cycle through samsara, having not yet reached nirvana, not yet liberated in mind, nothing can be guaranteed. May these two — decline and prosperity — not appear before me. May they not cause me to fall into circumstances that give rise to fierce, harsh, bitter suffering. Through the force of this reflection, I shall sincerely delight in abandonment and practice without heedlessness. By abiding often in this way, I shall put an end to all that is without benefit."

"Hold this deeply in mind."

[^decline_types]: The text identifies six types of decline (衰損): health, life, kin, wealth, discipline, and right view. The last two are the gravest, as they lead to evil destinies. The formula 病病法性死死法性 — "the nature of the ill is to fall ill; the nature of the mortal is to die" — is a compressed expression of the inevitability of suffering for one still in samsara.

### 32.3 The Sign of Delight

"Having apprehended the sign of disenchantment, you should then diligently apprehend the sign of delight.

"Examine your own ethical discipline (*shila*): is it pure or impure? 'Have I through heedlessness, lack of respect, excessive afflictions, or ignorance transgressed any of the training rules? If I have transgressed, I shall — in accord with the dharma and with my superior aspiration (*adhyashaya*) — develop firm determination regarding the training rules, never again to violate them. I shall do what should be done and refrain from what should not be done. In short: regarding the training rules, I shall perfect both my resolve and my practice.'

"When you examine yourself thus and know that the aggregate of your discipline is pure — then, without even intending 'I shall generate non-regret,' by the very nature of things, pure discipline gives rise to non-regret (*avipratisara*). And when non-regret arises, without even intending 'I shall generate gladness,' by the very nature of things, one who is free of remorse naturally generates gladness.[^non_regret_sequence]

"In this way, from the first basis of gladness, you should generate gladness preceded by non-regret. Then, from the joy that arises when obstacles are cleared, generate further joy: 'My discipline is pure. I have the power and capacity to abide in the training rules established by the Blessed One. In this very life, I can attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized.' From this, joyful delight arises.

"If you have obtained even a small portion of realization, then through its force you should generate conviction and joyful delight regarding the full attainments of others — the Tathagatas and the noble disciples — as well as regarding your own future attainments."

Two qualities of pleasant mind emerge here. The first — gladness — arises from a clean conscience. The second — joy — arises from confidence in what can be attained. Together they constitute delight. This is apprehending the sign of delight.

[^non_regret_sequence]: This describes the well-known progressive sequence: pure discipline → non-regret (*avipratisara*) → gladness (*pramodya*) → joy (*priti*). See AN 10.1 and 11.1 for the Pali parallels. Xuanzang distinguishes 歡悅 (gladness, *pramodya*, from clean conscience) from 喜悅 (joy, *priti*, from confidence in capacity) — both subsumed under 悅意 (delight).

### 32.4 The Sign of Danger and Entry into Shamatha

[T30.0460b01]

Having apprehended these signs, the teacher further instructs:

"Worthy One, through the sign of disenchantment you have disciplined your mind; through the sign of delight you have moistened it. You should dwell often in the state of having let go of worldly desire and grief. Whatever the object of your practice — whether you are working in shamatha (calm abiding) or vipashyana (insight) — bring the mind to rest, settle it inward, and allow it to become even.[^nine_stages_ref] From this will arise physical and mental pliancy and mental one-pointedness.

"When you have thus turned from the dark category toward the bright through disciplining and moistening the mind, you should then repeatedly apprehend the sign of danger — that is, the dangers in all signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions.[^three_disturbances]

"What are *signs*? The ten, beginning with form. What are *discursive thoughts*? The eight, beginning with desire. What are *secondary afflictions*? The five, beginning with sensual desire.

"You should apprehend the dangers in all of these. Signs cause the mind to become busy with activity. Discursive thoughts cause it to long and grow agitated. Secondary afflictions cause it to remain perpetually unsettled. The busy activity is the work of signs; the longing and agitation, the work of discursive thoughts; the perpetual unsettledness, the work of secondary afflictions. Through all three, the mind dwells in distress. Therefore these signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions are suffering, ignoble, drawing one toward what is without benefit — scattering the mind, agitating it, defiling it. You should apprehend the sign of danger in this way.

"Furthermore, relying on mental one-pointedness, mental stability, and mental non-disturbance, you should properly apprehend the sign through six aspects of contemplation — three deepening pairs:[^six_aspects]

First, the perception of signlessness — and within signlessness, the perception of non-activity. Second, the perception of non-discrimination — and within non-discrimination, the perception of being free from longing and agitation. Third, the perception of quiescence — and within quiescence, the perception of peaceful bliss free from all torment."

[^nine_stages_ref]: The instruction to "bring the mind to rest, settle it inward, and allow it to become even" (令心住內住等住) alludes to the first three of the nine stages of mental abiding (九住心) defined in section 30.10: (1) inward abiding (*citta-sthapana*), (2) settled abiding (*samsthapana*), and (3) re-established abiding (*avasthapana*). The teacher here compresses the initial shamatha instructions for the beginner.

[^three_disturbances]: The ten signs (十相), eight discursive thoughts (八尋思), and five secondary afflictions (五隨煩惱) are three categories of mental disturbance that obstruct shamatha. "Signs" refers to sensory appearances beginning with visible form (色); "discursive thoughts" (尋思, *vitarka*) to the eight types beginning with desire (欲); "secondary afflictions" (隨煩惱, *upaklesha*) to the five beginning with sensual desire (*kamacchanda*) — i.e., the five hindrances.

[^six_aspects]: These six perceptions (六想) progressively deepen the practitioner's experience of the undisturbed state. They form three pairs: signlessness and its non-activity, non-discrimination and its freedom from longing, quiescence and its peaceful bliss. Together they describe the positive qualities of the shamatha state — not merely the absence of disturbance, but the active presence of peace.

### 32.5 The Sign of Luminosity and Entering Practice

[T30.0460c01]

"Having apprehended the sign of danger, you should then repeatedly apprehend the sign of luminosity — the light of a lamp, the light of a great fire, the light of the sun, the light of the moon.

"Having apprehended the sign of luminosity, go to the charnel ground and apprehend the sign of the livid, and so on through to the skeleton. If you cannot go to the charnel ground, apprehend these signs from paintings or from sculptures of wood and stone.

"From these signs, return to your dwelling — whether a forest retreat, the base of a tree, or an empty room. On a large couch, a small rope-seat, or a seat of grass and leaves, first wash your feet, then sit cross-legged, body upright, attention settled, and establish mindfulness. First, on a single object, keep the mind from scattering and tether mindfulness before you. Then, within that, apply attention using the six perceptions: signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, and peaceful bliss free from torment.

"Within this practice, you should carefully and thoroughly discern the disturbed and undisturbed states. As they become clearly present before you — the more carefully you discern them, the more you can recognize the disturbance within signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions, and recognize the non-disturbance within mental one-pointedness cultivated through the six perceptions.

"The more thoroughly you discern these disturbed and undisturbed states, the more you can rest on a single object and abide in inner mental stillness. The mind's continuity and flow then move from moment to moment with one flavor — signless, non-discriminating, and at peace.[^one_flavor]

"Yet even when the mind has settled, it may lose mindfulness through habitual familiarity with signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions. Then images of the meditation object arise repeatedly, like reflections of a face in a mirror. Whenever they arise, apply non-attending attention: through the force of the previously apprehended sign of danger, dispel and dissolve those images so they no longer appear before you.

"Worthy One, know this: such objects are extremely subtle and difficult to penetrate. You should arouse fervent aspiration and apply diligent effort to penetrate them."

[^one_flavor]: The mirror simile is striking: even in a well-settled mind, habitual patterns can project images "like reflections of a face in a mirror" — vivid but insubstantial. The remedy is non-attending attention (不念作意), one of the five clearing types introduced in section 31.7.

### 32.6 The Oil-Bowl Simile

[T30.0461a01]

Regarding these meditation objects, the Blessed One spoke with implicit meaning:

"Bhikshus, you should know what 'a fine crowd' means: in a great gathering, the prime and beautiful forms. And the most excellent thing in this fine crowd is the singing, dancing, and musical entertainment.

"Suppose a wise man comes from outside and addresses someone: 'Here, good fellow! You must carry this bowl brimming with oil through this great crowd, avoiding the fine sights and the singing, dancing, and entertainment and the great beings there — without letting it overflow. An executioner follows you with a drawn sword. If a single drop of oil falls to the ground, he will sever your head and cut off your life.'

"Bhikshus, what do you think? Could this man — disregarding the oil bowl and the sword-bearing executioner and the uneven ground — attend instead to the fine crowd and the excellent entertainment?"

"No, Blessed One. Why not? Because seeing the executioner following with drawn sword, he would be gripped by great fear, thinking only: 'My bowl is brimming with oil. It will be extremely difficult to carry it through this crowd. If a single drop falls, this executioner will surely sever my head and cut off my life.' At that moment, he would not attend to, think about, or look at the fine crowd, the singing, dancing, entertainment, or the great beings — he would attend single-mindedly to the oil bowl, carefully guarding it."[^oil_bowl]

[^oil_bowl]: This simile (油鉢喻) appears in the Pali canon at SN 47.20 (the *Sedaka Sutta* group). The brimming bowl of oil represents the settled shamatha mind; the executioner represents the practitioner's own recognition of the danger in mental disturbance. The point is not that practice requires terror, but that genuine understanding of what is at stake naturally concentrates attention.

### 32.7 Decoding the Simile; Alternating Shamatha and Vipashyana

[T30.0461b01]

"In the same way, bhikshus, my disciples who cultivate the four foundations of mindfulness (*smrityupasthana*) with respect, earnestness, and focused recollection should be understood likewise."

The teacher decodes the simile point by point:

- The **fine crowd** represents conditions that fuel the entanglement of desire and other secondary afflictions.
- The **singing, dancing, and entertainment** represents conditions that fuel discursive thoughts, idle elaboration, and agitation.
- The **great beings** represent the ten signs beginning with visible form.
- The **wise man** represents the yoga teacher.
- The **bowl brimming with oil** represents the mind settled in shamatha — generating physical and mental pliancy and moistness. This is the meaning of shamatha.
- The **executioner following with drawn sword** represents the previously apprehended sign of danger regarding signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions.
- **Single-mindedly guarding against even a single drop falling** represents the shamatha path informed by careful, thorough discernment of the disturbed and undisturbed states.

Through this, by the power of effort applied without interruption, the mind's continuity and flow move from moment to moment with one flavor — signless, non-discriminating, and at peace — without giving rise to a single thought that reverts to signs, discursive thoughts, or secondary afflictions.

**Alternating shamatha and vipashyana.** The teacher further instructs the shamatha beginner:

"Worthy One, if you practice the shamatha path diligently — with skillful means, right mindfulness, alertness, and a joyful and happy mind — this is what is called the well-cultivated shamatha path.

"But habitual faults may arise. If deep joy and happiness do not come, and the practice feels forced and strained, you should quickly emerge from the non-discriminating meditation object and establish mindfulness on a formed, discriminating object — such as the impurity signs you previously apprehended. First apply only the sign-following mode of vipashyana: contemplate the livid, the festering, and so on through to the bones and the skeleton."[^alternation]

[^alternation]: This instruction is crucial for beginners: if formless shamatha becomes forced and joyless, one should not press on but return to formed-object practice (有分別所緣). The sign-following mode of vipashyana (隨相行毘鉢舍那) was defined in section 31.7 as the first of four vipashyana modes. Here it is applied to the ashubha (impurity) contemplation as the formed object.

### 32.8 Graduated Contemplation: From One to the Limitless

[T30.0461c01]

"When you first begin this contemplative practice,[^supervised_practice] generate imaginative attention (*adhimoksha-manaskara*)[^adhimoksha_practice] regarding a single livid corpse, and so forth through each stage to a single skeleton. When through sustained practice the contemplative path has grown clear and bright — when imaginative attention regarding the object flows clearly and continuously — then expand: to two, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, a hundred, a thousand, and onward until the livid fill every cardinal and intermediate direction. Generate a limitless, all-pervading, gapless field of imaginative attention, leaving not even a space the size of a staff-tip unfilled. As with the livid, so too through each stage to the skeleton.[^expansion]

"Relying on this imaginative attention, you should then enter real contemplation. When making this transition, reflect: 'The limitless livid corpses — and so forth through to the skeletons — that my imaginative attention has produced: the *actual* livid corpses and skeletons far exceed these and cannot be counted.

"'Why? From the beginningless past, cycling through birth and death in various realms and destinies, the corpses I have discarded upon dying — the livid corpses arising from them, through to the skeletons — are limitless and boundless, their origin beyond tracing. Even if one could gather all these discarded corpses and prevent them from decaying, the entire great earth could not contain them. The corpses and skeletons from a single kalpa alone, collected in one place, would form a heap equal to great Mount Vipula.[^vipula] As from the beginningless past, so too into the future — until one has brought suffering to an end.'

"In this way, relying on imaginative attention, enter real contemplation.

"Furthermore, this vipashyana — from the livid through to the skeleton — should not be practiced by observing all stages at once. Generate imaginative attention regarding a single livid corpse, then immediately return the mind to inner stillness. Sustain the contemplation only so long as the object appears joyful, clear, bright, free from disturbance, and without strained effort. The moment effort is required, return inward to cultivate stillness. Apply this same method through every stage — from the festering through to the skeleton — and through every level of expansion to the limitless.

"Having thus settled the mind in inner stillness, generate the imaginative attention of quiescence. Starting from the final limitless livid corpses through to the final limitless skeletons, withdraw the mind inward. Skillfully remove the manifold signs and set them aside so they no longer appear — yet without fully abandoning the formed object, and without engaging in discrimination. Simply rest the mind on this object, letting it move signless, non-discriminating, and at peace."

[^expansion]: The graduated expansion — from a single instance to an all-pervading field — is the defining method of imaginative attention (勝解作意). The practitioner stabilizes on one, then progressively expands numerically and spatially until no gap remains. "A space the size of a staff-tip" (一杖端處) is a standard Yogacara measure for completeness. The same expansion applies to every ashubha stage.

[^supervised_practice]: The contemplation instructions throughout this section presuppose the guidance of a qualified yoga teacher, as established at the opening of this fascicle (section 32.1). Traditional commentarial sources uniformly insist that cemetery contemplation be undertaken only under the supervision of an experienced teacher, as unsupervised practice can produce adverse psychological reactions including pathological fear or revulsion. See Visuddhimagga VI.1-4.

[^adhimoksha_practice]: Imaginative attention (*adhimoksha-manaskara*, 勝解作意) is not mere thinking about the object but the deliberate generation of a vivid mental image accompanied by deep conviction in its reality. The practitioner actively constructs the image in the mind's eye — its color, form, and location — and intensifies it by resolving "This is truly how it is." The image should be vivid enough to produce the appropriate somatic and emotional response: revulsion for impurity, warmth for loving-kindness, awe for dependent origination. "Imaginative" here does not mean fictional but refers to the mind's constructive capacity (*adhimoksha*) — the ability to make an absent object vividly present through conviction.

[^vipula]: Mount Vipula (脅山, Skt. *Vipula*, Pali *Vepulla*) is one of the five peaks surrounding Rajagriha. The SN 15.10 passage concerns a single person's accumulated bones; the Sravakabhumi adapts the simile to all corpses collectively. Even from a *single* kalpa, the heap would equal a mountain — and samsara's kalpas cannot be counted.

### 32.9 Light-Perception Applied; Entering the Foundations of Mindfulness

[T30.0462a01]

The teacher further instructs:

"Worthy One, the signs of luminosity you previously apprehended should be applied with proper attention and reflection in both your shamatha practice and your vipashyana practice. If you can cultivate shamatha and vipashyana with a mind accompanied by luminosity, accompanied by illumination, accompanied by clarity and brightness, free from darkness — this is what it means to cultivate the perception of luminosity on the paths of shamatha and vipashyana.

"If from the very beginning your meditation object is mostly unclear, and you repeatedly develop imaginative attention with an obscure sign, then all subsequent imaginative attention will likewise be unclear — even with much habitual practice, the sign remains dark. But if from the very beginning the object is mostly clear, and you develop imaginative attention with a vivid sign, then subsequent practice becomes ever more clear — even with little habitual practice, the sign is vivid.[^initial_clarity]

"In this way, you have well apprehended the sign of disenchantment, the sign of delight, the sign of shamatha, the sign of vipashyana, and the sign of luminosity. Alternating from time to time between inner stillness and the investigation of dharmas through sign-following vipashyana — through the force of this correct impurity practice, you gradually enter the foundations of mindfulness.

"When entering, first apprehend the signs of the thirty-six substances within the internal body — beginning with head-hair and body-hair through to urine. Regarding these impure substances within your own body, first generate the imaginative attention of impurity. Having generated it repeatedly, settle the mind in inner stillness. This is called contemplating the body within the internal body — because it arises based on one's own internal body."

[^initial_clarity]: A key practical principle: the quality of one's initial apprehension of the meditation object determines all subsequent practice. A clear beginning propagates clarity; a murky beginning propagates murkiness. The beginner should invest careful effort in the initial apprehension of the sign — a well-apprehended sign will naturally deepen, while an unclear one will resist all effort.

### 32.10 The Four Foundations via Asubha: External Body; Feeling, Mind, and Dharmas

[T30.0462b01]

"Next, well apprehend the signs of external impure things. Generate imaginative attention regarding the livid, and so forth through to the skeleton — whether limited, extensive, or limitless. Having generated it repeatedly, settle the mind in inner stillness. This is called contemplating the body within the external body — because it arises based on the external bodies of others.

"After this, apprehend clearly the signs of impurity both internal and external to your own body. Likewise, with the mind made clear, apprehend the internal and external impurity of others' bodies. Then turn to your own beloved body and generate imaginative attention: after death, carried out to the charnel ground and left upon the earth, reaching the livid stage, the festering stage, and so forth through to the skeleton. Having generated this imaginative attention repeatedly, settle the mind in inner stillness. This is called contemplating the body within the internal-and-external body — because it arises based on one's own and others' bodies, whether internal or external.

"You should further contemplate the four formless skandhas — apprehending their signs through the force of hearing and reflection, and generating imaginative attention in three divisions:

**(1)** In the **shamatha division**: when your mind withdraws inward, the six perceptions arise — signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, peaceful bliss free from torment. The four formless skandhas that arise undisturbed regarding the meditative object are moment by moment continuously different — only new, never the old, flowing in succession. Reflect properly on this and generate imaginative attention. This is called contemplating feeling, mind, and dharmas within the internal.

**(2)** In the **non-distraction division**: previously, when you apprehended various objects and fell into the unfixed state, those states passed away and were utterly destroyed. Yet now, through loss of mindfulness, the disturbed mind produces signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions. Regarding the four formless skandhas that arise under their influence, apply proper attention: all such dharmas are the work of deception and illusion — momentary, abruptly appearing, fraught with danger, impermanent, unreliable. Generate imaginative attention in this way. This is called contemplating feeling, mind, and dharmas within the external.[^external_formless]

**(3)** In the **vipashyana division**: having well apprehended the vipashyana sign, abide in attention that has signs and discrimination. The four formless skandhas that arise internally in connection with this discriminated object are moment by moment continuously different — only new, never the old, flowing in succession. Generate imaginative attention as before. This is called contemplating feeling, mind, and dharmas within the internal-and-external."

[^external_formless]: The "external" contemplation of feeling, mind, and dharmas is counterintuitive: it refers not to others' mental states but to one's own past disturbed states — the signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions that arose when the mind was scattered. These are "external" because they belong to the unfixed state outside the practitioner's current meditative abiding — the mind "apprehended various objects and fell into the unfixed state" (取諸境界墮不定地).

### 32.11 Conduct Between Sessions; The Fire-Drill Simile

[T30.0462c01]

"In this way, through the force of correctly practicing the impurity contemplation, you will enter the four foundations of mindfulness. Within that practice, from time to time cultivate superior shamatha and vipashyana.

"As you dwell in right mindfulness within these four foundations — at the edge of whatever village or settlement — even when the mind has followed, inclined toward, and entered the meditation object, you should set the object aside and enter the village to beg for food.

"You should carefully avoid dangerous elephants, horses, bulls, dogs, snakes, and wild beasts; pits, gorges, moats, and ditches; stumps, poisonous thorns, mud, water, and filth. Keep far from improper conduct and impure seats and bedding. Guard your body well.

"Where sense objects should not engage the faculties, do not pursue them — simply keep the senses well restrained. Where they do require engaging the faculties, exert yourself properly, dwell in right mindfulness, and prevent afflictions from manifesting. In summary: protect the body, restrain the senses, maintain right mindfulness, attend with proper attention, and know the proper measure in taking food and drink.

"With householders and renunciants alike, speak words that are measured, reasonable, timely, straightforward, and peaceful. Keep far from all worldly speech contrary to the dharma. Even when discussing dharma, do not engage in disputation. Why? When persons dwell in contentious speech, challenging and cross-questioning one another, the mind comes to dwell in idle elaboration (*prapanca*). From idle elaboration, the mind grows agitated. From agitation, it is not at peace. From lack of peace, the mind is driven far from samadhi.[^disputation_chain]

"Having conducted yourself in this way, return swiftly — without releasing your meditation object — and sit cross-legged. Regarding the signs of shamatha and vipashyana previously apprehended, practice through constant and thorough application — like the worldly method of drilling for fire, applying uninterrupted effort and earnest application. Practice constantly, practice thoroughly.

"Furthermore, generate this aspiration: 'Suppose the combined lifespans of every person in Jambudvipa were gathered into my single life. Even with such a vast span, I would devote every moment without pause to diligent practice of superior shamatha and vipashyana for the sake of abandonment. For I rightly understand that this practice yields great fruit and great benefit. How much more so this brief life — this short span of existence, at its longest barely a hundred years, which when reckoned carefully is but a moment.'

"Following these instructions, practice constantly and thoroughly. If you train in abandonment for this goal, you will certainly attain it. First you will realize an initial degree of physical and mental pliancy and mental one-pointedness. Later you will realize the vast and complete attainments, both worldly and supramundane."

[^disputation_chain]: The causal chain: contentious speech → idle elaboration (*prapanca*, 戲論) → agitation (*auddhatya*, 掉舉) → lack of peace → distance from samadhi.

### 32.12 The Remaining Four Objects; Loving-Kindness Contemplation

[T30.0463a01]

For a beginner first undertaking practice, the yoga teachers skilled in yoga instruct regarding the impurity contemplation in the manner described above — this is called correct instruction, and such practice is called correct practice. As it is said: the greedy conduct type is tamed by the contemplation of impurity; the angry conduct type is tamed by the contemplation of loving-kindness (*maitri*) and compassion (*karuna*); and so on through the remaining types, to the discursive-thought type, which is tamed by mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*). Understand the differences among them as appropriate. The remaining entry points are now shown.

**Entry into the loving-kindness contemplation.** For the beginner relying on the loving-kindness and compassion contemplation: having well apprehended the signs of friends, enemies, and neutral persons in the external world, sit in a proper seat. With meditative attention accompanied by the superior aspiration of benefit and happiness, first generate imaginative attention regarding one friend, one enemy, and one neutral person. Toward all three categories equally, with the superior aspiration of benefit and happiness, wish to give them happiness, reciting: "May all sentient beings who seek happiness attain happiness" — whether blameless desire-realm happiness, blameless happiness with joy, or blameless happiness without joy.[^three_happinesses]

Then expand: two friends, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty — as before, until friends fill every direction and quarter without gap, leaving not even a space the size of a staff-tip. As with friends, so with enemies and the neutral.

**Entering the foundations of mindfulness through loving-kindness.** Without abandoning the loving-kindness practice, through cultivating it one properly enters the foundations of mindfulness. How does one enter?

Generate this imaginative attention: "As others regard me — whether as friend, enemy, or neutral — I seek happiness and turn from suffering." This establishes the internal foundation of mindfulness.

"Others, too, have their friends, enemies, and neutral persons. As I seek happiness and turn from suffering, so do they." This establishes the external foundation.

"As I am, so are all beings. As I myself wish to attain supreme happiness, so do they. All beings are equal to me, similar to me — I should give them benefit and happiness." This establishes the internal-and-external foundation.

Because these foundations of mindfulness take all the skandhas together as their object, they are called "foundations with deconstructed referent" (壞緣念住). If the practitioner apprehends only form-signs — color, shape, and gesture — and generates imaginative attention regarding friends, enemies, and the neutral, this establishes only the body foundation of mindfulness.

**Transition to real contemplation.** Relying on imaginative attention, one then enters real contemplation. When making this transition, reflect: "I have generated imaginative attention toward limitless beings with the superior aspiration of benefit and happiness. But the friends, enemies, and neutral persons from my beginningless past — the beings who have fallen away and passed — are numberless, far exceeding those of my present imaginative attention.

"These past beings: those who were my friends later became my enemies; enemies later became my friends; friends and enemies later became neutral; the neutral later became friends and enemies. By this reasoning, all beings are truly equal — there is not the slightest friend-nature, enemy-nature, or neutral-nature that is real. Therefore, toward all three categories, generate an equal mind and give benefit and happiness equally.

[T30.0463b01]

"As from the beginningless past, so too into the future — as long as beings continue cycling through samsara, the same holds true. Moreover, for all those beings from the beginningless past toward whom I never generated loving-kindness — they have all passed. What benefit does today's loving-kindness bring them? It is solely to purify my own mind. Therefore I recite: 'May all past beings attain happiness. May all future beings not yet come into existence also attain happiness.'"

In this way one enters real contemplation. The merit generated through real contemplation of loving-kindness surpasses that accumulated through imaginative attention beyond all measure: the latter does not reach even a hundredth, a thousandth, or any *upanishad* fraction of the former.[^merit_comparison] The rest is as previously explained.

[^three_happinesses]: The practitioner wishes all three for all beings, encompassing happiness at every level of existence — distinguishing the joy-accompanied happiness of the first and second dhyanas (*priti* + *sukha*) from the joy-free happiness of the third dhyana and above. Note that the Sravakabhumi directs equal loving-kindness toward all three categories (friend, enemy, neutral) simultaneously from the outset, differing from the Visuddhimagga's graduated approach (Vism IX.1–58) where the practitioner extends from dear ones to neutral to hostile in stages.

[^merit_comparison]: This incomparability formula is standard in Buddhist sutras. The specific insight grounding the comparison: loving-kindness rooted in the recognition of universal equality across samsara — that all beings have been one another's friends and enemies across beginningless time — generates merit immeasurably greater than loving-kindness based on imaginative constructions that maintain the friend/enemy/neutral distinction.

### 32.13 Dependent Origination Contemplation

For the beginner relying on the dependent origination contemplation: through the force of the wisdom of hearing and reflection, discriminate and apprehend signs as follows. All sentient beings, caught in ignorance and delusion, mistake what is impermanent for something lasting, what is impure for something pure, what is suffering for happiness, and what has no self for a self.

With these manifold inversions as their cause, beings generate craving — both for their present experience and for the selves they will receive hereafter. Through craving, they create the various forms of karma that are the root of birth. Because this affliction and karma serve as causes and conditions, they bring about the future arising of a great mass of pure suffering.

Having well apprehended these signs, one generates this conviction within oneself: "My own present great mass of suffering arose in just this way. My successive selves — more than could ever be counted, stretching back without beginning — have also arisen in this way. All sentient beings, past, future, and present — their entire selves are encompassed by the mass of suffering. They have arisen thus and will arise thus."

This correct contemplation of dependent origination is entirely real contemplation. There is no separate phase of imaginative attention.[^all_real]

If one reflects with proper attention on the dependent arising of one's own present skandhas, this is called contemplating body, feeling, mind, and dharmas within the internal. If one reflects on the dependent arising of others' present skandhas, this is called contemplating within the external.

[T30.0463c01]

If one reflects on the dependent arising of one's own and others' past and future skandhas, this is called contemplating within the internal-and-external. The remainder of the practice follows the same pattern described above.

[^all_real]: A striking feature: unlike the other four contemplation objects, dependent origination involves no imaginative attention phase (勝解作意). It is entirely real contemplation (真實作意) — direct reflection on things as they actually are. The text explicitly states: "there is no separate imaginative attention" (更無所餘勝解作意). This is because the objects of contemplation — inversions, craving, karma, and the resulting mass of suffering — are real features of samsaric existence rather than mentally constructed scenarios (as in the imagined corpses of impurity contemplation or the visualized friends and enemies of loving-kindness). The term 勝解 (conviction) is used once in the passage, but the text classifies the entire practice as real contemplation.

### 32.14 Element Differentiation Contemplation

For the beginner relying on element differentiation contemplation: as before, one begins with imaginative attention — apprehending each element's signs vividly in mind — before transitioning to real contemplation.

First apprehend the external signs of solidity — the great earth, mountains and forests, grasses and trees, bricks and stones, tiles and gravel, gems and pearls, lapis lazuli, conch and shell, coral and jade. Having apprehended these signs, generate imaginative attention regarding the solidity within your own body.

Turn next to water. Bring to mind rivers and streams, reservoirs and lakes, ponds and marshes, wells and the like — then attend to the moisture within your own body. Then fire: the scorching sun of the hot season, blazing conflagrations, wildfires in mountains and marshlands, fires in kilns and furnaces — and then the warmth within. Then wind: winds from every direction, up to the cosmic wind-wheel — and the wind that moves through you. In each case, apprehend the external sign first, then generate imaginative attention regarding the corresponding element within.

Next apprehend the external signs of the space element — the unobstructed openness of the directions, and the cavities, gaps, and hollows within collections of form that can contain things. Having well apprehended these signs, generate imaginative attention regarding the space element within the body.

Finally, through the force of the wisdom of hearing and reflection, make fine discriminations to apprehend the sign of the consciousness element. When the internal eye faculty is unimpaired, external forms are present, and the generative attention properly arises, eye consciousness is produced; if any condition is absent, it cannot arise. The same principle extends through to the mental faculty, dharmas, and mental consciousness. Having apprehended these signs, generate imaginative attention: within this body of four great elements resides the seed-realm of all consciousnesses — their lineage and intrinsic nature.

Regarding these four great elements: first generate imaginative attention toward the body's limbs and major parts at a coarse level, then progressively analyze into finer and finer divisions — down to the size of a floating dust-mote, then down to the ultimate atom (*paramanu*). Even a single body part gives rise to the attention of innumerable ultimate atoms assembled together — how much more so for all the body's parts combined. This is called the refined imaginative attention that analyzes the form elements to their ultimate boundary within element differentiation.

For the space element: first generate imaginative attention regarding the coarse apertures — those of the eyes, ears, nose, and throat passage, the openings through which one swallows, and those from which impurity flows downward after digestion.

[T30.0464a01]

Then gradually generate ever finer attention, until one discerns all the minute pores throughout the entire body.

For the consciousness element: gradually generate limitless imaginative attention regarding its bases, its objects, the attention associated with it, the three times and their temporal divisions, and the varieties of type.[^consciousness_analysis] When generating this attention, one also attends to the ten types of derived form through the analysis of bases and objects — analyzing them with the same fine discrimination as the great elements.

If one generates imaginative attention regarding the various elements within one's own body, this is called contemplating within the internal among the foundations of mindfulness. If one generates attention regarding the elements of non-sentient things, this is contemplating within the external. If one generates attention regarding the elements of other sentient beings, this is contemplating within the internal-and-external.

There is an alternative approach. Generate imaginative attention regarding your own body approaching the time of death — as previously described, reaching the livid stage or the festering. Imagine pus flowing from the festering corpse, gradually expanding until it fills the great oceans and reaches the edges of the earth. Then imagine fire: the body's countless varieties of parts consumed to ash by a vast conflagration. When the fire goes out, attend to the remaining bones and ashes, then imagine these ground to fine powder. Then a great wind scatters this powder through every direction and quarter. Once scattered, one no longer perceives the scattered ash or the scattering wind — only the remaining vast, boundless space.

Thus through imaginative attention, relying on the internal-and-external impurity practice, one enters element differentiation — contemplating the body within the sign of the body.

From this one transitions to real contemplation. Reflect as follows: "The limitless, boundless signs of water, fire, earth, wind, and space that I generated in imaginative attention — the elements I have actually experienced through beginningless wandering in birth and death are limitless, boundless, and far exceed even these.

"From the death of parents, brothers, sisters, and relatives, from the separation and loss of friends, wealth, rank, and position — I have wept until tears ran like rain. The mother's milk I have drunk is beyond measure. As a thief — pressing in and plundering, breaking through walls and locks — I have suffered countless times: hands severed, feet cut off, head chopped, nose removed, body and limbs variously dismembered. Immeasurable blood has flowed.

[T30.0464b01]

"All the water of the four great oceans would not equal even a hundredth part of the accumulated tears, milk, and blood — as broadly explained before.

"The fires that have burned my corpses through death and rebirth across all existences and destinies are beyond all comparison. The bones abandoned and scattered on the ground are beyond comparison. The winds that have dispersed my corpses are beyond comparison. The apertures in all those corpses — and the consciousnesses that have transmigrated, arising newly in each successive corpse, again and again, up to now, this final corpse with consciousness still flowing — the future limit of all destinations remains unfixed." Thus one contemplates up to the limitless consciousness element.

[^consciousness_analysis]: The consciousness element is analyzed through five aspects: its bases (所依), objects (所緣), associated attention (作意), temporal dimension (三世時分), and varieties of type (品類差別). Through this analysis of bases and objects, the practitioner also attends to the ten types of derived form (*upadaya-rupa*, 所造色) — forms dependent on the four great elements, such as the visible, audible, and tangible qualities — applying the same atomic-level analysis used for the great elements themselves.

### 32.15 Mindfulness of Breathing Contemplation

For the beginner in the correct practice of mindfulness of breathing: first observe external wind — wind through front and back windows of dwellings, or the blowing-tubes and bellows of ironsmiths and goldsmiths, or wind entering and passing through external assemblies of things. Having well apprehended these signs, attend to the mindfulness of the internal in-breath and out-breath, and generate imaginative attention regarding your own breathing.

First generate attention regarding the subtle breath-wind traveling through the coarse passages of the heart and chest. Then gradually extend to the many winds throughout the body — until generating attention regarding wind entering through every pore. Throughout all the body's parts — permeated by wind, comprised of wind, concealing wind within, with innumerable gatherings of wind collected throughout — generate imaginative attention regarding these signs, as though the body were like tufts of *dutala* cotton or wisps of cotton-floss, light and floating.[^dutala]

Attending to one's own continuous breath establishes the internal foundation of mindfulness. Contemplating others' corpses at the livid stage and beyond, where breathing has ceased, establishes the external foundation. Generating imaginative attention regarding one's own approaching death — the breath ceasing after death, or even before death by the natural order of things — establishes the internal-and-external foundation.

[^dutala]: *Dutala* (妬羅) is a type of cotton tree whose fibers are notably light and airy.

### 32.16 Universal Shamatha Practice and Pliancy Attainment

[T30.0464c01]

Throughout all the correct practices described above, one should cultivate shamatha — both its supportive aspects and its essential components — with correct, unerring practice. All the rest is as previously explained.

When beginners receive correct instruction and cultivate correct practice, they abide with ardor, clearly knowing and mindful, having subdued all worldly craving and grief.

If in this correct practice one constantly and thoroughly applies attention without error, undisturbed by noise or other disturbances — this is "ardor." If one cultivates shamatha and vipashyana, carefully discerning the signs of distraction and non-distraction — this is "clearly knowing and mindful." If one well apprehends the signs of disenchantment and the signs of delight — this is "having subdued all worldly craving and grief." For these reasons the text declares that one abides with ardor through to the subduing of worldly craving and grief.

When first undertaking this correct practice, mental one-pointedness and body-mind pliancy operate faintly — difficult to perceive. But through cultivating superior shamatha and vipashyana, as body and mind become clear, supple, and pliant, the previously faint mental one-pointedness and pliancy gradually increase, growing strong enough to become easily perceptible. Through the mutual reinforcement of these causes, each nourishing the other, one will before long give rise to strong, easily perceptible body-mind pliancy and mental one-pointedness.[^pliancy_process]

A precursor sign appears: on the crown of the head, something like heaviness arises, but this is not a harmful sensation. Because this sign arises internally, the mental heaviness associated with the affliction category — which obstructs the joy of abandonment — is entirely eliminated. Its antidote arises: mental suppleness and mental pliancy. Because mental pliancy arises, bodily pliancy follows accordingly. The wind element predominantly increases, and the great elements come to pervade the body. Because of this, the bodily heaviness associated with the affliction category is entirely removed. Its antidote — bodily suppleness and bodily pliancy — pervades the entire body as if filling it to overflowing.

When this first arises, the mind leaps with joy and delight, accompanied by rapture and happiness. The nature of the meditative object appears vividly in the mind. After this initial surge, the force of pliancy gradually softens and eases. A subtle pliancy moves with the body, operating throughout it. The leaping exuberance of the mind gradually subsides, and — held by shamatha — the mind moves serenely upon its object.

[^pliancy_process]: This passage describing the arising of pliancy (*prashrabdhi*, 輕安) is one of the most important in the entire Sravakabhumi and is extensively commented upon by Tsongkhapa in the *Lamrim Chenmo* (Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path, Vol. 3). The sequence — mental heaviness eliminated, mental pliancy arises, bodily pliancy follows via the wind element, pervading the body, initial elation, then subsiding into serene attention — serves as the technical criterion for the attainment of genuine shamatha. The precursor sign on the crown of the head (於其頂上似重而起) is specifically noted as a non-harmful sensation (非損惱相) to reassure practitioners. The initial arising of pliancy commonly involves strong somatic sensations — warmth, involuntary movement, visual brightness — as the wind element pervades the body. These are normal and temporary; the text says the "leaping exuberance gradually softens." Practitioners experiencing these sensations should continue practice without either pursuing or suppressing them. Tsongkhapa warns that despite the remarkable qualities of bliss, clarity, and non-conceptuality that characterize this attainment, it is common to Buddhist and non-Buddhist meditators alike and should not be overestimated as a high spiritual realization (*Lamrim Chenmo* Vol. 3).

### 32.17 Attainment of the Status "Possessed of Attention"

[T30.0465a01]

From this point onward, the beginner in the practice of yoga is called "one possessed of attention" (*samanaskara*) — first entering the ranks of those so designated.[^possessed_attention] Why? Because for the first time one has obtained a small measure of the subtle, correct attention that belongs to the concentrated ground of the form realm. For this reason one is called "possessed of attention."

The beginner who attains this status has the following characteristics. One has obtained some degree of concentrated mind belonging to the form realm, and some degree of body-mind pliancy and mental one-pointedness. One has the strength and capacity to cultivate the meditation object that purifies afflictions, causing the mind to flow with continuity and suppleness. Protected and sustained by shamatha, one is able to purify conduct.

Although one moves amid various desirable objects, fierce entanglements of craving do not arise. Even if a slight craving arises, by relying on a subtle antidote and briefly applying attention, one can immediately dispel it. As with desirable objects, so too with objects that produce anger, delusion, pride, and discursive thought — the same applies.

Sitting quietly in a secluded room and briefly holding the mind, body-mind pliancy arises swiftly. One is not greatly oppressed by bodily heaviness. The hindrances do not arise with great frequency. Thoughts bound up with longing, displeasure, and worry do not excessively arise. Even when rising from samadhi and going out for walking meditation, a residual force of pliancy continues to operate in body and mind.

These are the pure characteristics of one possessed of attention.

[^possessed_attention]: "Possessed of attention" (有作意, *samanaskara*) marks a critical threshold: the practitioner has for the first time attained a level of mental concentration belonging to the form realm (*rupa-dhatu*) rather than the desire realm. This is the formal criterion for the attainment of genuine shamatha, the "initial degree of physical and mental pliancy" promised in section 32.11. It is the starting point for the Fourth Yoga Place (fascicle 33), where the practitioner proceeds to cultivate the seven types of attention and ascend through the dhyanas.
