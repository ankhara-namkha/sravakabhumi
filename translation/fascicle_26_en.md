### 26.1 Opening Questions and Summary Verse

[T30.0424a07]

**Question:** In the ground of renunciation (*naishkramya*) that was previously presented and elucidated: How many types of persons (*pudgala*) are able to realize renunciation? How are persons established? What are the objects of meditation (*alambana*)? What are the instructions? What is the training? What are the dharmas concordant with training? What is the deterioration of yoga? What is yoga? What is attention (*manaskara*)? What are the activities of yogins, and how many kinds of yogins are there? What is yogic cultivation? What are the fruits of cultivation? How many synonymous designations for persons are there? How many kinds of persons are there? How many causes for the establishment of persons are there? How many kinds of maras are there, and how many kinds of mara-activities? How does one set out upon what is empty and without fruit?[^emptyfruitless]

The summary verse (*uddana*) says:

> Persons, establishment, objects, instruction,
> Training, concordant dharmas, deterioration, yoga, attention,
> Yogins, activities, cultivation, fruits, synonyms, persons,
> Causes, mara-activities, fruitlessness — all shall be explained in full.

### 26.2 The Twenty-Eight Types of Persons

[T30.0424a19]

The varieties of persons are differentiated into twenty-eight types. What are the twenty-eight? They are:

1. Those of dull faculties
2. Those of sharp faculties
3. Those dominated by greed
4. Those dominated by aversion
5. Those dominated by delusion
6. Those dominated by pride
7. Those dominated by discursive thought
8. Those of balanced disposition
9. Those of thin-dust nature
10. Path-practitioners
11. Fruit-abiders
12. Faith-followers
13. Dharma-followers
14. Those resolved through faith
15. View-attainers
16. Body-witnesses
17. Those with at most seven lives
18. Clan-to-clan practitioners
19. Those with one interval
20. Those who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state
21. Those who attain parinirvana upon rebirth
22. Those who attain parinirvana without exertion
23. Those who attain parinirvana with exertion
24. Upstream-goers
25. Those liberated by occasion
26. Those of the immovable dharma
27. Those liberated by prajna
28. Those liberated in both respects

[T30.0424a27]

**(1) Persons of dull faculties.** What is a person of dull faculties? It is a person who possesses dull faculties and functions sluggishly and feebly with respect to objects of knowledge, as previously explained.[^first_yoga_place] There are furthermore two types, whose characteristics should be known: first, those who are by nature of a dull-faculty lineage (*gotra*); second, those who have not yet properly cultivated their faculties.

**(2) Persons of sharp faculties.** What is a person of sharp faculties? It is a person who possesses sharp faculties and functions neither sluggishly nor feebly with respect to objects of knowledge, as previously explained.[^first_yoga_place] There are likewise two types, whose characteristics should be known: first, those who are by nature of a sharp-faculty lineage; second, those who have already properly cultivated their faculties.

**(3) Persons dominated by greed.** What is a person dominated by greed? A greed-dominated person is one who has long cultivated and reinforced the affliction (*klesha*) of greed in previous lives. That habitual force carries forward: in this life, even slight occasions for desire arouse strong and persistent craving. This is called a person dominated by greed.

**(4)–(7) Persons dominated by aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought (*vitarka*).** The pattern is the same for each — the affliction entrenched through repeated past cultivation expresses in this life as a strong, persistent version of that tendency whenever its characteristic objects are encountered. A person dominated by aversion meets even minor provocations with fierce and enduring hostility. One dominated by delusion responds to even slight occasions for confusion with deep and lasting bewilderment. One dominated by pride, encountering any basis for self-comparison, gives rise to intense and persistent conceit. And one dominated by discursive thought, meeting any occasion for mental proliferation, is seized by strong and unrelenting ideation.

**(8) Persons of balanced disposition.** What is a person of balanced disposition? It is a person who in previous lives, though not having cultivated, habituated, or repeatedly cultivated greed, aversion, delusion, pride, or discursive thought, has nonetheless not yet seen the faults in those dharmas, has not yet become disenchanted with them, and has not yet properly investigated them. Due to this cause and condition, with regard to objects of desire, hatred, confusion, pride, and discursive thought, they have no fierce greed and no enduring greed — yet greed still manifests in accordance with those objects. The same applies to aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought. This is called a person of balanced disposition.

**(9) Persons of thin-dust nature.** What is a person of thin-dust nature? It is a person who in previous lives has not cultivated, habituated, or repeatedly cultivated the affliction of greed, and has been able to see many faults in it, has become disenchanted with it, and has properly investigated it. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of desire — even when encountering many beautiful and sublime objects of the highest grade — they give rise only to feeble greed; with regard to objects of middling and lesser grades, no greed arises at all. The same should be understood for aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought. This is called a person of thin-dust nature.

**(10) Path-practitioners.** What is a path-practitioner? It is a person who practices toward the four fruits. What are the four? First, one practicing toward the fruit of stream-entry (*srota-apanna*). Second, one practicing toward the fruit of once-returning (*sakridagamin*). Third, one practicing toward the fruit of non-returning (*anagamin*). Fourth, one practicing toward the fruit of arhatship. These are called path-practitioners.

**(11) Fruit-abiders.** What is a fruit-abider? It is a person who abides in the four fruits. What are the four? First, the fruit of stream-entry. Second, the fruit of once-returning. Third, the fruit of non-returning. Fourth, the fruit of arhatship. These are called fruit-abiders.

**(12) Faith-followers.** What is a faith-follower (*shraddhanusarin*)? It is a person who seeks instruction and guidance from others, and by the power of this cultivates the practice leading to realization of the fruits. They do not practice following the dharma through their own capacity and their own power, based on what they have heard, received, thoroughly investigated, thought about, measured, and examined. Rather, they practice solely by following other persons through faith. This is called a faith-follower.

**(13) Dharma-followers.** What is a dharma-follower (*dharmanusarin*)? It is a person who, in accordance with what they have heard, received, thoroughly investigated, thought about, measured, and examined, has their own capacity and their own power to practice following the dharma. They do not seek instruction and guidance from others to cultivate the practice leading to realization of the fruits. This is called a dharma-follower.

**(14) Those resolved through faith.** What is a person resolved through faith (*shraddhadhimukta*)? It is precisely the faith-follower who, through others' instruction and guidance, upon directly realizing one of the shramana fruits (the four attainments of the renunciant practitioner), is called a person resolved through faith.

**(15) View-attainers.** What is a view-attainer (*drishtiprapta*)? It is precisely the dharma-follower who, upon directly realizing one of the shramana fruits, is called a view-attainer.

**(16) Body-witnesses.** What is a body-witness (*kayasakshi*)? It is a person who enters and emerges from the eight liberations in forward and reverse order, bears witness through the body, and abides in them extensively, but has not yet attained the complete exhaustion of the outflows (*asrava*). This is called a body-witness.

**(17) Those with at most seven lives.** What is a person with at most seven lives (*saptakritbhava-parama*)? It is a person who, having permanently severed the three fetters (*samyojana*) — the view of the transitory collection (*satkaya-drishti*), attachment to rules and observances (*shila-vrata-paramarsha*), and doubt (*vicikitsa*) — has attained the fruit of stream-entry, has become one of the nature of non-falling, and is certainly destined for awakening (*bodhi*). With at most seven lives, going back and forth between the human and celestial realms, after at most seven returns they reach the limit of suffering (*duhkha*). Such is called a person with at most seven lives.

**(18) Clan-to-clan practitioners.** What is a clan-to-clan practitioner (*kulamkula*)? There are two types of clan-to-clan: first, celestial clan-to-clan; second, human clan-to-clan. A celestial clan-to-clan practitioner is one who, among the celestial realms, goes from family to family, coming and going until reaching the limit of suffering. A human clan-to-clan practitioner is one who, in the human realm, goes from family to family, coming and going until reaching the limit of suffering. It should be known that both of these are stream-enterer persons.

**(19) Those with one interval.** What is a person with one interval (*ekavicika*)? It is precisely a once-returner who, having practiced toward the fruit of non-returning, has permanently severed the upper and middling grades of desire-realm afflictions, with only the lower grade remaining. They take only one more existence in a desire-realm celestial state, and right there attain parinirvana, not returning again to be born in this world. This is called a person with one interval.

**(20) Those who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state.** What is a person who attains parinirvana in the intermediate state (*antaraparinirvana*)? There are three subtypes:

First, there is a type who, after passing away from this [realm], takes birth in the intermediate existence (*antarabhava*). Having been born in the intermediate existence, they immediately attain parinirvana — like a tiny spark from kindling that, barely rising, at once extinguishes.

Second, there is a type who, after passing away from this [realm], takes birth in the intermediate existence. Having been born in the intermediate existence, they remain for a short time, and before proceeding to birth-existence, they attain parinirvana — like a spark streaming from a blazing iron ingot being hammered, extinguishing before it falls.

Third, there is a type who, after passing away from this [realm], takes birth in the intermediate existence. Having been born in the intermediate existence, they proceed toward birth-existence, but before reaching it, they attain parinirvana — like a spark flying from hammered hot iron that, falling but not yet reaching the ground, at once extinguishes.

These three types of persons who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state are collectively designated as persons who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state.

**(21) Those who attain parinirvana upon rebirth.** What is a person who attains parinirvana upon rebirth (*upapadyaparinirvana*)? It is one who, having just been born there, immediately attains parinirvana. This is called a person who attains parinirvana upon rebirth.

**(22) Those who attain parinirvana without exertion.** What is a person who attains parinirvana without exertion (*anabhisamskara-parinirvana*)? It is one who, having been born there, without undertaking application (*prayoga*), without making effort, and without the path manifesting through exertion, attains parinirvana. This is called a person who attains parinirvana without exertion.

**(23) Those who attain parinirvana with exertion.** What is a person who attains parinirvana with exertion (*sabhisamskara-parinirvana*)? It is one who, having been born there, undertakes application and makes great effort, and with the path manifesting through extreme exertion, attains parinirvana. This is called a person who attains parinirvana with exertion.

**(24) Upstream-goers.** What is an upstream-goer (*urdhvasrotas*)? It is a non-returner who, after being born upward into the first dhyana (meditative absorption), abides there without attaining parinirvana. After passing away from there, they are successively reborn upward through the various planes of rebirth, reaching as far as the Akanishtha heaven or the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception. This is called an upstream-goer.

**(25) Those liberated by occasion.** What is a person liberated by occasion (*samayavimukta*)?[^occasion] It is a person of dull-faculty lineage who, with respect to the worldly pleasant abiding in the present life, is liable to regress. Out of fear of regression, they may contemplate harming themselves,[^selfharm] or alternatively they may guard their liberation by diligently cultivating the practice of non-negligence. That is to say, by the force of preventing regression, they either merely maintain their present level of wholesome qualities, or through successive days, nights, moments, eye-blinks, and instants, they strive and progress — yet they have not attained the most supremely acute [realization]. This is called a person liberated by occasion.

**(26) Those of the immovable dharma.** What is a person of the immovable dharma (*akopyadharma*)? It is a person who is the opposite of the above. This should be known as a person of the immovable dharma.

**(27) Those liberated by prajna.** What is a person liberated by prajna (*prajnavimukta*)? It is a person who has attained the complete exhaustion of all outflows but has not yet borne witness through the body to the eight liberations, nor abides in them fully. This is called a person liberated by prajna.

**(28) Those liberated in both respects.** What is a person liberated in both respects (*ubhayatobhagavimukta*)? It is a person who has attained the complete exhaustion of all outflows, and has borne witness through the body to the eight liberations and abides in them fully. Their mind is liberated with respect to both the affliction-obstruction portion and the liberation-obstruction portion.[^ubhaya] This is called a person liberated in both respects.

[^selfharm]: "Contemplate harming themselves" (思自害) refers to certain arhats of dull faculties who, fearing regression from their attainment, may consider ending their lives while still in the state of liberation. This is a descriptive observation about a specific doctrinal category, not a recommendation. The Abhidharma literature discusses this as a philosophical point about the nature of certain arhat attainments; the Vinaya literature, by contrast, strictly prohibits encouraging or facilitating the taking of life, including one's own.

[^ubhaya]: "Affliction-obstruction portion" (煩惱障分) refers to the obstruction of the afflictions (*klesha-avarana*); "liberation-obstruction portion" (解脫障分) refers to the obstruction preventing full mastery of the eight liberations — also called the concentration-obstruction (定障, *samadhi-avarana*; or *vimukti-avarana*). The person liberated in both respects has overcome both.

[^emptyfruitless]: "Empty and without fruit" (空無有果) does not refer to the Buddhist doctrine of emptiness (*shunyata*). It refers to fruitless practice — undertaking the path in a way that yields no result. This topic is treated in fascicle 29 (section 29.17).

[^occasion]: "Liberated by occasion" (時解脫, *samayavimukta*) means that this arhat's liberation depends on favorable conditions (*samaya*) — suitable time, place, and circumstances — and is therefore subject to possible regression. This contrasts with the "immovable" (*akopyadharma*) arhat whose liberation is irreversible.

### 26.3 Establishment of Persons: The Eleven Criteria

[T30.0425b20]

How are persons established? It is by eleven criteria of differentiation that persons should be understood as established. What are the eleven criteria of differentiation?

1. By differentiation of faculties
2. By differentiation of community
3. By differentiation of conduct
4. By differentiation of aspiration
5. By differentiation of course of progress
6. By differentiation of path and fruit
7. By differentiation of preparatory practice
8. By differentiation of meditative concentration
9. By differentiation of birth
10. By differentiation of regression and non-regression
11. By differentiation of obstruction

[T30.0425b25]

#### (1) By Faculties

How are persons established by differentiation of faculties? By differentiation of faculties, two types of persons are established: first, those of dull faculties; second, those of sharp faculties.

#### (2) By Community

How are persons established by differentiation of community?[^seven_communities] By differentiation of community, seven types of persons are established: bhikshus (monks), bhikshunis (nuns), shiksha-manas (probationary nuns), shramaneras (novice monks), shramanerikas (novice nuns), upasakas (male lay practitioners), and upasikas (female lay practitioners).

#### (3) By Conduct

How are persons established by differentiation of conduct? By differentiation of conduct, seven types of persons are established:[^carita] A person dominated by greed is called a person of greed-conduct. A person dominated by aversion is called a person of aversion-conduct. A person dominated by delusion is called a person of delusion-conduct. A person dominated by pride is called a person of pride-conduct. A person dominated by discursive thought is called a person of discursive-thought-conduct. A person of balanced disposition is called a person of balanced-conduct. A person of thin-dust nature [whose afflictions are naturally mild; see section 26.2] is called a person of thin-dust-conduct.

**Question:** What are the characteristics by which a person of greed-conduct should be known?

**Answer:** A person of greed-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of desire, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement (*paryavasthana*) [the active, manifest form of an affliction] of greed — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects. Moreover, this entanglement of greed dwells within their person, persists for a long time in continuous succession, and binds them for an extended period. Due to this entanglement of greed, they are overwhelmed by desirable dharmas [phenomena] and cannot overcome those desirable dharmas. Their faculties are pleased and gratified; their faculties are not rigid; their faculties are not rough; their faculties are not coarse. By nature they do not like to harm others with evil actions and speech. They are difficult to separate [from their attachments] and difficult to disenchant. They have inferior resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*). Their undertakings are firm and their undertakings are lasting. Their discipline is firm and their discipline is lasting. They are patient and accepting. With regard to material requisites, they are by nature attached and deeply cherish them. They are mostly joyful and delighted, far from frowning. Their face is open and smiling; they greet others readily and speak first.

**Question:** What are the characteristics by which a person of aversion-conduct should be known?

**Answer:** A person of aversion-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of hatred, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement of aversion — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects. Moreover, this entanglement of aversion dwells within their person, persists for a long time in continuous succession, and binds them for an extended period. Due to this entanglement of aversion, they are overwhelmed by hateful dharmas and cannot overcome those hateful dharmas. Their faculties are withered; their faculties are rigid; their faculties are harsh and rough; their faculties are coarse and dry. By nature they delight in harming others with evil actions and speech. They are easily caused to renounce, easily disenchanted. They are fierce and sharp-tongued. Their demeanor is forbidding and severe. They have little resolute confidence. Their undertakings are not firm, their undertakings are not lasting. Their discipline is not firm, their discipline is not lasting. They are impatient and unaccepting, mostly anxious and distressed. By nature they like to oppose; what they grasp is not in accord [with others]. By nature they are frequently melancholy. By nature they favor coarse speech. They often harbor resentment and hatred. Their dispositions are harsh and fierce. They are perverse and malicious, fault-finding and contentious, fond of confrontation. Upon receiving even a few words [of criticism], they give rise to great anger and resentment. They dwell in distress and readily give rise to fury. Their brows are perpetually furrowed, their countenance never relaxed. They look askance with downcast eyes. With regard to others' prosperity and success, they feel much hatred and jealousy. Such is the portrait of aversion-conduct.

**Question:** What are the characteristics by which a person of delusion-conduct should be known?

**Answer:** A person of delusion-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of confusion, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement of delusion — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects. Moreover, this entanglement of delusion dwells within their person, persists for a long time in continuous succession, and binds them for an extended period. Due to this entanglement of delusion, they are overwhelmed by confusing dharmas and cannot overcome those confusing dharmas. Their faculties are dim and dull; their faculties are foolish and benighted; their faculties are feeble and inferior. Their physical actions are slow and slack; their speech is slow and slack. They reason badly, speak badly, and act badly. They are lazy and indolent. Their initiatives are incomplete. Their verbal expression is weak. By nature they are not clever or sharp. Their mindfulness (*smriti*) is largely lost to forgetfulness. They dwell without alertness (*samprajanya*). What they grasp is distorted and mistaken. They are difficult to separate [from their confusion] and difficult to disenchant. They have inferior resolute confidence. They are dull and inarticulate, resorting to gestures rather than speech. They lack the ability to comprehend the meaning of dharma whether well or poorly explained. They are dragged along by circumstances, led away by others, directed and controlled by others. These are the marks of delusion-conduct.

**Question:** What are the characteristics by which a person of pride-conduct should be known?

**Answer:** A person of pride-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects that occasion pride, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement of pride — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects. Moreover, this entanglement of pride dwells within their person, persists for a long time in continuous succession, and binds them for an extended period. Due to this entanglement of pride, they are overwhelmed by dharmas that occasion pride and cannot overcome those dharmas. Their faculties are restless; their faculties are elated; their faculties are scattered. They diligently delight in adorning their person. Their speech is lofty and grand. They take no pleasure in humility. They are unable to dutifully attend to their parents, relatives, and teachers at all times. They are largely filled with arrogance. They are unable to bow in respect, greet with reverence, join palms, welcome and receive [elders], or perform acts of harmony and respect. They exalt and elevate themselves while disdaining others. They are attached to material gain, attached to veneration, and attached to worldly praise and fame. Their behavior is flippant, and they delight in mockery. They are difficult to separate [from their pride] and difficult to disenchant. They have expansive resolute confidence [in themselves] but their compassion is feeble. They hold to the view of a self, a sentient being, a life-force, a sustainer, a person, a born being, and so forth. Their pride is mostly of the upper grade, and they have many grievances and much hatred.

**Question:** What are the characteristics by which a person of discursive-thought-conduct should be known?

**Answer:** A person of discursive-thought-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of discursive thought, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement of discursive thought — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects. This entanglement of discursive thought dwells within their person, persists for a long time in continuous succession, and binds them for an extended period. Due to this entanglement, they are overwhelmed by dharmas that provoke discursive thought and cannot overcome those dharmas. Their faculties are unsettled; their faculties are flighty and elevated; their faculties are restless; their faculties are scattered. Their physical actions go astray; their speech goes astray. They are difficult to separate [from their discursiveness] and difficult to disenchant. They delight in and are attached to idle elaboration (*prapanca*).[^prapanca] They have much confusion and much doubt, and are largely filled with desire for pleasure. Their discipline is not firm, their discipline is not steady. Their undertakings are not firm, their undertakings are not steady. They are largely filled with fear and anxiety. Their mindfulness is largely lost to forgetfulness. They take no pleasure in seclusion and mostly delight in distraction and agitation. With regard to the various wonderful things of the world, they follow along with the flow of desire, energetically diligent and unflagging in pursuing and completing [worldly activities]. These are the marks by which a person dominated by discursive thought is recognized.

Such is what is called the establishment of persons by differentiation of conduct.

#### (4) By Aspiration

How are persons established by differentiation of aspiration? There are persons who have generated the correct aspiration for the shravaka vehicle. There are persons who have generated the correct aspiration for the pratyekabuddha vehicle. And there are persons who have generated the correct aspiration for the mahayana (great vehicle).

It should be known that among these, a person who has generated the correct aspiration for the shravaka vehicle may be of shravaka lineage (*gotra*)[^aspiration_lineage], pratyekabuddha lineage, or mahayana lineage. A person who has generated the correct aspiration for pratyekabuddha awakening may be of pratyekabuddha lineage, shravaka lineage, or mahayana lineage. A person who has generated the correct aspiration for the mahayana may be of mahayana lineage, pratyekabuddha lineage, or shravaka lineage.

If a person of shravaka lineage has generated the correct aspiration for pratyekabuddha awakening or for unsurpassed, complete awakening, because they are of shravaka lineage, they will subsequently and certainly abandon that aspiration and will definitely abide only in the aspiration for the shravaka vehicle. The same should be understood for persons of pratyekabuddha lineage and mahayana lineage.

Among these, the aspirations of all persons can be shifted and can be abandoned. But lineage definitely cannot be shifted or abandoned. In the present context, since the Sravakabhumi concerns the Hearer's Ground, it should be known that only persons of shravaka-vehicle aspiration and shravaka lineage are discussed.

Such is what is called the establishment of persons by differentiation of aspiration.

#### (5) By Course of Progress

How are persons established by differentiation of course of progress (*pratipada*)? The persons who were presented and elucidated above rely on four courses of progress to attain liberation.[^pratipada] What are the four?

1. The course of progress that is painful with slow penetration
2. The course of progress that is painful with swift penetration
3. The course of progress that is pleasant with slow penetration
4. The course of progress that is pleasant with swift penetration

It should be known that the course of progress of a person of dull-faculty nature who has not attained the root dhyana (*maula-dhyana*) [the actual first dhyana or higher, as distinguished from preliminary access concentration] is called painful with slow penetration. The course of progress of a person of sharp-faculty nature who has not attained the root dhyana is called painful with swift penetration. The course of progress of a person of dull-faculty nature who has attained the root dhyana is called pleasant with slow penetration. The course of progress of a person of sharp-faculty nature who has attained the root dhyana is called pleasant with swift penetration.

Such is what is called the establishment of persons by differentiation of course of progress.

#### (6) By Path and Fruit

How are persons established by differentiation of path and fruit? By those practicing toward the four fruits and those abiding in the four fruits.

Those practicing toward the four are: first, the person practicing toward the fruit of stream-entry; second, the person practicing toward the fruit of once-returning; third, the person practicing toward the fruit of non-returning; fourth, the person practicing toward the fruit of arhatship.

Those abiding in the four fruits are: first, the fruit of stream-entry; second, the fruit of once-returning; third, the fruit of non-returning; fourth, the fruit of arhatship.

Those who turn upon the path toward [a fruit] are called path-practitioners. By the paths toward [the fruits], four types of persons are established. Those who have attained the shramana fruits [the four fruits of liberation just described] are called fruit-abiders. By path and fruit, four types of persons are established.

Such is what is called the establishment of persons by differentiation of path and fruit.

#### (7) By Preparatory Practice

How are persons established by differentiation of preparatory practice (*prayoga*)? By faith-followers and dharma-followers. One who follows other persons through faith and diligently cultivates right practice is called a faith-follower. One who, with regard to the dharmas, does not depend on others, and following the vinaya [the code of discipline], diligently cultivates right practice is called a dharma-follower.

This completes the establishment of persons by preparatory practice.

#### (8) By Meditative Concentration

How are persons established by differentiation of meditative concentration? By the body-witness (*kayasakshi*) [one who has directly experienced the full range of meditative liberations through personal realization]. They have borne witness through the body to the eight liberations (*ashta-vimoksha*) and abide in them fully, yet have not attained the permanent exhaustion of all outflows (*asrava*) [the deep-seated mental taints whose elimination marks liberation].[^eight_libs]

It should be known that such a person [enters]: the liberation of one with form perceiving forms; the liberation of one without internal perception of form perceiving external forms; the liberation of the pure, bearing witness through the body and abiding fully; the liberation of the sphere of infinite space; the liberation of the sphere of infinite consciousness; the liberation of the sphere of nothingness; the liberation of the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception; and the liberation of the cessation of perception and feeling. They are able to enter and emerge from these in forward and reverse order freely.

This completes the establishment of persons by meditative concentration.

#### (9) By Birth

How are persons established by differentiation of birth? By those with at most seven lives, clan-to-clan practitioners, those with one interval, those who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state, those who attain parinirvana upon rebirth, those who attain parinirvana without exertion, those who attain parinirvana with exertion, and upstream-goers.[^birth_types]

This covers the establishment of persons by birth.

#### (10) By Regression and Non-Regression

How are persons established by differentiation of regression and non-regression? By regression, the occasion-liberated arhat (*samayavimukta*) is established. They are liable to regress from their pleasant abiding in the present life. By non-regression, the immovable-dharma arhat (*akopyadharma*) is established. They definitely cannot regress from their pleasant abiding in the present life.

This covers the establishment of persons by regression and non-regression.

#### (11) By Obstruction

How are persons established by differentiation of obstruction (*avarana*)? By the prajna-liberated arhat and the both-ways-liberated arhat. A prajna-liberated arhat (*prajnavimukta*) is one who has been liberated from the afflictive obstruction (*klesha-avarana*) but has not been liberated from the concentration obstruction (*samadhi-avarana*).[^ubhaya] A both-ways-liberated arhat (*ubhayatobhagavimukta*) is one who has been liberated from the afflictive obstruction and has also been liberated from the concentration obstruction. Therefore they are called "liberated in both respects."

This completes the establishment of persons by obstruction.

[^seven_communities]: The seven Buddhist communities (*sapta-parishat*) represent the standard classification of the Buddhist sangha by ordination status. Xuanzang renders *shramanera* (novice monk) as 勞策男, lit. "man who exerts effort," an etymological translation rather than a transliteration.

[^carita]: Seven conduct types are established, of which five receive detailed portraits here — those corresponding to the five affliction-dominated types defined in section 26.2 (types 3-7). The balanced-conduct and thin-dust-conduct types receive no separate portrait, as their characteristics were sufficiently described in 26.2. The Sanskrit term *carita* means "conduct" or "character-type." These dispositional types determine which meditation object (*alambana*) is prescribed as antidote, as explained in subsequent sections. The Visuddhimagga (III.74-103) provides a parallel discussion using six temperaments with a different taxonomy, pairing faith/greed, intelligence/hate, and speculation/delusion.

[^prapanca]: Idle elaboration (戲論, *prapanca*) refers to the tendency of the discursive mind to proliferate concepts, narratives, and imaginary scenarios beyond what is warranted by experience. In meditation, it manifests as an inability to remain with the object.

[^aspiration_lineage]: The distinction between aspiration (*pranidhana*) and lineage (*gotra*) is doctrinally significant. While a person's aspiration for a particular vehicle may change, their inherent lineage — their spiritual disposition — is fixed and will ultimately determine their path. A shravaka-lineage person who temporarily aspires to the mahayana will inevitably return to the shravaka vehicle. The Sravakabhumi presents a deterministic view: lineage cannot be shifted or abandoned. This position is characteristic of early Yogacara thought and is explicitly rejected by other major Buddhist traditions — notably the *Lotus Sutra*, the *tathagatagarbha* literature, and the Madhyamaka tradition — which hold that all beings possess the potential for full awakening. See the note on *agotra* at section 28.5.

[^pratipada]: The four courses of progress (*catur-pratipada*) classify practitioners along two axes: whether the path is "painful" or "pleasant" (determined by whether one has attained a root dhyana, i.e., the actual first dhyana or higher, which provides a stable basis for insight), and whether penetration is "slow" or "swift" (determined by faculty sharpness). Thus a person of dull faculties without dhyana faces the most difficult path, while a person of sharp faculties with dhyana has the smoothest. This classification appears in the Anguttara Nikaya (AN 4, Patipada Vagga) and Abhidharmakoshabhashya (VI.70).

[^eight_libs]: The body-witness (*kayasakshi*) is distinguished by having personally mastered all eight liberations (*ashta-vimoksha*) — the sequence of progressively subtler meditative attainments culminating in the cessation of perception and feeling — without yet having exhausted all outflows. This person possesses deep concentration but has not yet attained final liberation.

[^birth_types]: The eight person types listed here map to three levels of attainment: (1) "at most seven lives" and "clan-to-clan" are subtypes of stream-enterers; (2) "one interval" bridges once-returning and non-returning; (3) the remaining five — intermediate state, upon rebirth, without exertion, with exertion, and upstream-goer — are the standard five subtypes of non-returners (*panca-anagamin*), classified by where and how they attain final liberation. This is standard Abhidharma classification (cf. Abhidharmakosha VI.36-38).

2. Purifying-conduct objects of meditation
3. Skillful objects of meditation
4. Affliction-purifying objects of meditation

### 26.5 Pervasive Objects of Meditation

[T30.0427a24]

What are the pervasive objects of meditation? They are again fourfold:

1. The image with discernment (*savikalpaka-pratibimba*)
2. The image without discernment (*nirvikalpaka-pratibimba*)
3. The limit of things (*vastu-paryanta*)
4. The accomplishment of the purpose (*krtyanushthana*)

##### The Image with Discernment

What is the image with discernment?[^image_discernment] Suppose there is a person who, relying on either hearing the correct dharma, or on instruction and admonition, or on what is seen, heard, or discerned, forms an image corresponding to the knowable object. Through the vipashyana (insight) practice of the ground of meditative equipoise (*samahita-bhumi*), they examine and probe the object with sustained investigative attention.

The knowable objects are: impurity, loving-kindness and compassion, the nature of conditions and dependent origination, element differentiation, mindfulness of breathing, skillfulness in the skandhas (aggregates), skillfulness in the elements, skillfulness in the sense bases, skillfulness in dependent origination, skillfulness in the possible and impossible, the coarseness of the lower realms and the peacefulness of the upper realms, and the truth of suffering, the truth of the origin, the truth of cessation, and the truth of the path. These are called the knowable objects.

With regard to these knowable objects, relying either on instruction and admonition or on hearing the correct dharma, one brings the attention of the ground of meditative equipoise to bear. One gives rise to resolute confidence regarding those dharmas and regarding those knowable objects. At that time, with respect to the knowable objects, one's resolute confidence operates as if one were directly perceiving them. The meditator does not encounter the knowable object as it actually is, nor does some unrelated image arise. What appears is something in between: the object's likeness, conjured through the conviction-power of meditative equipoise. This likeness — appearing because the mind, in meditative steadiness, holds the object as if present — is called the meditation image, the image corresponding to the knowable object.

The practitioner of insight, investigating this image, examines and determines the virtues and faults within the original knowable objects. This is called the image with discernment.

##### The Image without Discernment

What is the image without discernment? The practitioner, having taken up the characteristic of such an image, no longer engages in investigative examination. Rather, with respect to this very object-image, they calm their mind through the practice of shamatha (calm abiding). This is the nine stages of mental abiding:[^nine_stages] making the mind internally abide, continuously abide, re-settle, closely abide, be tamed, be pacified, be completely pacified, be one-pointed, and settle in equipoise.

At that time, the practitioner accomplishes the object of the image without discernment. With respect to this very object-image, they settle their mindfulness in a single direction, one-pointedly, no longer engaging in investigative examination. This is called the image without discernment.

This very image is also called "the image," "the sign of samadhi," "the domain of samadhi," "the entrance of samadhi," "the gate of samadhi," "the basis of attention," "the internal discernment-body," and "the light-image."[^image_synonyms] Such and similar names should be known as the various designations for the image corresponding to the knowable object.

##### The Limit of Things

What is the limit of things? It is the full extent of what exists (*yavad-bhavikata*) and the nature of things as they are (*yathavad-bhavikata*) with respect to the object.[^limit_twofold]

What is called the full extent of what exists? Beyond the form skandha there is no further form. Beyond the feeling, perception, formation, and consciousness skandhas there is no further feeling, perception, formation, or consciousness. All conditioned phenomena are encompassed by the five dharmas. All dharmas are encompassed by the elements and sense bases. All knowable objects are encompassed by the four noble truths. This is called the full extent of what exists.

What is called the nature of things as they are? It is the true reality and the suchness of the object, which can be verified through four types of reasoning:[^four_reasonings] reasoning from dependence, reasoning from function, reasoning from proof, and reasoning from the nature of things.

Thus, the full extent of what exists and the nature of things as they are with respect to the object are together called the limit of things.

##### The Accomplishment of the Purpose

What is the accomplishment of the purpose? The practitioner of insight, by cultivating, habituating, and repeatedly practicing shamatha and vipashyana, brings to fruition the entire process of attending to the images of the objects. Due to this completion, they attain the transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravrtti*).[^transformation] All heaviness is entirely extinguished.

Having attained the transformation of the basis, they transcend the image. With respect to the knowable objects, non-conceptual direct-perception (*pratyaksha*) knowledge and vision arise.

At the moment of attaining each level of meditative absorption, direct perception arises corresponding to that level's domain. For the first dhyana, the domain of the first dhyana becomes directly perceived. For the second through fourth, each dhyana's domain in turn. For the formless attainments — infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception — the same principle holds throughout. This is called the accomplishment of the purpose.

##### Why These Are Called "Pervasive"

These four objects of meditation pervade everything and enter into all objects of meditation. They have been taught by all perfectly awakened ones of the past, present, and future. Therefore they are called pervasive objects.

Furthermore, these objects pervade the category of vipashyana, pervade the category of shamatha, pervade all phenomena, pervade true reality, and pervade the causal relations among phenomena. Therefore they are called pervasive. That is to say: the image with discernment corresponds to the category of vipashyana. The image without discernment corresponds to the category of shamatha. The limit of things corresponds to all phenomena and true reality. The accomplishment of the purpose corresponds to the causal relations among phenomena.

##### The Dialogue with Elder Revata

As the Blessed One once taught Elder Revata[^revata] this meaning. It was heard that Elder Revata asked the Blessed One:

"Venerable One, those bhikshus who diligently practice insight, who are yogins, and who are able to settle their minds upon an object — upon what objects do they settle their minds? How do they settle their minds upon the objects? At what point is the mind said to be well settled?"

The Buddha addressed Elder Revata: "Excellent, excellent! You are now skillfully able to ask about this matter. Listen carefully now and consider it thoroughly. I shall declare and reveal it to you."

"Revata, those bhikshus who diligently practice insight, who are yogins, and who are able to settle their minds upon an object — whether they delight in purifying conduct, or delight in developing skill, or delight in liberating the mind from all outflows (*asrava*) — they settle their minds upon a matching object. They settle their minds upon a similar object. They settle their minds upon the object without inversion. They are able, within this, not to abandon their dhyana."

**(a) Settling upon a matching object**[^matching_objects]

"How does the bhikshu yogin settle the mind upon a matching object? If the bhikshu is of predominantly greed-conduct, they should settle the mind upon the object of impurity. If of predominantly aversion-conduct, they should settle the mind upon loving-kindness and compassion. If of predominantly delusion-conduct, they should settle the mind upon the nature of conditions and dependent origination. If of predominantly pride-conduct, they should settle the mind upon element differentiation. If of predominantly discursive-thought-conduct, they should settle the mind upon mindfulness of breathing. This is what is meant by settling the mind upon a matching object."

"Revata, furthermore, if the bhikshu is confused about the individual characteristics of formations — confused about the reality of self, sentient being, life-force, born being, sustainer, and person — they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the skandhas.

If confused about causes, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the elements. If confused about conditions, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the sense bases. If confused about impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in dependent origination and in the possible and impossible.

If delighting in freedom from desire for the desire realm, they should settle the mind upon the coarseness of desires and the peacefulness of forms. If delighting in freedom from desire for the form realm, they should settle the mind upon the coarseness of forms and the peacefulness of the formless. If delighting in penetrating and being liberated from the transitory collection (*satkaya*)[^satkaya] everywhere, they should settle the mind upon the truth of suffering, the truth of the origin, the truth of cessation, and the truth of the path. This is what is meant by the bhikshu yogin settling the mind upon a matching object."

**(b) Settling upon a similar object**

"Revata, how does the bhikshu yogin settle the mind upon a similar object? In order to investigate, thoroughly investigate, comprehensively examine, and comprehensively analyze the various knowable objects, the bhikshu — with respect to what was previously seen, heard, sensed, or known, and through the predominant force of seeing, hearing, sensing, and knowing — thinks and discriminates with the attention of the ground of meditative equipoise and gives rise to resolute confidence. Although one cannot observe the original knowable objects as actually present, the image arises in similarity to the original objects. With respect to that object, the image is similar to it — consisting only of cognition, only of seeing, only of correct recollection."

"Furthermore, that bhikshu, from time to time calms the mind, and from time to time diligently practices vipashyana based on superior wisdom.[^adhiprajna] This is what is meant by the bhikshu yogin settling the mind upon a similar object."

**(c) Settling upon the object without inversion**

"Revata, how does the bhikshu yogin settle the mind upon the object without inversion? When the bhikshu yogin settles the mind upon the object, they come to understand the knowable domain as appropriate. They can thoroughly know it truly, without inversion. This is what is meant by the bhikshu yogin settling the mind upon the object without inversion."

**(d) Not abandoning dhyana**

"Revata, how does the bhikshu yogin not abandon dhyana? When the bhikshu yogin practices correctly with respect to the object, they engage in uninterrupted application and earnest application. From time to time they cultivate the sign of calming, the sign of uplifting, and the sign of equanimity.[^three_signs]"

"Through cultivation, habituation, and repeated practice, all heaviness is entirely extinguished. They thereupon realize and directly experience the purification of the basis. With respect to the knowable objects, through direct seeing, they thereupon realize and directly experience the purification of the object. Through freedom from desire, they thereupon realize and directly experience the complete purification of the mind. Through freedom from ignorance, they thereupon realize and directly experience the complete purification of knowledge.[^four_purifications] This is what is meant by the bhikshu yogin not abandoning dhyana."

"Revata, it is for this purpose that the bhikshu settles the mind upon the object. In this way they settle the mind upon the object. Having thus settled the mind upon the object, the mind is called well settled."

##### First Verse and Commentary

The Blessed One further spoke this verse:

> The practitioner practices the signs;
> knowing all true meaning,
> constantly upon the image in dhyana —
> they attain realization and complete purification.

[T30.0428c02]

Here, "the practitioner practices the signs" refers to the practitioner of insight cultivating the signs of calming, uplifting, and equanimity through uninterrupted and earnest practice. "Knowing all true meaning" refers to the limit of things. "Constantly upon the image in dhyana" refers to the image with discernment and the image without discernment. "They attain realization and complete purification" refers to the accomplishment of the purpose.

##### Second Verse and Commentary

The Blessed One herein further spoke this verse:

> Thoroughly knowing the mind's signs,
> able to receive the taste of seclusion,
> in dhyana with constant, earnest mindfulness —
> receiving joy and pleasure, free from attachment.

[T30.0428c10]

Here, "thoroughly knowing the mind's signs" means: the image with discernment and the image without discernment are designated by the name "the mind's signs," and the limit of things is designated by the name "thorough knowing."

"Able to receive the taste of seclusion" refers to the practitioner who practices correctly with respect to the object, delighting in abandonment and delighting in cultivation.

"In dhyana with constant, earnest mindfulness" refers to constantly and diligently cultivating shamatha and vipashyana, practicing with earnest training.

"Receiving joy and pleasure, free from attachment" refers to the accomplishment of the purpose.

It should be known that these pervasive objects are in accord with the pure teaching and in agreement with correct reasoning. This is what is called the pervasive objects of meditation.

### 26.6 Purifying-Conduct Objects: Impurity

[T30.0428c18]

What are the purifying-conduct objects of meditation (objects that counteract specific habitual tendencies)? They are the various objects distinguished as impurity, loving-kindness and compassion, the nature of conditions and dependent origination, element differentiation (analysis into component elements), and mindfulness of breathing.

##### The Impurity Object

What is the impurity object of meditation? In brief, there are six types of impurity:[^six_impurities]

1. Impurity of putrefaction
2. Impurity of suffering
3. Impurity of inferiority
4. Impurity by comparison
5. Impurity of afflictions
6. Impurity of rapid disintegration

**(1) Impurity of putrefaction.** This impurity is based on two things: first, the internal; second, the external.

What is the internal impurity of putrefaction? It is the impurities within one's own body: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, dust and grime, skin, flesh, bones, sinews, veins, heart, gallbladder, liver, lungs, large intestine, small intestine, the upper stomach, the lower stomach, belly, spleen, kidneys, pus, blood, hot phlegm, fat, grease, muscle, marrow, brain membrane, mucus, saliva, tears, sweat, excrement, and urine.[^body_parts] Such and similar things are called the internal impurity of putrefaction.

What is the external impurity of putrefaction? It comprises the livid, the festering, the decayed, the bloated, the gnawed, the blood-red, the scattered, bones, sinew-connected bones, and the skeleton;[^corpse_stages] or what is made of excrement, made of urine, made of saliva, made of mucus, smeared with blood, smeared with pus, or a place of filth. Such and similar things are called the external impurity of putrefaction.

The internal and external impurities of putrefaction are together called the impurity of putrefaction.

**(2) Impurity of suffering.** It is the unpleasant and disturbing feeling — whether of body or mind — that arises conditioned by contact with what is painful. This is called the impurity of suffering.

**(3) Impurity of inferiority.** The most inferior thing, the most inferior realm, is the desire realm. Apart from this, there is no realm lower, more inferior, or more base than this. This is called the impurity of inferiority.

**(4) Impurity by comparison.** Suppose there is something of lesser purity: when compared to something of greater purity, it appears impure. Just as the form realm, when compared to the superior purity of the formless realm, appears impure. And everything up to the summit of existence appears impure when compared to nirvana, the cessation of the transitory collection[^satkaya] (*satkaya*). All such things are called impurity by comparison.

**(5) Impurity of afflictions.** All the fetters, bonds, latent tendencies, secondary afflictions, and entanglements[^five_affliction_types] throughout the three realms are called the impurity of afflictions.

**(6) Impurity of rapid disintegration.** The five appropriated skandhas — the aggregates as grasped by attachment — (*pancopadana-skandha*)[^upadana_skandha] are impermanent, non-enduring, unreliable, and of a nature to change and decay. This is called the impurity of rapid disintegration.

##### The Five Types of Desire and Their Purification

These impurities serve as the objects for purifying greed-conduct. There are five types of desire:[^five_desires]

1. Desire and craving for one's own body
2. Sensual desire and craving for others' bodies
3. Desire and craving for objects
4. Desire and craving for the form realm
5. Desire and craving for the transitory collection (*satkaya*)

In order to cause these five types of desire and craving to be abandoned, eliminated, and rendered inactive, six types of impurity objects are established.

Through the internal impurity of putrefaction as object, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for one's own body. Through the external impurity of putrefaction as object, one purifies the mind from sensual desire and craving for others' bodies.

Sensual craving has four subtypes:[^four_sensual] (1) desire for color, (2) desire for shape, (3) desire for fine touch, and (4) desire for attendance. Through the four external impurity contemplations, the mind is purified from these four corresponding types of sensual craving:

- Contemplating the livid, the festering, the decayed, the bloated, or the gnawed purifies the mind from desire for color.
- Contemplating the blood-red purifies the mind from desire for shape.
- Contemplating bones, sinew-connected bones, or the skeleton purifies the mind from desire for fine touch.
- Contemplating the scattered purifies the mind from desire for attendance.

These four together constitute the purification from sensual craving.

For this reason, all the various external impurities of putrefaction that the Blessed One taught are established in terms of four charnel ground paths. The sutra's charnel ground instructions map onto these external impurity stages. "Seeing a corpse — dead one day, two days, seven days — being devoured by crows, magpies, hungry dogs, vultures, foxes, jackals, and beasts; one then takes up this sign and applies it to one's own body: 'This body too is of such a nature, of such a type, and cannot transcend this condition'": this covers the stages from the livid corpse through the gnawed. "Seeing a corpse stripped of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins": this is the blood-red stage. "Seeing bones, sinew-connected bones, or a skeleton": this covers the third category. And the detailed description of scattered bones — hand bones in one place, foot bones in another, hip bones, knee bones, arm bones, elbow bones, spine, shoulder bones, rib bones, jawbone, teeth, and skull, each in different places; or after one to seven years, white as conch shell or dove-colored; or bones mixed with dust and earth — corresponds to the scattered stage.

Thus, through the external impurity of putrefaction as object, the mind is purified from the four types of sensual craving.

Through the impurity of suffering and the impurity of inferiority as objects, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for objects. Through the impurity by comparison as object, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for the form realm. Through the impurity of afflictions and the impurity of rapid disintegration as objects, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for the transitory collection — from the desire realm up through the summit of existence.

This is called the impurity object for purifying greed-conduct. As stated above, this is a general presentation covering all the impurity objects that collectively serve as the antidote. However, the primary intention in this context is specifically the impurity of putrefaction. The remaining types of impurity also serve as purifying-conduct objects in their own right.

### 26.7 Purifying-Conduct Objects: Loving-Kindness and Compassion

What is the loving-kindness and compassion object of meditation? Whether toward those in the category of friends, the category of enemies, or the category of the neutral, one dwells with an equally established intention of benefit. This gives rise to resolute confidence — a settled conviction within the state of samadhi — capable of bringing about lower, middle, and upper grades of happiness.

In this context, the categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral are the objects. The intention of benefit that can bring about happiness, and the resolute confidence in the state of samadhi, are the subjective aspect. Object and subject, summarized together, are called the loving-kindness and compassion object of meditation.

When the sutra says "with a mind imbued with loving-kindness" (*maitri-sahagatena cetasa*), this indicates the intention of benefit toward the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral.

When it further says "without enmity, without hostility, without harm," this indicates that the intention of benefit has three characteristics. "Without enmity" names the supreme intention of benefit. This freedom from enmity is made clear by two phrases: "without hostility" means not wishing to oppose or contend; "without harm" means not wishing to withhold benefit.[^avyabadha]

When it says "vast and immeasurable," this indicates the capacity to bring about lower, middle, and upper grades of happiness. The happiness of the desire realm is called "vast." The happiness of the first and second dhyanas is called "great." The happiness of the third dhyana is called "immeasurable."[^three_grades]

When it says "with resolute confidence, pervading and dwelling fully," this indicates the resolute confidence in the state of samadhi capable of bringing about happiness.

Furthermore, this resolute confidence is the resolute confidence and attention that accompany the supreme intention to bring about beneficial happiness.

Among the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral, three distinct attitudes are distinguished:

- **Loving-kindness** (*maitri*): With respect to beings who are without suffering and without happiness — wishing equally to give them happiness.
- **Compassion** (*karuna*): With respect to beings who have suffering — wishing equally to remove their suffering.
- **Sympathetic joy** (*mudita*): With respect to beings who have happiness — wishing to rejoice in their happiness.

Beings with suffering are the object of compassion. Beings with happiness are the object of sympathetic joy. This is called the loving-kindness and compassion object of meditation.

If a person of aversion-conduct (one whose dominant tendency is irritability and ill will) practices loving-kindness and compassion toward all sentient beings (*sattva*), their aversion becomes attenuated. This is called the purification of the mind from aversion.

[^four_objects]: The four *alambana-vastu* form the Sravakabhumi's complete meditation-object framework: (1) pervasive objects — the universal structure underlying all practice; (2) purifying-conduct objects — matched to behavioral type (sections 26.6–26.7 and continuing through fascicle 27); (3) skillful objects — developing analytical understanding (section 27.4); (4) affliction-purifying objects — directly removing afflictions through insight (section 27.5).

[^image_discernment]: The "image with discernment" (*savikalpaka-pratibimba*) is the central object of vipashyana practice. It is a mental representation — formed through hearing, instruction, or prior experience — which the practitioner subjects to analytical investigation while in meditative equipoise. The image is not the object itself but a "corresponding" (同分) representation that appears similar to the actual object through the power of meditative resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*). The paired "image without discernment" is the same mental representation rested upon without analysis, i.e., the shamatha object.

[^nine_stages]: The nine stages of mental abiding (*nava-akara-citta-sthiti*) are listed here in abbreviated form. They receive their full, detailed treatment in fascicle 30 (section 30.10), where each stage is defined and the six powers and four types of attention that drive progression through them are explained. The names are: (1) inward abiding, (2) settled abiding, (3) re-established abiding, (4) close abiding, (5) taming, (6) pacifying, (7) fully pacifying, (8) one-pointed attention, (9) equipoise.

[^image_synonyms]: Most of these synonyms are self-descriptive. The notable term is "light-image" (光影): Tsongkhapa renders it as "appearing image," suggesting 光影 may describe the mode of appearance of the mental representation — how it manifests to awareness — rather than a luminous quality per se.

[^limit_twofold]: The "limit of things" (*vastu-paryanta*) has two aspects that together constitute complete knowledge: (1) *yavad-bhavikata* — knowing exhaustively the full range of what exists, classified by the skandhas, elements, sense bases, and four truths (quantitative completeness); (2) *yathavad-bhavikata* — knowing things as they truly are in their suchness, verified by the four types of reasoning (qualitative accuracy).

[^four_reasonings]: The four types of reasoning (*yukti*) are a key Yogacara epistemological framework for establishing the true nature of phenomena: (1) reasoning from dependence (*apeksha-yukti*) — things arise in dependence on conditions; (2) reasoning from function (*karya-karana-yukti*) — things perform specific functions; (3) reasoning from proof (*upapatti-sadhana-yukti*) — claims can be verified through valid evidence; (4) reasoning from the nature of things (*dharmata-yukti*) — the fundamental nature of reality is as it is.

[^transformation]: "Transformation of the basis" (*ashraya-paravrtti*) is a central Yogacara concept. In this Sravakabhumi context, it refers specifically to the cognitive shift from the desire-realm mind to the level of meditative equipoise through elimination of heaviness (*dausthulya* — the subtle physical and mental coarseness that obstructs meditation). At this point the practitioner transcends the mental image and attains direct, non-conceptual perception (*pratyaksha*) of the knowable objects themselves.

[^revata]: Revata (頡隸伐多, Skt. *Revata*; Pali *Revata*) is an elder (*sthavira*) to whom the Buddha addresses a teaching on meditation; this dialogue, embedded within Asanga's treatise, presents the pervasive objects framework as originating from the Buddha's own instruction.

[^matching_objects]: The five-fold matching of conduct type to meditation object is a cornerstone of the Sravakabhumi's practical framework: greed → impurity; aversion → loving-kindness and compassion; delusion → dependent origination; pride → element differentiation; discursive thought → mindfulness of breathing. These five purifying-conduct objects are the antidotes for the five affliction-dominated types defined in section 26.2 (types 3-7) and portrayed in section 26.3. Their detailed treatment begins in section 26.6. The second set of matchings (for those "confused about" various topics) maps to the skillful objects and affliction-purifying objects, completing the full meditation-object framework.

[^three_signs]: The three signs (*nimitta*) — calming (止相), uplifting (舉相), and equanimity (捨相) — are the practitioner's tools for balancing the mind during meditation. When the mind is agitated, one applies the sign of calming; when dull or sluggish, the sign of uplifting; when balanced, the sign of equanimity. This threefold regulation is a fundamental shamatha technique elaborated in detail in fascicle 30 (sections 30.10-30.12).

[^four_purifications]: The four purifications describe the progressive maturation of shamatha-vipashyana practice, corresponding to the accomplishment of the purpose (*krtyanushthana*): (1) purification of the basis (*ashraya-vishuddhi*) — the elimination of heaviness from body and mind; (2) purification of the object (*alambana-vishuddhi*) — direct perception (*pratyaksha*) of the meditation objects themselves, transcending the meditative image; (3) complete purification of the mind (*citta-vishuddhi*) — freedom from desire and attachment; (4) complete purification of knowledge (*jnana-vishuddhi*) — freedom from ignorance.

[^satkaya]: The "transitory collection" (*satkaya*, 薩迦耶) refers to the five skandhas considered as the basis for the mistaken notion of a unified self. Here 薩迦耶事 ("the matter of the transitory collection") refers to the aggregates themselves, as distinct from 薩迦耶見 (*satkaya-drishti*, "the view of the transitory collection"), which is the cognitive error of grasping them as a self.

[^adhiprajna]: The Chinese 依增上慧法毘鉢舍那 means "relying on the dharma of superior prajna, [practicing] vipashyana." The phrase indicates that the practitioner's insight practice is grounded in the higher wisdom that develops through sustained meditation, as distinct from the mere conceptual understanding of initial study.

[^six_impurities]: The six types of impurity (*ashubha*) constitute the detailed meditation object for purifying greed-conduct. Only the first — impurity of putrefaction — is the primary object for cemetery contemplation in the strict sense. The remaining five extend the scope of impurity contemplation to encompass suffering, the inferior nature of the desire realm, the relativity of purity across realms, afflictions themselves, and the impermanence of the aggregates. Together they address all five types of desire described below.

[^body_parts]: This list of internal bodily components parallels the parts of the body (*kaya-gata-sati*) enumerated in the Pali suttas (cf. MN 10, Khuddakapatha 3), traditionally counted as thirty-two (the number varies slightly by source). The Sravakabhumi's count is slightly different (approximately thirty-four items) and includes terms not in the standard Pali list (e.g., "dust and grime," separate stomach terms). The overall scope — solid structures, organs, fluids, and waste — is the same.

[^corpse_stages]: These external impurity stages correspond broadly to the cemetery contemplations (*shitavana-bhavana*) taught in the sutras (cf. MN 10, the Satipatthana Sutta), though the Sravakabhumi's enumeration is distinctive: it includes stages (such as the decayed and the blood-red) not found in the Visuddhimagga's tenfold *ashubha* (Visuddhimagga ch. VI), and omits others present there (cut-up, hacked-and-scattered, worm-infested). The progression — from the freshly dead to the fully disintegrated — systematically dismantles attachment to the body's appearance.

[^upadana_skandha]: The "five appropriated skandhas" (*pancopadana-skandha*, 五取蘊) are the five skandhas specifically as they are objects of grasping (*upadana*). The term emphasizes that ordinary beings relate to the aggregates through attachment, making them a basis for the construction of a false self.

[^five_desires]: The five types of desire represent a Yogacara analysis of the varieties of craving that impurity contemplation addresses: (1) craving for one's own body (attachment to the internal); (2) sensual craving for others' bodies (sexual desire); (3) craving for objects and circumstances (environmental attachment); (4) craving for the form realm (*rupa-dhatu*); (5) craving for the transitory collection (self-grasping, from desire realm through the summit of existence). This fivefold scheme is unique to the Sravakabhumi's presentation.

[^four_sensual]: The four subtypes of sensual craving analyze sexual desire into its component aspects: desire for color/appearance (*varna-raga*), desire for physical shape (*samsthana-raga*), desire for tactile pleasure (*sparsha-raga*), and desire for the attendance/service of another person (*paricaryya-raga*). Each is counteracted by a specific external corpse contemplation.

[^five_affliction_types]: These five terms represent standard Abhidharma categories of mental affliction, together encompassing the full range of afflictive mental activity from dormant seeds to active states: fetters (*samyojana*, 結) bind beings to cyclic existence; bonds (*bandhana*, 縛) are binding ties; latent tendencies (*anushaya*, 隨眠) are dormant afflictive seeds that persist even when not manifest; secondary afflictions (*upaklesha*, 隨煩惱) derive from the root afflictions (e.g., anger, jealousy, concealment); entanglements (*paryavasthana*, 纏) are active afflictive states that ensnare the mind in the moment. This fivefold classification appears in the Abhidharmakoshabhashya (ch. V) and throughout the Yogacarabhumi.

[^avyabadha]: The Chinese 不欲不饒益 is literally "not wishing to not-benefit" — a double negative expressing the active intention to ensure the benefit of others. The rendering "not wishing to withhold benefit" preserves this positive nuance, which is stronger than mere non-harm: the practitioner does not even wish to deprive others of benefit.

[^three_grades]: The Sravakabhumi here parses the sutra formula 廣大無量 (*vipula-apramana*) into three distinct grades of happiness mapped to cosmological levels: "vast" (廣) for the desire realm, "great" (大) for the first and second dhyanas, and "immeasurable" (無量) for the third dhyana. This is a distinctive Yogacara exegesis — the broader commentarial tradition typically treats these terms as describing the boundless scope of brahmavihara practice toward all beings, not as three cosmological grades.
