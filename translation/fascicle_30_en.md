## Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Third Yoga Place, Part One

本地分中聲聞地第十三第三瑜伽處之一

---

### 30.1 Transition and Summary Verse

[T30.0448b25]

The preceding chapters have covered the person types, the objects of meditation, and the full range of practice — fruitful and fruitless alike.[^third_yoga_transition] How the teacher properly establishes the student in all of this, I shall now explain. The summary verse (*uddana*) states:

> Approaching, encouragement, inquiry, investigation;
> Establishment, guarding the resources;
> Seclusion, one-pointed mind;
> Purification of obstacles, cultivating attention.[^uddana_30]

[^third_yoga_transition]: The Third Yoga Place marks the transition from theoretical framework to practical instruction. The Second Yoga Place (fascicles 26-29) established *what* the practitioner cultivates: person types (who practices), meditation objects (what is practiced), the thirty-seven factors (the path structure), and the four fruits (the goal). The Third Yoga Place (fascicles 30-32) now addresses *how* one actually cultivates — beginning with the teacher-student relationship, proceeding through the nine stages of mental abiding and vipashyana, and culminating in the beginner's practical guide.

[^uddana_30]: This *uddana* outlines the ten topics of the Third Yoga Place. The first four (approaching the teacher, encouragement, inquiry, investigation) describe the preliminary encounter between student and teacher. Item five, "establishment," is the overarching framework, comprising five specific domains: (6) guarding samadhi resources, (7) seclusion, (8) one-pointed mind, (9) purification of obstacles, and (10) cultivating attention. Topics 1–8 are covered in this fascicle; topics 9–10 continue in Fascicle 31.

---

### 30.2 Approaching the Teacher

[T30.0448c01]

A beginner who genuinely seeks their own benefit — someone newly undertaking practice — should first settle the mind in four areas of mindfulness before approaching a teacher skilled in yoga. That teacher may be their preceptor (*upadhyaya*), their discipline master (*acharya*), or another respected senior.

What are the four areas? (1) Coming only to understand, with no intention to dispute or contradict. (2) Approaching with deep respect and without arrogance. (3) Keeping attention on what is excellent and wholesome, not on demonstrating one's own capacities. (4) Motivated purely by the wish to cultivate virtue for oneself and others — not by desire for support, honor, or renown.

Having arrived at the teacher's dwelling with this mindfulness, one first asks permission and inquires about a suitable time. Then, composedly, one makes one's request. At the moment of asking, one bares the right shoulder, places the right knee on the ground — or takes a lower seat and bows — and, joining the palms with deep reverence, gaze lowered and voice gentle, speaks: "I wish to receive training in this yogic practice. Out of compassion, please explain it to me."

---

### 30.3 The Teacher's Encouragement

[T30.0448c13]

Having been asked in this way, the yoga teacher — wishing to establish the beginner's yogic attention — should respond with compassionate, gentle words of praise, encouragement, congratulation, and comfort, and should extol the merits of cultivation and abandonment, saying:

"Well done, well done, worthy one!

"Among beings who fall into heedlessness and delight in heedlessness, who sink into their objects and cling to them — you alone are free from heedlessness and delight in the practice of renunciation.

"Among beings long imprisoned in the perilous dungeons of manifold sorrow and suffering — you alone seek liberation from these prisons.

"Among beings constantly bound by the chains and fetters of desire, aversion, and delusion — you alone seek to break these bonds.

"Among beings who have entered the perilous wilderness paths of samsara — you alone seek passage beyond this wilderness.

"Among beings impoverished in roots of virtue — you alone seek to attain an abundance of wholesome roots.

"Among beings who have fallen prey to the great terrors of those bandit-enemies, the afflictions — you alone seek the ultimate safety and lasting happiness of nirvana.

"Among beings consumed by the grave illness of afflictions — you alone seek the supreme freedom from illness, the lasting happiness of nirvana.

"Among beings swept away and drowning in the four floods — you alone seek to cross beyond them.

"Among beings who have entered the vast darkness of ignorance — you alone seek the great light of wisdom.[^encouragement_similes]

"Elder, know this: if you practice with such diligence, you will deserve to be called one who honors the faithful offerings of the people, who carries out the Tathagata's teaching without abandoning it, who accomplishes dhyana and deepens vipashyana, who delights in solitude — a true companion in the Dharma, diligent in the yoga of one's own benefit, who brings no disgrace upon the wise companions in pure conduct.

"You have come to ask out of a wish to practice rightly, for your own benefit and for others'. You come with compassion for the world — for gods, humans, asuras, and all beings — so that they may attain benefit and happiness."

---

### 30.4 The Four Inquiry Points

[T30.0449a10]

Having thus praised, encouraged, congratulated, and comforted the student with gentle words, and having extolled the merits of cultivation and abandonment, the teacher should then examine the student on four points of inquiry:

"Elder, have you exclusively taken refuge in the Buddha, the Dharma, and the Sangha — and not in non-Buddhist teachers, their wrong teachings, or their assemblies of disciples?"[^exclusive_refuge]

"From the very beginning of your pure conduct, have you purified your shila (ethical discipline) and established right view?"

"Have you received and studied, whether in brief or in detail, the dharma of the noble truths — their general presentation and their detailed analysis?"

"Have you gone forth with genuine faith in nirvana, with the aim of realizing cessation?"

---

### 30.5 The Four-Fold Investigation

[T30.0449a17]

If the student answers affirmatively to all four points, the teacher should then properly investigate four domains using four methods.

**The Four Domains**

What are the four domains? (1) One should investigate the student's aspiration (*pranidhana*). (2) One should investigate their spiritual lineage (*gotra*).[^gotra_investigation] (3) One should investigate their faculties (*indriya*). (4) One should investigate their disposition (*carita*).

**The Four Methods**

What are the four methods? (1) Investigation through direct questioning. (2) Investigation through discourse. (3) Investigation through observed conduct. (4) Investigation through direct knowledge of others' minds (*paracitta-jnana*) — available only to teachers who have cultivated this attainment.

**Investigating Aspiration Through Questioning**

How does one investigate aspiration through direct questioning? One asks: "Elder, toward what have you directed your aspiration — the shravaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, or the vehicle of supreme perfect awakening (*anuttara-samyak-sambodhi*)?" The student answers according to their own aspiration.

**Investigating Lineage, Faculties, and Disposition Through Questioning**

How does one investigate lineage, faculties, and disposition through direct questioning? One asks: "Elder, can you examine yourself honestly? Consider three things. First, your innate lineage — whether it inclines toward the shravaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, or the great vehicle. Second, your faculties — whether they are dull, middling, or sharp. Third, your disposition — whether it is rooted in greed, aversion, or one of the other conduct types."

If the student is discerning, they can recognize these distinctions within themselves, apprehend the characteristics, and answer accordingly. If they are dull by nature, without the self-knowledge to discern these distinctions, the questioning yields nothing — they simply cannot answer.

**Investigating Through Discourse**

From this point, the teacher should investigate lineage, faculties, and disposition through discourse.

*Lineage.* Before the student, using clear, logically coherent, varied, elegant, and easily understood words, the teacher first expounds the teaching of the shravaka vehicle. If the student possesses the shravaka lineage, they respond with the utmost joy and elation, generating deep faith and conviction. Those with the pratyekabuddha or great-vehicle lineage show no such response. The teacher then expounds the pratyekabuddha vehicle's teaching, and finally the great vehicle's. In each case, the student's strongest response of joy and faith reveals their true lineage.[^three_vehicles]

*Faculties.* Those with dull faculties, even hearing coarse and straightforward teachings, can comprehend the dharma's meaning only through strenuous reflection. Those with sharp faculties quickly penetrate even profound and subtle teachings. Those with middling faculties fall between these extremes.

*Disposition.* Each of the five conduct types responds to the dharma in a distinctive way:

Those of greed-conduct (*raga-carita*), when they hear teachings of what is pure and beautiful, generate the utmost pure faith and loving delight, entering into its meaning. Their body-hair stands on end, tears stream from their eyes. Outwardly their body takes on a moistened, supple appearance; inwardly their mind becomes soft and joyful.

Those of aversion-conduct (*dvesha-carita*) respond in the opposite way: where the greed-conduct type opens and softens, they stiffen and withdraw. Praise and beauty provoke suspicion; they hold themselves apart.

Those of delusion-conduct (*moha-carita*) are startled and frightened when they hear teachings aimed squarely at nirvana and dispassion. Like students with dull faculties, they struggle to comprehend and absorb what they hear.

Those of pride-conduct (*mana-carita*), when they hear the dharma, do not show great respect. They incline their ears and are willing to listen, but they do not deeply settle into seeking understanding or following the teaching with a receptive mind. Even when the teacher uses skillful means to bring them around, they give no open acknowledgment.

Those of discursive-thought conduct (*vitarka-carita*), although they gather their ears to listen, have scattered minds and receive what they hear poorly. Whatever they comprehend is neither firm nor stable — they lose it as quickly as they receive it, and repeatedly ask the same questions again.[^disposition_signs]

**Investigating Through Observed Conduct**

How does one investigate through observed conduct? The behavioral characteristics of persons with the shravaka lineage and the various conduct-types, as described above, constitute their observable conduct. Through these outward signs, one properly investigates lineage, faculties, and disposition as appropriate.

**Investigating Through Knowledge of Others' Minds**

How does one investigate lineage, faculties, and disposition through knowledge of others' minds? When a yoga teacher accomplished in practice has attained the knowledge that discerns others' minds, through this knowledge they directly and accurately know the student's lineage, faculties, and disposition.

### 30.6 The Five Establishments — Overview

[T30.0449c15]

Having properly investigated the four domains by means of the four methods, the teacher then establishes the student in five areas, as appropriate. What are the five? (1) Guarding the samadhi resources. (2) Seclusion. (3) One-pointed mind. (4) Purification of obstacles. (5) Cultivating attention.

---

### 30.7 The First Establishment: Guarding the Samadhi Resources

[T30.0449c15]

What is guarding the samadhi resources (*samadhi-sambhara*)? One who has achieved the discipline of moral conduct (*shila-samvara*) dwells in non-heedlessness to prevent regression. As the Buddha has instructed and permitted, one perfects the points of training within the aggregate of discipline, practicing diligently and never slackening. In this way, one does not regress from the path of training in moral conduct already attained, and one also attains superior paths of training not yet realized.

The same applies to sense restraint (*indriya-samvara*), moderation in food, wakefulness through the first and last watches of the night, alertness (*samprajanya*) in one's activities, and all the shramana adornments (*shramana-alamkara*). Whatever wholesome dharmas of this kind have already been obtained, one guards carefully against regression. For those not yet attained, one generates aspiration and zealous enthusiasm, practicing with constant diligence to bring them to swift fulfillment — precisely as taught, without addition or omission. This is called guarding the samadhi resources. (Cf. Visuddhimagga I–II for a parallel treatment of preparatory purification.)

Having thus guarded against factors conducive to regression and cultivated factors conducive to progress, one delights in seclusion.

---

### 30.8 The Second Establishment: Seclusion

[T30.0450a03]

What is seclusion? It has three aspects: perfection of place, perfection of deportment, and perfection of seclusion proper.

**Perfection of Place**

What is perfection of place? Whether a forest dwelling (*aranyaka*), the base of a tree, or an empty room[^three_dwelling_types] — mountain caves, straw-pile shelters, and the like are called "empty rooms"; the depths of a great forest are called "tree-base"; remote burial grounds and border regions for sitting and lying are called "forest dwelling." These are collectively called the "place."

Perfection of place has five qualities:

(1) A place beautiful in form and pleasing to all — clean, free from impurity, graced with gardens, groves, and pools; level ground, clear of thorns and debris. Seeing such a place, the mind is purified. Dwelling there, one takes up the practice of relinquishment with joy, the mind gladdened and sustained in samadhi. This is the first perfection of place.

(2) A place where by day there is little commotion, and by night little noise — free from mosquitoes and gnats, sheltered from wind and harsh sun, with no snakes, scorpions, or other hazardous creatures. This is the second.

(3) A place free from dangerous lions, tigers, leopards, jackals, and wolves; free from enemies and bandits, humans and non-humans, and all causes for alarm. In such a place one dwells with body and mind at ease, entirely free from anxiety. This is the third.

(4) A place where the necessities of life are easily obtained — where seeking robes and other requisites is not excessively difficult, and food and drink are not lacking. This is the fourth.

(5) A place attended by spiritual friends (*kalyanamitra*) and inhabited by wise companions in the pure life. What has not yet been understood, they can properly explain; what has already been understood, they make still clearer. Through skillful and diligent instruction, they enable one's wisdom and insight to be swiftly purified, penetrating the profound meaning. This is the fifth perfection of place.

**Perfection of Deportment**

What is perfection of deportment? During the day, one alternates between walking meditation and sitting in meditation. During the first watch of the night, one does the same. During the middle watch, one sleeps lying on the right side. During the last watch, one rises quickly to resume walking and sitting meditation.

Using the bedding permitted by the Buddha — large and small rope-beds, cushions of grass and leaves, and the like — one takes the cross-legged posture as described in the traditional accounts.

Why sit cross-legged? One correctly observes five reasons: (1) Because the body is compact, pliancy (*prashrabdhi*) arises swiftly — this posture is supreme for generating pliancy. (2) This seated posture can be sustained for a long time without quickly producing bodily fatigue. (3) This is a distinctive dharma practice, not found among non-Buddhist and other traditions. (4) This posture is dignified and elegant, inspiring faith and respect in those who see it. (5) This posture is praised by all Buddhas and noble ones. Observing these five reasons, one should sit cross-legged.

"Straightening the body" means making the body upright and erect. "Setting the intention aright" means making the mind free from deceit and dishonesty — flexible and upright. Because the body is held erect, the mind is not overrun by torpor and drowsiness. Because the mind is free from deceit, it is not overrun by distraction toward external objects.

**Counter-Mindfulness**

[T30.0450b15]

What is "establishing counter-mindfulness"? Mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) is called counter-mindfulness (背念)[^counter_mindfulness] because it opposes and dispels all unwholesome qualities. Mindfulness that takes the sign of samadhi as its object is likewise called counter-mindfulness because it opposes and dispels all objects of the non-concentrated state.

This constitutes perfection of deportment.

**Perfection of Seclusion Proper**

What is perfection of seclusion proper? It has two aspects: physical seclusion and mental seclusion.

Physical seclusion means not mingling with householders or monastic communities but dwelling alone, without companions.

Mental seclusion means abandoning all afflicted and indeterminate mental engagement, and cultivating wholesome attention that leads toward samadhi and its prerequisites — the attention of the preparatory stage.

Thus, perfection of place, perfection of deportment, physical seclusion, and mental seclusion are gathered together under the single heading of "seclusion."

### 30.9 The Third Establishment: One-Pointed Mind

[T30.0450b27]

What is mental one-pointedness? Samadhi — also called wholesome mental one-pointedness — is the mind sustained within a consistent field of knowable objects: mindfulness returning again and again to that same field, flowing continuously, free from affliction, conjoined with ease.[^samadhi_def]

What is "repeated mindfulness"? Through hearing and retaining the correct dharma, and through the dominant force of the teacher's instructions and guidance, the characteristics of the concentrated state become present. Taking these as one's object, one's mindfulness turns and abides with ease, free from fault — this is repeated mindfulness.

What is a "homogeneous object-field"? The objects of the concentrated state are not one fixed thing but many and varied. Yet taking these as one's object causes the mind to proceed correctly, and this is called samadhi. Such objects are called "homogeneous" because they belong to the same class of knowable things — sharing a similar nature.

Furthermore, this mindfulness moves upon its object without scattering, without gaps, without interruption — earnest and unbroken, conjoined with ease: this is what is meant by "flowing, conjoined with ease." It also operates without any afflicted quality, in a state of deep stability, conjoined with the ease of a well-worn path: this is "without fault, conjoined with ease."

This, then, is the meaning of samadhi as mental one-pointedness: sustained, unbroken, ease-conjoined attention within a consistent field.

This mental one-pointedness belongs either to the shamatha (calm abiding) class or to the vipashyana (insight) class.[^samadhi_dual_scope] Mental one-pointedness within the nine stages of mental abiding (*nava-akara-citta-sthiti*) belongs to the shamatha class. Mental one-pointedness within the four modes of wisdom-practice belongs to the vipashyana class.

[^samadhi_def]: This definition of samadhi is one of the most important passages in the Sravakabhumi, cited extensively in Tibetan commentarial literature. Tsongkhapa quotes it in *The Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path* (Vol. 3) as the authoritative definition of samadhi. The key elements are: (1) repeated mindfulness — not a single act but sustained application; (2) homogeneous object — the mind stays within a consistent field; (3) flowing — continuity without gaps; (4) without fault — free from affliction; (5) conjoined with ease — the mark of genuine concentration versus forced attention.

### 30.10 The Nine Stages of Mental Abiding

[T30.0450c18]

What are the nine stages of mental abiding? In sequence, a bhikshu first places the mind inward and settles it; then re-establishes and holds it close; tames, pacifies, and fully pacifies it; brings it to one-pointed attention; and finally achieves equipoise. These are the nine stages of mental abiding.[^nine_stages]

**(1) Inward abiding.** One withdraws the mind from all external objects, binds it internally, and prevents it from scattering. This is the very first binding of the mind — holding it inward, not allowing it to scatter outward. Hence "inward abiding."

**(2) Settled abiding.** The mind initially bound in this way is by nature coarse and agitated; one cannot yet make it settle evenly throughout. Therefore, within this same object-field, through sustained and purifying methods, one subdues and refines the mind, gathering it to abide. Hence "settled abiding."

**(3) Re-established abiding.** Although the mind has been placed inward and settled, one may lose mindfulness and scatter toward external objects. One then re-collects the mind and re-establishes it upon the internal object. Hence "re-established abiding."

**(4) Close abiding.** One cultivates increasingly close familiarity with mindful placement. Through this mindfulness, one repeatedly directs attention inward, not allowing the mind to drift far toward external objects. Hence "close abiding."

**(5) Taming.** Various signs cause the mind to scatter: signs of form, sound, smell, taste, and touch, as well as signs of desire, aversion, delusion, male and female, and so forth. One first takes up the perception of danger in these signs. Through the dominant force of this perception, one restrains the mind from flowing toward them. Hence "taming."

**(6) Pacifying.** Various unwholesome thoughts — of desire, ill-will, and harmfulness — together with secondary afflictions such as the hindrance of sensual desire, disturb and agitate the mind. One first takes up the perception of danger in these. Through the dominant force of this perception, one stills the mind and prevents it from flowing toward them. Hence "pacifying."

**(7) Fully pacifying.** When, through loss of mindfulness, either unwholesome thoughts or secondary afflictions briefly arise, one does not tolerate them for even a moment but immediately cuts them off, drives them out, and purges them. Hence "fully pacifying."

**(8) One-pointed attention.** With effort and exertion, one's samadhi continues without gaps and without interruption. Hence "one-pointed attention."

**(9) Equipoise.** Through repeated cultivation — practicing again and again, training extensively — one attains an effortless, spontaneous flow. No longer requiring effort or exertion, the mind's samadhi continues of its own accord, unbroken and free from distraction. Hence "equipoise."

[^nine_stages]: These nine stages form the foundational framework of shamatha practice in the Yogacara tradition. The Sanskrit names from the tradition are: (1) *citta-sthapana*, (2) *samsthapana*, (3) *avasthapana*, (4) *upasthapana*, (5) *damana*, (6) *shamana*, (7) *vyupashamana*, (8) *ekotikarana*, (9) *samadhana*. Tsongkhapa's extensive commentary on this passage in *The Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path* (Vol. 3, ch. 2–5) became the standard presentation of the shamatha path in Tibetan Buddhism. Kamalashila's *Bhavanakrama* I–III (see *Stages of Meditation*) provides an important early parallel treatment.

### 30.11 The Six Powers

These nine stages are accomplished through six powers, each supporting the next: (1) the power of hearing, (2) the power of reflection, (3) the power of mindfulness, (4) the power of alertness, (5) the power of diligence, and (6) the power of habituation.

Initially, through repeated hearing and reflection, one places the mind on the internal object, then through sustained and purifying methods settles it evenly throughout. Having thus bound the mind inward, through the power of mindfulness one repeatedly directs attention, gathering the mind and preventing scattering — this is re-established abiding and close abiding. From this point forward, through the power of alertness, one tempers the mind, preventing it from flowing toward signs, unwholesome thoughts, and secondary afflictions — taming and pacifying. Through the power of diligence, when unwholesome thoughts or secondary afflictions briefly arise, one does not tolerate them but immediately cuts them off, drives them out, and purges them — this is full pacification. With this same diligence sustained, one achieves one-pointed attention. Through the power of habituation, equipoise is brought to completion.

---

### 30.12 The Four Types of Attention

Within these same nine stages of mental abiding, there are also four types of attention: (1) forceful attention, (2) interrupted attention, (3) uninterrupted attention, and (4) effortless attention.

Forceful attention accompanies the first two stages — inward abiding and settled abiding. Interrupted attention spans the middle five, from re-established abiding through fully pacifying. Uninterrupted attention characterizes one-pointed attention. Effortless attention is present during equipoise.

These four types of attention, as they operate within the nine stages, belong to the shamatha class. Furthermore, one who has attained inner shamatha and then diligently cultivates vipashyana employs these same four types of attention. Therefore they also belong to the vipashyana class.

### 30.13 The Four Types of Vipashyana

[T30.0451b13]

What are the four types of vipashyana? Relying on inner shamatha, a bhikshu engages in correct discernment, thorough discernment, comprehensive investigation, and comprehensive examination of all dharmas. These are the four types of vipashyana.[^four_vipashyana]

What is correct discernment? Regarding purifying-conduct objects, skillful objects, or affliction-purifying objects, one correctly discerns the full extent of what exists (*yavad-bhavikata*).

What is thorough discernment? Regarding those same objects, one thoroughly discerns the nature of things as they are (*yathavad-bhavikata*).

What is comprehensive investigation? One brings wise attention — here still operating with conceptual thought — to these same objects, taking up their characteristics for comprehensive investigation.

What is comprehensive examination? Regarding those same objects, one scrutinizes and probes them carefully, examining them thoroughly.

[^four_vipashyana]: Chinese: 正思擇, 最極思擇, 周遍尋思, 周遍伺察. The first two operations (discernment) correspond to the two fundamental modes of knowing: the full extent of what exists (盡所有性) — knowing the complete range of phenomena — and the nature of things as they are (如所有性) — knowing their ultimate reality. The latter two operations (investigation and examination) correspond to the Abhidharma pair of investigation (*vitarka*) and examination (*vicara*).

### 30.14 The Three Doors and Six Aspects of Vipashyana

Furthermore, this vipashyana has additional distinctions through three doors and six aspects of its objects.

**The Three Doors**

What are the three doors of vipashyana? (1) Vipashyana following appearances, (2) vipashyana following investigation, and (3) vipashyana following examination.

*Vipashyana following appearances* works as follows: regarding the teachings one has heard and retained, or the instructions and guidance received, one enters meditative equipoise with wise attention and briefly contemplates — without yet thinking through, evaluating, analyzing, or examining in detail. This is vipashyana that follows appearances alone.

When one then thinks through and analyzes the material, this becomes *vipashyana following investigation*.

When, having analyzed, one reviews the established conclusions with careful, thorough scrutiny, this becomes *vipashyana following examination*.

These are the three doors of vipashyana.

**The Six Aspects**

What is vipashyana through the six aspects of its objects? During investigation, one investigates six things: (1) meaning, (2) things, (3) characteristics, (4) categories, (5) time, and (6) reasoning. Having investigated, one also examines them thoroughly.

**(1) Meaning.** What do these words signify? One considers how each term carries its particular meaning.

**(2) Things.** One turns attention to the domain of things themselves — internal phenomena and external.

**(3) Characteristics.** Each dharma has two characteristics: its own-characteristic (*svalakshana*), which is specific to it, and its common characteristic (*samanya-lakshana*), which it holds in common with others.

**(4) Categories.** All dharmas fall into two categories: dark and bright. One examines the faults and dangers of the dark, and the virtues and benefits of the bright.

**(5) Time.** One considers each matter in its temporal dimension — whether it belongs to the past, present, or future.

**(6) Reasoning.** One investigates four types of reasoning:[^four_reasonings]

*(a) Reasoning from dependence.* One investigates the conventional as conventional, the ultimate as ultimate, and causes and conditions as causes and conditions.

*(b) Reasoning from function.* One investigates the specific function of each dharma — what each one actually does.

*(c) Reasoning from proof.* One investigates three means of valid cognition: scriptural authority (*agama-pramana*), inference (*anumana-pramana*), and direct realization (*pratyaksha-pramana*). One asks: "Is there scriptural authority for this meaning? Can it be directly realized? Can it be inferred?"

*(d) Reasoning from the nature of things.* Some dharmas have an inconceivable, self-abiding nature that simply is what it is. Here the appropriate response is not further analysis or conceptual elaboration but faith and understanding.

**The Three Doors and Six Aspects Together**

The vipashyana of the six aspects, together with the three doors described above, encompasses all forms of vipashyana in brief.

Why are these six aspects established? Because they map onto three distinct modes of understanding. Investigating meaning opens up comprehension of what words convey. Investigating things and their own-characteristics reveals the limits of things as they are. Investigating common characteristics, categories, time, and reasoning brings one to an understanding of reality itself.

For the practitioner of yoga, these two — knowing what words mean and knowing what things are, in their full extent and as they truly are — exhaust the entire field of knowable objects.

[^four_reasonings]: The four types of reasoning (四種道理) are fundamental to Yogacara epistemology. The reasoning from dependence (觀待道理) examines how phenomena function in relation to their conditions. The reasoning from function (作用道理) examines what each dharma does. The reasoning from proof (證成道理) validates through three means of knowledge. The reasoning from the nature of things (法爾道理) marks the boundary of analysis — where conceptual investigation yields to faith in the nature of dharmas.

### 30.15 Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Impurity

[T30.0452a11]

How does one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of impurity (*ashubha*) practice the six-aspect vipashyana?

**Meaning.** Based on the correct teachings concerning impurity — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — one enters meditative equipoise with wise attention and understands their meaning: impurity is truly impure, deeply repulsive, its nature decaying and putrid, rank with the stench of corruption. Through these and other aspects, one understands the meaning of the correct teachings on impurity previously heard.

**Things.** Having understood the meaning, one observes that impure things are established in two divisions: internal and external.

**Own-Characteristic — Internal Impurity.** Regarding the internal body, one arouses the conviction of its impure, decaying contents. One knows that within the body there are hair, body-hair, and so forth through to brain-membrane and urine. One further classifies these impure substances by element: the solids from hair and body-hair through to feces belong to earth; the fluids from tears and filth through to urine belong to water. This is investigating the own-characteristic of internal impurity.

**Own-Characteristic — External Impurity.** Regarding external impure things, one develops conviction through the stages of decomposition, beginning with lividity.

First, one takes up the sign of a corpse — whether seen personally, heard about from others, or contemplated through mental imagery. This corpse may be male, female, or of indeterminate sex; a dear one, an enemy, or a stranger; of low, middle, or high status; young, middle-aged, or old.

Having taken up the sign, one contemplates the stages of decay:

(1) *Lividity.* A corpse dead one day — the blood has dried but putrefaction has not begun.

(2) *Putrefaction.* Dead two days — putrefaction has begun but worms have not yet appeared.

(3) *Decay and bloating.* Dead seven days — worms have appeared, the body has broken down, and bloating has set in.

(4) *Being devoured.* The corpse is consumed by foxes, wolves, owls, eagles, vultures, crows, magpies, and hungry dogs.

(5) *Stripped to sinew and bone.* After being devoured, skin, flesh, and blood are gone; only sinew binds the bones.

(6) *Scattering.* The devoured limbs lie scattered in various places — some with flesh, some without, some with traces of flesh remaining.

(7) *Bare bones.* The bones of the corpse lie scattered: hand-bones in one place, foot-bones in another — knees, thighs, hips, shoulders, arms, spine, jaw, teeth, and skull, all separated.

(8) *Linked skeleton.* One contemplates the skeletal remains still connected, not yet scattered. Taking only the gross appearance without detailed attention to the joints and articulations, one develops the conviction of a linked skeleton.

(9) *Bone-chain.* When one attends in detail to the joints and their articulations, one develops the conviction of a bone-chain.

There are two types of linked skeleton: the trunk chain — running from the sacrum and spine up to the skull — and the limb chain — the linked bones of arms, shoulders, thighs, and shins. The trunk chain is called simply "linked skeleton"; the limb chain is called "bone-chain."

There are also two ways of taking up the skeleton-sign: from depictions — paintings, carvings in wood or stone, clay models — or from actual skeletal remains. Contemplating a depicted skeleton produces the conviction of a "linked" representation. Contemplating an actual skeleton produces the conviction of a bone-chain.

Furthermore, these external forms undergo three types of deterioration: (1) natural deterioration — from lividity through bloating; (2) deterioration caused by others — from being devoured through scattering; (3) combined deterioration — bare bones, linked skeleton, and bone-chain. Truly knowing external impurity in this way is investigating the own-characteristic of external impurity.

**Common Characteristic.** One's own body, with its outwardly clean appearance, has not yet deteriorated. The external corpse has already deteriorated. Yet by recognizing that one's own body shares the same impure nature as the corpse, one can know: "My seemingly clean form carries this same nature." The bodies of all beings, however presentable, carry it too — just as the deteriorated forms before one. This is investigating the common characteristic of impurity.

**Categories.** One reflects: "Not truly recognizing the impure nature beneath apparently clean forms, and generating desire toward them — this is inversion (*viparyasa*). It belongs to the dark category: conflicted, painful, laden with suffering. From it arise future birth, aging, illness, death, grief, and all the rest of samsaric disturbance. If I truly observe the impure nature underlying these outwardly clean forms, this is non-inversion — belonging to the bright category: free from conflict, free from suffering and harm. Through this, future birth, aging, and all disturbance are extinguished. The dark I should not tolerate but should cut off and drive away. The bright, if not yet arisen, I should generate; once arisen, I should maintain and develop."

**Time.** One reflects: "My own apparently clean forms exist in the present. External impure forms also exist in the present. Forms that were clean in the past — though clean then — are now progressively impure in these various ways. My own seemingly clean forms in the present — though clean now — will inevitably become impure in the future, just like the external impure forms before me. My body, in past, future, and present, was, will be, and is of this same impure nature — it never transcends it."

**Reasoning.** One investigates impurity through the four types of reasoning:

*Reasoning from dependence.* "Neither internally nor externally is there any self or being who can be called pure or impure. There are only forms, only bodily shapes. Within these, purely by convention and designation, we speak of 'pure' or 'impure.' As it is said: when life, warmth, and consciousness abandon the body, it lies bereft of its sustaining forces — without thought, like a log. After death, the body progressively deteriorates through knowable stages, from lividity through to bone-chain. My present body was brought into being by prior karma and afflictions, born from the union of my parents' impure substances, sustained and grown by food. So even now, though it temporarily presents clean forms, impurities fill it at every moment, in every part."

*Reasoning from function.* "By thoroughly and extensively cultivating this contemplation of impurity, one can eradicate sensual desire; this is certain."

*Reasoning from proof.* "The Blessed One has taught: 'If one thoroughly cultivates the contemplation of impurity, one can eradicate sensual desire.' This is scriptural authority. I myself can directly perceive that when I attend to and contemplate impurity, the fetter of sensual desire does not arise when absent, and when present, it is driven away — this is direct realization. Inference is also available: how could attending to the antidote cause the very affliction it counters to arise?"

*Reasoning from the nature of things.* "That impurity practice eradicates sensual desire is simply the nature of dharmas — established, inconceivable, not subject to further proof. Here analysis has reached its edge. What remains is confident acceptance."

This is the six-aspect vipashyana for one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of impurity.

### 30.16 Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Loving-Kindness

[T30.0453a29]

How does one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of loving-kindness (*maitri*) practice the six-aspect vipashyana?

**Meaning.** Based on the correct teachings concerning loving-kindness — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — and moved by the intention to benefit and bring happiness, one directs attention toward giving happiness to all sentient beings, generating resolute confidence. This is the defining mark of loving-kindness. Understanding this meaning is investigating the meaning of loving-kindness.

**Things.** Having understood the meaning, one further discerns: "These are dear ones, these are enemies, these are neutral ones." Since all three belong to the continuums of others, one regards them as external things. Alternatively, dear ones may be considered internal, while enemies and neutral ones are external.

**Own-Characteristic.** One further discerns these three categories. Those whose orientation is beneficial are called dear ones, those whose orientation is harmful are called enemies, and those whose orientation is neither are called neutral. For beings in these three categories who neither suffer nor possess happiness but wish to obtain it, three kinds of happiness-seeking can be distinguished: (1) seeking the pleasures of the desire realm, (2) seeking the joyful and delightful pleasures of the form realm, and (3) seeking the pleasures that lie beyond joy. If beings lack desire-realm pleasures, one wishes that they may all obtain blameless pleasures. If they lack the higher pleasures — those of joy or those beyond joy — one extends the same wish.

**Common Characteristic.** One further reflects on dear ones, enemies, and neutral ones together: "Toward all of them, I should cultivate an equal mind — seeing them as alike and treating them without distinction."[^equality_argument]

Why? Giving happiness to dear ones is not difficult. Giving happiness to neutral ones is also not very difficult. But giving happiness to enemies — that is extremely difficult. Yet even toward enemies I should aspire to give happiness. How much more, then, should I give it to dear ones and neutral ones!

Why should this be so? When examined closely, there is no insulter and no one insulted, no angry one and no object of anger, no striker and no one struck — nothing but sounds and names. The category "enemy" is a conventional designation, not a given reality.

Moreover, this body of mine, however it has arisen, is material, coarse and heavy, constructed from the four great elements. Wherever it abides, it is pressed upon by contacts of two kinds: the contact of sounds, and the contact of hands, feet, clods, knives, sticks, and the like. Both the body and these contacts are impermanent. The one who causes harm is also impermanent.

Furthermore, all sentient beings without exception are subject to birth, aging, sickness, death, and the rest — their fundamental nature is suffering. I should not heap further suffering upon beings who are inherently suffering but should wish them happiness instead.

I should not fail to serve as a spiritual friend (*kalyanamitra*) even to enemies, nor fail to embrace all sentient beings as my own.

The Blessed One has said: "I do not see any class of being who, from beginningless time, through the long course of birth, death, and wandering, has not at some point been one's father or mother, brother or sister, one's acharya or upadhyaya, or another honored person." For this reason, all enemies are without exception one's own dear ones.

Furthermore, "enemy" and "dear one" have no fixed reality. Dear ones at other times become enemies; enemies at other times become dear ones. Nothing about these designations is certain.

For all these reasons, I should develop toward all sentient beings an equal mind and an equal vision, with a consistent intention to benefit and bring happiness, and the resolve to give them happiness.

**Categories.** One reflects: "If toward those who are harmful I generate anger, this is inversion — belonging to the dark category: conflicted, painful, and harmful," and so forth as described above. "If toward them I do not generate anger, this is non-inversion — belonging to the bright category: free from conflict, free from suffering and harm," likewise as above.

**Time.** One reflects: "Beings of the past who sought happiness have all passed away — how could I give them happiness now? For beings of the present, I wish them happiness at all times throughout the entire future."

**Reasoning.** One investigates loving-kindness through the four types of reasoning:

*Reasoning from dependence.* "In this, there is no self, no sentient being, no seeker of happiness, no giver of happiness. There are only skandhas, only formations. Within these, purely by conventional designation, we speak of 'this one seeks happiness' and 'this one gives happiness.' Moreover, those formations arise from karma, afflictions, and other dependent conditions."

*Reasoning from function.* "If one thoroughly cultivates, practices, and extensively develops loving-kindness, one can eradicate anger."

*Reasoning from proof.* "This is confirmed by scripture, by direct experience in meditation, and by logical inference."

*Reasoning from the nature of things.* "That loving-kindness eradicates anger belongs to the established, inconceivable, abiding nature of dharmas. This is not a matter for further analysis but for resolute confidence."

This is the six-aspect vipashyana for one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of loving-kindness.

[^equality_argument]: This is the standard maitri-extension argument in which the mind is gradually opened to include all beings through progressive impartiality. The "beginningless samsara" argument — that every being has at some point been one's parent or teacher — appears in the Samyutta Nikaya (SN 15.14–19) and is a standard support for universal loving-kindness.

[^gotra_investigation]: The investigation of spiritual lineage (*gotra*, 種姓) is a distinctively Yogacara concern. *Gotra* refers to the innate spiritual disposition that determines which vehicle — shravaka, pratyekabuddha, or Mahayana — a practitioner is suited for. The concept is controversial: Madhyamaka critics (e.g., Candrakirti, Madhyamakavatara VI) reject fixed lineage determination, arguing all beings possess buddha-nature. The Yogacara position, as presented here, is that accurate assessment of the student's gotra is essential for the teacher to assign the appropriate teaching.

[^counter_mindfulness]: "Counter-mindfulness" (背念, lit. "back-turned mindfulness" or "opposing mindfulness") is a distinctive Sravakabhumi term not found in the standard Pali commentarial vocabulary. It has two senses: (1) mindfulness conjoined with wise attention that opposes all unwholesome qualities, and (2) mindfulness that takes the sign of samadhi as its object, thereby opposing all objects of the unconcentrated state. In practice, establishing counter-mindfulness means deliberately calling to mind the quality of settled attention — its stillness, its clarity, its distinction from the scattered state — and holding this as the reference point for one's session. It functions as a threshold: the practitioner crosses from ordinary awareness into meditative awareness by recollecting what samadhi feels like and inclining toward it.

[^encouragement_similes]: The teacher's encouragement employs a sequence of eight samsaric metaphors in the rhetorical form "among beings who X, you alone seek Y" — a progressive intensification characteristic of the sutra genre. Similar motivational addresses appear in the Upali Sutta (MN 56) and the Maha-Rahulovada Sutta (MN 62).

[^exclusive_refuge]: The emphasis on *exclusive* refuge — not merely taking refuge in the Three Jewels but explicitly renouncing non-Buddhist teachers, teachings, and communities — reflects the Yogacara concern with distinguishing the well-taught (*svakhyata*) from the wrongly-taught (*durakhyata*) dharma-vinaya.

[^three_vehicles]: The three-vehicle (*triyana*) framework — shravaka, pratyekabuddha, and Mahayana — is foundational to Yogacara soteriology. The method described here — presenting all three vehicles and observing the student's affective response as an indicator of their innate lineage — is a distinctively Yogacara approach, treating each vehicle as a genuine path suited to a specific lineage.

[^disposition_signs]: The Visuddhimagga (III.74–102) offers a parallel but distinct set of behavioral diagnostics for the meditation teacher.

[^three_dwelling_types]: These three types of dwelling are the standard canonical settings for meditation, appearing throughout the sutras in the stock formula "gone to the forest, to the base of a tree, or to an empty room" (cf. MN 10, DN 22). The fifth quality — proximity to a *kalyanamitra* — reflects the Yogacara emphasis on the teacher-student relationship.

[^samadhi_dual_scope]: The assertion that mental one-pointedness (*ekagrata*) belongs to both the shamatha and vipashyana class is doctrinally significant. It means that vipashyana is not merely conceptual analysis but involves genuine concentration — a unified, one-pointed mind operating within the analytical mode. Tsongkhapa relies heavily on this passage in the *Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path* (Vol. 3) to argue that both shamatha and vipashyana involve *samadhi*, differing in their mode of operation (resting vs. analyzing) rather than in their degree of concentration.
