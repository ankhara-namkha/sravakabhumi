### 27.1 Purifying-Conduct Objects: Dependent Origination

[T30.0430a07]

What is the object of the nature of conditions and dependent origination?[^pratityasamutpada_object] It is as follows. Throughout the three times — past, present, and future — there are only formations (*samskara*), only dharmas, only events, only causes, and only results. All of this falls within correct reasoning: reasoning from dependence, reasoning from function, reasoning from proof, and reasoning from the nature of things.[^four_reasonings_ref] Only dharmas give rise to dharmas; there is no agent and no experiencer. This is called the object of the nature of conditions and dependent origination.

Through attending to this object, a person dominated by delusion-conduct (*moha-carita*) has all their delusion-conduct attenuated, and their mind is purified from delusion-conduct. This is called the object of the nature of conditions and dependent origination.

### 27.2 Purifying-Conduct Objects: Element Differentiation

[T30.0430a14]

What is the element-differentiation object of meditation?[^dhatu_prabheda] It is the differentiation of the six elements:

1. The earth element
2. The water element
3. The fire element
4. The wind element
5. The space element
6. The consciousness element

##### (1) The Earth Element

What is the earth element (*prithivi-dhatu*)? The earth element is twofold: internal and external.

The internal earth element is the specific quality of solidity within one's own body — included in the category of the solid and hard, included in the category of earth — which is personally appropriated (*upadatta*).

The external earth element is the quality of solidity outside the body — included in the category of the solid and hard, included in the category of earth — which is not personally appropriated.

What are the instances of the internal earth element? They are: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, dust and grime, skin, flesh, bones, sinews, veins, liver, gallbladder, heart, lungs, spleen, kidneys, belly, stomach, large intestine, small intestine, the upper stomach, the lower stomach, and excrement and filth. These are called the internal earth element.

What are the instances of the external earth element? They are: tiles, wood, clods of earth, gravel, trees, stones, mountains, and crags. Such and similar things are called the external earth element.

##### (2) The Water Element

What is the water element (*ap-dhatu*)? The water element is twofold: internal and external.

The same twofold pattern applies. The internal water element is the quality of moisture within one's own body, personally appropriated: tears, sweat, mucus, saliva, fat, grease, oil, marrow, hot phlegm, pus, blood, brain membrane, urine, and so forth. The external water element is moisture outside the body, not personally appropriated: wells, springs, ponds, marshes, reservoirs, lakes, rivers, and oceans.

##### (3) The Fire Element

**The fire element** (*tejas-dhatu*) is warmth. Internally, the metabolic heat that causes heat, uniform heat, and thorough heat throughout the body. By this warmth, what is eaten, drunk, chewed, or tasted is properly digested and transformed; when it increases excessively, it becomes feverish heat. Externally, fire produced by fire-drills, cow dung, and other materials — capable of burning thickets, wilderness, mountains, villages, cities, and entire countries.

##### (4) The Wind Element

**The wind element** (*vayu-dhatu*) is movement and lightness. Internally, the twelve winds that circulate through the body: the upward-moving wind, the downward-moving wind, the flank wind, the back wind, the waist wind, the hip wind, the small-knife wind, the great-knife wind, the needle-piercing wind, the *pippala* wind, the wind of inbreathing and outbreathing, and the wind that follows the limbs and joints.[^twelve_winds] Externally, the directional and elemental winds: east, west, south, north, dusty and dustless, narrow and vast, the *vishva* wind, the *vairambha* wind, the cosmic wind-wheel, and the seasonal great wind. When these suddenly arise and accumulate, they can break trees, topple walls, crumble mountains, and churn the seas. After blowing and gusting, having nothing to depend upon, they naturally subside. When sentient beings wish to produce wind, they wave their clothes, shake fans, or use *tala* palm leaves.

##### (5) The Space Element

What is the space element (*akasha-dhatu*)? It is the openings and cavities of the eyes, ears, nose, mouth, throat, and so forth. Through these one swallows; within these the food is swallowed; and after swallowing, through these openings it passes downward and is discharged. Such and similar things are called the space element.

##### (6) The Consciousness Element

What is the consciousness element (*vijnana-dhatu*)? It is the six types of consciousness: eye consciousness, ear consciousness, nose consciousness, tongue consciousness, body consciousness, and mind consciousness. Furthermore, the threefold distinction of heart (*citta*), mind (*manas*), and consciousness (*vijnana*) is called the consciousness element.[^citta_manas_vijnana]

##### Application to Pride-Conduct

When a person of pride-conduct (*mana-carita*) attends to element differentiation, they abandon the notion of unity[^eka_samjna] with regard to the body and obtain the perception of impurity.[^impurity_from_elements] They no longer exalt themselves; their arrogance and pride become attenuated and their mind is purified from pride-conduct. These are persons of pride-conduct; element differentiation is their purifying-conduct object.

### 27.3 Purifying-Conduct Objects: Mindfulness of Breathing

What is the object of mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*)?[^anapanasati_object] It is as follows. The mindfulness that takes the inbreath and outbreath as its object is called mindfulness of breathing. The inbreath, outbreath, and related phenomena that are the objects of this mindfulness are called the object of mindfulness of breathing.

##### Types of Breath

One should know that herein inbreath is twofold. What are the two? (1) The inbreath, and (2) the intermediate inbreath. Outbreath is also twofold. What are the two? (1) The outbreath, and (2) the intermediate outbreath.

The inbreath is the wind that, immediately after the outbreath ceases, moves inward through the inner gate and reaches as far as the navel.

The intermediate inbreath is as follows: after the inbreath has ceased and before the outbreath has arisen, in the interval at the place where the breath rests, a brief, similar, subtle wind arises. This is called the intermediate inbreath. As with the inbreath and the intermediate inbreath, the outbreath and intermediate outbreath should be understood in the same way. The difference is that immediately after the inbreath ceases, wind moves outward through the outer gate, starting from the navel and reaching the face-gate, or reaching the tip of the nose, or even going outside.

##### Causes, Supports, Movements, and Channels

The inbreath and outbreath have two causes. What are the two? (1) Impelling karma. (2) The aperture at the navel, or the apertures throughout the upper body.

The inbreath and outbreath have two supports. What are the two? (1) The body, and (2) the mind. Why is this? Because the inbreath and outbreath operate in dependence on both body and mind, each according to its proper function.[^body_mind_breath]

Three counter-examples demonstrate this. If breathing depended on body alone, beings in the attainment of non-perception, the attainment of cessation, or born in the non-perception heavens would still breathe — but they do not. If it depended on mind alone, those in the formless attainments or born in the formless realm would breathe — but they do not. And if it required only body-and-mind without proper coordination, beings in the fourth dhyana or born in that realm, as well as beings in the earliest embryonic stages of *kalala*, *arbuda*, *peshi*, and so forth[^embryonic_stages] — would breathe, yet they do not. Therefore the breath requires both body and mind, each functioning in its proper mode.

The inbreath and outbreath have two modes of movement. What are the two? (1) The inbreath moves downward. (2) The outbreath moves upward.

The inbreath and outbreath have two types of channels. What are the two? (1) Coarse channels, and (2) fine channels. What are the coarse channels? They are the passages from the aperture at the navel up to the face-gate and the nose-gate, and from the face-gate and nose-gate back down to the aperture at the navel. What are the fine channels? They are all the pores throughout the body.

##### Four Names and Two Faults

The inbreath and outbreath have four alternative names. What are the four? (1) Wind, (2) *anapana*, (3) inbreath and outbreath, and (4) body formations (*kaya-samskara*).[^body_formations] The name "wind" is the common name shared with all wind; the remaining three are names specific to the breath.

Those who cultivate the inbreath and outbreath face two faults. What are the two? (1) Excessively lax effort, and (2) excessively intense effort.

Through excessively lax effort, one gives rise to laziness, or one's mind is entangled and disturbed by torpor and drowsiness (*styana-middha*), or one's mind becomes scattered outward.

Through excessively intense effort, one produces imbalance in the body, or imbalance in the mind. How does bodily imbalance arise? When one forcefully holds the inbreath and outbreath, because the breathing is constrained, uneven wind circulates within the body. First, trembling and agitation arise throughout all the limbs and joints — this is called "trembling agitation." When this trembling-agitation wind increases, it can produce illness. For this reason, illnesses arise in the limbs and joints. This is called bodily imbalance.[^breath_safety] How does mental imbalance arise? One's mind may become scattered, or one may be oppressed by extremely heavy worry and distress. This is called mental imbalance.

##### Five Cultivations of Mindfulness of Breathing

Furthermore, this mindfulness of breathing should be understood as having, in brief, five types of cultivation. What are the five?

1. Counting cultivation
2. Cultivation of comprehending the skandhas
3. Cultivation of comprehending dependent origination
4. Cultivation of comprehending the noble truths
5. Cultivation of the sixteen victory practices

##### (1) Counting Cultivation

What is the counting cultivation?[^counting_cultivation] There are, in brief, four types of counting cultivation. What are the four? (1) Counting one as one. (2) Counting two as one. (3) Forward counting. (4) Reverse counting.

**(i) Counting one as one.** What is counting one as one? When the inbreath enters, by attending to it one places mindfulness on it and counts it as one. When the inbreath ceases and the outbreath arises, going outward, one counts it as the second. In this way one counts alternately until reaching ten. Because this counting is neither too brief nor too extensive, one counts only to ten. This is called counting one as one.

**(ii) Counting two as one.** What is counting two as one? When the inbreath has entered and ceased, and the outbreath has arisen and gone out, one then combines them and counts the pair as one. By this counting method, one counts to ten. This is called counting two as one. The inbreath and outbreath are called "two"; combining the two and counting them as one — therefore it is called counting two as one.

**(iii) Forward counting.** What is forward counting? Whether by counting one as one or by counting two as one, counting forward in sequence up to ten is called forward counting.

**(iv) Reverse counting.** What is reverse counting? Reverse counting follows the same methods but counts backward from ten to one.

Beyond these four foundational methods, the text continues with advanced stages that build upon them:

**(v) Advanced counting.** When the practitioner, relying on either counting one as one or counting two as one, has become well-practiced in both forward and reverse counting, and in the process the mind is free from distraction — when with an undistracted mind one has mastered the counting, one should then be taught the advanced counting.[^advanced_counting]

What is the advanced counting? Relying on either counting one as one or counting two as one, one combines two units into one and counts them. If relying on counting one as one, one combines each inbreath and outbreath pair into a single count. If relying on counting two as one, one combines four breaths into a single count. In this way one counts alternately until reaching ten. Thus progressively increasing, one counts up to one hundred as one. With one hundred as one count, one counts sequentially until reaching ten. When one diligently cultivates counting-breath mindfulness in this way, eventually counting ten sets of ten as one unit, counting sequentially until completing ten — when the mind remains undistracted throughout this ten-as-one counting, one is said to have become well-practiced.

Furthermore, when one diligently cultivates counting-breath mindfulness and the mind becomes distracted midway, one should return to the beginning and start counting again, either forward or reverse.

**(vi) Transcending the counting stage.** When through thorough habituation in counting, the mind naturally rides the effortless path and abides on the object of the inbreath and outbreath, continuing without break, without interruption, in unbroken continuity — first apprehending the inbreath as it turns; when the inbreath has ceased, apprehending the empty interval between breaths; then apprehending the outbreath as it turns; when the outbreath has ceased, apprehending the empty interval between breaths — in this way alternating in continuous flow, without movement, without wavering, without the activity of distraction, turning with delight — at this point one has transcended the stage of counting and should no longer count. One should only cause the mind to abide on the object of the inbreath and outbreath. One should correctly follow along with the inbreath and outbreath and thoroughly comprehend them. All the phase-distinctions of the inbreath and outbreath and the two intervals between them — whether turning or returning — should be clearly discerned. This is called the counting cultivation.

**(vii) Application to dull and sharp faculties.** Furthermore, this counting cultivation of breath-mindfulness should be taught to those of dull faculties. Through this practice, in the midst of distraction, they cause the mind to settle and to find delight. If their mindfulness of breathing lacked this counting method, their minds would be entangled and disturbed by torpor and drowsiness, or their minds would race outward in distraction. But through diligently cultivating counting-breath mindfulness, none of these arise.

If there are those of sharp faculties, possessing keen wisdom and intelligence, they do not delight in employing this counting practice. If the counting practice is taught to them, they can quickly and without error understand it, yet they take no pleasure in it. Such persons, having established mindfulness on the object of the inbreath and outbreath — wherever it turns, however far it turns, in whatever manner it turns, and whenever it turns — in all of this, through established mindfulness, they are able to correctly follow along and correctly comprehend.

This practice has such characteristics. Through cultivating, practicing, and repeatedly cultivating this practice, as a causal condition, bodily pliancy and mental pliancy arise. One attains mental one-pointedness. One enters into the object with delight. When one has thus thoroughly cultivated counting-breath mindfulness —

##### (2) Cultivation of Comprehending the Skandhas

One further attends to and contemplates both sides of the breathing experience — the breath and body as what is known (the grasped) and the mind and mental factors as what knows (the grasper)[^grasped_grasper] — comprehending the skandhas. How does one comprehend them?

By attending to the inbreath, outbreath, and the body that supports the breath, one comprehends the form skandha. By attending to the feeling that accompanies the mindfulness apprehending the inbreath and outbreath, one comprehends the feeling skandha. By attending to the discrimination that accompanies that mindfulness, one comprehends the perception skandha. By attending to the volition, wisdom, and other mental factors associated with that mindfulness, one comprehends the formations skandha. By attending to the heart, mind, and consciousness associated with that mindfulness, one comprehends the consciousness skandha.

When the practitioner thus abides extensively within the skandhas, this is called having comprehended them. This is the cultivation of comprehending the skandhas.

##### (3) Cultivation of Comprehending Dependent Origination

When one can see and know without error that there are only skandhas, only formations, only events, only dharmas — at that time one is able to comprehend dependent origination with respect to those formations. How does one comprehend?

The contemplating practitioner investigates: what does this inbreath and outbreath depend on, what is its condition? Having investigated, one truly comprehends: the inbreath and outbreath depend on the body and are conditioned by the body; they depend on the mind and are conditioned by the mind. One further investigates: what does this body and mind depend on? Having investigated, one truly comprehends: this body and mind depend on and are conditioned by the life-faculty.[^life_faculty] One further investigates: what does this life-faculty depend on? Having investigated, one truly comprehends: the life-faculty depends on and is conditioned by prior formations. One further investigates: what do these prior formations depend on? Having investigated, one truly comprehends: prior formations depend on and are conditioned by ignorance (*avidya*).

Thus one knows: ignorance conditions prior formations; prior formations condition the life-faculty; the life-faculty conditions body and mind; body and mind condition the inbreath and outbreath. Furthermore one knows: because ignorance ceases, formations cease; because formations cease, the life-faculty ceases; because the life-faculty ceases, body and mind cease; because body and mind cease, the inbreath and outbreath cease. This is called comprehending dependent origination.[^anapana_pratityasamutpada]

When one's comprehension of dependent origination is such that one abides in it extensively, this is called having thoroughly cultivated it. This is the cultivation of comprehending dependent origination.

##### (4) Cultivation of Comprehending the Noble Truths

When one has thus thoroughly cultivated comprehension of dependent origination, one further truly knows that all formations arise from multiple conditions, and comprehends impermanence. That is, one comprehends that because formations are impermanent, they were originally non-existent and then came into being; having come into being, they disperse and cease. Whatever was originally non-existent and then came into being, and having come into being disperses and ceases — this is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death. Whatever is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death — this is suffering. Whatever is suffering is without self, not autonomous, and devoid of a sovereign. In this way, through contemplating formations as impermanent, suffering, empty, and without self, one comprehends the truth of suffering.

Furthermore, one correctly comprehends that all formations arise from multiple conditions, their nature is suffering — like illness, like a boil — and all are conditioned by craving. One further comprehends that this very craving which gives rise to manifold suffering, if it is abandoned without remainder, is ultimate quiescence and subtlety. One reflects: "If I thus know, thus see, and thus abide extensively in this, I shall be able to abandon craving without remainder." In this way one comprehends the truth of the origin, the truth of cessation, and the truth of the path.

When one has abided extensively in this comprehension, one attains direct realization (*abhisamaya*) of the truths. This is the cultivation of comprehending the noble truths.[^four_truths_realization]

When one has thus thoroughly cultivated comprehension within the noble truths, all afflictions to be abandoned by the path of seeing are permanently abandoned. Only the afflictions to be abandoned by the path of cultivation remain. To abandon those, one further advances to cultivate the sixteen victory practices.

##### (5) Cultivation of the Sixteen Victory Practices

What are the sixteen victory practices?[^sixteen_victory_practices] They are as follows:

*First tetrad (body):*[^sixteen_in_out]

(1) "I now train in being mindful of the inbreath and outbreath." (2) "I now train in knowing whether the breath is long or short." (3) "I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing in and breathing out." (4) "I now train in calming body formations while breathing in and breathing out."

*Second tetrad (feeling):*

(5) "I now train in knowing joy while breathing in and breathing out." (6) "I now train in knowing pleasure while breathing in and breathing out." (7) "I now train in knowing mental formations (*citta-samskara*) while breathing in and breathing out." (8) "I now train in calming mental formations while breathing in and breathing out."

*Third tetrad (mind):*

(9) "I now train in knowing the mind while breathing in and breathing out." (10) "I now train in gladdening the mind while breathing in and breathing out." (11) "I now train in concentrating the mind while breathing in and breathing out." (12) "I now train in liberating the mind while breathing in and breathing out."

*Fourth tetrad (dharmas):*

(13) "I now train in contemplating impermanence while breathing in and breathing out." (14) "I now train in contemplating abandonment while breathing in and breathing out." (15) "I now train in contemplating dispassion while breathing in and breathing out." (16) "I now train in contemplating cessation while breathing in and breathing out."

**Commentary on the first tetrad (body).** **Question:** What are the distinctions among these sixteen?

**Answer:** A learner (*shaiksha*) who has seen the path,[^learner_seen_path] having already attained the four foundations of mindfulness and so forth, attending to the object of the inbreath and outbreath, further advances in cultivation in order to abandon the remaining fetters. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in being mindful of the inbreath and outbreath."

When one takes the inbreath and outbreath as object, one resolves: "I now train in being mindful of the long inbreath and the long outbreath." When one takes the intermediate inbreath and intermediate outbreath as object, one resolves: "I now train in being mindful of the short inbreath and the short outbreath." Just as one knows that the regular inbreath and outbreath turn as long breaths, and the intermediate inbreath and intermediate outbreath turn as short breaths, so one knows them. This is what is meant by "whether long or short."

The fine channels throughout the body are the pathways through which the breath enters and pervades, reaching even the pores. Holding these channels as one's object and generating resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*) regarding them, one resolves: "I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing in and breathing out."

Between every outbreath and inbreath, and between every inbreath and outbreath, there is an empty interval where the breath is momentarily absent. Taking that gap as object — the still point where one breath has ceased and the next has not yet arisen — one resolves: "I now train in calming body formations while breathing in and breathing out."

Furthermore, through cultivating, practicing, and repeatedly cultivating this as a causal condition, the harsh, painful sensations that accompanied the breath when one had not yet become accustomed to it are now all calmed because one has become accustomed to the inbreath and outbreath. Gentle, pleasant sensations accompany the breath instead. One then resolves: "I now train in calming body formations while breathing in and breathing out."

**Commentary on the second tetrad (feeling).** Furthermore, when one who diligently cultivates mindfulness of breathing attains the first or second dhyana, one resolves: "I now train in knowing joy while breathing in and breathing out."[^dhyana_anapanasati_limit]

When one attains the third dhyana, which is free of joy, one resolves: "I now train in knowing pleasure while breathing in and breathing out."

Above the third dhyana, there is no further basis for the practice of mindfulness of breathing. Therefore breath-mindfulness is taught as the preparatory practice extending only through the third dhyana.

Furthermore, when one who is thus knowing joy or knowing pleasure temporarily loses mindfulness, thoughts may arise: "There is a self," or "There is something that is mine"; self-conceit arises; or one thinks "I will exist" or "I will not exist," "I will have form" or "I will be formless," "I will have perception" or "I will be without perception," or "I will have neither perception nor non-perception." Such delusion arises — accompanied by perception and intention, manifesting as various agitations of conceit, idle elaborations, fabrications, and craving.[^mental_formations_breath] But as soon as these arise, one is able swiftly to penetrate them with wisdom without becoming deeply attached, and to skillfully eliminate, remove, and expel them. Through this practice one resolves: "I now train in knowing mental formations while breathing in and breathing out. I now train in calming mental formations while breathing in and breathing out."

**Commentary on the third tetrad (mind).** Furthermore, when one has attained the root first, second, or third dhyana, one has certainly already attained the access concentration (*anagamya-samapatti*) of the first dhyana.[^access_concentration] Relying on this, one observes the states of mind that arise. That is, one truly knows and truly discerns:[^mind_states]

Whether there is a mind with desire or a mind free from desire; a mind with aversion or a mind free from aversion; a mind with delusion or a mind free from delusion; a contracted mind or a scattered mind; a sunken mind or an agitated mind; a mind with restlessness or a mind without restlessness; a mind with stillness or a mind without stillness; a mind in meditative equipoise or a mind not in meditative equipoise; a well-cultivated mind or a poorly cultivated mind; a well-liberated mind or a poorly liberated mind.

Regarding all such states of mind, one truly knows and truly discerns them. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in knowing the mind while breathing in and breathing out."

If at times one sees that the mind is covered and obstructed by the hindrance of torpor and drowsiness due to excessive inward abiding in quiescence, then one attends outward to a pure and excellent object — showing, guiding, praising, encouraging, gladdening, and energizing the mind. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in gladdening the mind while breathing in and breathing out."

If at times one sees that the mind is covered and obstructed by the hindrance of restlessness and remorse due to excessive outward abiding in agitation, then one settles inward, abiding in stillness, and restrains the mind. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in concentrating the mind while breathing in and breathing out."

When through thoroughly cultivating, practicing, and extensively cultivating the mind as a causal condition, all the active hindrances are removed and the mind is purified from the hindrances, one resolves: "I now train in liberating the mind while breathing in and breathing out."

**Commentary on the fourth tetrad (dharmas).** When one's mind has been liberated from the hindrances that obstruct the path of cultivation, there remain latent tendencies (*anushaya*) that must still be abandoned. In order to abandon them, one gives rise to the path and brings it before one — that is, one earnestly and diligently contemplates with correct reasoning the nature of impermanence in all formations. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in contemplating impermanence while breathing in and breathing out."

Furthermore, previously — whether relying on the lower three dhyanas or on access concentration — one practiced the yoga of shamatha. Now, based on the contemplation of impermanence, one additionally practices the yoga of vipashyana. Having thus refined the mind through both shamatha and vipashyana, one seeks liberation from the latent tendencies within the realms. What are these realms? They are the three realms:[^three_cessation_realms]

1. The realm of abandonment (*prahana-dhatu*): the abandonment of all formations to be abandoned by the path of seeing.
2. The realm of dispassion (*viraga-dhatu*): the abandonment of all formations to be abandoned by the path of cultivation.
3. The realm of cessation (*nirodha-dhatu*): the cessation of all bases of existence.

Contemplating these three realms as peaceful, secure, and without danger, one cultivates shamatha and vipashyana. Through cultivating and extensively cultivating, one's mind is liberated from the remaining afflictions to be abandoned by the path of cultivation. Therefore one resolves: "I now train in contemplating abandonment, contemplating dispassion, and contemplating cessation while breathing in and breathing out."

Thus, because all afflictions to be abandoned by both seeing and cultivation are permanently abandoned, one becomes an arhat. The outflows (*asrava*) are forever exhausted. After this there is no further task to be done; one has reached the ultimate in what was to be discerned. This is called the sixteen victory practices.

##### Closing: Application to Discursive-Thought Conduct

Cultivating in this way is called the five cultivations of mindfulness of breathing.

Persons of discursive-thought conduct (*vitarka-carita*) should diligently train in this and delight in employing it. If regarding one's meditation object there are hurried thoughts and distraction, one should individually practice such contemplation within. If one diligently cultivates this, discursive thoughts and scattered agitation all cease to exist; the mind quickly settles on the object and deeply delights in it. This is the fifth purifying-conduct object: the purifying-conduct object for persons of discursive-thought conduct.

This is called, collectively, the purifying-conduct objects.

### 27.4 Skillful Objects

[T30.0433c01]

What are the skillful objects?[^skillful_objects] These objects are, in brief, of five types:

1. Skill in the skandhas
2. Skill in the elements
3. Skill in the sense bases
4. Skill in dependent origination
5. Skill in the possible and impossible

##### (1) Skill in the Skandhas

Regarding skill in the skandhas: What are the skandhas? What is skill in the skandhas? The skandhas are five: (1) the form skandha, (2) the feeling skandha, (3) the perception skandha, (4) the formations skandha, and (5) the consciousness skandha.

What is the form skandha? All form whatsoever is entirely constituted by the four great elements (*catvari-mahabhutani*) and what is derived from the four great elements. Furthermore, whether past, future, or present; whether internal or external; whether coarse or subtle; whether inferior or superior; whether far or near — all of this is collectively called the form skandha.

What is the feeling skandha? Feelings conditioned by contact that accords with pleasure, feelings conditioned by contact that accords with pain, and feelings conditioned by contact that accords with neither pleasure nor pain. Furthermore, there are six groups of feeling: feeling born of eye-contact; feeling born of ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. All of this is collectively called the feeling skandha.

What is the perception skandha? Perception with a sign and perception without a sign; narrow perception and broad perception; immeasurable perception and the perception of the base of nothingness. Furthermore, there are six groups of perception: perception born of eye-contact; perception born of ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. All of this constitutes the perception skandha.

What is the formations skandha? The six groups of volition: volition born of eye-contact; volition born of ear-contact, nose-contact, tongue-contact, body-contact, and mind-contact. Furthermore, all remaining mental factors apart from feeling and perception constitute the formations skandha.

What is the consciousness skandha? Heart (*citta*), mind (*manas*), and consciousness (*vijnana*). Furthermore, there are six groups of consciousness: eye consciousness; ear, nose, tongue, body, and mind consciousness. These together are the consciousness skandha.

The preceding feeling, perception, and formations skandhas, as well as this consciousness skandha, all have the distinctions of past, future, present, internal, external, and so forth, as previously explained in detail. These are called the skandhas.

What is skill in the skandhas? It is thoroughly knowing the variety of the skandhas as described, their multiplicity and diversity, and that apart from these dharmas nothing further can be apprehended or discriminated. This is the brief explanation of the meaning of skill in the skandhas.

What is the variety of the skandhas? The form skandha is distinct; the feeling skandha is distinct; and so on through to the consciousness skandha being distinct. This is called variety.

What is the multiplicity of the skandhas? The form skandha is not one but of many types, because there are distinctions between the great elements and derived form, and distinctions among types such as past, future, and present. This is called the multiplicity of the form skandha. The remaining skandhas should be understood in the same way, each as appropriate.

What does it mean that apart from these dharmas nothing further can be apprehended or discriminated? Only the skandhas can be apprehended; only events can be apprehended. Apart from the skandhas, no self can be apprehended — no permanent, eternal, unchanging dharma is to be found. Nor is there the slightest dharma that is "mine." Therefore, apart from these, nothing further can be apprehended or discriminated.

##### (2) Skill in the Elements

What are the elements? What is skill in the elements? The elements (*dhatus*) are eighteen:[^eighteen_dhatus] the eye element, form element, and eye-consciousness element; the ear element, sound element, and ear-consciousness element; the nose element, smell element, and nose-consciousness element; the tongue element, taste element, and tongue-consciousness element; the body element, tangible element, and body-consciousness element; the mind element, dharma element, and mind-consciousness element. These are called the elements.

Skill in the elements means knowing that these eighteen dharmas each arise from their own distinct element, seed, and lineage — from their own causal matrix. To truly know, accept, and investigate this is to be skilled in causation. Therefore this is called skill in the elements.

##### (3) Skill in the Sense Bases

What are the sense bases (*ayatana*)? What is skill in the sense bases? The sense bases are twelve: the eye base and form base; the ear base and sound base; the nose base and smell base; the tongue base and taste base; the body base and tangible base; the mind base and dharma base. These are called the sense bases.

Regarding skill in the sense bases: the eye serves as the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*); form serves as the object condition (*alambana-pratyaya*); the just-ceased preceding mind serves as the immediately preceding condition (*samanantara-pratyaya*). From these three conditions arises eye consciousness and its associated mental factors.[^three_conditions] The ear serves as the dominant condition; sound serves as the object condition; the just-ceased preceding mind serves as the immediately preceding condition. From these arises ear consciousness and its associated mental factors. And so on, until: mind serves as the immediately preceding condition; the attention (*manaskara*) arising from this serves as the dominant condition; dharmas serve as the object condition. From these arises mind consciousness and its associated mental factors.

In this way, the six groups of consciousness and their associated mental factors all operate through three conditions: the dominant condition, the object condition, and the immediately preceding condition. When one becomes skilled in the conditions with regard to these internal and external sense bases, this is called skill in the sense bases.

##### (4) Skill in Dependent Origination

What is dependent origination? What is skill in dependent origination? Ignorance conditions formations; formations condition consciousness; consciousness conditions name-and-form; name-and-form conditions the six sense bases; the six sense bases condition contact; contact conditions feeling; feeling conditions craving; craving conditions grasping; grasping conditions becoming; becoming conditions birth; birth conditions aging-and-death — and thus one gathers this entire great mass of suffering. This is called dependent origination.

To know dependent origination skillfully is to know this: only dharmas produce dharmas, only conditions give rise to conditions. No formation is self-caused. Whatever arises from causes and conditions was not there before, comes into being, and then disperses — and precisely because it arises and disperses, it is impermanent. Because it is impermanent, it is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death — to the full range of suffering from grief and lamentation through anguish and distress. And because it is suffering, it cannot be a self: no one is the sovereign of what causes them pain, and one's power is feeble and weak. In dependent origination, therefore, there is no agent, no self.

If within such dependently arisen dharmas, through such various aspects and modes one becomes skilled — comprehending the knowledge of impermanence, the knowledge of suffering, and the knowledge of non-self — this is called skill in dependent origination.

##### (5) Skill in the Possible and Impossible

Furthermore, skill in the possible and impossible should be understood as a variant of skill in dependent origination.[^sthana_asthana] The distinction is as follows. Through skill in the possible and impossible, one correctly knows the principle that causes and results are not unequal. That is, wholesome and unwholesome dharmas have their matured results (*vipaka*). Wholesome dharmas produce desirable matured results; unwholesome dharmas produce undesirable matured results. If one truly knows this, it is called skill in the possible and impossible.

##### Summary of the Five Skills

These five skills are, in brief, reduced to two: (1) skill in own-characteristics (*svalakshana*), and (2) skill in common characteristics (*samanya-lakshana*). Skill in the skandhas reveals skill in own-characteristics. The remaining four skills reveal skill in common characteristics. This is called, collectively, the skillful objects.

### 27.5 Affliction-Purifying Objects

[T30.0434b14]

What are the affliction-purifying objects (*klesha-vishodhana-alambana*)?[^affliction_purifying_objects] It is as follows. One contemplates the coarseness of the lower realm and the peacefulness of the upper realm — as the desire realm compared to the first dhyana, and so on up to the base of nothingness compared to the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception.

##### Worldly Path: Contemplation of Coarseness and Peacefulness

What is coarseness? Coarseness is twofold: (1) substantial coarseness, and (2) numerical coarseness.

What is substantial coarseness? The desire realm compared to the first dhyana — although both possess the five skandhas, the desire realm has grave dangers and afflictions, with suffering and misery predominant, being most base and loathsome.

[T30.0434b20]

Therefore it is called substantially coarse. The first dhyana is not like this; it is supremely peaceful and subtle. Therefore it is called substantially peaceful.

What is numerical coarseness? The form skandha of the desire realm has many varieties that should be known and abandoned; and so on through to the consciousness skandha, likewise. Therefore it is called numerically coarse. In the same way, the upper realms compared to each other in succession — whether substantially coarse or numerically coarse — should be understood similarly, each as appropriate.

Thus this coarseness, as the realms are compared to each other in succession, extends up to the base of nothingness. In all lower realms, suffering and affliction are more numerous and lifespan is shorter. In all upper realms, suffering and affliction are fewer and lifespan is longer. The base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception is exclusively peaceful and subtle, because there is no higher realm that surpasses it.

In summary, the meaning of "having dangers" is the meaning of "coarseness." If in a given realm the dangers are more numerous, then by that very nature of increased dangers it is called "coarse." If in a given realm the dangers are fewer, then by that very nature of decreased dangers it is called "peaceful."

This is the worldly affliction-purifying object, through the worldly path (*samvriti-marga*).[^worldly_path] Why? Because one observes the many dangers of the lower realm — like illness, like a boil, like a poisoned arrow — regarding its unsettled nature as coarseness. One observes the upper realm, which is the opposite of this, as peacefulness. One thereby abandons all the afflictions of the lower realm. This proceeds from the desire realm up to the base of nothingness. This is a temporary abandonment, not an ultimate abandonment, because at a later time the afflictions resume their continuity.

##### Supramundane Path: The Four Noble Truths

The supramundane path's (*lokottara-marga*) affliction-purifying objects are furthermore of four types: (1) the noble truth of suffering, (2) the noble truth of the origin, (3) the noble truth of cessation, and (4) the noble truth of the path.

What is the noble truth of suffering? Birth is suffering, aging is suffering, illness is suffering, death is suffering. Association with what is hateful is suffering. Separation from what is loved is suffering. Not obtaining what one seeks is suffering. In brief, the five appropriated skandhas are suffering. This is called the noble truth of suffering.

What is the noble truth of the origin? It is craving — craving for continued existence (*punarbhava-trishna*), craving accompanied by delight and passion (*nandi-raga-sahagata-trishna*), and craving for this and that pleasure (*tatra-tatrabhiratika-trishna*), and so forth.[^three_craving_types] This is called the noble truth of the origin.

What is the noble truth of cessation? It is the complete, remainderless abandonment and cessation of this very craving and so forth. This is called the noble truth of cessation.

What is the noble truth of the path? It is the eightfold and related noble path. This is called the noble truth of the path.

One should know that herein the four truths are established on the basis of the fruit and cause of the dark and bright categories (*krishna-paksha* and *shukla-paksha*): the truth of suffering is the fruit of the dark category; the truth of origin is the cause of the dark category; the truth of cessation is the fruit of the bright category; the truth of the path is the cause of the bright category, because it can be attained and realized.

Furthermore, the truth of suffering is like illness: it should first be fully known. The truth of origin is like the cause of illness: it should next be abandoned. The truth of cessation is like the absence of illness: it should next be directly realized. The truth of the path is like effective medicine: it should be cultivated and extensively cultivated.

Again, the suffering-meaning of the truth of suffering, and so on through to the path-meaning of the truth of the path — this is truly so, not untrue; it is without distortion, not distorted. Therefore they are called "truths." Their own-characteristics are without deception, and by seeing them, non-distorted awareness operates. Therefore they are called "truths."

**Question:** Why are these truths called exclusively "noble truths"?

**Answer:** Only the noble ones (*arya*) regarding these truths equally acknowledge them as truths. They truly know and truly see. All ordinary beings (*prithagjana*) do not truly know and do not truly see. Therefore the truths are called exclusively "noble truths." Furthermore, for ordinary beings they are called "truths" only by the nature of things (*dharmata*), not by awakened understanding. For the noble ones, they are truths by both.

##### Suffering as Cause, Not as Essence

Furthermore, regarding "birth is suffering": at the time of birth, various bodily and mental painful feelings arise. It is not that birth itself is suffering; it is the cause and condition of suffering. Therefore it is called "suffering."[^suffering_as_cause] The same reasoning applies to each of the remaining sufferings — aging, illness, death, association with what is hateful, separation from what is loved, and not obtaining what one seeks: because of the condition of not obtaining what one seeks, various bodily and mental painful feelings arise. It is not that not-obtaining itself is suffering; it is the cause and condition of suffering. Therefore it is called "suffering."

Thus one should know that "in brief, the five appropriated skandhas are suffering" means the following: through the alternative expressions of birth and so forth, only the suffering of suffering (*duhkha-duhkhata*) is revealed. Through "the five appropriated skandhas are suffering," the remaining suffering of change (*viparinama-duhkhata*) and suffering of conditioned existence (*samskara-duhkhata*) are also revealed.[^three_sufferings] Why? Because the five appropriated skandhas comprehensively include the three types of feeling. Thus they serve as the container for the suffering of suffering as described above. One should know that they also inherently include the suffering of change and suffering of conditioned existence not previously mentioned.

**Question:** Why did the Blessed One state the suffering of suffering alone by its own name, while stating the suffering of change and suffering of conditioned existence by alternative expressions?

**Answer:** Regarding the suffering of suffering, whether ordinary or noble, all equally have the wisdom-awareness of suffering operating. Its nature is also extremely repugnant. For those who have not previously cultivated wisdom, it is the easiest to grasp when first taught. And regarding the truths as a whole, it provides the readiest entry point for beings who are to be tamed and transformed.

##### The Three Types of Suffering

How are the three types of suffering established?

**(1) The suffering of suffering.** The previously stated birth-suffering through not-obtaining suffering reveals painful feeling and its physical basis as the nature of the suffering of suffering. This is the establishment of the suffering of suffering.

**(2) The suffering of change.** Whatever serves as the antidote to those forms of suffering — youth is the antidote to aging; health is the antidote to illness; life is the antidote to death; meeting with the beloved is the antidote to association with the hateful; non-separation from the loved is the antidote to separation from the loved; obtaining what one seeks is the antidote to not-obtaining. In addition, the afflictions that arise from painful feeling and its physical basis, and the afflictions that arise from health and other occasions for pleasant feeling and the feelings generated by those — all of this collectively constitutes the nature of the suffering of change.

Herein, pleasant feeling and its physical basis, being impermanent, undergo change and alteration. One experiences the manifold sufferings born from the dominance of that change. When afflictions arise in any circumstance, they bind the mind and cause it to deteriorate, immediately producing manifold suffering. Therefore it is called the suffering of change.

As the Blessed One said: "With a mind subject to change, one grasps a woman's hand" — and so on in detail. Furthermore, as it is said: "Abiding in the bonds of sensual desire, one experiences the bodily and mental grief born from the conditions of those bonds." Similarly, abiding in the bonds of aversion, torpor and drowsiness, restlessness and remorse, and doubt, one experiences the bodily and mental grief born from the conditions of those bonds. By this teaching, the foremost teaching, in all afflictions the meaning of suffering and the meaning of deterioration can be found. Therefore afflictions are said to constitute the nature of the suffering of change. This is the establishment of the suffering of change.

**(3) The suffering of conditioned existence.** The nature of the suffering of conditioned existence pervades all five appropriated skandhas. In summary: one excludes the suffering of suffering; one excludes the affliction-comprised suffering of change; and one excludes the pleasant-feeling-comprised suffering of change and its physical basis. What remains — all skandhas accompanied by neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, those born from such feelings, the conditions giving rise to them, and the skandhas that serve as the basis for their arising and abiding — this is the nature of the suffering of conditioned existence.

The skandhas of conditioned existence are "suffering" in a third and subtler sense. They are impermanent — arising and ceasing. They carry the seeds of clinging. They are shadowed by the heaviness of all three types of feeling. They offer no security, no refuge free from the first two types of suffering. And they cannot be commanded or controlled — no one is their sovereign. All of this is what it means to say they are suffering by the very fact of being conditioned. This is the establishment of the suffering of conditioned existence.

##### The Truth of the Origin: Three Types of Craving

Furthermore, that craving is also called yearning, also called eager desire, also called delight. This yearning operates through three modes: (1) yearning for continued existence, (2) yearning for objects already obtained, and (3) yearning for objects not yet obtained. Yearning for continued existence is called craving for continued existence. Regarding objects already obtained, there is craving accompanied by joyful attachment; regarding objects not yet obtained, there is craving accompanied by yearning for union. One should know that craving accompanied by joyful attachment for already-obtained objects is called craving accompanied by delight and passion. Craving accompanied by yearning for union with not-yet-obtained objects is called craving for this and that pleasure.

##### Cessation and the Path

Cessation is of two types: (1) cessation of afflictions, and (2) cessation of the physical basis. The path is of two types: (1) the path of the learner, and (2) the path of the non-learner.

Thus one should know that this is called the supramundane path's affliction-purifying objects. Thus the four types of meditation objects have been explained: (1) pervasive objects, (2) purifying-conduct objects, (3) skillful objects, and (4) affliction-purifying objects.[^four_objects_complete]

### 27.6 Four Types of Instruction

[T30.0435b23]

What is instruction (*avavada*)?[^four_instructions] There are four types of instruction: (1) non-distorted instruction (*aviparita-avavada*), (2) gradual instruction (*anupurva-avavada*), (3) teaching-based instruction (*agama-avavada*), and (4) realization-based instruction (*adhigama-avavada*).

**(1) Non-distorted instruction.** What is non-distorted instruction? It is to proclaim the meaning of the Dharma without distortion, enabling the student to receive, uphold, recite, and study it — truly going forth and completely exhausting all suffering, reaching the end of suffering. This is called non-distorted instruction.

**(2) Gradual instruction.** What is gradual instruction? It is to proclaim the meaning of the Dharma suited to the occasion. First one enables the student to receive and recite the elementary; only afterward does one direct them to study the profound. Furthermore, to bring about the initial direct realization of the truths, one first teaches the truth of suffering and afterward the truths of origin, cessation, and the path. Furthermore, to bring about the attainment of the dhyanas, one first teaches the initial dhyana and afterward teaches the remaining dhyanas. Such and similar patterns should be known as gradual instruction.

**(3) Teaching-based instruction.** What is teaching-based instruction? It is instruction received from a venerable one, or from one resembling a venerable — one who understands yoga, its rules, and personal instruction — or from the Tathagatas or the Buddha's disciples. Having heard the correct teaching, one instructs others exactly as taught, neither adding nor subtracting. This is called teaching-based instruction.

**(4) Realization-based instruction.** What is realization-based instruction? It is as follows: what one has personally, dwelling alone in seclusion, attained, touched, and realized regarding dharmas — in order to enable others to likewise attain, touch, and realize — one skillfully instructs them. This is called realization-based instruction.

##### Instruction Replete with All Marks: Three Supernormal Displays

[T30.0435c09]

Furthermore, there is instruction replete with all marks. What does this involve? It is instruction through the three supernormal displays (*pratiharya*).[^three_supernormal_displays] The three supernormal displays are: (1) the supernormal display of magical transformation (*riddhi-pratiharya*), (2) the supernormal display of mind-reading declaration (*adesana-pratiharya*), and (3) the supernormal display of instruction (*anushasani-pratiharya*).

Through the supernormal display of magical transformation, one can manifest various miraculous phenomena, causing others to develop utmost respect for oneself. Because they develop respect, they listen attentively with utmost reverence, applying their minds to yoga.

Through the supernormal display of mind-reading declaration, one can investigate the differences in others' mental states and tendencies.

Through the supernormal display of instruction, according to the student's faculties, tendencies, and capacity for understanding, one teaches the correct Dharma and can correctly instruct them in their practice.

Therefore, the three supernormal displays encompass instruction replete with all marks.

[^pratityasamutpada_object]: The meditation on dependent origination (*pratyaya-pratityasamutpada*, 緣性緣起) directly counteracts delusion-conduct by revealing that all phenomena arise solely through causes and conditions, without any creator or experiencer. The fuller treatment of this practice appears in the skillful objects section (27.4) and in the five cultivations of anapanasati (27.3); here only the essential principle is stated as the purifying-conduct object for delusion types.

[^four_reasonings_ref]: The four types of reasoning (*yukti*) — dependence, function, proof, and the nature of things — are defined in detail in the vipashyana framework (section 30.14).

[^dhatu_prabheda]: The "elements" here are not the chemical elements of modern science but the six fundamental constituents of experience recognized in Abhidharma analysis: the four material elements (earth, water, fire, wind), space, and consciousness. This six-element analysis closely parallels the Dhatuvibhanga Sutra (MN 140), which presents the same six elements with internal/external subdivisions. The Visuddhimagga's element-determination (XI.27ff) covers a related but narrower four-element framework without space and consciousness.

[^twelve_winds]: The Sravakabhumi's twelve internal winds (*vayu*) are a more detailed enumeration than the Nikaya parallel, which gives only six (MN 140). The twelve reflect the Sarvastivada/Yogacara Abhidharma tradition. Representative examples: the upward-moving wind governs belching and speech; the downward-moving wind governs elimination; the *pippala* wind describes a pervasive trembling sensation throughout the body. Cf. Visuddhimagga XI.74-78.

[^citta_manas_vijnana]: The Sravakabhumi here lists the threefold distinction of "heart" (*citta*, 心), "mind" (*manas*, 意), and "consciousness" (*vijnana*, 識) alongside the six sense-consciousnesses, comprehensively enumerating all aspects of the consciousness element. At the most general level these three terms can be used interchangeably to denote the mental faculty: *citta* as the accumulated continuum, *manas* as the thinking faculty, and *vijnana* as active cognition.

[^eka_samjna]: The "notion of unity" (一合想, *eka-samjna*) is the mistaken perception that the body is a single, unified whole — the basis for self-identification and pride. By analyzing the body into its six constituent elements, the practitioner sees that what appears to be "one body" is in fact a convergence of earth (solidity), water (moisture), fire (warmth), wind (movement), space (cavities), and consciousness (awareness) — none of which is a self, and none of which constitutes a unity. This directly counteracts pride (*mana*), which depends on regarding oneself as a substantial entity.

[^impurity_from_elements]: The text states that element differentiation yields the perception of impurity (*ashubha-samjna*) as well as dissolving the notion of unity. Once the body is seen as a mere assemblage of disparate elements — solidity, moisture, warmth, movement, cavities, and awareness — it is also perceived as lacking inherent beauty or worth. This integration of impurity perception into element analysis is characteristic of the Yogacarabhumi's treatment and is not found in the Nikaya parallel (MN 140), where element analysis leads to dispassion and non-self realization without explicit mention of impurity perception.

[^anapanasati_object]: Mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*, 阿那波那念) is the fifth and final purifying-conduct object, prescribed for persons dominated by discursive thought (*vitarka-carita*). It is treated at far greater length than the other four purifying-conduct objects because it encompasses the entire path from beginner's concentration through arhatship. The Sravakabhumi's specific five-cultivation framework (counting, skandhas, dependent origination, noble truths, sixteen victory practices) is distinctive to the Yogacara tradition. While the Pali Nikayas present the sixteen steps without progressive scaffolding, the Patisambhidamagga (I.163-190) and Visuddhimagga (VIII.145-244) do present their own progressive frameworks for anapanasati (including counting), though with different structures and rationales.

[^body_mind_breath]: This argument — that breathing requires *both* body and mind in proper coordination — is characteristic of Yogacara Abhidharma. The three counter-examples (non-perception attainment, formless attainment, fourth dhyana/embryonic stages) demonstrate that body alone, mind alone, or body-and-mind without proper conditions are each insufficient to sustain breathing.

[^embryonic_stages]: *Kalala* (羯羅藍), *arbuda* (頞部曇), and *peshi* (閉尸) are the first three of five embryonic stages in Buddhist Abhidharma: (1) *kalala* — the initial embryonic substance (first seven days after conception); (2) *arbuda* — a nodule or bud (second seven days); (3) *peshi* — a lump of flesh (third seven days). At these stages the embryo possesses both body and mind but breathing has not yet begun, demonstrating the "proper function" qualification.

[^breath_safety]: This warning about the physical consequences of forced breathing is consistent with guidance across Buddhist contemplative traditions. The Visuddhimagga (VIII.172-177) gives similar cautions. Mindfulness of breathing should observe the natural breath without controlling or manipulating it. A practitioner who experiences trembling, pain, or agitation during breath meditation should immediately relax the effort and consult a qualified meditation instructor.

[^body_formations]: The breath is called "body formations" (*kaya-samskara*) because it is a conditioned bodily process — one of the conditions sustaining bodily existence. This designation is important for the sixteen victory practices later in this section, where "calming body formations" means calming the breathing process itself. Cf. the Anapanasati Sutra (MN 118) and Analayo, *Mindfulness of Breathing*, ch. II.

[^counting_cultivation]: The counting cultivation (*ganana-bhavana*) is the foundational preliminary for mindfulness of breathing, designed to stabilize attention through the structure of numerical counting. The Visuddhimagga (VIII.182-190) presents a similar counting practice but with different procedural details. The Sravakabhumi's four-type framework (one-as-one, two-as-one, forward, reverse) plus the advanced counting is uniquely detailed.

[^advanced_counting]: The "advanced counting" (*abhyasa-ganana*) progressively increases the number of breaths grouped as a single count — from pairs to sets of four, and so on up to one hundred per count — training the practitioner to maintain unbroken attention over longer spans. When the mind remains undistracted throughout this extended counting, the practitioner has "become well-practiced" and is ready to transcend counting altogether.

[^grasped_grasper]: "Grasped and grasper" (所取能取) — the objective and subjective poles of experience. The "grasped" (*grahya*) is the breath and body (form skandha); the "grasper" (*grahaka*) comprises the mental skandhas: feeling, perception, formations, and consciousness. This analysis dissolves the apparent unity of the breathing experience into its five skandha components.

[^life_faculty]: The life-faculty (*jivita-indriya*, 命根) is the faculty that sustains the life-span of a sentient being. In the dependent origination chain presented here, the Sravakabhumi traces an abbreviated causal sequence: ignorance → prior formations → life-faculty → body-and-mind → breath. This differs from the standard twelve-link chain but applies the same principle of conditioned arising to the immediate experience of breathing.

[^anapana_pratityasamutpada]: This dependent origination analysis is distinctive to the Sravakabhumi's anapanasati teaching. Rather than the standard twelve links, it traces a five-step chain from ignorance through prior karma, the life-faculty, body-and-mind, to the breath — and then applies both the arising formula (ignorance conditions formations...) and the cessation formula (when ignorance ceases, formations cease...). This bridges the practitioner's direct experience of breathing with the deepest causal structure of samsara.

[^four_truths_realization]: The four comprehensions — impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self — correspond to the standard Abhidharma analysis of the truth of suffering under four aspects (*akara*). The progression follows a logical chain: what is impermanent is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death; what is subject to these is suffering; what is suffering cannot be a self (since no one would choose suffering).

[^sixteen_victory_practices]: The sixteen victory practices (十六勝行) correspond to the sixteen steps of mindfulness of breathing in the Anapanasati Sutra (MN 118). The Sravakabhumi organizes them into four tetrads: (1) the body tetrad (practices 1–4: mindfulness, long/short, entire body, calming body formations), (2) the feeling tetrad (practices 5–8: joy, pleasure, mental formations, calming mental formations), (3) the mind tetrad (practices 9–12: knowing the mind, gladdening, concentrating, liberating), and (4) the dharma tetrad (practices 13–16: impermanence, abandonment, dispassion, cessation). Crucially, the Sravakabhumi places these sixteen practices at the culmination of a five-stage progressive path, undertaken only by learners who have already abandoned the afflictions of the path of seeing. Cf. Analayo, *Mindfulness of Breathing*, ch. III–IV.

[^sixteen_in_out]: The Chinese text presents each practice as two separate resolutions — one for the inbreath, one for the outbreath (e.g., 學知息入。學知息出。). They are consolidated here as pairs for readability, following the convention of Bodhi's translation of MN 118.

[^learner_seen_path]: A "learner" (*shaiksha*, 有學) who has "seen the path" (見迹) is one who has attained at least stream-entry (*srota-apatti*) — the first of the four shramana fruits — and has thus entered the noble sangha. "Having already attained the four foundations of mindfulness and so forth" means having completed the path of seeing and established the factors of awakening. The sixteen victory practices serve to complete the path of cultivation and achieve arhatship.

[^dhyana_anapanasati_limit]: The Sravakabhumi explicitly states that mindfulness of breathing can be practiced only through the third dhyana, because in the fourth dhyana there is no breathing — as demonstrated in the causes-and-supports section above, where breathing requires body and mind functioning "each according to its proper function," a condition that does not obtain in the fourth dhyana. Joy (*priti*) is present in the first and second dhyanas; pleasure (*sukha*) remains in the third dhyana after joy has ceased. The Theravada tradition holds a different view: the Visuddhimagga (VIII.189-190) teaches that anapanasati can develop all four dhyanas, since the increasingly subtle breath can still serve as a meditation object even as breathing approaches cessation. The Sravakabhumi's position that anapanasati extends only through the third dhyana is distinctive to the Sarvastivada/Yogacara tradition. Cf. Analayo, *Mindfulness of Breathing*, ch. III-IV.

[^mental_formations_breath]: "Mental formations" (*citta-samskara*, 心行) in this context are the speculative views and conceit-driven thoughts that arise even during meditative joy and pleasure. The seven views — "I will exist," "I will not exist," "I will have form," "I will be formless," "I will have perception," "I will be without perception," "I will have neither perception nor non-perception" — are conceit-rooted speculations about future existence. These are what the practitioner learns to know and calm in practices 7–8.

[^access_concentration]: "Access concentration" (近分未至依定, *anagamya-samapatti*) is the preliminary concentration that precedes the first dhyana — stable enough for analytical observation but without the full absorption of the root dhyanas. The text specifies that one relies on this access concentration (rather than the root dhyanas themselves) to observe mental states, because in the root dhyanas the mind is too absorbed for the discriminating awareness required by the third tetrad's practices of knowing, gladdening, concentrating, and liberating the mind.

[^mind_states]: This list of paired mental states closely parallels the third foundation of mindfulness (contemplation of mind, *cittanupassana*) in the Satipatthana Sutra (MN 10 / DN 22). Both texts enumerate mind-states in contrasting pairs: with desire/without, with aversion/without, contracted/scattered, concentrated/unconcentrated, liberated/unliberated. The Sravakabhumi modifies the standard Nikaya list, replacing pairs such as "expansive/unexpansive" (*mahaggata/amahaggata*) and "supreme/not supreme" (*sa-uttara/anuttara*) with "sunken/agitated," "with restlessness/without restlessness," "with stillness/without stillness," and "well-cultivated/poorly cultivated," reflecting the Yogacara Abhidharma's more detailed analysis of meditative obstacles.

[^three_cessation_realms]: The "three realms" (三界) here are not the three realms of existence (desire, form, formless) but three aspects of cessation: (1) the realm of abandonment (*prahana-dhatu*, 斷界) — what the path of seeing eliminates, (2) the realm of dispassion (*viraga-dhatu*, 離欲界) — what the path of cultivation eliminates, and (3) the realm of cessation (*nirodha-dhatu*, 滅界) — final cessation of all conditioned existence. These three correspond to the last three of the sixteen practices (contemplating abandonment, dispassion, and cessation), replacing MN 118's single final step of "relinquishment" (*patinissagga*) with a more analytically precise framework.

[^skillful_objects]: The five skillful objects (*kaushala-alambana*) represent the analytical dimension of meditation practice — developing insight into the structure of experience through Abhidharma categories. The first three (skandhas, elements, sense bases) present the same reality parsed differently: the five skandhas classify by type of phenomenon, the eighteen elements by the triad of faculty/object/consciousness, and the twelve sense bases by the subject-object pairing. The fourth (dependent origination) reveals causal structure, and the fifth (possible/impossible) reveals moral causation. These correspond to standard topics elaborated in the Abhidharmakosha and other compendia. Cf. Mipham, *Gateway to Knowledge* Vol. 1.

[^eighteen_dhatus]: The eighteen elements (*ashtadasha dhatu*) here are the standard Abhidharma analysis of experience into six triads of faculty, object, and consciousness: eye/form/eye-consciousness, and so on for the six senses. This differs from the six elements (*sad-dhatu*) of section 27.2 (earth, water, fire, wind, space, consciousness), which classified the material and mental constituents of a person. The eighteen-element scheme focuses on the process of cognition rather than material composition.

[^three_conditions]: The three conditions (*pratyaya*) for consciousness to arise are foundational Sarvastivada/Yogacara Abhidharma: (1) the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*) — the sense faculty that governs cognition; (2) the object condition (*alambana-pratyaya*) — the object that consciousness apprehends; (3) the immediately preceding condition (*samanantara-pratyaya*) — the prior moment of consciousness whose cessation enables the next. For the five sense consciousnesses, the respective sense organ is the dominant condition. For mind consciousness, the text specifies that attention (*manaskara*) serves as the dominant condition, since there is no separate physical organ for mental cognition — a position characteristic of the Yogacara tradition (the Abhidharmakosha generally treats the mind faculty itself as the dominant condition for mind consciousness).

[^sthana_asthana]: The "possible and impossible" (*sthana-asthana*) is a form of causal reasoning: it is "possible" (*sthana*) that wholesome actions produce pleasant results, and "impossible" (*asthana*) that they produce unpleasant results, and vice versa. This is treated as a variant of dependent origination skill because both concern the lawful operation of causation. The topic features prominently in the Bahudhatu Sutra (MN 115) and in Mipham's *Gateway to Knowledge* Vol. 1.

[^affliction_purifying_objects]: The affliction-purifying objects (*klesha-vishodhana-alambana*, 淨惑所緣) are the fourth and final category of meditation objects. Unlike the purifying-conduct objects (which target specific character types) and the skillful objects (which develop analytical understanding), the affliction-purifying objects directly eliminate afflictions. The category comprises two paths: the worldly path of contemplating coarseness and peacefulness (a temporary method that suppresses afflictions realm by realm) and the supramundane path of the four noble truths (which eliminates afflictions permanently). Cf. Mipham, *Gateway to Knowledge* Vol. 1.

[^worldly_path]: The worldly or conventional path (*samvriti-marga*, 世俗道) achieves only temporary suppression of afflictions, not their root elimination. By contemplating the dangers of a lower realm and the relative peace of a higher realm, one develops dispassion toward the afflictions of that lower realm — but the underlying tendencies remain and can re-emerge. This proceeds realm by realm from the desire realm up to the base of nothingness; the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception has no higher realm against which to appear coarse.

[^three_craving_types]: The three types of craving (*trishna*) correspond to the standard Sarvastivada/Pali analysis of the truth of the origin: (1) craving for continued existence (*punarbhava-trishna*, 後有愛) — the desire for rebirth and perpetuation; (2) craving accompanied by delight and passion (*nandi-raga-sahagata-trishna*, 喜貪俱行愛) — attachment to objects already obtained; (3) craving for this and that pleasure (*tatra-tatrabhiratika-trishna*, 彼彼喜樂愛) — desire for objects not yet obtained.

[^suffering_as_cause]: The Sravakabhumi carefully distinguishes between suffering as an event and the *cause* of suffering. Birth itself (生) is not the painful feeling; rather, birth is the cause and condition (*hetu-pratyaya*) through which painful bodily and mental feelings arise. This precision reflects the Abhidharma position that the first noble truth identifies the conditions of suffering, not merely its sensation. The same analysis applies to each of the seven specific sufferings (birth through not-obtaining).

[^three_sufferings]: The three types of suffering (*tri-duhkhata*) are a foundational Abhidharma classification: (1) suffering of suffering (*duhkha-duhkhata*, 苦苦) — outright painful feeling and its basis, recognized by all beings; (2) suffering of change (*viparinama-duhkhata*, 壞苦) — the inherent instability of pleasant states, which deteriorate into pain, along with the afflictions that bind the mind when pleasure is present; (3) suffering of conditioned existence (*samskara-duhkhata*, 行苦) — the pervasive unsatisfactoriness of all conditioned skandhas, even those accompanied by neutral feeling. The suffering of conditioned existence is the most subtle: it is the baseline condition of all conditioned existence, operating beneath the surface of both pleasure and pain. Cf. Tsongkhapa, *The Great Treatise* Vol. 1, ch. on the truth of suffering; Visuddhimagga XVI.34-38.

[^four_objects_complete]: This sentence closes the entire taxonomy of meditation objects that spans fascicles 26–27: (1) pervasive objects (*sarvatraga-alambana*, 26.5) — the four objects shared by all shamatha and vipashyana practices; (2) purifying-conduct objects (*charya-vishodhana-alambana*, 26.6–27.3) — five objects paired with five character types: impurity for greed-conduct (26.6), loving-kindness for aversion-conduct (26.7), dependent origination for delusion-conduct (27.1), element differentiation for pride-conduct (27.2), and mindfulness of breathing for discursive-thought conduct (27.3); (3) skillful objects (*kaushala-alambana*, 27.4) — five analytical skills; (4) affliction-purifying objects (*klesha-vishodhana-alambana*, 27.5) — the worldly and supramundane paths of affliction elimination. The next section (27.6) transitions from objects to instruction methods.

[^four_instructions]: The four types of instruction (*avavada*, 教授) classify how qualified teachers transmit the path to students. This section marks the transition from the meditation objects taxonomy (what to meditate on) to the methodology of teaching (how the path is transmitted). The four types ensure that instruction is accurate (non-distorted), appropriately sequenced (gradual), grounded in tradition (teaching-based), and informed by personal experience (realization-based).

[^three_supernormal_displays]: The three supernormal displays (*tri-pratiharya*, 三神變) appear in DN 11 (Kevaddha Sutra), where the Buddha discusses the relative value of each. The Buddha prizes the "miracle of instruction" (*anushasani-pratiharya*) above the other two: magical powers may impress but can be imitated by non-Buddhists, and mind-reading may unsettle, but only correct instruction tailored to the student's capacity leads to liberation. The Sravakabhumi here presents all three as components of "instruction replete with all marks" — a teaching that combines the authority-establishing function of magical display, the diagnostic function of mind-reading, and the liberating function of personalized instruction.
