# Translator's Introduction

## The Sravakabhumi in Context

The *Sravakabhumi* (The Hearer's Ground) is a major treatise within the *Yogacarabhumi-sastra* (Treatise on the Stages of Yoga Practice), one of the foundational texts of the Yogacara school of Buddhist philosophy. Modern scholarly consensus attributes the *Sravakabhumi* to Asanga (c. late 4th–early 5th century CE), though the Chinese text follows the traditional attribution to Maitreya Bodhisattva. The work presents a comprehensive account of the path of the *shravaka* — the Buddhist practitioner who seeks liberation through direct hearing and practice of the Buddha's teaching.

The *Yogacarabhumi* as a whole is a vast encyclopedic work covering the entire range of Buddhist practice and doctrine. The *Sravakabhumi* occupies a specific section within it, devoted to the practitioner's path from initial orientation through meditation training to the attainment of the fruits of practice. It is at once a theoretical treatise and a practical manual, presenting both the doctrinal framework and the experiential details of the contemplative life.

## Significance of This Translation

The *Sravakabhumi* has exercised an outsized influence on the contemplative traditions of Asia, yet it has remained largely inaccessible to the English-speaking world. Its account of the nine stages of mental abiding (*nava-akara-citta-sthiti*) became the standard framework for shamatha instruction across both the Tibetan and East Asian traditions. Its detailed treatment of the four types of vipashyana, the thirty-seven factors of awakening, and the graduated cultivation of samadhi provided the doctrinal backbone for meditation manuals composed centuries later. Tsongkhapa's *Lam Rim Chen Mo*, Kamalashila's *Stages of Meditation*, and the contemplative traditions of all four major Tibetan Buddhist schools all draw substantially on this text.

Despite its importance, no complete English translation of the *Sravakabhumi* has previously existed. Florin Deleanu's landmark 2006 study translated and annotated the chapter on the mundane path (*laukikamarga*), but the remainder — the detailed meditation instruction, the practitioner typology, the path factors, the teacher-student protocols — has been available only in Xuanzang's Classical Chinese, Shukla's partially extant Sanskrit critical edition, and the Tibetan canon. Several factors account for this gap: the sheer scale of the *Yogacarabhumi* as a whole, the specialized nature of Xuanzang's Classical Chinese, the limited survival of the Sanskrit, and the fact that scholarly attention has historically favored shorter, more self-contained texts.

This translation aims to close that gap. It provides the first complete English rendering of the *Sravakabhumi* (fascicles 26–33) from any source language, covering the full scope of Asanga's treatment of the hearer's path: from the initial assessment of the practitioner's disposition, through the detailed objects and methods of meditation, to the attainment of the dhyanas, formless absorptions, and the superknowledges.

The text is organized into three major divisions — the Second, Third, and Fourth Yoga Places (*yogasthana*), each a major stage of the practitioner's path — each comprising one or more chapters. The translation covers fascicles 26 through 33 of Xuanzang's Chinese text (each fascicle is a scroll-volume unit of the Taisho Tripitaka). Bracketed references such as [T30.0424a07] cite the Chinese source text by volume, page, column, and line. All translator commentary appears in footnotes; conventions for abbreviations, terminology, and romanization are detailed in the appendices.

While the *Sravakabhumi* is studied as a historical and doctrinal text, it is also a practice manual — and its meditation instructions were designed for transmission from a qualified teacher to a prepared student. The graduated contemplations of fascicle 32, the breathing practices of fascicle 27, and the attentional training methods throughout presuppose a teacher-student relationship that this translation cannot replicate. Readers who wish to undertake the practices described here are encouraged to do so under the guidance of an experienced meditation teacher. The text's own framework — which opens every major practice section with the instruction of "a teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga" — underscores this point.
