**F30-N06**
- **Location:** Line 106, the four-fold investigation
- **Passage:** "(1) One should investigate the student's aspiration (*pranidhana*). (2) One should investigate their spiritual lineage (*gotra*). (3) One should investigate their faculties (*indriya*). (4) One should investigate their disposition (*carita*)."
- **Priority:** H (gotra is a major Yogacara concept appearing here without annotation)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^gotra_investigation]: The investigation of spiritual lineage (*gotra*, 種姓) is a distinctively Yogacara concern. *Gotra* refers to the innate spiritual disposition that determines which vehicle -- shravaka, pratyekabuddha, or Mahayana -- a practitioner is suited for. The concept is controversial: Madhyamaka critics (e.g., Candrakirti, Madhyamakavatara VI) reject fixed lineage determination, arguing all beings possess buddha-nature. The Yogacara position, as presented here, is that accurate assessment of the student's gotra is essential for the teacher to assign the appropriate teaching. The four methods of investigation (questioning, discourse, observation, mind-reading) reflect the difficulty of accurately discerning something so subtle. Cf. Bodhisattvabhumi, *Gotra-patala*; Mahayanasutralamkara III.`

- **Draft footnote:**

**F30-N08**
- **Location:** Line 126, investigation through discourse on the three vehicles
- **Passage:** "the teacher first expounds the teaching of the shravaka vehicle... the pratyekabuddha vehicle's teaching, and finally the great vehicle's"
- **Priority:** M (the three-vehicle framework is a significant doctrinal teaching)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^three_vehicles]: The three-vehicle (*triyana*) framework -- shravaka, pratyekabuddha, and Mahayana -- is foundational to Yogacara soteriology. The method described here -- presenting all three vehicles and observing the student's affective response as an indicator of their innate lineage -- is a distinctively Yogacara approach. The Lotus Sutra (Saddharmapundarika, chapter 3) presents the three vehicles as skillful means (*upaya*), ultimately converging in the one vehicle (*ekayana*). The Sravakabhumi, by contrast, treats each vehicle as a genuine path suited to a specific lineage. This passage is one of the earliest systematic descriptions of the teacher's method for vehicle-assignment.`

**F30-N09**
- **Location:** Line 130-140, disposition investigation through discourse
- **Passage:** "Those of greed-conduct (*raga-carita*), when they hear teachings of what is pure and beautiful, generate the utmost pure faith and loving delight... Their body-hair stands on end, tears stream from their eyes."

The Visuddhimagga (III.74-102) offers a parallel but distinct set of behavioral diagnostics.`

**F30-N10**
- **Location:** Line 164-166, guarding samadhi resources
- **Passage:** "practicing in even accord with what has been taught, neither adding to nor diminishing from it"
- **Priority:** M (the phrase "samadhi-sambhara" is a key technical concept)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^samadhi_sambhara]: "Samadhi resources" (*samadhi-sambhara*, 定資糧) are the prerequisite conditions for concentration. The Sravakabhumi identifies these as: moral discipline (*shila-samvara*), sense restraint (*indriya-samvara*), moderation in food, wakefulness in the first and last watches of the night, alertness (*samprajanya*) in activities, and the shramana adornments. The phrase "neither adding to nor diminishing" (*na adhika na una*) indicates that the practitioner should follow the Buddha's instructions precisely, without inventing additional observances or neglecting prescribed ones. **F30-N11**
- **Location:** Line 180-182, perfection of place
- **Passage:** "Whether a forest dwelling (*aranyaka*), the base of a tree, or an empty room"
- **Priority:** M (standard canonical dwelling places with Vinaya significance)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^three_dwelling_types]: The three types of dwelling -- *aranyaka* (阿練若, forest dwelling), tree-base (林樹下), and empty room (空閑室) -- are the standard canonical settings for meditation practice, appearing throughout the sutras in the stock formula "gone to the forest, to the base of a tree, or to an empty room" (*arannagato va rukkhamulagato va sunnagaragato va*; MN 10, MN 119, DN 22). The fifth quality, proximity to a *kalyanamitra*, reflects the Yogacara emphasis on the teacher-student relationship as essential to meditative progress.`

**F30-N12**
- **Location:** Line 196-200, cross-legged posture and five reasons
- **Passage:** "Why sit cross-legged? One correctly observes five reasons"
- **Priority:** L (distinctive Yogacara enumeration)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^five_reasons_posture]: The five reasons for cross-legged posture (*padmasana*) given here -- generating pliancy, sustaining practice, distinctiveness from non-Buddhists, inspiring faith, and the praise of Buddhas and noble ones -- form a characteristically Yogacara enumeration combining practical, social, and traditional considerations. The Visuddhimagga (III.128) simply prescribes the posture without offering reasons. The Sravakabhumi's third reason -- that cross-legged sitting is a "distinctive dharma practice, not found among non-Buddhist and other traditions" -- is notable, as similar postures are in fact used in some Indian yogic traditions; the claim may refer specifically to the full lotus combined with the other prescribed attitudes (straightened body, settled intention, counter-mindfulness).`

**F30-N13**
- **Location:** Line 206-208, counter-mindfulness
- **Passage:** "Mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) is called counter-mindfulness (背念) because it opposes and dispels all unwholesome qualities."
- **Priority:** H (distinctive and potentially confusing technical term)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^counter_mindfulness]: "Counter-mindfulness" (*pratismriti*?, 背念, lit. "back-turned mindfulness" or "opposing mindfulness") is a distinctive Sravakabhumi term that does not appear in the standard Pali commentarial vocabulary. The Chinese 背 means "to turn one's back on" or "to oppose." The term has two senses: (1) mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) that opposes all unwholesome qualities, and (2) mindfulness that takes the sign of samadhi as its object, thereby opposing all objects of the unconcentrated state. The term emphasizes the active, discriminating quality of mindfulness -- not merely neutral observation but purposeful turning away from the unwholesome toward the wholesome. This two-aspect definition anticipates the later Tibetan discussion of mindfulness as both a *retaining* and a *guarding* function.`

**F30-N14**
- **Location:** Line 228-236, samadhi definition
- **Passage:** "This mental one-pointedness belongs either to the shamatha (calm abiding) class or to the vipashyana (insight) class."
- **Priority:** M (key doctrinal point about the scope of samadhi)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^samadhi_dual_scope]: The Sravakabhumi's assertion that mental one-pointedness (*ekagrata*) belongs to both the shamatha and the vipashyana class is doctrinally significant. It means that vipashyana is not merely conceptual analysis but involves genuine concentration -- a unified, one-pointed mind operating within the analytical mode. This distinguishes the Yogacara position from interpretations that treat vipashyana as purely discursive. Tsongkhapa relies heavily on this passage in *The Great Treatise on the Stages of the Path* (Vol. 3) to argue that both shamatha and vipashyana involve *samadhi*, differing in their mode of operation (resting vs. analyzing) rather than in their degree of concentration.`

**F30-N16**
- **Location:** Line 298-306, four types of vipashyana section
- **Passage:** "correct discernment... thorough discernment... comprehensive investigation... comprehensive examination"
- **Priority:** H (four terms that define the entire vipashyana taxonomy, need clear differentiation)
- **Draft footnote:**
  `[^four_vipashyana_distinction]: The four vipashyana operations form two pairs. The first pair -- correct discernment (*samyak-pravicarana*, 正思擇) and thorough discernment (*paramastha-pravicarana*, 最極思擇) -- correspond to the two modes of knowing: discerning the full extent of what exists (*yavad-bhavikata*) is *quantitative* completeness (how many types, how many instances); discerning the nature of things as they are (*yathavad-bhavikata*) is *qualitative* accuracy (what they truly are). The second pair -- comprehensive investigation (*samantato-vitarka*, 周遍尋思) and comprehensive examination (*samantato-vicara*, 周遍伺察) -- are the two standard Abhidharma mental factors of *vitarka* (coarse investigation) and *vicara* (subtle examination), applied "comprehensively" (*samantato*) to the full range of objects. Investigation is the initial conceptual engagement; examination is the refined scrutiny that follows. Cf. Abhidharmakosha II.33 for the vitarka/vicara distinction.`

## 8. Pattern Diagnosis

The under-annotation of F29 and F30 follows a consistent pattern:

1. **Section-summary notes only.** Both fascicles have one footnote per major section (29.1-29.17, 30.1-30.16) that summarizes the section's place in the thirty-seven factors or the Third Yoga Place. What is missing is *inline* annotation -- glosses at the point of first use, cross-tradition parallels for individual passages, and textual-critical notes.

2. **Missing first-use glosses.** F26 consistently provides parenthetical glosses at first mention of a technical term, often with a footnote expanding on it. F29 and F30 provide the Sanskrit parenthetical but omit the footnote. Terms like *nivarana*, *samyojana*, *avetya-prasada*, *arya-kanta-shila*, *gotra*, *paracitta-jnana*, and *pratismriti/counter-mindfulness* all appear without explanatory notes.

3. **Missing cross-tradition parallels.** F26 routinely cites Pali Nikaya parallels, Abhidharmakosha references, and Visuddhimagga comparisons. F29 and F30 restrict such references to the section-summary notes and omit them from the body of the text.

4. **Missing practitioner-oriented notes.** The nine stages of mental abiding (F30) and the four right efforts (F29) are among the most practice-relevant passages in the entire Sravakabhumi. A practitioner reader would benefit from notes connecting these instructions to lived meditation experience -- e.g., the balance of uplifting and holding, the danger of overestimation, the physical signs of disposition types.
