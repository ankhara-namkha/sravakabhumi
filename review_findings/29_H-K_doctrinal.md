## Findings

### 1. [m] Four Wholesome Roots: Mundane Status Correctly Presented

**Section 29.8, lines 211 and 230; footnote [^four_roots]**

The translation correctly presents the four wholesome roots as mundane stages preceding the supramundane path. The footnote accurately states: "represent the final stages of mundane practice before entry into the supramundane path" and "supreme worldly dharma is the highest point of mundane realization -- the moment immediately preceding entry into the path of seeing." The fire analogy correctly maps supreme worldly dharma to the flameless fire (still mundane) and the blazing flames to the supramundane dharmas that follow.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound. No issue.

---

### 2. [m] Fire Analogy: Flameless Fire = Supreme Worldly Dharma

**Section 29.8, line 229-230**

The Chinese 如無焰火欻然流出。世第一法當知亦爾 maps flameless fire (無焰火) to supreme worldly dharma. The translation correctly says "The flameless fire bursting forth corresponds to supreme worldly dharma." Then the blazing flames that follow "without interruption" (無間) correspond to the supramundane undefiled noble dharmas. This accurately captures the critical doctrinal point that supreme worldly dharma, though the highest mundane attainment, is itself NOT yet supramundane -- it is the flameless fire, not the blaze.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound. The mapping is precise and doctrinally important.

---

### 3. [M] Seven Factors of Awakening: "Certainty of Correctness" Needs Clarification

**Section 29.9, line 238**

The translation says: "For persons who have already entered the certainty of correctness (samyaktva-niyama)." The footnote [^seven_bodhyanga] correctly identifies this as stream-entry. However, the main text does not explicitly identify "certainty of correctness" as stream-entry. The Chinese 已證入正性離生 literally means "already entered the fixed nature of leaving birth" (samyaktva-niyama), which is a standard Abhidharma technical term for the moment of entering the path of seeing / stream-entry.

A reader unfamiliar with Abhidharma terminology may not understand what "certainty of correctness" means from the main text alone. The footnote handles this, but the gap between main text and footnote is large enough that it could cause momentary confusion.

**Recommendation:** Not a doctrinal error per se, but consider adding a brief gloss on first use in the main text -- e.g., "the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*) -- that is, stream-entry" -- to make the critical doctrinal point immediately clear.

### 4. [m] Shamatha/Vipashyana Classification of Awakening Factors: Correct

**Section 29.9, lines 242-244**

The classification is:
- Vipashyana: investigation of dharmas, effort, joy
- Shamatha: ease, samadhi, equanimity
- Both (pervasive): mindfulness

This matches the Chinese source exactly (line 102): 擇法覺支。精進覺支。喜覺支。此三觀品所攝。安覺支。定覺支。捨覺支。此三止品所攝。念覺支一種俱品所攝。說名遍行。

This classification is consistent with both the Yogacara-Sarvastivada and the Theravada traditions. The Visuddhimagga (Ch. XXII, sections 38-43) presents the same grouping. The Samyutta Nikaya (SN 46.53) also distinguishes between awakening factors suited for an overly sluggish mind (investigation, effort, joy = vipashyana) and those for an overly agitated mind (ease, samadhi, equanimity = shamatha), with mindfulness being always useful.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound.

### 5. [M] Eightfold Path: Right Effort in Prajna Training -- Correct but Footnote Should Be More Explicit About the Doctrinal Significance

**Section 29.10, line 252; footnote [^eightfold_path]**

The translation places right effort in the prajna (wisdom) training: "right view, right thought, and right effort belong to the training of wisdom (prajna)." This directly follows the Chinese source (line 104): 正見正思惟正精進。慧蘊所攝.

This is the **Yogacara-Sarvastivada classification**, which differs from the **Theravada classification** (where right effort belongs to the samadhi/concentration aggregate). The Theravada classification is attested at MN I 301 (cited in Visuddhimagga XVI.95-98):
- Concentration aggregate: right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration
- Understanding aggregate: right view, right intention

The footnote [^eightfold_path] correctly notes: "The assignment of right effort to the prajna training (rather than samadhi) and right mindfulness to the samadhi training follows the Yogacara-Sarvastivada Abhidharma classification." This is accurate and important.

However, the footnote could be more explicit about *why* this classification differs. In the Yogacara-Sarvastivada system, the rationale is that right effort functions primarily to support the operation of prajna (energizing investigation and wisdom) rather than serving as a direct component of meditative stabilization. The Visuddhimagga's rationale for placing it in samadhi is that effort assists concentration by "exerting" while mindfulness assists by "preventing wobbling" (XVI.96-98). The different placement reflects genuinely different understandings of the functional relationship between effort and the path.

Additionally, the secondary two-fold classification (line 294) -- right view, right thought, and right effort as vipashyana; right mindfulness and right samadhi as shamatha -- further confirms this is consistent with the Yogacara framework where effort is grouped with the insight/wisdom functions.

**Recommendation:** The footnote handles this well. Consider adding one sentence about the Sarvastivada rationale: that effort energizes the discriminative functions (investigation, view, thought) and thus belongs to prajna, whereas in the Theravada, effort sustains the stability required for concentration and thus belongs to samadhi.

### 6. [m] Non-Active vs. Active Path Factor Distinction: Correct

**Section 29.10, line 294**

"The non-active are right speech, right action, and right livelihood. The active are further divided into two: shamatha and vipashyana."

The Chinese (line 120): 無所作者。謂正語正業正命。住所作者。復有二種。謂奢摩他毘鉢舍那。

This distinction between "non-active" (無所作) and "active" (住所作) path factors is a distinctive Yogacara-Sarvastivada teaching. The three abstention-based factors (speech, action, livelihood) are "non-active" because they are obtained at the moment of stream-entry as abstentions (離, virati) -- they represent a cessation of wrong conduct, not an active practice. The remaining five are "active" because they require ongoing cultivation.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound. This is an important and often-overlooked distinction that the translation handles well.

### 7. [m] Discipline Beloved of Noble Ones: Five Abstentions Correctly Identified

**Section 29.10, lines 282**

The five abstentions are:
1. Never knowingly speak falsehoods (妄語)
2. Never intentionally take the life of a sentient being (害眾生命)
3. Never take what is not given (不與而取)
4. Never engage in sexual misconduct (欲邪行)
5. Never unlawfully seek robes and the like (非法求衣服等 = wrong livelihood)

The Chinese source (line 116) gives exactly these five: 終不正知而說妄語。終不故思害眾生命。終不故思不與而取。終不故思行欲邪行。終不非法求衣服等。

These correspond to the standard formulation of the arya-kanta-shila obtained at stream-entry: abstention from the four unwholesome bodily/verbal actions plus wrong livelihood. This is doctrinally correct and matches the Anguttara Nikaya's description of the stream-enterer's discipline.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound.

### 8. [m] Path of Cultivation vs. Path of Seeing: Translation Handles a Subtle Point

**Section 29.10, line 296**

"What unfolds through sustained practice over long duration is the path of cultivation -- because extended cultivation cuts off afflictions. What arises in a single flash of wisdom is the path of seeing -- because momentary wisdom permanently severs afflictions."

The Chinese (line 120): 長時相續名為修道。多時串習斷煩惱故。率爾智生名為見道。暫時智起即能永斷諸煩惱故。

This captures the standard Abhidharma distinction between:
- Path of seeing (darshana-marga): a single momentary arising of wisdom that permanently severs the afflictions abandoned by seeing (view-related afflictions: identity view, doubt, wrong grasp of rules)
- Path of cultivation (bhavana-marga): extended practice over time that gradually attenuates and eventually eliminates the afflictions abandoned by cultivation (desire, aversion, etc.)

The phrase "single flash of wisdom" for 率爾智生 is a reasonable rendering of what is technically the momentary wisdom-consciousness at the path of seeing.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound.

### 9. [m] Right Speech, Action, Livelihood "First Established Within the Path of Cultivation"

**Section 29.10, line 296**

"For this reason, right speech, right action, and right livelihood are first established within the path of cultivation."

The Chinese (line 120): 由是因緣。正語業命於修道中方始建立。

This is a subtle and important doctrinal point. The text says that right speech, action, and livelihood are "first established" (方始建立) in the path of cultivation. This might seem to contradict line 274 which says they were "already obtained at the time of realizing the undefiled attention belonging to the awakening factors" (i.e., at stream-entry / path of seeing).

The resolution: The three abstention factors are *obtained* at the moment of stream-entry as non-active (virati) factors. But they are *first established as path factors* -- i.e., first function as active components of the eightfold path in their full sense -- during the path of cultivation, when the practitioner actively cultivates right speech, action, and livelihood as bases for shamatha and vipashyana practice. The footnote captures this distinction between "non-active" and "active" uses.

**Verdict:** Doctrinally sound but potentially confusing. The apparent contradiction between lines 274 and 296 is real in the source text and correctly preserved. The footnote helps but a reader might still stumble.
