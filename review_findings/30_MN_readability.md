**Lines:** 293–364

## Overall Assessment

Sections 30.13–30.14 are substantially stronger than earlier fascicle 30 drafts. The three-doors passage (lines 320–326) in particular is well-written — clear, alive, and genuinely instructive. The six-aspects section holds together structurally but has scattered issues: some translated-ese in the repeated investigative frames, minor formula monotony, and a few nested constructions. No catastrophic failures. The material should be accessible to a dedicated practitioner with minimal Buddhist background, though the final philosophical synthesis (lines 356–362) reads somewhat compressed and abstract.

## Findings

### F1 — Translated-ese / Formula monotony (lines 332–341)

**Type:** (1) Translated-ese + (2) Formula monotony

The six-aspect definitions for items (1) through (5) all follow the same rigid template: "One investigates [object]." This is accurate but deadening — five consecutive sentences sharing identical structure with only the noun changing.

**Flagged text:**
```
(1) Meaning. One investigates how such-and-such words bear such-and-such meanings.
(2) Things. One investigates the two domains of things: internal and external.
(3) Characteristics. One investigates the two characteristics of all dharmas: own-characteristic (svalakshana) and shared characteristic (samanya-lakshana).
(4) Categories. One investigates the two categories of all dharmas: the black category and the white category — investigating the faults and dangers of the black, and the virtues and benefits of the white.
(5) Time. One investigates the three times — past, present, and future: "This matter existed in the past"; "This will exist in the future"; "This exists in the present."
```

**Problem:** Five "One investigates..." openers in sequence. Item (4) then adds a second "investigating..." mid-sentence, creating a repetition within repetition. The quoted speech in (5) sounds artificial — like a parsing exercise, not a meditation instruction.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> **(1) Meaning.** What do these words signify? One considers how each term carries its particular meaning.
>
> **(2) Things.** One turns attention to the domain of things themselves — internal phenomena and external.
>
> **(3) Characteristics.** Each dharma has two characteristics to be known: its own-characteristic (*svalakshana*), which is specific to it, and its shared characteristic (*samanya-lakshana*), which it holds in common with others.
>
> **(4) Categories.** All dharmas fall into two categories: black and white. One examines the faults and dangers of the black; one examines the virtues and benefits of the white.
>
> **(5) Time.** One considers each matter in its temporal dimension — whether it belongs to the past, present, or future.

### F2 — Nested qualification / Translated-ese (line 304)

**Type:** (1) Translated-ese + (3) Nested qualification

**Flagged text:**
> "Regarding those same objects, through attention conjoined with wisdom and accompanied by conceptual thought, one grasps their characteristics and investigates them comprehensively."

The phrase "attention conjoined with wisdom and accompanied by conceptual thought" is technically precise but structurally awkward — three adverbial modifiers stacked before the main verb. The phrase "grasps their characteristics and investigates them comprehensively" also feels assembled rather than written.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Regarding those same objects, one brings them into focus through wise, conceptually engaged attention — examining their characteristics in a thorough and systematic way."

Or more flowing:
> "One brings wise attention — here still operating with conceptual thought — to these same objects, taking up their characteristics for comprehensive investigation."

### F3 — Unexplained jargon (line 300)

**Type:** (6) Unexplained jargon

**Flagged text:**
> "purifying-conduct objects, skillful objects, or affliction-purifying objects"

These three object categories appear without definition. A practitioner coming to this fresh has no handle on what distinguishes them. The footnote at line 308 explains *yavad-bhavikata* and *yathavad-bhavikata* but says nothing about these three object types, which are equally opaque.

**Suggested fix:**
Add a brief bracketed or inline gloss at first use:

> "purifying-conduct objects [objects that support proper ethical conduct], skillful objects [beneficial mental factors and their bases], or affliction-purifying objects [objects whose investigation dissolves the afflictions]"

Or add a footnote parallel to footnote `four_vipashyana`.

### F4 — Register drift (lines 356–362)

**Type:** (4) Register drift

**Flagged text:**
> "Why are these six aspects established? They rest on three types of understanding. Investigating meaning gives rise to understanding of linguistic meaning. Investigating things and own-characteristics gives rise to understanding of the limits of things. Investigating shared characteristics, categories, time, and reasoning gives rise to understanding of reality.
>
> The entire field of knowledge for the practitioner of yoga consists in just this: linguistic meaning and knowable things — the full extent of what exists and the nature of things as they are."

This closing synthesis is the most philosophically dense moment in the two sections, and it reads like a compressed academic summary rather than a living conclusion. The three-sentence enumeration of correspondences (investigating X gives rise to understanding of Y) is mechanical. The final sentence ("The entire field of knowledge for the practitioner of yoga consists in just this...") is doing real work — it's the payoff — but "the practitioner of yoga" is abstract and the sentence's grammar is slightly stiff.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> Why are these six aspects established? Because they map onto three distinct modes of understanding. Investigating meaning opens up comprehension of what words convey. Investigating things and their own-characteristics reveals the limits of things as they are. Investigating shared characteristics, categories, time, and reasoning brings one to an understanding of reality itself.
>
> For the practitioner of yoga, these two — knowing what words mean and knowing what things are, in their full extent and as they truly are — exhaust the entire field of knowable objects.

The restructuring keeps the three-part correspondence but varies the sentence openers, and the final sentence now reads as a satisfying summation rather than a formula.

### F5 — Minor: "such-and-such" (lines 332, 346)

**Type:** (1) Translated-ese

**Flagged text (line 332):**
> "One investigates how such-and-such words bear such-and-such meanings."

**Flagged text (line 346):**
> "One investigates the specific function of each dharma — how such-and-such dharmas have such-and-such functions."

"Such-and-such" is a translator's placeholder rendering of a Chinese demonstrative. It reads as bureaucratic English. Both occurrences should be naturalized.

**Line 332 suggested rewrite:**
> "One investigates what meaning each word or phrase conveys."

**Line 346 suggested rewrite:**
> "One investigates the specific function of each dharma — what each one actually does."

### F6 — Long sentence (line 350)

**Type:** (5) >50-word sentence

**Flagged text:**
> "Regarding the established nature of dharmas as they truly are — their inconceivable nature, their abiding nature — one generates faith and understanding rather than attempting further analysis or conceptual elaboration."

Word count: 36 words. Does not technically trigger the >50-word flag. However, the em-dash parenthetical ("— their inconceivable nature, their abiding nature —") buries the main verb "one generates faith." This is the most contemplatively significant statement in the passage — the point where discursive investigation stops and faith begins — and its impact is softened by the appositive interruption.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Some dharmas have an inconceivable, self-abiding nature that simply is what it is. Here the appropriate response is not further analysis or conceptual elaboration but faith and understanding."

This brings the key epistemic move (stopping analysis, opening to faith) into direct, punchy relief.
