## Critical Issues

**C1 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "dharma" (line 185)**
Severity: Critical

The word "dharma" appears multiple times in the conduct portraits (e.g., "desirable dharmas," "hateful dharmas," "confusing dharmas," "dharmas that occasion pride," "dharmas that provoke discursive thought"). A reader with no background has no idea what "dharmas" means in this context. It does not function as a proper noun here; it is being used to mean something like "mental objects," "things," or "phenomena." The usage is especially opaque because the same word is used elsewhere in the text to mean "the Buddhist teachings" (e.g., "the meaning of dharma whether well or poorly explained," line 193; "following the vinaya" and "dharma-followers," line 244).

The footnote on prapanca (line 284) explains that term, but there is no footnote or inline gloss for "dharma" in this specialized sense.

Suggested fix: Add a bracketed gloss on first specialized use: "desirable dharmas [mental phenomena associated with desire]." Alternatively, add a translator's note at the beginning of section (3) explaining the technical usage. At minimum, ensure consistency — if "dharma" is glossed once, it need not be repeated.

---

**C2 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "outflows" (line 250, 290)**
Severity: Critical

"Permanent exhaustion of all outflows" appears without explanation. A reader unfamiliar with Abhidharma will not know that "outflows" (*asrava*) refers to the mental taints or effluents — the deepest afflictive tendencies — whose elimination marks arhatship. The footnote (line 290) alludes to this ("without having exhausted all outflows — i.e., they have deep concentration but have not yet become an arhat") but does not define "outflows" itself.

Suggested fix: First use at line 250 should include a gloss: "outflows (*asrava*) [the deep-seated mental taints whose elimination marks liberation]" or a cross-reference footnote.

---

**C3 — Passage requiring guesswork: "thin-dust-conduct" (line 181)**
Severity: Critical

The text lists "a person of thin-dust nature is called a person of thin-dust-conduct" but provides no portrait and no explanation whatsoever. The footnote (line 282) notes only that "The balanced-conduct and thin-dust-conduct types receive no separate portrait here, as their characteristics were sufficiently described in 26.2." But a reader of only section 26.3 (which is a standalone titled section) has no way to retrieve that information. The term "thin-dust" is opaque on its face. What does it mean to have a "thin-dust nature"?

Suggested fix: Add a brief translator's note here (not only in the [^carita] footnote) pointing the reader back to 26.2, or add a parenthetical: "thin-dust nature [a person whose afflictions are naturally mild and close to the surface; see section 26.2]."

---

**C4 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "gotra" / "lineage" in section (4) (lines 209–213)**
Severity: Critical

The term "lineage" (*gotra*) is introduced and then immediately used in a doctrinally specialized way: a person's lineage is fixed and determines their final path regardless of their current aspiration. This is a distinctive and non-obvious Yogacara doctrine. Without explanation at the point of introduction, the reader cannot follow the argument. The paragraph at line 209 assumes the reader knows what spiritual lineage means and why it differs from aspiration.

The footnote (line 286) explains the distinction well but is at the end of the section. Readers who do not follow footnotes will be lost at the critical move in the argument.

Suggested fix: Move the core explanation of lineage vs. aspiration into a brief bracketed translator's note immediately after the first use of "lineage (*gotra*)" in line 209, or restructure the footnote as an inline note. At minimum, make the footnote reference marker appear on the word "lineage" at line 209 rather than buried at line 211.

---

**C5 — Passage requiring guesswork: "liberation of one without internal perception of form perceiving external forms" (line 252)**
Severity: Critical

The eight liberations are listed in running prose (lines 252–252) without any orienting explanation at point of entry. The descriptions are highly compressed and use Abhidharma technical language ("one with form perceiving forms," "one without internal perception of form perceiving external forms," "the liberation of the pure"). These phrases are not self-explanatory. A practitioner reader cannot determine what these states mean or how they differ from one another.

The footnote (line 290) gives a numbered list with brief descriptions, but it appears after the prose list and some readers will not connect them.

Suggested fix: Either (a) replace the prose list with a numbered list matching the footnote's structure for easier reference, or (b) move the footnote content inline as a short table or parenthetical gloss for each liberation, or (c) add a sentence before the list: "These are technical states described in detail in the footnote below; the key point is that this person has mastered all of them."

## Moderate Issues

**M1 — Sentence over 50 words (line 185, greed-conduct portrait)**
The sentence beginning "A person of greed-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of desire..." continues through "...binds them for an extended period." and then immediately continues with additional compound sentences. The longest single sentence in this paragraph is approximately 55 words.

More significantly, the paragraph as a whole is a continuous block of approximately 220 words with no paragraph breaks, listing approximately 15–20 distinct characteristics. This creates severe readability fatigue.

Suggested fix: Break the conduct portraits into short thematic sub-groups with blank lines between them. For example: (1) the pattern of entanglement; (2) physical/facial demeanor; (3) relational behavior; (4) mental/emotional traits.

---

**M2 — Sentence over 50 words (line 189, aversion-conduct portrait)**
The aversion portrait paragraph is approximately 280 words with no breaks and contains approximately 20 distinct characteristics listed in rapid succession. Individual sentences are largely short, but the cumulative run-on effect is significant.

Suggested fix: Same structural fix as M1 — add thematic sub-groups.

---

**M3 — Awkward English: "overwhelmed by desirable dharmas and cannot overcome those desirable dharmas" (line 185)**
The repetition of "desirable dharmas" in consecutive clauses is stilted. The phrasing "overwhelmed by X and cannot overcome those X" is a literal rendering of a Chinese rhetorical pattern that does not transfer naturally into English.

Suggested fix: "They are overwhelmed by desirable phenomena and cannot free themselves from them."

---

**M4 — Awkward English: "They think badly what they think, speak badly what they speak, and do badly what they do" (line 193)**
This triple phrase is a rendering of a Chinese rhetorical figure. In English it reads as a logical tautology and sounds more like a children's riddle than a descriptive passage. It is technically meaningful (the point is that the quality of their thinking, speaking, and acting is poor) but the construction is unnatural.

Suggested fix: "Whatever they think, they think it poorly; whatever they say, they say it poorly; whatever they do, they do it poorly."

---

**M5 — Unexplained jargon: "paryavasthana" (line 185)**
The Sanskrit term *paryavasthana* appears in parentheses after "entanglement" on first use. The choice to provide the Sanskrit is appropriate, but "entanglement" as a rendering of this term is used throughout all five portraits without any explanation of what it means technically. An "entanglement" in ordinary English is something that traps you; here it appears to mean something like "active manifestation of an affliction." The term is used as a technical category throughout the portraits.

Suggested fix: Add a bracketed gloss on first use: "entanglement (*paryavasthana*) [the active, surface-level expression of an affliction]."

---

**M6 — Awkward English: "They are obtuse and mute, using gestures in place of words" (line 193)**
"Obtuse and mute" in English primarily suggests intellectual dullness and literal inability to speak. In context the text means something more specific: they are intellectually slow and use nonverbal communication because of that slowness. "Mute" is misleading — it implies a speech impediment, not a cognitive one.

Suggested fix: "They are dull and inarticulate, relying on gestures rather than speech."

---

**M7 — Logical flow breaks: section (4) aspiration/lineage argument (lines 209–213)**
The argument in section (4) moves through three distinct claims: (a) persons of any lineage can generate aspiration for any vehicle; (b) but lineage is fixed and will override aspiration; (c) therefore this text only discusses shravaka-lineage persons with shravaka aspiration. The reader must hold all three steps together to follow the conclusion at line 213.

The transition from (b) to (c) is abrupt: "In the present context, it should be known that only persons of shravaka-vehicle aspiration and shravaka lineage are discussed." This sentence appears without a logical connector making clear why this scope restriction follows from what preceded.

Suggested fix: Add a transitional phrase: "Since the Sravakabhumi concerns the Hearer's Ground, only persons of shravaka-vehicle aspiration and shravaka lineage are discussed here."

---

**M8 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "root dhyana" (*maula-dhyana*) (line 226)**
The term "root dhyana" is used to explain the difference between painful and pleasant courses of progress but is not explained. A reader knows that "dhyana" means a meditative state (if they have read earlier material with the glossary note), but "root dhyana" vs. something else is not clarified. The footnote (line 288) explains "painful/pleasant" relates to whether one has attained the first dhyana or higher, but this explanation is in the footnote, not inline.

Suggested fix: Add a parenthetical: "root dhyana (*maula-dhyana*) [the actual first dhyana or higher meditative absorption, as distinguished from the preliminary access states]."

---

**M9 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "shramana fruits" (line 238)**
"Shramana fruits" appears once without explanation. A reader who does not know that "shramana" (renunciant) is being used here as an umbrella term for the four fruits of stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, and arhatship will not understand why this term appears in a sentence about fruit-abiders.

Suggested fix: Either remove "shramana" (it is redundant given context) or add a gloss: "the shramana fruits [the four fruits of liberation just described]."

---

**M10 — Unexplained jargon on first use: "vinaya" (line 244)**
"Vinaya" is used without any gloss or context. A practitioner reader may not know this refers to the monastic code or, more broadly, to the rules of practice/discipline.

Suggested fix: "following the vinaya [the code of discipline]" on first appearance.

---

**M11 — Unexplained jargon: "body-witness" (*kayasakshi*) (line 250)**
The term "body-witness" is introduced as a category of person established by meditative concentration. The name is metaphorical and not self-explanatory in English. The footnote (line 290) explains the distinction (mastery of the eight liberations without arhatship) but a reader unfamiliar with the idiom will not understand the name itself.

Suggested fix: Add an inline gloss: "the body-witness (*kayasakshi*) [one who has directly experienced, 'witnessed through the body,' the full range of meditative liberations]."

---

**M12 — Unexplained jargon: "clan-to-clan practitioners" and "those with one interval" (line 258)**
Section (9) lists eight types of persons by birth using technical terms — "at most seven lives," "clan-to-clan practitioners," "those with one interval," "those who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state" — without any explanation. This is the only section that provides a bare list with no description whatsoever. The contrast with sections (1)–(8), each of which has some explanatory prose, makes this abrupt.

A reader cannot determine what "clan-to-clan" or "one interval" mean, nor how "parinirvana in the intermediate state" differs from "parinirvana upon rebirth."

Suggested fix: Either (a) expand the section with brief descriptions (even one sentence each), or (b) add a translator's note explaining that detailed treatment appears in a later section and giving a forward reference, or (c) at minimum add a note: "[These types are defined in detail in fascicle XX; here they are listed for structural completeness.]"

---

**M13 — Awkward English: "kleshaavarana" and "samadhyavarana" (line 270)**
The compound Sanskrit terms appear with a double vowel ("kleshaavarana," "samadhyavarana") due to sandhi or a rendering choice. In English these look like typos. "Kleshaavarana" with two a's is not standard in English-language Buddhist scholarship.

Suggested fix: Render as "klesha-avarana" and "samadhi-avarana" (hyphenated) or "klesavarana" and "samadhi-avarana" following standard transliteration.

## Notes for Agent Synthesis

- The conduct portraits (section 3) are the most readability-intensive passage in 26.3. They are structurally clear but dense. Chunking the paragraphs would significantly improve accessibility without altering content.
- The eight liberations (section 8) and the birth-types (section 9) are the most opaque to a non-specialist reader. Section 9 in particular may require the most editorial attention.
- Footnotes are generally well-written but are inconsistently positioned relative to the terms they gloss. Several footnote markers appear at the end of long passages rather than at the precise term requiring explanation.
- No sentence-level grammar errors were found. The prose is formal but generally correct.
