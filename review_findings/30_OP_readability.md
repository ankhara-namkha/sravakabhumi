# Agent R — Readability Review
## Fascicle 30, Section 30.15: "Vipashyana Applied to the Contemplation of Impurity"
## Lines 366–430

---

## Overall Assessment

This section is largely strong. The enumerated stages of corpse decomposition (items 1–9) are genuinely well rendered — precise, uncluttered, and would hold a practitioner's attention. The "Reasoning from dependence" paragraph is the most problematic passage: it is dense, over-long, and sounds assembled rather than written. Several other passages have buried main verbs, minor formula monotony, and one case of unexplained jargon. The "Categories" section is commendable but also slightly unwieldy. The section overall passes at a B/B+ level. Specific issues follow.

---

## Findings

### Issue 1 — Formula Monotony (Flag 2) + Nested Qualification (Flag 3)
**Location:** Lines 376–377 (Own-Characteristic — Internal Impurity)

**Original:**
> "One knows that within the body there are hair, body-hair, and so forth through to brain-membrane and urine. One further classifies these many impure substances into two elements. From hair and body-hair through to feces, one develops the conviction of the earth element. From tears and filth through to urine, one develops the conviction of the water element."

**Problem:** "One develops the conviction of X element" appears twice in immediate succession. On its own that's not a violation of the 3-repetition rule, but the phrase "develops the conviction of" also appears four more times in the following paragraph (lines 377–402). Across the full section, "develops the conviction of" / "one develops the conviction" appears at least six times: lividity (384), putrefaction (386), linked skeleton (398), bone-chain (400), and both elements here. That is formula monotony.

Secondary problem: "From X through to Y, one develops the conviction of the Z element" is a mechanical rendering of a Chinese pivot-structure. It reads as gloss.

**Suggested rewrite of the internal-impurity passage:**
> "One knows that within the body there are hair, body hair, and so forth through to brain-membrane and urine, and classifies these impure substances according to element: the solids from hair and body hair through to feces belong to earth; the fluids from tears and filth through to urine belong to water."

For the corpse stages, vary the formula. Rather than repeating "one develops the conviction of" at every step, use it once at the opening of the enumeration (something like: "At each stage, one cultivates a clear conviction of what the body has become:") and then let the subsequent items stand without repeating the phrase. The practitioner understands the contemplative act; re-stating it at every step is mechanical rather than instructive.

### Issue 2 — Overly Long Sentence / Nested Qualification (Flags 3, 5)
**Location:** Line 416 (Reasoning from dependence, the main block-quote paragraph)

**Original (measured at roughly 130 words in one sentence-block):**
> "Neither internally nor externally is there any self or being who can be called pure or impure. There are only forms, only bodily shapes. Within these, purely by convention and designation, we speak of 'pure' or 'impure.' As it is said: when life, warmth, and consciousness abandon the body, it lies bereft of its sustaining forces — without thought, like a log. After death, the body progressively deteriorates through knowable stages, from lividity through to bone-chain. My present body was brought into being by prior karma and afflictions, born from the union of my parents' impure substances, sustained and grown by food. For these reasons, although temporarily it appears to have clean forms, within the body — at all times — various impurities fill it completely."

**Problem:** The final sentence of this passage carries three layers of qualification: "although temporarily it appears to have clean forms, within the body — at all times — various impurities fill it completely." The em-dash interruption around "at all times" is awkward and weakens the landing. More broadly, this passage presents five distinct logical moves in rapid succession (no-self claim → conventional designation → post-death deterioration → conditions of origination → present impurity). Each deserves a breath. The passage sounds like a list of doctrinal points rather than the experience of a contemplative working through reasoning.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Neither internally nor externally is there any self or being who could be called purely pure or impure — only forms, only bodily shapes, designated as 'pure' or 'impure' by convention alone. When life, warmth, and consciousness abandon the body, it lies bereft of its sustaining forces, without thought, like a log; and from that point it deteriorates through every stage described above, from lividity to bone-chain. As for this present body: it was brought into being by prior karma and afflictions, born from the union of my parents' impure substances, sustained and grown by food. So even now, though it temporarily presents clean forms, impurities fill it at every moment, in every layer."

This breaks the passage into three shorter logical units, removes the awkward em-dash, and grounds the conclusion in the practitioner's own body rather than ending on an abstract claim.

### Issue 3 — Nested Qualification (Flag 3) + Translated-ese (Flag 1)
**Location:** Line 408 (Shared Characteristic paragraph)

**Original:**
> "Yet through the conviction that the impure nature of one's own body is equal to and of the same kind as the corpse, one can know: 'My apparently clean form possesses this same nature.' All beings who possess apparently clean forms — their clean forms likewise possess this same nature, just as the external impure forms."

**Problem:** "apparently clean form / apparently clean forms" appears three times in this short paragraph alone (and also twice in the "Time" section at line 412, and twice more in "Categories" at line 410). That is seven occurrences in roughly four paragraphs — formula monotony verging on numbing. Additionally, the phrase "equal to and of the same kind as" is a translated-ese doublet; in English one of these words does the work of both.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Yet by recognizing that one's own body shares the same impure nature as the corpse — however clean it looks now — one can see: 'My body carries this same nature.' The bodies of all beings, however presentable, carry it too — just as the deteriorated corpse before me does."

For the broader "apparently clean" repetition across the section: vary the phrasing. Alternatives: "outwardly clean," "seemingly clean," "presenting a clean surface," "the body as it presently appears." The repetition of the exact phrase "apparently clean forms" signals that no editorial pass has varied it.

### Issue 4 — Register Drift / Catalog Feel (Flag 4)
**Location:** Lines 384–402 (the nine stages of decomposition)

**Assessment:** This is the best-rendered portion of the section. The numbered list format is appropriate and each entry is concise. However, items (8) and (9) — "Linked skeleton" and "Bone-chain" — drop from the clear contemplative voice of items 1–7 into a technical-distinction register ("When one attends in detail to the joints and their articulations..."). The explanatory paragraph after item 9 (lines 402–406) clarifies the distinction between linked skeleton and bone-chain, which is necessary, but reads as a scholarly footnote inserted into the meditation instruction.

**No rewrite required,** but consider moving the technical distinction about trunk chain vs. limb chain (line 402) into a footnote, and keeping the meditation instruction stripped to the practitioner's experience. Alternatively, integrate the distinction in plain language: "whether the bones remain in their rough overall shape or still show the articulation of the joints."

### Issue 5 — Unexplained Jargon (Flag 6)
**Location:** Line 408 (Shared Characteristic paragraph) — "inversion" (line 410, Categories)

**Original:**
> "this is inversion — belonging to the black category"

**Problem:** "Inversion" (顛倒, viparyasa) is a technical Yogacara/Abhidharma term. It is not glossed. A practitioner without background will not understand what an "inversion" is — inverting what? The subsequent contrast (non-inversion = seeing things as they are) makes the meaning inferrable, but only after reading ahead. A brief gloss is needed at first use.

**Suggested fix:**
> "this is inversion (*viparyasa* — perceiving the impure as pure, the impermanent as permanent) — belonging to the black category..."

Or, if a footnote is preferred:
> "this is inversion[^viparyasa] — belonging to the black category..."
> [^viparyasa]: Inversion (*viparyasa*): the fundamental cognitive distortion of perceiving things contrary to their nature — taking the impure as pure, the impermanent as permanent, suffering as pleasure, the non-self as self. The impurity contemplation is the antidote to the first of these.

### Issue 6 — Translated-ese (Flag 1)
**Location:** Line 418 (Reasoning from function)

**Original:**
> "If one thoroughly cultivates, practices, and extensively develops this contemplation of impurity, one can eradicate sensual desire. This sensual desire will surely be eradicated."

**Problem:** "thoroughly cultivates, practices, and extensively develops" is a triple verbal phrase that in Chinese reflects 修習, 多修習 — a doublet meaning "cultivates and extensively cultivates." The English expansion into three verbs ("cultivates, practices, and extensively develops") is clumsy. The final sentence — "This sensual desire will surely be eradicated" — repeats the content of the prior sentence without adding anything. It reads as filler required by the Chinese source structure but not needed in English.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "By thoroughly and repeatedly cultivating this contemplation of impurity, one can eradicate sensual desire — and it will surely be eradicated."

Or, if the repetition is a rhetorical device (affirmation after statement), flag it with a slight structural shift:
> "By thoroughly cultivating this contemplation of impurity, one can eradicate sensual desire; this is certain."

### Issue 7 — Formula Monotony (Flag 2)
**Location:** Lines 384–402 (the nine stages) + cross-section recurrence

**Pattern:** "One develops the conviction of X" appears at lines 376 (twice), 378, 384, 386, 388, 398, 400, 402, 404, 406 — approximately ten times in the section. In the corpse-stage enumeration alone (items 1–9), five of the nine items use this exact phrase. This is formula monotony by any count.

**Suggested approach:**
Open the enumeration with a framing sentence that establishes "develops conviction" as the contemplative act (e.g., "At each stage, one cultivates a clear, stable recognition of what the body has become:"), then vary or drop the phrase in individual items:

> (1) *Lividity.* Dead one day — the blood has dried but putrefaction has not yet begun. The color and feel of lividity become vividly present to the mind.
> (2) *Putrefaction.* Dead two days — putrefaction has begun, but worms have not yet appeared.
> (3) *Decay and bloating.* Dead seven days — worms are present and the body has broken down.

Items (4)–(7) are already free of the formula and are fine as written.
