## SUMMARY VERDICT

The front matter is substantially publication-ready. The Introduction is scholarly, clearly written, and accurately represents the scope and methodology of the work. ## SECTION 1: INTRODUCTION ACCURACY

### 1.1 CRITICAL — Incorrect Sanskrit Term

**Location:** Introduction, "Scope of This Translation," Third Yoga Place paragraph, line 15.

**Issue:** The Introduction refers to the nine stages of mental abiding as "*nava-akara-manasikara-sthiti*." This form does not appear anywhere else in the project. The correct Sanskrit recorded in the master glossary (line 176) and reader glossary (line 159) is *nava-akara-citta-sthiti* (九種心住). A second entry in the master glossary (line 524) records the alternate *navavidha-citta-sthiti*. Neither uses *manasikara-sthiti* ("stages of attention-abiding"). The compound *manasikara* means mental application or attention; *citta* means mind. The Chinese 九種心住 is literally "nine kinds of mind-abiding," confirming *citta-sthiti* is correct.

**Fix:** Change *nava-akara-manasikara-sthiti* to *nava-akara-citta-sthiti* in the Introduction.

---

### 1.2 MODERATE — Terminology Inconsistency: "factors of awakening" vs. "factors conducive to awakening"

**Location:** Introduction, Second Yoga Place paragraph.

**Issue:** The Introduction uses "thirty-seven factors of awakening (*bodhipakshya-dharma*)." The reader glossary (line 66) and master glossary render bodhipakshya-dharma as "factor(s) **conducive to** awakening." The term *bodhyanga* (覺支) is reserved for "factors **of** awakening" in the narrower sense (the seven factors specifically; reader glossary line 68). Using "factors of awakening" for bodhipakshya-dharma conflates the two terms at the level of English rendering.

The structural overview table for fascicle 29 also capitalizes "Bodhipakshya-dharmas in detail" inconsistently with how the term appears in the running paragraphs.

**Fix:** Change "thirty-seven factors of awakening" in the Introduction to "thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening." Lowercase "Bodhipakshya-dharmas" in the structural table row.

---

### 1.3 MINOR — Asanga's dates span fourth and fifth centuries

**Location:** Introduction, opening paragraph.

**Issue:** "Asanga (c. 4th century CE)" is slightly imprecise. The modern scholarly consensus places Asanga approximately 375–430 CE, meaning his active career extended into the early fifth century. "c. 4th–5th century CE" or "c. late 4th to early 5th century CE" would be more accurate. The fascicle 26 translation file correctly hedges with a footnote on authorship attribution; the introduction's date claim warrants the same precision.

**Fix:** Change "c. 4th century CE" to "c. late 4th–early 5th century CE."

### 1.5 MODERATE — Glossary size claim needs verification

**Location:** Introduction, "Terminology" subsection.

**Issue:** The Introduction states "a master glossary of over 630 entries." Analysis of the master glossary file (640 lines total starting with `|`) gives the following breakdown:

- 571 pipe-character rows total
- 28 header and separator rows (confirmed)
- Approximately 543 data rows

The "over 630 entries" claim appears to count total pipe-character rows or include some form of duplication. The master glossary has 16 sections; several terms appear in multiple sections with updated/refined entries (e.g., *vitarka* appears at line 97 and with a revised note at the end; *priti* at line 598 after an initial entry). The actual count of unique terms is approximately 520-543, depending on how duplicates are handled.

**Fix:** Either (a) verify the count method and correct the claim to "over 500 entries" or "approximately 540 entries," or (b) retain "over 630" if the count is based on total table rows including all section appearances. A note explaining the counting method would serve scholars.

**Issue:** The paragraph says the Third Yoga Place "begins with the protocol for approaching a teacher and the four inquiry points." ### 2.3 MODERATE — Taisho reference gaps in ToC

**Issue:** Numerous sections in the ToC have blank Taisho reference cells. These are sections that fall within a continuous Taisho passage and do not have their own bracketed inline reference in the translation files. Specifically:

The sections are part of continuous passages, so the preceding section's Taisho ref is the correct starting point.

**Fix (optional for publication):** Either (a) add a footnote explaining that blank Taisho refs indicate continuation from the preceding section's reference, or (b) supply approximate Taisho refs from the source files for all sections. Option (a) is less work and equally informative.

### 3.3 CRITICAL — Duplicate entry for vitarka-carita

**Location:** Reader glossary, lines 294 and 298.

**Issue:** *vitarka-carita* appears twice:
- Line 294: `| vitarka-carita | 尋思行 / 尋思增上者 | discursive-thought conduct | Fifth conduct type; mindfulness of breathing is its purifying object |`
- Line 298: `| vitarka-carita | 多尋思行 | discursive-thought conduct | Person dominated by discursive thought |`

These are two distinct Xuanzang renderings (尋思行/尋思增上者 vs. 多尋思行) for the same underlying concept. They should be merged into a single entry noting both Chinese renderings, or differentiated by distinct Sanskrit keys. As-is, a reader will encounter the same Sanskrit entry twice in sequence, which looks like an error.

**Fix:** Merge into: `| vitarka-carita | 尋思行 / 多尋思行 / 尋思增上者 | discursive-thought conduct | Fifth conduct type; mindfulness of breathing is its antidote. Chinese has two renderings: 尋思行/尋思增上者 (as behavioral category) and 多尋思行 (as person type). |`

This is also covered at the start of "Structural Fidelity": "Translator's notes appear in footnotes." And the ToC itself uses the format. The sentence is harmless but repeats information visible in the text itself.

"The nine stages of mental abiding (*nava-akara-citta-sthiti*) — which became foundational for later shamatha instruction in both the Tibetan and East Asian traditions" conveys the same claim with evidence instead of adjective.

## SECTION 5: MISSING FRONT MATTER (UNIVERSITY PRESS STANDARD)

The following elements are standard in university press translations of classical Buddhist texts (Wisdom Publications, Columbia University Press, Princeton University Press Buddhist series) and are absent from the current front matter:

### 5.1 IMPORTANT — Abbreviations and Reference Conventions Page

A university press publication would include a dedicated "Abbreviations and Conventions" page covering:
- T = Taisho Tripitaka (with volume/text/page/column/line format explained)
- The use of Sanskrit vs. Pali forms (and why Sanskrit throughout)
- How to read the Taisho references: [T30.0424a07] = vol. 30, page 0424, column a, line 07
- Italicization conventions (all Sanskrit/Chinese terms on first use)
- Footnote vs. translator's note conventions

None of this is currently explained in the front matter. The reader must infer the Taisho reference format from examples.

### 5.2 IMPORTANT — Note on Romanization and Diacritics

This translation omits diacritics throughout (shamatha not śamatha, prajna not prajñā, dhyana not dhyāna). This is a deliberate choice (NTC convention for common dharma-English terms), but it is not explained anywhere. A university press publication requires a note explaining the romanization system used and why diacritics are omitted. Without this, scholars may infer the omission is an error rather than a choice.

### 5.3 MODERATE — Bibliography Absent

No bibliography is provided. The Introduction cites Deleanu (2006), Shukla (1973), and lists nine reference texts. A publication would require full bibliographic citations for at minimum:
- Shukla, Karunesha. *Śrāvakabhūmi of Ācārya Asaṅga*. Patna: K.P. Jayaswal Research Institute, 1973.
- Deleanu, Florin. *The Chapter on the Mundane Path (Laukikamārga) in the Śrāvakabhūmi*. Tokyo: International Institute for Buddhist Studies, 2006.
- The nine reference library texts (full publication data)
- The Taisho Tripitaka (Takakusu Junjiro et al., eds.)

### 5.4 MODERATE — Acknowledgments Page

No acknowledgments page exists. For a university press volume, this would cover: institutional support, human editorial reviewers, permission for reference text quotations, and any scholars consulted. Even if the acknowledgments are brief, their absence would be noted by a press acquisitions editor.

### 5.5 MINOR — No Note Explaining Scope Limitation

The translation covers the Second, Third, and opening of the Fourth Yoga Place (fascicles 26-33). The Sravakabhumi continues into further fascicles. Fascicle 33 itself closes with a footnote (note 32 in the translation) stating that "the remaining topics continue into subsequent fascicles." A university press volume would require a Note to the Reader or introduction paragraph explicitly explaining: (1) the full scope of the Sravakabhumi, (2) which portion this translation covers, (3) what is not translated, and (4) whether further volumes are planned. The current Introduction addresses (1) and (2) implicitly through the structural overview but does not address (3) or (4).

### 5.6 MINOR — Colophon Placement

The colophon "*English translation from the Chinese by the computational translation system, with human editorial oversight, 2025-2026*" currently appears at the bottom of the Introduction file. In a standard university press volume, the colophon and copyright information belong on the copyright page (verso of title page), not embedded in the Introduction. This is a layout convention issue rather than a content error, but it will require repositioning for typesetting.

## SECTION 6: AI DISCLOSURE ASSESSMENT

### 6.1 CONFIRMED — AI Disclosure Present and Appropriate

Per design-invariants.md: "The translation is produced by computational (AI) with human editorial oversight."

The Introduction's "Note on Method" section (lines 61-63) states: "This translation was produced by the computational translation system with human editorial oversight. The translation process for each section followed a consistent protocol: reading the Chinese source, updating the glossary, translating sentence by sentence against the source, self-verification, and then a multi-agent review examining terminology consistency, doctrinal accuracy, readability, and source fidelity."

The colophon repeats: "English translation from the Chinese by the computational translation system, with human editorial oversight, 2025-2026."

This disclosure is: accurate, prominent (in its own named section), methodologically specific, and appropriately scoped. It does not overstate human involvement nor understate AI production. The description of the multi-agent review process as a quality control mechanism is specifically useful for scholarly credibility. No changes needed.

**Note:** Whether this level of disclosure will satisfy all university press policies (some require author/translator to be a human legal entity) is a contractual and legal question beyond the scope of this review.
