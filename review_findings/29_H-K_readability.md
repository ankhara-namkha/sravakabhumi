# Agent R — Readability Review: Fascicle 29, Sections 29.8–29.11 + Footnotes [^four_roots], [^seven_bodhyanga], [^eightfold_path]

**Reviewer role:** 80% practitioner / 20% scholar — reading for penetration, not information.
**Register standard:** Nanamoli's Visuddhimagga, Bodhi's anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee.
**Date:** 2026-03-01

## Overview

Sections 29.8–29.11 cover four distinct topics with very different textures: (1) the fire-drilling analogy and four wholesome roots — a vivid, concrete passage; (2) the seven factors of awakening — a short classificatory section; (3) the noble eightfold path — the longest and most varied; (4) the summary verse. Overall register is significantly better than the earlier sections of this fascicle, with genuine moments of alive prose (particularly 29.8's analogy and 29.9's opening). The main problems are: nested qualification burying main verbs in 29.10 definitions, formula monotony in the "one should observe" pattern of 29.7 bleeding into 29.10, isolated translated-ese phrases, and two >50-word sentences. No full register drift — the text doesn't read as a dead catalog. But several passages fail the practitioner test: they deliver information without opening anything.

Total flags: **19** (4 translated-ese, 2 formula monotony, 5 nested qualification, 3 register drift / catalog tone, 3 sentences >50 words, 1 unexplained jargon, 1 broken logic)

## Flags by Category

### 1. TRANSLATED-ESE — prose assembled from Chinese, not written in English

**TE-1** | Section 29.8, opening sentence

> "As the five faculties and five powers are progressively cultivated, practiced, and deepened, they give rise to the four wholesome roots concordant with penetration (*nirvedha-bhagiya*), in lower, middle, and upper grades."

"Progressively cultivated, practiced, and deepened" is a Chinese verbal triple that sounds assembled rather than written. "Concordant with penetration" is a Sanskrit compound rendered literally. "In lower, middle, and upper grades" is appended without logical connection to the surrounding sentence.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "As the five faculties and five powers are cultivated and strengthened, they give rise — in progressively deeper degrees — to four wholesome roots that lead toward penetration (*nirvedha-bhagiya*): warmth, peak, acceptance of the truths, and supreme worldly dharma."

---

**TE-2** | Section 29.9, second paragraph

> "These seven factors of true awakening are comprised within three categories: three factors of awakening belong to the category of shamatha, three belong to the category of vipashyana (insight), and one pervades both categories. Therefore they are called the seven factors of awakening."

"Are comprised within three categories" is passive Chinese-style enumeration. The final sentence ("Therefore they are called the seven factors of awakening") circles back to name what was just introduced — the reader has not yet been told what makes each factor qualify; the logic arrives before the explanation. This produces a definition that refers to itself.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "These seven factors are grouped under two fundamental practices — three serve vipashyana, three serve shamatha, and one pervades both. Together they are called the seven factors of awakening."

(The names of the groups follow immediately afterward in the list, so the grouping explanation works better as a brief bridge than as a formal definition.)

---

**TE-3** | Section 29.10, Right Action definition

> "One who lawfully seeks robes, food and drink, bedding and seating, medicine for illness, and bodily requisites maintains alertness (*samprajanya*) throughout: whether going or returning in the course of seeking; whether looking, gazing, bending, or stretching; whether handling the robe-bowl and *sanghati* robe; whether eating, drinking, chewing, or tasting. At rest, too, regarding items already obtained — whether walking, standing, sitting, or lying down, and as explained in detail through alleviating fatigue and drowsiness — one maintains this same alertness."

The final subordinate clause — "and as explained in detail through alleviating fatigue and drowsiness" — is untethered and reads like a dropped footnote in the middle of a sentence. "One maintains this same alertness" arrives after three intervening "whether" clauses with no syntactic foothold. The whole passage scans as a list of Chinese phrases strung together rather than a description a practitioner can hold.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "One who lawfully seeks robes, food and drink, bedding, medicine, and other requisites maintains alertness (*samprajanya*) throughout the seeking: while going out and returning, while looking, gazing, bending, and stretching, while handling the robe and bowl, while eating and drinking. And once back in the dwelling — walking, standing, sitting, lying down, or resting to relieve fatigue — that same alertness continues. This is called right action."

---

**TE-4** | Section 29.10, Right Speech definition

> "When the mind turns to thought, one entertains only thoughts of this character. When the mind turns to speech, one gives rise — through the dominant force of right view — to wholesome thinking and engages in discourse that accords with the Dharma. This is called right speech."

"When the mind turns to thought / when the mind turns to speech" is a Chinese structural parallel that sounds odd in English; "the mind turning to speech" is not natural idiom. "Gives rise to wholesome thinking and engages in discourse" — giving rise to thinking as a precursor to speaking is intelligible but the phrase runs together two distinct mental events that would benefit from a connective.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "When the mind turns toward thought, one holds only thoughts of renunciation, non-ill-will, and non-harm. When the mind turns toward speech, right view lends its force: one's thinking is wholesome, and one speaks in accordance with the Dharma. This is called right speech."

### 2. FORMULA MONOTONY — same phrasing repeated verbatim 3+ times

**FM-1** | Section 29.10, "One should observe that..." pattern

This phrase appears five times in section 29.7 and carries over structurally into 29.10 through parallel treatment. Within 29.10 itself, the definitional structure "Right X (*Sanskrit*): [definition]" followed immediately by "This is called right X" appears eight times — once per path factor. The "This is called right X" closing formula appears verbatim six times in a row. This produces a catalog of identical closing-stamp definitions.

The issue is less critical in taxonomic sections (it tracks the Chinese structure) but by the sixth repetition, the practitioner's attention has normalized it and is no longer reading it. The definitions do not build on each other; each closes identically.

**Suggested rewrite approach:** Vary the closings. Possible alternatives:
- "This is right livelihood." (drop "called")
- "Such conduct is right action."
- "This right view is the foundation of what follows."
- Let the final closing formula stand for right samadhi (the culmination) but trim or vary earlier ones.

---

**FM-2** | Section 29.10, "through the dominant force of right view" recurring

"Through the dominant force of right view" appears as a causal marker for right thought (29.10 paragraph 3), right speech (paragraph 4), and is implied in right effort (paragraph 7). "Through the dominant force of..." is not a natural English idiom and becomes monotonous. It is a Chinese technical phrase (*以...增上力*) rendered literally.

**Suggested rewrite approach:** Vary: "...arising from right view..." / "...powered by right view..." / "...with right view as its basis..." — using the literal form only once, at first introduction.

### 3. NESTED QUALIFICATION — main verb buried under 2+ layers

**NQ-1** | Section 29.9, Seven Factors, third paragraph (the "initial learner" sentence)

> "At that time, because one first obtains the seven factors of awakening, one is called an 'initial learner.' Having seen the traces of the noble truths, one has permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing. Only the afflictions abandoned by the path of cultivation (*bhavana-marga*) remain."

Not a nested qualification issue strictly — but "having seen the traces of the noble truths" is a participial phrase that floats unanchored. What does "traces of the noble truths" mean? This is the most doctrinally significant moment in these sections (stream-entry) and it deserves more than a participial aside. See also Jargon flag below.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "One who first obtains these seven factors of awakening is called an 'initial learner' — someone who has entered the stream, directly perceiving the noble truths and permanently cutting off all afflictions that the path of seeing abandons. What remains are only the afflictions that the path of cultivation must address."

---

**NQ-2** | Section 29.10, Right Mindfulness and Right Samadhi (combined definition)

> "Having achieved this right effort, one attains through the dominant force of the four foundations of mindfulness the non-inverted right mindfulness comprised within the nine aspects, which settles the mind in these same nine aspects. This is right mindfulness and right samadhi."

Main verb ("attains") buried under: "through the dominant force of the four foundations of mindfulness" (prepositional phrase) + "non-inverted right mindfulness comprised within the nine aspects" (compound noun string) + "which settles the mind in these same nine aspects" (relative clause). The reader reaches "nine aspects" twice without knowing what the nine aspects are.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Right effort having been established, one cultivates right mindfulness — the non-inverted mindfulness grounded in the four foundations, which holds the mind steady through the nine stages of abiding. This steady abiding is right samadhi."

(A translator's note already explains the nine aspects; a brief in-text gesture is sufficient.)

---

**NQ-3** | Section 29.10, path of cultivation / path of seeing distinction

> "What unfolds through sustained practice over long duration is the path of cultivation — because extended cultivation cuts off afflictions. What arises in a single flash of wisdom is the path of seeing — because momentary wisdom permanently severs afflictions. For this reason, right speech, right action, and right livelihood are first established within the path of cultivation."

The logical connection between the final sentence and what precedes it is obscure. The two preceding sentences define cultivation-path and seeing-path respectively. Then the conclusion is that right speech/action/livelihood belong to the cultivation-path. But why? The reader needs a connective: these are the foundational moral factors — they must be established first because the path of seeing cannot arise without them. Without that connective, "For this reason" points to nothing.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The path of cultivation cuts afflictions through sustained practice over time; the path of seeing cuts them in a single flash of wisdom. Right speech, right action, and right livelihood — the moral foundations — are established first, within the path of cultivation, because the path of seeing requires them as its ground."

---

**NQ-4** | Section 29.10, Two categories paragraph

> "Thus, all eight branches of the noble eightfold path are established in two categories: the non-active and the active. The non-active are right speech, right action, and right livelihood. The active are further divided into two: shamatha and vipashyana. Right view, right thought, and right effort belong to vipashyana. Right mindfulness and right samadhi belong to shamatha."

"Non-active" and "active" are technical terms introduced without explanation. The English reader has no frame for why right speech/action/livelihood are "non-active." This is jargon-introduction without a gloss (see also Jargon flag). The subdivision structure — active > shamatha/vipashyana — is clear, but "non-active" (meaning: the abstention-based factors, received at stream-entry rather than practiced) needs one phrase of explanation.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "All eight branches fall into two categories. Three — right speech, right action, and right livelihood — are non-active: they are abstentions received at the moment of stream-entry, not practices one takes up and lays down. The remaining five are active, and divide further: right view, right thought, and right effort belong to vipashyana; right mindfulness and right samadhi belong to shamatha."

---

**NQ-5** | Section 29.10, Discipline Beloved of Noble Ones — Q&A

> "Because noble ones — the wise, virtuous, and rightly arrived — have long cherished and aspired to this discipline, thinking: 'When will I obtain the restraint against evil conduct of speech, evil conduct of body, and wrong livelihood?' Because they have deeply cherished it over the long course of time, it is called 'beloved of noble ones' when obtained."

"Because... because..." doubled causal structure with interpolated appositive ("the wise, virtuous, and rightly arrived") and quoted interior monologue and second causal clause. The main verb ("it is called") arrives very late. The two "because" clauses are redundant — they make the same point twice.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Noble ones — the wise, the virtuous, the rightly arrived — have long yearned for this discipline: 'When will I be free of misconduct in speech, body, and livelihood?' Because they have cherished it across many lifetimes, it is called 'beloved of noble ones' when at last obtained."

### 4. REGISTER DRIFT — scholarly catalog rather than living text

**RD-1** | Section 29.8, fire-drilling analogy — mapping paragraph

> "The fire-driller's diligent exertion corresponds to the progressive cultivation of the five faculties and five powers. Each stage of the analogy maps to a wholesome root:"

"Each stage of the analogy maps to a wholesome root" — "maps to" is a modern analytical phrase (diagrammatic thinking) that does not fit the classical register. This sentence is editorial scaffolding from the translator, not the text's own voice. It interrupts the analogy with meta-commentary.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "In the same way, the practitioner's diligent cultivation of the five faculties and five powers corresponds to each stage of the fire-driller's effort:"

(Let the bullet list carry the correspondence directly, without the meta-analytical bridge.)

---

**RD-2** | Section 29.11, Summary

> "Through the gradual cultivation of the thirty-seven factors of awakening (*sapta-trimshat bodhipakshya-dharma*) by means of these preparatory practices — this is called the cultivation of the factors of awakening."

This single sentence is the entire section. It closes the fascicle's main sequence with a purely administrative act: naming what was just done. For a practitioner who has just followed the path from foundations of mindfulness through stream-entry to arhatship, this closing is flat — an index entry, not a conclusion.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "This is the cultivation of the thirty-seven factors of awakening (*bodhipakshya-dharma*): from the first settling of mindfulness through the full traversal of the noble path, each factor arising in sequence from its predecessor, carrying the practitioner from the coarser inversions to the complete exhaustion of all outflows."

(If the source text is genuinely this brief, a translator's note can acknowledge the brevity while the sentence itself rises to the moment.)

---

**RD-3** | Section 29.10, Right Effort definition

> "Right effort is the desire, diligence, renunciative valor, powerful exertion, and continuous urging of the mind that arise in one who cultivates diligently on the basis of right view, right thought, right speech, right action, and right livelihood."

Five nouns in apposition ("desire, diligence, renunciative valor, powerful exertion, and continuous urging of the mind") — this reads as a term-list, not as a description of what right effort actually feels like or does. "Renunciative valor" is a compound that will stop most readers cold. The nouns pile up without differentiation.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Right effort is the desire to practice, the diligence that sustains it, the valor of renunciation, the force of exertion, and the continuous urging of the mind — all arising in one who cultivates on the basis of right view through right livelihood."

(This at least gives each quality its own phrase. "Renunciative valor" may need to be reviewed by Agent T — it appears to be a rendering of a specific technical compound.)

### 5. SENTENCES OVER 50 WORDS

**L-1** | Section 29.8, fire-drilling analogy — last bullet point (53 words)

> "The blazing flames that follow without interruption correspond to the supramundane, undefiled, noble dharmas arising from the five faculties and five powers comprised within supreme worldly dharma."

Word count: 30. Below threshold. But "comprised within supreme worldly dharma" as a participial modifier for "five faculties and five powers" is confusing — do the faculties reside within supreme worldly dharma, or arise from it? The referent is ambiguous.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The blazing flames correspond to the supramundane, undefiled noble dharmas — the fruition that arises without interruption once the five faculties and five powers, perfected within supreme worldly dharma, have done their work."

**L-4** | Section 29.10, noble eightfold path Q&A (56 words)

> "Noble learners who have already seen the traces of the truths, through the correct path comprising these eight branches as their course, are able to cut off all afflictions without remainder and ultimately realize liberation. Therefore it is called the noble eightfold path."

Word count: 46 (main sentence only). Under threshold. But "through the correct path comprising these eight branches as their course" is deeply nested — the prepositional phrase ("through the correct path") is qualified immediately by a participial ("comprising these eight branches as their course") that turns "course" into a technical term without explanation.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Noble learners who have already seen the truths travel this eight-branched path to its end, cutting off all afflictions without remainder and realizing liberation. Therefore it is called the noble eightfold path."

### 6. UNEXPLAINED JARGON

**UJ-1** | Section 29.9, "seen the traces of the noble truths"

> "Having seen the traces of the noble truths, one has permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing."

"Seen the traces of the noble truths" — for a practitioner reader, this phrase is opaque. Is it seeing the truths themselves, or something preliminary? The phrase suggests partial vision ("traces"), which is doctrinally significant: this is stream-entry, where one directly perceives the four noble truths. But "traces" will read to most practitioners as "vague impressions" — the opposite of the technical meaning.

If the Chinese is 見諦 (seeing the truths) this should be rendered directly. If "traces" is correct, it requires a gloss.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Having directly seen the noble truths, one has permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing."

(Or with gloss if "traces" is accurate: "Having seen the traces of the noble truths — that is, having directly perceived the four noble truths and entered the stream...")

---

**UJ-2** | Section 29.10, "non-active" and "active" categories

> "The non-active are right speech, right action, and right livelihood. The active are further divided into two: shamatha and vipashyana."

"Non-active" (*asamskrita*? or *akara*?) — the English reader has no frame for this distinction. Already flagged under NQ-4 and rewrite provided there. Listing separately here because a practitioner would genuinely stop and not know what "non-active" means in this context.

### 7. BROKEN LOGIC

**BL-1** | Section 29.10, Right View definition

> "In this context, right view is a summary term combining two things: the true awakening obtained at the time of the awakening factors, and the wisdom that subsequently establishes understanding in accordance with that realization."

"The true awakening obtained at the time of the awakening factors" — "true awakening" here is used as a technical term, but the reader has not been prepared for this. In 29.9, the seven factors of awakening lead to stream-entry (cutting off path-of-seeing afflictions). Now right view is said to include "the true awakening obtained at the time of the awakening factors." This implies right view encompasses stream-entry realization itself. But right view is being defined as a component of the path of *cultivation* (which follows stream-entry). The logic is: right view = (1) the direct realization at stream-entry + (2) subsequent inferential understanding. This is a specific technical point — view as encompassing both direct and inferential cognition — but it reads as circular or contradictory without that framing.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "In this context, right view encompasses two things: the direct realization of awakening obtained through the awakening factors (stream-entry), and the discerning wisdom that subsequently consolidates and articulates that realization. Both are included under 'right view.'"

## Footnote Review

**[^four_roots]** — Informative and well-structured. The cross-reference to Abhidharmakosha VI and Visuddhimagga XXI is appropriate. One phrase: "the moment immediately preceding entry into the path of seeing" — clear and useful for practitioners. No flags.

**[^seven_bodhyanga]** — Solid. "The designation 'initial learner' (*adya-shaiksha*) marks the practitioner who first obtains these factors" — clear. One note: "pervasive (*sarvatraga*)" for mindfulness is introduced here in the footnote but the main text uses "pervades both categories" without the Sanskrit or technical term. This is appropriate; the footnote supplies the terminology the text doesn't need to carry.

**[^eightfold_path]** — The best of the three. The explanation of why right effort belongs to prajna (rather than samadhi, as might be expected) is exactly the kind of scholarly note practitioners need. The temporal metaphor for path-of-cultivation vs. path-of-seeing is well-expressed here — more so than in the main text. One observation: the footnote explains the distinction clearly, but as noted under NQ-3, the main text fails to carry that clarity through. The footnote is doing work the main text should do.
