### 31.1 Vipashyana Applied to Dependent Origination

**Finding 1 (m): "No agent" thesis — doctrinally sound but footnote could be more precise**

The translation's rendering of the three denials — "no sovereign creator, no progenitor, no transformer" — correctly captures 自在作者, 生者, 化者. The footnote [^no_agent] rightly identifies 自在作者 as alluding to Ishvara and frames the denial as "a core Yogacara thesis." This is doctrinally correct: the denial of any directing agent behind pratityasamutpada is standard across all Buddhist schools, not exclusively Yogacara. However, the Yogacara framing is defensible because the Sravakabhumi develops this specifically within its systematic framework.

One minor note: the phrase "no intermediary person transforms dharmas from within" (士夫中間能轉變者轉變諸法) specifically targets the Samkhya purusha as inner witness/transformer. The footnote does not mention Samkhya. This is a minor omission — the allusion is implicit, and the translation itself is clear.

**Severity: m** — Footnote could name Samkhya alongside Ishvara for scholarly completeness.

**Finding 2 (O): Two causes / two fruits analysis — excellent doctrinal accuracy**

The translation of the drawing cause (牽引因, *akshepaka-hetu*) and generating cause (生起因, *abhinirvartaka-hetu*) analysis is doctrinally precise and matches the Lamrim Chenmo's presentation (Vol. 1, ch. 21, "Abbreviated classification of the factors"). Tsongkhapa's text uses "projecting" and "actualizing" (following Asanga's Compendium of Knowledge) where the Sravakabhumi uses "drawing" and "generating" — the translation correctly preserves the Sravakabhumi's own terminology rather than importing Tsongkhapa's. The footnote [^two_causes] accurately notes this as "distinctive to the Yogacara school" and provides the correct Sanskrit reconstructions.

The mapping is correct:
- Drawing cause = ignorance + formations + seed-aspect of consciousness through feeling (projecting factors in Tsongkhapa)
- Generating cause = craving + grasping + becoming (actualizing factors in Tsongkhapa)

The footnote says "the drawing cause maps to the first two-and-a-half links (ignorance, formations, and the seed-aspect of consciousness through feeling)." This is accurate: it covers the projecting factors (ignorance, compositional activity, consciousness of causal period) that project the four-and-a-half factors from resultant-period consciousness through feeling. The "two-and-a-half" count follows the standard Yogacara division of consciousness into causal and resultant periods.

**Severity: O** — Correct and well-documented.

**Finding 3 (m): "Imperturbable formations" footnote — minor doctrinal imprecision**

Footnote [^imperturbable] says imperturbable formations lead to rebirth "in the form and formless realms." The standard Abhidharma/Yogacara classification is that imperturbable karma (*aninjya-karma*) pertains specifically to the **formless realm** absorptions, while meritorious karma (*punya-karma*) covers the desire realm and form-realm rebirths. However, the Sravakabhumi and Lamrim Chenmo both use "invariable karma" (不動行/不動業) to cover both form and formless realm karma (see Lamrim Chenmo: "Invariable karma is contaminated virtuous karma in the minds of beings of the form or formless realms"). So the footnote follows the broader Yogacara usage and is defensible.

The issue is the footnote's explanation: "so called because the resulting states are stable and undisturbed, unlike the turbulent formations of the desire realm." This is correct etymology. No change required, but the reviewer notes that some Abhidharma traditions restrict *aninjya* to the formless realm only, and the broader usage here is specifically Yogacara/Sarvastivada.

**Severity: m** — Acceptable within Yogacara framework. A note acknowledging the narrower Abhidharma definition would add scholarly precision.

### 31.3 Vipashyana Applied to Mindfulness of Breathing

**Finding 7 (M): Breath as "external sense-base" — needs doctrinal footnote**

The translation says: "One discerns that in-breaths and out-breaths are internally obtained, since they belong to the body; yet they are classified under the external sense-base."

This is a doctrinally significant and potentially confusing point. In Abhidharma classification, the breath (wind element in its respiratory function) belongs to the body internally but is classified under the *external sense-base* (外處) because it is an *object* of tactile cognition, not a *faculty*. The twelve ayatanas divide into six internal (faculties) and six external (objects). The breath, as a tangible object (*sprashtavya*), falls under the external base of tangibles (*sprashtavya-ayatana*).

This classification is standard Abhidharma but highly non-obvious for practitioners. Given that the text explicitly raises this paradox ("internally obtained... yet classified under the external sense-base"), a footnote explaining the Abhidharma classification of ayatanas into internal faculties and external objects is warranted. Without it, a practitioner-reader will be baffled.

**Severity: M** — Missing footnote on a doctrinally significant technical point. The text itself is correct, but the reader has no way to understand the apparent paradox without Abhidharma background.

### 31.6 Approach 5: Timely Practice

**Finding 15 (M): Calming/observation/uplifting/equanimity — correct framework, missing doctrinal link to obstacles**

The four meditation activities (calming, observation, uplifting, equanimity) are correctly defined and their timings are correctly assigned:
- Calming: when agitated or in danger of agitation
- Observation: when sinking or in danger of sinking
- Uplifting: when sunk low or in danger of sinking low
- Equanimity: when freed from agitation in both shamatha and vipashyana

These match standard Yogacara meditation instruction. The distinction between "observation" (used when the mind sinks — because analytical investigation energizes) and "calming" (used when agitated — because settling pacifies) is standard shamatha-vipashyana pedagogy found in both the Yogacara tradition and the Lamrim Chenmo.

Footnote [^four_obstacles] in section 31.8 correctly maps the four obstacles to the four meditation activities. However, this mapping should have been mentioned in 31.6 as well (or cross-referenced), since the reader encounters the four activities in 31.6 before encountering the four obstacles in 31.8. The structural connection — that timely practice is precisely the skill of knowing which activity counters which obstacle — is the key doctrinal insight, and it is not made explicit in the translation or footnotes for this section.

**Severity: M** — Cross-reference gap. The doctrinal connection between 31.6 and 31.8 is the heart of the Yogacara obstacle-purification system, and the reader must discover it on their own.

**Finding 19: Improper attention mapping — excellent doctrinal accuracy**

Footnote [^improper_five] correctly identifies that the five examples of improper attention correspond to the five purifying-conduct objects and five behavioral types. This is one of the most precise doctrinal footnotes in the fascicle. The mapping is:
1. Attending to purity instead of impurity -> greed type
2. Attending to anger instead of loving-kindness -> aversion type
3. Attending to darkness instead of brightness -> torpor type (not delusion type!)
4. Attending to memories/distractions instead of shamatha -> discursive-thought type
5. Conceiving I/mine instead of dependent origination -> delusion type (via self-view)

**HOWEVER**: The footnote says "(3) brightness/light for the torpor type." But the five behavioral types in the Sravakabhumi are greed, aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive-thought — there is no "torpor type" in this system. The Chinese source at this point (若不作意思惟明相而於闇相作意思惟) does not specify a behavioral type; it is one of five examples of improper attention that parallel the five objects. The third object in the standard sequence should map to **delusion-conduct** (since dependent origination is the object for delusion types), but here the "darkness/brightness" pairing does not match dependent origination — it corresponds to the specific obstacle of torpor/sloth (*styana-middha*), which is one of the five hindrances.

Looking more carefully: the five improper-attention examples do NOT map one-to-one to the five behavioral types as the footnote claims. They map to the five *hindrances* and their antidotes:
1. Purity vs. impurity -> sensual desire hindrance
2. Anger vs. loving-kindness -> ill-will hindrance
3. Darkness vs. brightness -> sloth-and-torpor hindrance
4. Memories/distractions vs. shamatha -> restlessness hindrance
5. I/mine vs. dependent origination -> doubt hindrance (via wrong view)

The footnote's claim that these correspond to the five behavioral types is **partially but not fully accurate**. Items 1, 2, and 5 map cleanly to behavioral types. Items 3 and 4 map to hindrances rather than behavioral types. The brightness antidote for torpor is standard hindrance-counteraction (see Visuddhimagga IV.92: perception of light as antidote for sloth-and-torpor), not character-type treatment.

This is a **footnote doctrinal error** but the translation text itself is correct.

**Severity: M (in footnote)** — The footnote incorrectly claims a clean five-fold mapping to behavioral types. The mapping is to the five hindrances and their antidotes, which only partially overlaps with the behavioral types. The footnote should be revised to note this as a hindrance-antidote mapping, with the observation that the hindrances partially correspond to the behavioral types but are not identical.

### 31.9 Shamatha and Vipashyana: Mutual Growth, Completion Criteria, and Paired Operation

**Finding 20: Completion criteria — CRITICAL PASSAGE, doctrinally accurate**

The completion criteria for each of the five purifying-conduct contemplations are correctly translated:
- Impurity: "the aspect of impurity naturally predominates without deliberate effort" and "the mind does not enter into... conditions that give rise to the entanglement of desire"
- Loving-kindness: "a loving mind predominates, displacing anger"
- Dependent origination: "impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and no-self come to the fore, dissolving... permanence, pleasure, and the delusion accompanying self-view"

- Mindfulness of breathing: "inner tranquility and shamatha predominate, leaving idle elaboration behind"

These criteria are standard Yogacara markers of mastery. Each criterion has the same structure: (1) the contemplation's characteristic aspect arises spontaneously, and (2) the mind no longer enters conditions that sustain the corresponding affliction. This structure is consistent with the Yogacara path-model.

The Chinese: 齊此名為於不淨觀已得究竟 — "At this point, the practitioner should know: 'I have attained the contemplation of impurity.'" The translation is accurate.

**Severity: O** — Correct, important, well-rendered.

**Finding 21: Paired operation (*yugananddha-marga*) — mostly accurate, footnote needs nuance**

The translation of the paired-operation passage is accurate: "When one has attained the ninth of the nine stages of mental abiding — meditative equipoise (*samahita*) — and, using this perfected samadhi as a basis, cultivates higher wisdom through dharma-investigation: at that point, through dharma-investigation, the path flows spontaneously, operating without effort and without deliberate exertion."

Footnote [^paired] says: "Tsongkhapa discusses this passage at length in the *Lamrim Chenmo* Vol. 3, noting that genuine union requires the ninth stage of mental abiding (balanced settling) as its foundation — not merely occasional alternation between calming and insight."

### 31.10 Intermediate Summary Verse

**Finding 23: Verse accuracy — one ordering issue**

The verse lists the nine approaches as: "First, appropriate practice; next, habituated and unslackened; / Non-inverted, timely, comprehending, insatiable; / Not abandoning the wholesome yoke; and last, correct practice."

The order in the verse is: appropriate, habituated, unslackened, non-inverted, timely, comprehending, insatiable, not-abandoning-the-yoke, correct. But the prose enumeration in 31.5 lists them as: (1) appropriate, (2) habituated, (3) non-inverted, (4) unslackened, (5) timely, (6) comprehending, (7) insatiable, (8) not-abandoning-the-yoke, (9) correct.

The verse has "habituated and unslackened" (positions 2-3) with "non-inverted" following (position 4), while the prose enumeration has "non-inverted" at 3 and "unslackened" at 4. Checking the Chinese verse:

初相應加行　次串習無緩
無顛倒應時　解了無厭足
不棄捨善軛　最後正加行

The verse: 初相應 (first, appropriate), 次串習 (next, habituated), 無緩 (unslackened), 無顛倒 (non-inverted), 應時 (timely)... So the verse order is: appropriate, habituated, unslackened, non-inverted — which differs from the prose enumeration (appropriate, habituated, non-inverted, unslackened). The translation correctly preserves this difference. Footnote [^order_note] in 31.5 already addresses this discrepancy.

**Severity: O** — Correctly handled; the discrepancy is in the source text itself and is appropriately noted.

### 31.11 Cultivation of Attention: Four Types, Objects, Goldsmith Simile

**Finding 24: Four types of attention — consistent with 30.12**

The four types of attention here are:
1. Attention that disciplines the mind (調練心作意)
2. Attention that moistens the mind (滋潤心作意)
3. Attention that generates pliancy (生輕安作意)
4. Attention that purifies wisdom-vision (淨智見作意)

Footnote [^four_attentions_31] correctly notes these were introduced in section 30.12. However, 30.12 discusses a *different* set of four attentions: (1) forceful, (2) interrupted, (3) uninterrupted, (4) effortless. These are not the same as the four in 31.11.

Checking the Chinese: section 30.12 (30.0451a23) discusses 力勵運轉作意, 有間缺運轉作意, 無間缺運轉作意, 無功用運轉作意 — the four types of attention *within the nine stages*. The four in 31.11 (調練心作意, 滋潤心作意, 生輕安作意, 淨智見作意) are a *different* set — the four types of *cultivation attention* for the yogin's overall practice.

Looking more carefully at the translation of F30: section 30.12 is titled "The Four Types of Attention" and discusses the four attentions of the nine stages. But the question is whether the "four types of attention" in 31.11 were actually introduced somewhere in F30. Checking the Chinese of F30 more carefully: the text at the end of fascicle 30 mentions 修作意 (cultivating attention) as the tenth topic in the outline, and says it will be discussed in its proper place. Section 31.11 is that discussion.

The footnote's claim that "these four types of attention were introduced in section 30.12" is **incorrect** — 30.12 introduces a different set of four attentions. The four cultivation-attentions (disciplining, moistening, pliancy-generating, wisdom-purifying) are first introduced here in 31.11, not in 30.12. What 30.12 introduced is the four operational attentions (forceful, interrupted, uninterrupted, effortless).

**Severity: M (in footnote)** — Incorrect cross-reference. The footnote conflates two different sets of four attentions. Should be corrected to note that these are a distinct set from the four attentions of 30.12, and that this section provides their first full treatment.

**Finding 26: Three objects of delight — recollection of Three Jewels**

The three objects of delight are: (1) recollecting the Three Jewels, (2) recollecting pure discipline, (3) confidence in one's own attainments. These correspond to standard Buddhist recollection practices (*anussati*). The Visuddhimagga (Ch. VII) treats the six recollections (Buddha, Dharma, Sangha, virtue, generosity, devas) — the first four overlap with the three presented here. The Sravakabhumi condenses the three recollections of the Three Jewels into a single category and separates out discipline and self-confidence as distinct categories. This is a characteristically Yogacara reorganization and is doctrinally sound.

**Severity: O** — No issue.
