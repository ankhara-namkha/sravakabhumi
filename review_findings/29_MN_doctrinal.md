# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 29, Sections 29.13-29.14

**Reviewer:** Agent D (Doctrinal)
**Sections:** 29.13 (Person Typology: Behavioral Types and the Thin-Dust Person) and 29.14 (Six Categories of Persons)
**Reference texts consulted:** Bodhi, *In the Buddha's Words* (Ch. X on noble persons); Nanamoli, *Visuddhimagga* (Ch. III on temperaments, Ch. XIII on direct-knowledges, Ch. XIX on kamma and obstructions); Chinese source T30.0445c28-0447b13.

## Summary

Overall doctrinally sound. The translation accurately represents the Sravakabhumi's Yogacara-Sarvastivada framework throughout both sections. No critical doctrinal errors found. Three moderate issues and three minor issues identified.

## Findings

### M-1. Victorious shramana: alternative definition scope (Moderate)

**Location:** Line 404
**Text:** "Alternatively: learners (*shaiksha*) and those beyond learning (*ashaiksha*) are called victorious shramanas, because through the undefiled path they crush all afflictions abandoned by the paths of seeing and cultivation."

**Issue:** The Chinese 學無學名勝道沙門 says learners AND non-learners are called victorious shramanas. The translation correctly renders this. However, a potential doctrinal concern arises: a learner (stream-enterer, once-returner, or non-returner on the path) has NOT yet crushed ALL afflictions abandoned by the paths of seeing and cultivation -- only a non-learner (arhat) has done so. A stream-enterer has only crushed the three fetters via the path of seeing; a once-returner has attenuated sensory desire and ill will; a non-returner has fully eliminated the five lower fetters but retains the five higher fetters abandoned by the path of cultivation.

The Chinese itself says 以無漏道摧滅一切見修所斷諸煩惱故 -- "because through the undefiled path they crush all afflictions abandoned by the paths of seeing and cultivation." This is the SOURCE TEXT'S own doctrinal claim, so the translation is faithful. But the claim bundles learners with non-learners under a justification that strictly applies only to non-learners (or to learners collectively as a class that WILL eventually do so). Standard Abhidharma (e.g., Abhidharmakosha VI, and Bodhi's discussion of the four noble persons in *In the Buddha's Words* Ch. X) treats learners as being ON the path to complete elimination, not as having achieved it.

**Recommendation:** This is a tension within the source text, not a translation error. A brief translator's footnote could note: "Strictly, only the non-learner (arhat) has crushed all afflictions of both paths. The text likely designates learners as 'victorious' because they are irreversibly established on the undefiled path and will inevitably complete the process." This would prevent a doctrinally literate reader from stumbling.

**Severity:** M (the translation is faithful to the Chinese; the issue is in the source text's compressed phrasing, but a footnote would prevent misunderstanding)

### M-2. Three obstructions terminology: "maturation" vs. "ripening" (Moderate)

**Location:** Lines 364-370
**Text:** Three obstructions are given as: karmic (業障), afflictive (煩惱障), and maturation (異熟障).

**Issue:** The standard Yogacara-Abhidharma framework recognizes these three obstructions (*trividha-avarana*). The translation renders 異熟障 as "maturation obstruction," which is correct. However, the Sravakabhumi's three obstructions here differ from the more commonly discussed Yogacara pair of *kleshaavarana* (afflictive obstruction) and *jneyavarana* (cognitive obstruction). The translation should make clear that this is a different classification system -- the three obstructions to the *noble path specifically*, not the two obstructions to *buddhahood*.

Additionally, when describing maturation obstruction (line 370), the translation says "born in a state where the noble path can neither arise nor develop." The Chinese says 若生處聖道依彼不生不長 -- "born in a place where the noble path depending on that [birth] neither arises nor grows." The Visuddhimagga (Ch. XIX.15) discusses weighty kamma similarly -- that matricide and other anantariya-karmas prevent the path from arising. The doctrinal alignment is correct: certain births inherently preclude the noble path (the eight unfavorable conditions, *astaksana*).

The second form of maturation obstruction includes deafness, stupidity, dullness, blindness, muteness. The Chinese includes 聾騃愚鈍盲瞽瘖瘂以手代言 -- the translation renders this as "deafness, stupidity, dullness, blindness, muteness, or the inability to communicate except by hand." The term 瞽 is a specific kind of blindness (congenital/total), which may merit a note, but this is minor. More importantly, the doctrinal content aligns with the traditional *astaksana* teachings on unfavorable births.

**Recommendation:** Consider a brief footnote at the three obstructions section noting that this classification differs from the better-known twofold Yogacara obstruction scheme (afflictive + cognitive).

**Severity:** M (no doctrinal error, but potential for reader confusion with the standard Yogacara two-obstruction framework)

### M-3. Pure conduct of "ultimate severance" -- scope of attainments (Moderate)

**Location:** Line 418
**Text:** "The third belongs to noble ones who have attained the fruit of non-returning and the supreme fruit of arhatship."

**Chinese:** 畢竟斷梵行者。謂諸聖者得不還果。復得最上阿羅漢果。

**Issue:** The translation says "noble ones who have attained the fruit of non-returning AND the supreme fruit of arhatship." The Chinese 復得 ("and additionally attained") could be read either as: (a) those who have attained non-returning AND ALSO arhatship (i.e., arhats who passed through non-returning), or (b) those who have attained non-returning OR the supreme fruit of arhatship (i.e., both non-returners and arhats). Reading (b) is doctrinally more coherent, since the definition is about permanent severance of sexual desire (brahmacaryya), and non-returners have already permanently severed all sensory desire (kama-raga, the fourth fetter -- see Bodhi, *In the Buddha's Words*, Ch. X). Both non-returners and arhats qualify independently.

The current translation with "and" slightly favors reading (a), which could mislead a reader into thinking only arhats who previously held non-returner status qualify. Reading (b) -- "noble ones who have attained the fruit of non-returning, as well as those who have attained the supreme fruit of arhatship" -- would be clearer.

**Recommendation:** Revise to: "The third belongs to noble ones who have attained the fruit of non-returning, as well as those who have attained the supreme fruit of arhatship." Or: "...noble ones: those who have attained the fruit of non-returning and those who have attained the supreme fruit of arhatship."

**Severity:** M (the current phrasing is ambiguous rather than wrong, but the ambiguity could mislead)

### m-1. Five acts of immediate retribution -- unexpanded reference (minor)

**Location:** Line 366
**Text:** "the five acts of immediate retribution (*pancha-anantarya-karma*)"

**Issue:** The five anantarya-karmas (matricide, patricide, killing an arhat, drawing blood from a tathagata with malicious intent, and creating schism in the sangha) are named but not enumerated. The Chinese also does not enumerate them (五無間業). While the translation faithfully follows the source, a footnote listing the five acts would be helpful for the practitioner audience, since the term is technical and the specific acts are doctrinally important. The Visuddhimagga (XIX.15) discusses weighty kamma including matricide as having anantariya-like properties.

**Recommendation:** Add a footnote listing the five acts.

**Severity:** m (omission follows the source; a footnote would add clarity)

### m-2. "Shame and remorse" for 有羞有悔 (minor)

**Location:** Line 406
**Text:** "possessing shame and remorse"

**Chinese:** 有羞有悔

**Issue:** The standard Buddhist doctrinal pair is *hri* (shame/conscience, 慚) and *apatrapya* (moral dread/remorse, 愧). However, the Chinese here uses 羞 and 悔 rather than the standard 慚 and 愧. While 羞 is close to 慚, the character 悔 normally means "regret/repentance" (*kaukritya*), not *apatrapya*. In standard Abhidharma, *kaukritya* (悔/惡作) is a different mental factor entirely -- it is even listed among the secondary afflictions in some systems. The translation "shame and remorse" captures the Chinese characters but may inadvertently conflate two doctrinally distinct categories.

That said, the Chinese source itself uses 羞悔 rather than 慚愧, so the translation is source-faithful. The variance is in Xuanzang's wording choice here, not in the translation.

**Recommendation:** No change to translation text needed, but awareness that 悔 here likely functions as a near-synonym for 愧 in this context rather than as *kaukritya* proper.

**Severity:** m (source-level terminological variation; translation follows the source accurately)

### m-3. "Warmth of wholesome dharmas" metaphor (minor)

**Location:** Line 406
**Text:** "Such a person possesses the warmth of wholesome dharmas and can generate the life-faculty of noble wisdom: being alive and not dead, one is therefore called a shramana who lives by the path."

**Chinese:** 由彼現有諸善法煗。堪能生長聖慧命根。名活非死。是故名為活道沙門。

**Issue:** The "warmth" (煗, *ushmagata*) metaphor here is doctrinally significant and connects to the first of the four wholesome roots concordant with penetration (*nirvedha-bhagiya*) -- warmth, peak, acceptance, supreme worldly dharma. The translation correctly renders the metaphor but does not flag this connection. The passage is explaining why this ordinary person is called a "living-path shramana" -- precisely because they possess at least the warmth stage, meaning they have genuine capacity to generate the noble path. This is a specific Abhidharma technical claim, not merely a metaphor.

**Recommendation:** A footnote connecting "warmth of wholesome dharmas" to the ushmagata stage of the four roots concordant with penetration would illuminate the precise doctrinal meaning. The footnote at [^four_roots] in the earlier section (line 460) already discusses these four roots, so a cross-reference would suffice.

**Severity:** m (the translation is accurate; the connection is implicit but could be made explicit)

## Issues Not Found (Positive Confirmations)

1. **Four shramana types:** The classification into victorious, teaching, living-by, and corrupting shramanas is well-attested in both Pali (the four recluses of MN 40/Culaassapura Sutta) and Sarvastivada-Yogacara sources. The translation's rendering is doctrinally accurate.

2. **Brahmin threefold classification:** By birth, by designation, by right conduct -- this maps correctly to the standard Buddhist reinterpretation of brahmin status found across the Nikayas/Agamas (cf. the Assalayana Sutta, MN 93, and numerous Dhammapada verses). The definition of "brahmin by right conduct" as one whose task is complete aligns with the arhat identification standard in early Buddhism.

3. **Five types of bhikshu:** The fivefold classification (by begging, self-proclaimed, by designation, destroyer of afflictions, formally ordained) is standard Vinaya-Abhidharma material. No doctrinal issues.

4. **Three types of diligence and the latent/active distinction:** The ceasing of latent tendencies (*anushaya*) vs. active entanglements (*paryavasthana*) at line 428 is doctrinally precise and consistent with the Sravakabhumi's framework established in earlier fascicles.

5. **Six categories as "alternative designations for the person":** The Chinese 補特伽羅異門 is correctly rendered as "alternative designations for persons." The concluding line (434) correctly identifies all six as designations for *pudgala*, which is the Sravakabhumi's consistent framework.

6. **Noble eightfold path exclusivity claim:** Line 410's statement that the four shramana types exist only where the noble eightfold path is established, and are "entirely absent" in non-Buddhist teachings, faithfully renders the Chinese and reflects a standard Theravada/Sarvastivada doctrinal claim (cf. SN 45.14-15, the Subaddha Sutta at DN 16).
