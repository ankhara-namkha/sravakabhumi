## Classification Criteria Applied

- **ESSENTIAL:** Reader genuinely needs this to follow or trust the text. Doctrinal clarifications that affect meaning, attribution controversies, translation decisions that change the sense, significant tradition comparisons, Taisho/manuscript issues.
- **VALUABLE:** Enriches understanding but text is comprehensible without it. Parallel passages, cross-references with genuine illumination, Sanskrit etymology for less familiar terms, tradition comparisons where the difference matters.
- **MARGINAL:** (a) Obvious from context or already stated in the body text; (b) cross-reference that adds no context; (c) etymology for terms so standard that any reader of this translation knows them; (d) note provides information that specialist readers would know and non-specialists don't need.
- **REDUNDANT:** Restates something already established in an earlier footnote in the same or earlier fascicle, or simply repeats what the body text just said.

## Analysis: Where Footnotes Are Over-Dense

### Pattern 1: Term re-definitions across fascicles
The `[^attr]` note (7 redundant copies), `[^three_sufferings]` (2 copies), `[^four_inversions]` (2 copies), and the cross-references to the First Yoga Place (2 copies) account for most of the redundancy. In print, these should appear once each.

### Pattern 2: Notes restating the body text
The largest group of MARGINAL footnotes (approx. 30) are notes where the body text already explains the concept clearly. Examples: [^two_applications], [^outflows], [^three_realms], [^bhavagra], [^two_attentions], [^body_threefold], [^formless_aggregates], [^five_objects]. These were added as annotations during the drafting process but don't add reader value.

### Pattern 3: One-line cross-references without added content
About 15 footnotes function purely as cross-references (e.g., [^conduct_types], [^body_parts], [^scope_note], [^three_object_types], [^five_faults], [^quiescence_close]). These tell the reader "see section X" or "this parallels Y" without explaining what the comparison reveals. They should either be expanded into genuinely illuminating notes or removed.

### Pattern 4: Etymology for well-established terms
Several notes provide Sanskrit etymology for terms so well-established in Buddhist English that any reader of this translation knows them: [^outflows] (*asrava*), [^three_realms] (kama/rupa/arupya dhatu), [^bhavagra] (summit of existence). These were appropriate as first-instance glosses but are MARGINAL as standalone footnotes.
