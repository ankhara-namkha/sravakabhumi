# Agent R: Readability Review — Fascicle 30, Sections 30.6–30.9

**Reviewer:** Agent R (Readability)

## Summary Assessment

Sections 30.6–30.9 are largely solid work. The prose is cleaner than what Agent R typically finds in mid-fascicle material. However, several issues warrant attention: one severe nested-qualification sentence in 30.7, a pattern of academic register in 30.9's definitions, mild formula monotony in the definition-repetition structure of 30.9, and a handful of sentences that cross the 50-word threshold. Overall severity: moderate. No section requires wholesale revision.

## Findings by Issue Type

---

### (1) Translated-ese — Prose assembled from Chinese rather than written in English

**R-1 [M] 30.7, second paragraph**

> "As with moral conduct, so too with sense restraint (*indriya-samvara*), moderation in food, wakefulness through the first and last watches of the night, alertness (*samprajanya*) in one's activities, and all the shramana adornments. Whatever wholesome dharmas pertaining to the samadhi resources one has already obtained, one protects them against regression."

The sentence beginning "Whatever wholesome dharmas pertaining to the samadhi resources one has already obtained" is a Chinese relative-clause construction transposed into English. The participial phrase before the subject makes it feel assembled. "One protects them against regression" is also thin — it drops abruptly after the long preceding list.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The same applies to sense restraint (*indriya-samvara*), moderation in food, wakefulness through the first and last watches of the night, alertness (*samprajanya*) in one's activities, and all the other adornments of the shramana life. Whatever wholesome dharmas of this kind have already been obtained, one guards carefully against regression."

---

**R-2 [M] 30.8, "Perfection of Place," item (1)**

> "A place that from the beginning is beautiful in form, pleasing to all, clean and free from impurity, complete with gardens, groves, and pools. Clear and delightful, with level ground, free from thorns and debris. Seeing it, the mind is purified; dwelling there, one joyfully applies oneself to the practice of abandonment, and the mind is gladdened and sustained in concentration."

The opening fragment ("A place that from the beginning is beautiful in form...") is a dangling nominal construction common in Chinese texts but ungrammatical in English. The sentence never completes. "Practice of abandonment" also sounds clinical and unexplained; the practitioner reader will stumble.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "A place beautiful in form and pleasing to all — clean, free from impurity, graced with gardens, groves, and pools; level ground, clear of thorns and debris. Seeing such a place, the mind is purified. Dwelling there, one takes up the practice of relinquishment with joy, the mind gladdened and sustained in concentration."

Note: "practice of abandonment" → "practice of relinquishment" is also a terminology question (for Agent T to flag), but even within current wording the fragment construction needs repair.

---

**R-3 [L] 30.8, "Perfection of Deportment"**

> "Using the bedding permitted by the Buddha — large and small rope-beds, cushions of grass and leaves, and the like — one sits in the cross-legged posture, and so forth as extensively explained."

"And so forth as extensively explained" is a Chinese cross-reference formula that lands as a loose, awkward tail in English. It signals incompleteness without pointing the reader anywhere.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Using the bedding permitted by the Buddha — large and small rope-beds, cushions of grass and leaves, and the like — one takes the cross-legged posture as described in the traditional accounts."

### (2) Formula Monotony — Same phrasing repeated 3+ times

**R-4 [H] 30.9, definition-restatement pattern**

The passage in 30.9 opens with a definition of samadhi (the long sentence ending "this is called samadhi, also called wholesome mental one-pointedness"), then restates it word-for-word at the close:

> Line 228: "...causing the mind to continue — this is called samadhi (*samadhi*), and it is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness."
> Line 236: "Therefore it is said: repeated mindfulness of a homogeneous object-field, flowing without fault, conjoined with ease, causing the mind to continue — this is called samadhi, also called wholesome mental one-pointedness."

This is the Chinese structure of presenting a definition, unpacking it element by element, then restating it wholesale. In Chinese this is clean mnemonic structure. In English it reads as circular — the reader has just read this sentence two paragraphs ago. The closing restatement should either be compressed or dropped.

**Suggested fix:**
> Replace the closing restatement (line 236) with a brief orienting summary:
> "This, then, is the meaning of samadhi as mental one-pointedness: sustained, unbroken, ease-conjoined attention within a consistent field."

Or omit the restatement entirely and move directly to the following paragraph about shamatha/vipashyana classes.

---

**R-5 [M] 30.9, "conjoined with ease" repetition**

Within two consecutive sentences, "conjoined with ease" appears three times:

> "...flowing, conjoined with ease..."; "...without fault, conjoined with ease..."; "...repeated mindfulness of a homogeneous object-field, flowing without fault, conjoined with ease..."

This is deliberate doctrinal elaboration (two distinct senses of "conjoined with ease" are being distinguished), but the triple repetition within a short span, combined with the wholesale sentence restatement noted in R-4, creates a monotony problem. The solution is to resolve R-4 first; if the terminal restatement is removed, this repetition drops to two occurrences and is manageable.

### (3) Nested Qualification — Main verb buried under 2+ layers

**R-6 [H] 30.7, second paragraph (50+ words, nested)**

> "For the superior wholesome dharmas not yet attained, one generates the aspiration and heightened enthusiasm to bring them to swift and complete fulfillment — neither adding to nor diminishing from what has been taught — and one dwells with constant, vigorous diligence."

This sentence has four qualifications stacked around a simple action: (a) "for the superior wholesome dharmas not yet attained" (condition), (b) "one generates the aspiration and heightened enthusiasm" (main verb phrase), (c) "to bring them to swift and complete fulfillment" (purpose), (d) "neither adding to nor diminishing from what has been taught" (em-dash qualification), (e) "and one dwells with constant, vigorous diligence" (secondary verb). The em-dash qualification buries the conclusion.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "For wholesome dharmas of the samadhi resources not yet attained, one generates aspiration and zealous enthusiasm, dwelling with constant, vigorous diligence to bring them to swift and complete fulfillment — neither adding to nor diminishing from what has been taught."

Restructuring brings the main verbs forward and places the limiting qualification at the end where it belongs.

---

**R-7 [M] 30.8, "Counter-Mindfulness" sub-section**

> "Mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) is called counter-mindfulness — because it turns its back on and opposes all unwholesome qualities. Also, mindfulness that takes the sign of samadhi as its object is called counter-mindfulness — because it turns its back on and dispels all objects belonging to the non-concentrated state."

Two parallel definitions, each with the same structure: term → definition via long em-dash reason → second parallel. This is technically acceptable (not heavily nested), but the "turns its back on and" construction is slightly translated-ese. The conjunction "opposes" or "dispels" does the same work without the idiom.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisomanaskara*) is called counter-mindfulness because it opposes and dispels all unwholesome qualities. Mindfulness that takes the samadhi sign as its object is likewise called counter-mindfulness because it opposes and dispels all objects of the non-concentrated state."

Note: also removes the repetitive "turns its back on and" formula (appears twice).

### (4) Register Drift — Scholarly catalog rather than living text

**R-8 [M] 30.9, opening definition paragraph (lines 228–232)**

The extended definition of samadhi is important doctrine but reads as a highly technical scholastic gloss — multiple sub-definitions stacked in sequence ("What is 'repeated mindfulness'? ... What is a 'homogeneous object-field'?"). This is appropriate structure, but the language becomes flat and process-like by the third sub-definition. A practitioner reading the Visuddhimagga encounters this same structure but the prose retains some warmth.

The sentence "The objects of the concentrated state are not a single thing but many and varied" is a useful clarification, but "not a single thing but many and varied" is a clunky phrase.

**Suggested revision:**
> "The objects of the concentrated state are not one fixed thing but many and varied."

Minimal change, removes the heaviness. The rest of the sub-definition paragraph can stand.

---

**R-9 [L] 30.8, "Perfection of Place" items (2)–(4)**

Items (2), (3), and (4) in the five perfections list each end with "This is the [ordinal]." — which is a fine compact form. But item (2)'s content is awkward:

> "A place that by day is free from commotion and by night has little sound, with few mosquitoes, gnats, wind, sun, snakes, scorpions, and other harmful contacts."

"Few...wind, sun" is odd — wind and sun are not discrete countable nuisances in the same category as mosquitoes. The Chinese likely means "little wind, little harsh sun." The English as written sounds like a list of creatures that includes weather phenomena.

**Suggested revision:**
> "A place where by day there is little commotion, and by night little noise — free from mosquitoes and gnats, sheltered from wind and harsh sun, with no snakes, scorpions, or other hazardous creatures."

### (5) Sentences Over 50 Words

**R-10 [M] 30.7, second paragraph — Long sentence**

> "Whatever wholesome dharmas pertaining to the samadhi resources one has already obtained, one protects them against regression. For the superior wholesome dharmas not yet attained, one generates the aspiration and heightened enthusiasm to bring them to swift and complete fulfillment — neither adding to nor diminishing from what has been taught — and one dwells with constant, vigorous diligence."

Word count of second sentence: approximately 60 words. Already flagged in R-6 above; the rewrite there resolves this.

---

**R-11 [M] 30.9, line 234 — Long compound sentence**

> "Furthermore, this mindfulness operates upon its object without scattering, without gaps, without interruption — with earnest, unbroken application, conjoined with ease. This is called 'flowing, conjoined with ease.' Also, this mindfulness operates upon its object without any afflicted quality, in a state of extreme stability, conjoined with the ease of a well-practiced path. This is called 'without fault, conjoined with ease.'"

No single sentence exceeds 50 words here, but the structure of "this is X. Also, this is Y. This is called Y" over four sentences creates a rhythmic flatness. The two-part distinction being made (flowing vs. fault-free ease) is important and should be preserved, but the parallel phrasing becomes mechanical.

**Suggested revision:**
> "Furthermore, this mindfulness moves upon its object without scattering, without gaps, without interruption — earnest and unbroken, conjoined with ease: this is what is meant by 'flowing, conjoined with ease.' It also operates without any afflicted quality, in a state of deep stability, conjoined with the ease of a well-worn path: this is 'without fault, conjoined with ease.'"

Replacing "Also, this mindfulness operates upon its object" with "It also operates" avoids reopening the subject unnecessarily and keeps the two halves parallel without being identical.

### (6) Unexplained Jargon

**R-12 [M] 30.7 — "shramana adornments"**

> "...and all the shramana adornments."

"Shramana adornments" appears without gloss. The practitioner reader will not know what this means. The term likely refers to the contemplative qualities (restraint, contentment, vigilance, etc.) that adorn or distinguish a shramana's conduct — but this is not obvious from the phrase alone.

**Suggested fix:** Add a brief gloss:
> "...and all the adornments of the shramana life — the qualities of restraint, contentment, and vigilance that distinguish the contemplative path."

Or use a bracketed note: "...and all the shramana adornments [the practical disciplines that distinguish a monastic contemplative]."

---

**R-13 [L] 30.8 — "preparatory stage"**

> "...cultivating wholesome attention that leads toward samadhi and its prerequisites — the attention of the preparatory stage."

"The attention of the preparatory stage" (preparatory to what?) is a technical term whose referent is clear within the broader treatise but not within this sentence. A brief gloss would help.

**Suggested fix:**
> "...cultivating wholesome attention that leads toward samadhi and its prerequisites — the attention cultivated in the preparatory stages of meditation practice."

---

**R-14 [L] 30.9 — "nine stages of mental abiding" / "four modes of wisdom-practice"**

Both terms are introduced at the end of 30.9 without prior establishment in these sections:

> "Mental one-pointedness within the nine stages of mental abiding belongs to the shamatha class. Mental one-pointedness within the four modes of wisdom-practice belongs to the vipashyana class."

If these terms receive detailed treatment later in the text, this is acceptable as a forward reference. If not, brief parenthetical glosses are warranted: "the nine stages of mental abiding (described in detail below)" or similar.

This is flagged low-severity because the forward reference may be intentional.

No broken logic found in 30.6–30.9. The argumentation in all four sections is coherent and internally consistent.
