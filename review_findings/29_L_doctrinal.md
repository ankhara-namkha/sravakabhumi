# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 29, Section 29.12 (The Four Shramana Fruits)

**Reviewer:** Agent D (Doctrinal)
**Scope:** Lines 308-345 of translation/fascicle_29_en.md
**References consulted:** Bodhi, *In the Buddha's Words* (Ch. X introduction + notes); Nanamoli, *Visuddhimagga* (I.14, XXII); Chinese source fascicle_29.md lines 124-138

## Summary

Section 29.12 is doctrinally sound on the major points. The four shramana fruits, their ordering, and the relationship between path and fruit are correctly presented. The three-domain mapping of the three fetters is a distinctively Yogacara analysis faithfully rendered. Two issues of note, both minor.

---

## Findings

### 1. (m) Once-returner: "one more heavenly birth and one more human birth" — accurate to source but diverges from standard Nikaya formulation

**Location:** Line 336
**Translation:** "one will at most undergo one more heavenly birth and one more human birth"
**Chinese:** 極唯更受天有一生人有一生

**Assessment:** The translation accurately renders the Chinese, which specifies one heavenly birth (天有一生) and one human birth (人有一生) as the maximum for a once-returner. This is a more specific formulation than the standard Pali account. Bodhi (*In the Buddha's Words*, p. 4501/4513) describes the once-returner as coming back to "this world" (i.e., the sense-sphere realm) "only one more time." The Sravakabhumi's formulation — specifying both a heavenly and a human birth as separate remaining existences — appears to follow the Sarvastivada-Yogacara Abhidharma analysis, which counts heavenly and human existences separately rather than using the Pali shorthand of "one more return to this world."

**Severity:** m (minor). The translation correctly follows the Chinese. A footnote might clarify the divergence from the more familiar Pali formulation, but this is editorial, not doctrinal error. The existing footnotes do not address this point.

**Recommendation:** Consider a brief translator's note explaining that the Yogacara tradition counts heavenly and human births separately for the once-returner, whereas the Pali formula simply says "once more to this world." This would help a reader familiar with the Nikaya account understand the difference.

### 2. (m) "Thinning greed, aversion, and delusion" — textual variant handled well in footnote

**Location:** Line 344
**Translation:** "thinning greed, aversion, and delusion"
**Chinese:** 縛貪瞋癡 (where 縛 = bound/fettered, not 薄 = thinned)

**Assessment:** The footnote [^thin_or_bound] (line 382) correctly identifies the textual issue: the Taisho text has 縛 (bound), but the standard doctrinal formulation (SN 48.18, AN 9.12; Shukla Sanskrit edition) uses *tanu* (thinned/attenuated). Bodhi (*In the Buddha's Words*, p. 4501) confirms: "attenuates the three unwholesome roots — lust, hatred, and delusion — so that they do not arise often and, when they do arise, do not become obsessive." The translator's decision to follow the reading 薄 (thinned) is doctrinally correct and well justified in the footnote.

**Severity:** m (minor). The footnote is exemplary — it flags the textual problem, cites the Shukla edition, and explains the doctrinal reasoning. No further action needed.

### 3. No issue — Stream-entry guarantee correctly stated

**Location:** Lines 324, 378 (footnote)
**Translation:** Stream-entry severs afflictions that propel toward evil destinies; guarantees at most seven further existences in human or heavenly realms.

**Assessment:** Fully consistent with Bodhi (*In the Buddha's Words*, p. 4499): "The stream-enterer is assured of attaining full enlightenment in at most seven more existences, which will all take place either in the human realm or the heavenly worlds. The stream-enterer will never undergo an eighth existence and is forever freed from rebirth in the three lower realms." Also consistent with note 7 (p. 5869): "the stream-enterer is bound to reach liberation in at most seven more lives passed either in the human world or in celestial realms." No issue.

---

### 4. No issue — Three fetters correctly identified

**Location:** Lines 324-332

**Assessment:** The three fetters are correctly identified as: (1) view of the transitory collection (*satkaya-drishti*), (2) attachment to rules and observances (*shila-vrata-paramarsha*), (3) doubt (*vicikitsa*). This matches the standard formulation across all traditions. Bodhi (p. 4499): "identity view (sakkāyadiṭṭhi)... doubt (vicikicchā)... wrong grasp of rules and observances (sīlabbataparāmāsa)." The Sanskrit forms used are appropriate per project conventions.

---

### 5. No issue — Non-returner and five lower fetters

**Location:** Line 344
**Translation:** "permanently severing the five lower fetters (*pancha-avarabhagiya-samyojana*)"

**Assessment:** Correct. Bodhi (p. 4503): "The nonreturner (anāgāmī) eradicates the five 'lower fetters.' That is, in addition to the three fetters eliminated by the stream-enterer, the nonreturner eradicates two additional fetters, sensual lust and ill will." The footnote at line 5867 confirms: "The 'five lower fetters' are: identity view, doubt, grasping of rules and observances, sensual lust, and ill will." The translation does not enumerate the five individually at this point, following the Chinese, which also does not enumerate them here. No issue.

---

### 6. No issue — Path-as-shramana identification

**Location:** Line 316
**Translation:** "The noble path is called 'shramana.' The severance of afflictions is called 'fruit.'"

**Assessment:** This is a characteristic Sarvastivada-Yogacara identification (path = shramana, severance = fruit) that differs from the Pali usage where *samana* typically refers to the practitioner. The footnote [^shramana_fruits] correctly identifies this as a Sarvastivada-Yogacara formulation and cross-references the Abhidharmakosha. Doctrinally accurate and well-annotated.

---

### 7. No issue — Three-domain mapping of fetters

**Location:** Lines 326-331

**Assessment:** The mapping of the three fetters to three domains (householder, wrong teaching, right teaching) is presented as a distinctively Yogacara analysis. The footnote [^three_fetters_domains] correctly notes this is "not found in the Pali Nikayas or in the Abhidharmakosha's standard treatment." This is an accurate observation — this three-domain scheme is unique to the Sravakabhumi and represents Asanga's own analytical framework. No doctrinal error; the sequential-barrier interpretation (fear -> wrong path -> doubt on right path) is internally coherent and faithful to the Chinese.

---

## Overall Assessment

**Critical issues:** 0
**Major issues:** 0
**Minor issues:** 2

The section is doctrinally sound. The four shramana fruits, three fetters, stream-entry guarantee, and progression through the fruits are all correctly presented and consistent with established Buddhist doctrine. The two minor findings are: (1) a suggestion for an additional footnote on the Yogacara-specific once-returner formulation, and (2) acknowledgment that the 縛/薄 textual variant is already well handled. The existing footnotes demonstrate strong doctrinal awareness and appropriate cross-referencing.
