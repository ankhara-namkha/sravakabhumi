# Contemplative Practice Review: Meditation Instruction Passages

**Reviewer perspective:** Experienced meditation teacher and lineage holder evaluating whether a serious practitioner could actually practice from this translation.

**Files reviewed:**
- Fascicle 30 (30.8-30.15): Nine stages, six powers, four attentions, four types of vipashyana
- Fascicle 31 (31.3-31.9): Shamatha-vipashyana mutual support, nine approaches, timely practice, goldsmith simile, completion criteria
- Fascicle 32 (all): Beginner's practical guide, five signs, oil-bowl, graduated contemplation, four foundations, pliancy attainment
- Fascicle 33 (33.1-33.9): Seven attentions, dhyana progression
- Fascicle 26 (26.6-26.7): Impurity and loving-kindness objects
- Fascicle 27 (27.3): Mindfulness of breathing

---

## I. OVERALL ASSESSMENT

This translation is remarkably well suited for practice. A serious meditator could take fascicle 32 in particular and, with a qualified teacher, work through it as an actual practice manual. The teacher-student voice comes through clearly, the graduated instruction is logically sequenced, and the similes genuinely illuminate. The nine stages (30.10), four attentions (30.12), and seven attentions (33.4-33.7) are presented with sufficient precision for a practitioner to locate themselves within the progression.

The translation achieves what is hardest: it is both precise enough for a scholar to trace every claim to the source and alive enough for a practitioner to sit with. The register holds throughout -- this reads as instruction, not as a catalog.

What follows are specific points where a meditator might stumble, ordered by severity.

---

## II. SAFETY CONCERNS

[Safety] [32.16:Pliancy attainment] -- The description of the precursor sign on the crown of the head ("something like heaviness arises, but this is not a harmful sensation") followed by the surge of joy and rapture is handled well, and the footnote citing Tsongkhapa's warning against overestimation is exactly right. However, the passage does not explicitly state that this experience can be accompanied by unusual somatic phenomena (involuntary movement, heat, visual phenomena) that can alarm the practitioner. A footnote such as: "The initial arising of pliancy commonly involves strong somatic sensations -- warmth, involuntary movement, visual brightness -- which are signs of the wind element pervading the body. These are normal and temporary. The text says the 'leaping exuberance gradually softens'; a practitioner experiencing these should continue practice without either pursuing or suppressing them." This would prevent a common misunderstanding where beginners either chase the experience or panic at it.

[Safety] [33.6:Attention of investigation] -- The self-test where the yogin deliberately exposes the mind to an attractive object to check whether latent tendencies remain is one of the most important practical passages in the entire text, and it is translated with excellent clarity. The footnote on temporary suppression vs. permanent eradication (^temporary_suppression) is critical and well placed. No change needed. This passage alone justifies the entire worldly-path section.

[Safety] [33.14:Formless attainments] -- The passage on the sphere of nothingness describes generating conviction that "there is nothing whatsoever remaining." For a practitioner with strong samadhi but weak doctrinal grounding, this could be confused with a nihilistic realization or even mistaken for some form of awakening. The footnote on "provisional conviction" partially addresses this. Consider adding a brief note: "This conviction in 'nothingness' is a meditative technique -- a deliberately constructed perception that serves as the object for this attainment -- not a philosophical conclusion about the nature of reality."

[Safety] [33.15:Attainment of non-perception] -- The passage clearly states this attainment is entered "only by ordinary beings" who mistake the cessation of perception for liberation. The footnote is excellent. However, the running text itself could benefit from one sentence making the danger explicit: the phrase "Only non-perception is peaceful and subtle" is presented as the ordinary being's thought, but the quotation marks do not clearly distinguish it as a wrong view. The overall framing is adequate, but a practitioner reading quickly could miss that this is presented as an error. The footnote handles this, but given the severity of the mistake (pursuing a dead-end attainment), a parenthetical in the text such as "(this is the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning)" after the quoted thought would provide an additional safeguard.

[Safety] [27.3:Two faults of breath practice] -- The warning about excessively intense effort causing bodily imbalance ("trembling agitation," illness in the limbs) is well translated and clinically clear. A practitioner reading this would know to back off. This is one of the best safety passages in the text. No change needed.

## III. ACTIONABILITY ISSUES

[Actionability] [30.10:Nine stages, stage 5 "Taming"] -- The transition from stage 4 (close abiding) to stage 5 (taming) is the most significant shift in the nine stages -- from working with the mind internally to dealing with external signs and afflictions. The translation makes this clear: "Various signs cause the mind to scatter: signs of form, sound, smell, taste, and touch, as well as signs of desire, aversion, delusion, male and female, and so forth. One first takes up the perception of danger in these signs." This is actionable. However, a practitioner might wonder: how does one "take up the perception of danger" concretely? The danger-sign instruction in 32.4 provides the answer, but the cross-reference is not made here. A brief parenthetical or footnote -- "(see section 32.4 for the practical method of apprehending danger-signs)" -- would close this gap.

[Actionability] [30.13-30.14:Four types of vipashyana / three doors / six aspects] -- These sections are analytically clear but might leave a practitioner wondering: what do I actually DO on the cushion with these? The four types (correct discernment, thorough discernment, comprehensive investigation, comprehensive examination) are defined abstractly. Sections 30.15-30.16 then apply the six aspects to impurity and loving-kindness, which makes the framework concrete. The issue is that a practitioner reading 30.13-30.14 in isolation would not know how to practice. This is a structural feature of the text, not a translation problem. No change needed, but a teacher guiding a student through this material would want to assign 30.13-30.14 together with 30.15 or later, never alone.

[Actionability] [31.7:Correct practice -- the painter simile and "clearing"] -- The instruction to alternate between "generating conviction" and "clearing" the object is one of the most distinctive and practically important passages in the Sravakabhumi. The painter simile is excellent and illuminates perfectly. The five types of clearing are listed clearly. However, the key operational instruction -- "When one generates confidence regarding a particular object, one must clear that same object -- not a different one" -- is buried in what reads like a technical clarification. For a practitioner, this is a cardinal rule of the practice. It might benefit from slight emphasis, perhaps by starting a new paragraph or adding a brief note: "This principle is crucial: the clearing must be of the same object at the same scope. Clearing a different object, or at a different scope, does not produce the progressive refinement described."

[Actionability] [32.8:Graduated contemplation] -- The instruction to expand from one livid corpse to a limitless field is very clear. The five-quality criterion for when to return to shamatha ("joyful, clear, bright, free from disturbance, and without strained effort") is precisely what a practitioner needs. The instruction "The moment effort is required, return inward to cultivate stillness" is golden -- actionable, precise, memorable. No change needed.

[Actionability] [32.7:Alternating shamatha and vipashyana] -- The instruction for what to do when shamatha becomes "forced and strained" is excellent: "quickly emerge from the non-discriminating meditation object and establish mindfulness on a formed, discriminating object -- such as the impurity signs you previously apprehended." The footnote reinforces this. This is exactly the kind of practical advice a beginner needs, and it is delivered with the teacher's voice intact. No change needed.

[Actionability] [27.3:Counting cultivation -- advanced counting] -- The progression from basic counting (1-10) through advanced counting (counting groups of 100 as one, then counting ten groups of ten-as-one) is somewhat complex. A practitioner could follow this, but it requires careful reading. The text does state "When the mind remains undistracted throughout this ten-as-one counting, one is said to have become well-practiced," which provides a clear criterion. The transcending-counting passage is beautiful: "first apprehending the inbreath as it turns; when the inbreath has ceased, apprehending the empty interval between breaths; then apprehending the outbreath as it turns; when the outbreath has ceased, apprehending the empty interval between breaths." A practitioner would know exactly what to do. No change needed.

[Actionability] [27.3:Sharp vs. dull faculties] -- The distinction between teaching counting to dull faculties and allowing sharp faculties to proceed directly to following is an important pedagogical point that comes through clearly. A practitioner who naturally dislikes counting would be reassured. No change needed.

## IV. COMPLETENESS CONCERNS

[Completeness] [30.11:Six powers] -- The mapping of six powers to nine stages is compressed into a single paragraph. The footnote provides the mapping (hearing -> 1; reflection -> 2; mindfulness -> 3-4; alertness -> 5-6; diligence -> 7-8; habituation -> 9). However, in the body text, the mapping is implicit rather than explicit for stages 3-4 (mindfulness) and 5-6 (alertness). A practitioner trying to understand which power to cultivate at their current stage would need to work this out. The footnote is sufficient for a careful reader, but a table format would be clearer. This is a minor issue -- the information is present.

[Completeness] [31.9:Completion criteria] -- The criteria for knowing when each contemplation is "fully attained" are among the most practically significant passages in the text, as the footnote correctly notes. The impurity criterion is fully spelled out. The criteria for the other four contemplations are given in summary form ("for loving-kindness, the aspect of a loving mind predominates, displacing anger..."). A practitioner working with loving-kindness would benefit from the same level of detail as impurity. However, this appears to be a feature of the source text itself (the Chinese summarizes the remaining four), not a translation gap. No change needed.

[Completeness] [32.1-32.4:Five signs] -- After the footnote audit removed approximately 50 notes, I checked whether any context critical for practice was lost. The five signs are well footnoted. The teacher-frame footnote (^teacher_frame) is essential and present. The footnote on the five signs (^five_signs) noting that the first two correspond to disciplining and moistening attentions provides the cross-reference a practitioner needs. No critical context appears to be missing.

[Completeness] [32.12:Loving-kindness entry] -- The instruction to begin with "one friend, one enemy, and one neutral person" and then expand is clear and actionable. The three grades of happiness (desire-realm, joy-accompanied, beyond-joy) are specified. The transition to real contemplation through the beginningless-samsara argument is well handled. However, the text does not specify in what order the practitioner should extend loving-kindness (friends first? enemies first? all simultaneously?). The text says "toward all three categories equally," suggesting simultaneous extension. A practitioner accustomed to the Visuddhimagga's graduated approach (self -> friend -> neutral -> enemy) might find this surprising. A brief footnote noting this difference would be helpful: "The Sravakabhumi directs equal loving-kindness toward all three categories simultaneously from the outset, differing from the Visuddhimagga's graduated approach (Vism IX.1-58) where the practitioner extends from dear ones to neutral to hostile in stages."

[Completeness] [32.15:Mindfulness of breathing contemplation] -- This section is notably brief compared to the detailed treatment in 27.3. It provides the entry method (observing external wind first, then internal breath) and the three-fold foundation mapping. For a practitioner, the full counting method and sixteen victory practices from 27.3 would be needed alongside this. The text assumes the practitioner has already received those teachings. A brief cross-reference footnote would help: "For the complete breathing practice including counting methods and the sixteen victory practices, see section 27.3."

## V. TEACHER-STUDENT FRAMING

[Teacher-student] [32.1-32.17:Entire fascicle] -- The teacher-student framing is one of the great achievements of this translation. The opening "Welcome, Worthy One" is warm without being sentimental. The instructional voice is maintained throughout with consistency. The teacher speaks in the second person ("you should..."), gives direct instructions, and the student's perspective is present implicitly. The simile explanations (oil-bowl, fire-drill) are handled as the teacher decoding them for the student, which preserves the pedagogical relationship.

[Teacher-student] [32.4:Sign of danger] -- The three pairs of deepening perceptions (signlessness/non-activity, non-discrimination/freedom from longing, quiescence/peaceful bliss) are taught in a way that a student could actually work with them progressively. The teacher's voice is clear: "you should properly apprehend the sign through six aspects of contemplation." No change needed.

[Teacher-student] [32.11:Conduct between sessions] -- The instructions for leaving the meditation seat ("set the object aside and enter the village to beg for food"), avoiding physical dangers, guarding the senses, and speaking with measure are delivered in the teacher's voice with great specificity. The prohibition against disputation -- "When persons dwell in contentious speech... the mind comes to dwell in idle elaboration" -- is exactly the kind of advice a teacher gives. The fire-drill simile is well placed here as a motivational close. No change needed.

[Teacher-student] [32.17:Attainment of "Possessed of Attention"] -- The description of what the attainment looks and feels like -- "Sitting quietly in a secluded room and briefly holding the mind, body-mind pliancy arises swiftly... Even when rising from samadhi and going out for walking meditation, a residual force of pliancy continues to operate" -- is exactly what a student needs to recognize when they have crossed this threshold. This is one of the clearest descriptions of what genuine shamatha feels like in any text I know. No change needed.

## VI. SIMILE CLARITY

[Simile] [32.6:Oil-bowl simile] -- Excellent. The simile is presented fully, then decoded point by point in 32.7. The key insight -- "The point is not that practice requires terror, but that genuine understanding of what is at stake naturally concentrates attention" -- is captured in the footnote. A practitioner would understand both the simile and its application. No change needed.

[Simile] [31.11:Goldsmith simile] -- The mapping (firing = disciplining attention; cool water = moistening; expertise and tools = pliancy-generating and wisdom-purifying) is explicit in the footnote and clear in the text. The simile illuminates. A practitioner would understand the principle of alternation. No change needed.

[Simile] [31.7:Painter simile] -- This simile is the most practically important of all. The point -- that painting without ever erasing produces only muddy layers, while the cycle of drawing and erasing produces progressive refinement -- is vivid and memorable. The application to meditation practice (generating and clearing the object) is explicit. A practitioner would carry this image into their practice. No change needed.

[Simile] [32.11:Fire-drill simile] -- Brief but effective. Used here as motivational: "like the worldly method of drilling for fire, applying uninterrupted effort and earnest application." The implication -- that stopping and restarting loses all accumulated heat -- is implicit but would be understood by any practitioner. No change needed.

[Simile] [33.14:Room and open space] -- "When entering the dhyanas, the body's state is like being in a room. When entering the formless attainments, the body's state is like being in open space." This is a useful experiential pointer. Brief, clear, helpful for a practitioner navigating the form/formless boundary. No change needed.

## VII. STAGE PROGRESSION CLARITY

[Progression] [30.10-30.12:Nine stages / six powers / four attentions] -- The progression logic is clear. Each stage is defined by what the practitioner does and what changes. The four attentions (forceful, interrupted, uninterrupted, effortless) provide the experiential markers: a practitioner at stage 3-7 would recognize "interrupted attention" (effort punctuated by lapses) as their experience. The shift to "uninterrupted" at stage 8 and "effortless" at stage 9 gives clear goals. A practitioner could identify their stage. Well done.

[Progression] [33.4-33.7:Seven attentions] -- The seven-attention framework is the most complex graduated sequence in the text, and it is handled with real skill. The functional summary in 33.7 is especially valuable -- grouping the seven into four functional categories (conforming, counteracting, investigation-conforming, purity-conforming) helps a practitioner understand the logic of the sequence rather than memorizing seven items. The progression from conceptual investigation (attention to characteristics, still mixed with hearing and reflection) to pure cultivation (attention of conviction) to active severance (separation, joyful engagement, completed application) to fruit is clear.

[Progression] [33.8:Extension to higher attainments] -- The principle that the same seven-attention framework applies at every transition (first dhyana -> second, second -> third, etc.) is stated explicitly. The specific coarse-peaceful analysis for the first-to-second transition (recognizing examination and analysis as coarse) gives the practitioner a concrete model for how to apply the framework at each level. This is well structured.

[Progression] [32.1-32.17:Beginner's path overall] -- The overall arc of fascicle 32 -- from the five signs through graduated contemplation, the four foundations, conduct between sessions, and finally pliancy attainment -- is a complete beginner's curriculum. The progression logic is clear at every step. A practitioner working through this with a teacher would know exactly where they are and what comes next. This is the strongest section of the entire translation from a practice standpoint.

## VIII. SPECIFIC CLARIFICATION SUGGESTIONS (prioritized)

**High priority:**

1. [32.16] Add a footnote on common somatic phenomena during initial pliancy arising. (Safety)

2. [33.14] Add a brief note clarifying that "nothingness" conviction is a technique, not a metaphysical conclusion. (Safety)

3. [33.15] Add a parenthetical "(this is the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning)" to the non-perception passage. (Safety)

**Medium priority:**

4. [30.10, stage 5] Add a cross-reference to 32.4 for the practical method of apprehending danger-signs. (Actionability)

5. [32.12] Add a footnote noting the difference from the Visuddhimagga's graduated loving-kindness approach. (Completeness)

6. [32.15] Add a cross-reference to 27.3 for the full breathing practice. (Completeness)

**Low priority:**

7. [31.7] Consider slight emphasis on the cardinal rule about clearing the same object at the same scope. (Actionability)

## IX. PASSAGES REQUIRING NO CHANGE (commendations)

The following passages are exemplary practice instruction, requiring no modification:

- 27.3: Two faults of breath practice (bodily/mental imbalance from excessive effort)
- 27.3: Transcending the counting stage ("apprehending the empty interval between breaths")
- 30.9: Definition of samadhi as mental one-pointedness
- 31.9: Completion criteria for each contemplation
- 32.4: Sign of danger and entry into shamatha
- 32.6-32.7: Oil-bowl simile and its decoding
- 32.8: Graduated contemplation with the five-quality return-to-shamatha criterion
- 32.11: Conduct between sessions
- 32.16-32.17: Pliancy attainment and "possessed of attention" description
- 33.5: Six-aspect analysis of desire (the eight similes passage)
- 33.6: The attention of investigation self-test

These passages combine precision, clarity, and contemplative depth in a way that serves both scholar and practitioner. A meditator could sit with any of them and find the instruction opening something in their understanding.
