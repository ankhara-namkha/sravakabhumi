## Issues

### Issue 1: Footnote [^carita] -- "Five behavioral portraits" count is misleading (MODERATE)

**Location:** Line 282, footnote [^carita]

**Problem:** The footnote states "The five behavioral portraits given here correspond to the five affliction-dominated types defined in section 26.2 (types 3-7)." While technically accurate that only five types receive detailed portraits (greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive thought), the section explicitly establishes **seven** conduct types. The footnote does acknowledge that balanced-conduct and thin-dust-conduct "receive no separate portrait here," but leading with "five behavioral portraits" when the classification itself is sevenfold risks confusing the reader about the actual doctrinal category.

The Visuddhimagga (III.74) gives **six** temperaments (greedy, hating, deluded, faithful, intelligent, speculative), not five or seven. Citing it as a "parallel discussion" without noting this numerical difference could mislead a reader into thinking the systems are equivalent.

**Recommendation:** Revise the footnote opening to: "Seven conduct types are established, of which five receive detailed portraits here -- those corresponding to the five affliction-dominated types defined in section 26.2 (types 3-7)." Add a note that the Visuddhimagga's parallel system uses six temperaments with a different taxonomy (the Theravada scheme pairs faith/greed, intelligence/hate, speculation/delusion).

### Issue 2: Footnote [^aspiration_lineage] -- Gotra doctrine stated correctly but missing nuance on contested nature (MODERATE)

**Location:** Line 286, footnote [^aspiration_lineage]

**Problem:** The footnote correctly identifies that the gotra doctrine -- wherein lineage is fixed and determines ultimate path regardless of aspiration -- is "a characteristic position of the Yogacara school." It also correctly notes this "was debated in later Buddhist thought." However, two points of doctrinal nuance are missing:

(a) The Yogacarabhumi itself (this very text) represents one of the earliest formulations of the fixed-gotra position. The footnote implies this is a downstream Yogacara position when in fact the reader is looking at a primary source. The note should acknowledge this text as a locus classicus.

(b) The note does not mention the "indeterminate lineage" (aniyata-gotra) category recognized in later Yogacara (e.g., Madhyantavibhaga, Mahayanasutralamkara), which softened the deterministic position. Without this, a reader might take the passage as representing the final Yogacara position on gotra, when in fact the tradition developed further.

**Doctrinal accuracy:** The footnote is not wrong; it is incomplete. The claim that "A shravaka-lineage person who temporarily aspires to the mahayana will inevitably return to the shravaka vehicle" accurately represents what the source text says. The issue is missing context for a reader unfamiliar with the broader debate.

**Recommendation:** Add one sentence noting that the Sravakabhumi is a primary source for this position, and that later Yogacara texts introduced the concept of indeterminate lineage (aniyata-gotra), which allowed for the possibility of change.

### Issue 3: Footnote [^pratipada] -- AKBh reference should be VI.70, not VI.58 (MODERATE)

**Location:** Line 288, footnote [^pratipada]

**Problem:** The footnote cites "Abhidharmakoshabhashya (VI.58)" for the four courses of progress. The standard discussion of the four pratipadas in Vasubandhu's Abhidharmakoshabhashya is at chapter VI (Marga), karika 70 and commentary (La Vallee Poussin edition), not karika 58. Karika 58 treats a different topic (the stages of the path of seeing). The AN reference (AN 4.162) is correct.

Additionally, the Yogacara interpretation given in the translation text itself differs slightly from the Abhidharma/Nikaya interpretation. In the Nikayas (e.g., AN 4.162-163), the painful/pleasant axis is sometimes explained by the presence or absence of the five hindrances, not specifically by attainment of a root dhyana. The Sravakabhumi's explanation (painful = no root dhyana; pleasant = root dhyana attained) reflects a specifically Abhidharmic/Yogacara systematization. The footnote does not flag this interpretive tradition.

**Recommendation:** Correct the reference to AKBh VI.70. Consider adding a note that the Nikaya treatment of "painful" is broader (sometimes linked to five hindrances or intensity of afflictions) while the Sravakabhumi follows the Abhidharmic specification.

### Issue 4: Body-witness (criterion 8) -- "forward and reverse order freely" slightly expands the source (MODERATE)

**Location:** Line 252

**Translation:** "They are able to enter and emerge from these in forward and reverse order freely."

**Source text (T30.0427a04):** 已能順逆入出自在 -- "already able to enter and emerge in forward and reverse order freely"

**Problem:** The translation is accurate to the Chinese. However, the claim that the body-witness enters all eight liberations "in forward and reverse order freely" (順逆入出自在) is a strong doctrinal claim. In the Pali parallel tradition (MN 70, Kitagiri Sutta), the body-witness "contacts with the body and dwells in those liberations that are peaceful and formless" but there is no explicit mention of forward-and-reverse mastery. The Yogacara text makes a stronger claim about the body-witness's concentrative mastery than the Nikaya parallel.

This is not an error in the translation -- it faithfully represents the Chinese source. But the footnote [^eight_libs] says the body-witness is "distinguished by mastery of all eight but without having exhausted all outflows," which is an accurate summary. No doctrinal error here; noting for completeness.

**Status:** Not an error. No action required.

### Issue 5: Section (10) Regression -- "pleasant abidings in the present life" scope (MINOR)

**Location:** Lines 263-264

**Translation:** "They are liable to regress from their pleasant abidings in the present life" / "They definitely cannot regress from their pleasant abidings in the present life."

**Source:** 彼於現法樂住容有退失 / 彼於現法樂住定無退失

**Problem:** The term "pleasant abidings in the present life" (現法樂住, drishta-dharma-sukha-vihara) as used here specifically refers to the dhyanas and meditative attainments, not to arhatship itself. The translation accurately renders this. However, a reader without background might misunderstand this to mean the arhat can regress from arhatship. In Sarvastivada/Yogacara doctrine, the samayavimukta arhat can regress from dhyana attainments (and in some formulations, from arhatship itself -- a contested point), while the akopyadharma arhat cannot. The text is deliberately ambiguous about whether this regression extends to arhatship or only to meditative attainments.

The glossary entry for 現法樂住 is correct. A reader might still be confused, but the existing footnote at [^samaya] in section 26.2 (line 137) already addresses this.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding a brief cross-reference to footnote [^samaya] at this location, or adding a note here that the regression applies specifically to meditative attainments, and that whether this entails regression from arhatship itself was a topic of debate in Abhidharma.

### Issue 6: Section (11) Obstruction -- "concentration obstruction" vs. "liberation obstruction" terminology (MODERATE)

**Location:** Lines 270-272

**Translation:** "A prajna-liberated arhat (prajnavimukta) is one who has been liberated from the afflictive obstruction (kleshaavarana) but has not been liberated from the concentration obstruction (samadhyavarana)."

**Source:** 謂已解脫煩惱障未解脫定障

**Problem:** The Chinese 定障 is rendered as "concentration obstruction" (samadhyavarana). This is reasonable and matches the glossary. However, in the section 26.2 description of the same person type (line 135/141, footnote [^ubhaya]), the paired term is "liberation-obstruction portion" (解脫障分), rendered from 解脫障分. The glossary entry for 定障 notes "Also 解脫障 (liberation obstruction)."

The issue is that in 26.2 the text uses 解脫障分 (liberation-obstruction portion) while 26.3 uses 定障 (concentration obstruction) for what appears to be the same concept. The translation handles this correctly -- rendering each term as the Chinese has it -- but no footnote in 26.3 explains to the reader that 定障 and 解脫障 are the same obstruction under different names. The footnote in 26.2 ([^ubhaya]) does make this connection, but 26.3 has no corresponding note.

This could confuse a careful reader who wonders whether "concentration obstruction" in 26.3 and "liberation-obstruction" in 26.2 are distinct categories.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief footnote in 26.3 section (11) noting: "The 'concentration obstruction' (定障, samadhyavarana) is also called the 'liberation obstruction' (解脫障), as in the 26.2 discussion of this person type. Both terms refer to the obstacle preventing mastery of the eight liberations."

### Issue 7: Section (7) Preparatory Practice -- dharma-follower definition slightly compressed (MINOR)

**Location:** Line 244

**Translation:** "One who, with regard to the dharmas, does not depend on others, and following the vinaya, diligently cultivates right practice is called a dharma-follower."

**Source:** 若於諸法不待他緣。隨毘柰耶勤修正行。名隨法行補特伽羅。

**Problem:** The translation "does not depend on others" for 不待他緣 is adequate but slightly compressed. The Chinese reads more literally "does not await conditions from others" (不待他緣), meaning they do not wait for or depend on external conditions from other persons. The current rendering captures this. The phrase 隨毘柰耶 ("following the vinaya") is correctly rendered. No doctrinal error.

**Status:** Acceptable. No action required.

### Issue 8: Section (9) Birth -- list of person types is correct but context unclear for non-specialist (MINOR)

**Location:** Lines 257-259

**Translation lists:** "those with at most seven lives, clan-to-clan practitioners, those with one interval, those who attain parinirvana in the intermediate state, those who attain parinirvana upon rebirth, those who attain parinirvana without exertion, those who attain parinirvana with exertion, and upstream-goers."

**Problem:** This list is doctrinally correct and complete per the Chinese. However, a reader encountering this for the first time would not know that: (a) the first three are subtypes of stream-enterers and once-returners, and (b) the remaining five (intermediate state parinirvana, upon-rebirth, without exertion, with exertion, upstream-goers) are the classic five subtypes of non-returners (panca-anagamin). This classification is standard in Abhidharma (cf. AKBh VI.36-38) and in the Nikayas (cf. SN 46:3, AN 9:12). The section provides no footnote explaining this structure.

**Recommendation:** Add a footnote explaining that the eight person types listed here map to three stages: (1) "at most seven lives" and "clan-to-clan" are subtypes of stream-enterers; (2) "one interval" bridges once-returning and non-returning; (3) the remaining five are the standard five subtypes of non-returners (panca-anagamin) classified by where and how they attain parinirvana. This is standard Abhidharma classification and crucial context.

### Issue 10: Footnote [^eight_libs] -- body-witness "mastery" claim needs qualification (MINOR)

**Location:** Line 290, footnote [^eight_libs]

**Problem:** The footnote states "The body-witness (kayasakshi) is distinguished by mastery of all eight but without having exhausted all outflows -- i.e., they have deep concentration but have not yet become an arhat."

This implies the body-witness is necessarily below arhat level. While the Sravakabhumi text here indeed says 而未能得諸漏永盡 ("has not yet attained the permanent exhaustion of all outflows"), in the broader Abhidharma system (and per MN 70 as discussed in Bhikkhu Bodhi's notes), the body-witness category can include persons from stream-entry up to the path of arhatship (i.e., anyone with formless attainments who has destroyed *some* but not *all* taints). Bhikkhu Bodhi's note on MN 70 states: "This includes all those from persons on the path to arahantship down to stream-enterers who attain the formless attainments."

The footnote's phrasing "they have deep concentration but have not yet become an arhat" is accurate for the Sravakabhumi's usage but could be read as excluding the possibility that a body-witness could be very advanced (e.g., a non-returner). Adding "ranging from stream-entry to the path of arhatship" would be more precise.

**Recommendation:** Revise to: "The body-witness is distinguished by mastery of all eight liberations without having exhausted all outflows -- they may range from stream-enterer to one on the path of arhatship, possessing deep concentration but not yet having attained final liberation."
