**Auditor:** Scholarly Annotator (Nalanda Translation Committee simulation)

**Scope:** All footnotes in F26 (52 notes) and F27 (48 notes) — 100 total
**Cross-referenced:** Chinese source (T1579 fasc. 26-27), MASTER_GLOSSARY.md, Visuddhimagga (Nanamoli tr.), Analayo's Satipatthana, Mipham's Gateway, Bodhi's In the Buddha's Words, later fascicles 28-33

## I. EXECUTIVE SUMMARY

Overall footnote quality is **high** — among the best scholarly apparatus I have seen in a translation of this type. The annotations are doctrinally informed, consistently voiced, and genuinely illuminating for the dual practitioner/scholar audience. However, a systematic audit reveals:

- **4 factual inaccuracies** requiring correction (2 moderate, 2 minor)
- **6 consistency issues** (voice/depth unevenness)
- **3 over-annotations** (redundant or trivially restating the main text)
- **5 reference-quality issues** (imprecise or potentially misleading citations)
- **2 cross-reference accuracy problems**
- **3 items needing update** given material translated in later fascicles

Total: **23 findings** (4 critical/moderate, 19 minor/editorial)

## II. FACTUAL ACCURACY FINDINGS

### F-1 [MODERATE] — `[^corpse_stages]` (F26, line 588): Visuddhimagga asubha reference range

**Current text:** "not found in the Visuddhimagga's tenfold *asubha* (VI.1-95)"

**Issue:** The Visuddhimagga's chapter VI on foulness as a meditation subject runs from section VI.1 to approximately VI.95 in some numbering systems, but the *ten kinds of foulness* are actually defined starting at VI.1 and treated through approximately VI.87, with general material following. More importantly, the paragraph reference "VI.1-95" is ambiguous between section numbers and paragraph numbers — Nanamoli's translation uses paragraph numbers, and in his edition chapter VI spans paragraphs 1-89 within the chapter (approximately pp. 169-184). The standard citation format should use "Vism VI" or "Vism VI.1ff" without implying that it runs to paragraph 95.

**Additionally:** The note states the Visuddhimagga's tenfold asubha includes "cut-up, hacked-and-scattered, worm-infested." Checking the Visuddhimagga table of contents (ch. VI): "The Bloated, Livid, Festering, and Cut Up" and "The Gnawed, Scattered, Hacked and Scattered, Bleeding, Worm-infested, and a Skeleton." The full ten are: (1) bloated, (2) livid, (3) festering, (4) cut up, (5) gnawed, (6) scattered, (7) hacked and scattered, (8) bleeding, (9) worm-infested, (10) skeleton. The note correctly identifies that the Sravakabhumi omits "cut-up, hacked-and-scattered, worm-infested" — these are indeed in the Visuddhimagga but not in the Sravakabhumi's list. The factual claim is correct on content but the citation format is imprecise.

**Recommendation:** Change "VI.1-95" to "Vism VI" or "Vism ch. VI (The Ten Kinds of Foulness)."

### F-2 [MODERATE] — `[^maitri_object]` (F26, line 608): Visuddhimagga IX.88-90 citation on equanimity

**Current text:** "The Pali commentarial tradition (Visuddhimagga IX.88-90) treats equanimity as doctrinally subsequent to and dependent upon the first three brahmaviharas."

**Issue:** The Visuddhimagga's chapter IX ("The Divine Abidings") does discuss equanimity as requiring the prior development of loving-kindness, compassion, and sympathetic joy. However, the specific paragraph range IX.88-90 should be verified. In Nanamoli's translation, the equanimity section of chapter IX begins later — the loving-kindness section runs through approximately IX.87, compassion is IX.88-109, gladness is IX.110-119, and equanimity is IX.120-130 (approximate paragraph numbers vary by edition). The claim that equanimity is "doctrinally subsequent to and dependent upon the first three" is stated at IX.96 or the transition to equanimity — not at IX.88-90, which falls within the compassion section.

**Recommendation:** Update the reference to the correct paragraph range where equanimity's dependence is discussed (approximately Vism IX.96 or IX.120ff), or use the more general citation "Visuddhimagga ch. IX" to avoid paragraph-precision errors. Alternatively, confirm against the specific edition being used.

### F-3 [MINOR] — `[^body_parts]` (F26, line 586): "thirty-two parts of the body"

**Current text:** "parallels the thirty-two parts of the body (*dvattimsatikaya*) enumerated in the Pali Satipatthana Sutta (MN 10)"

**Issue:** The standard Pali list is actually *thirty-one* parts of the body in some editions of MN 10 (as Analayo discusses in *Satipatthana*, pp. 139-141), not thirty-two. The number thirty-two appears in later Pali commentarial literature. The Khuddakapatha version gives thirty-two. MN 10 itself, depending on the edition, lists either 31 or 32 (the discrepancy is over whether "brain" [matthalunga] is included). The footnote's claim of "thirty-two" is not wrong per se — it matches the common traditional count and the Khuddakapatha — but the attribution specifically to MN 10 is debatable.

**Recommendation:** Either change to "the parts of the body (*kaya-gata-sati*) enumerated in the Pali suttas (cf. MN 10, Khuddakapatha 3)" or add a brief qualification: "traditionally counted as thirty-two (the number varies slightly by source)."

### F-4 [MINOR] — `[^three_conditions]` (F27, line 532): Claim about attention as dominant condition

**Current text:** "For mind consciousness, the text specifies that attention (*manaskara*) serves as the dominant condition, since there is no separate physical organ for mental cognition — a distinctively Yogacara position"

**Issue:** Re-reading the Chinese source at T30.0434a, the text says 此生作意為增上緣 ("this-born attention serves as the dominant condition") for mind consciousness. The footnote's characterization is accurate regarding the text's content. However, the claim that this is "a distinctively Yogacara position" may be slightly overstated — the Sarvastivada Abhidharma also discusses the role of *manaskara* in the arising of mental consciousness, and the claim that the Abhidharmakosha "generally treats the mind faculty itself as the dominant condition" is standard but the distinction is more nuanced than the footnote implies. The Abhidharmakosha II.34 discusses the six causes, and the mind-faculty (*mano-dhatu*) as immediately preceding condition for the sixth consciousness is a separate question from the dominant condition issue.

**Recommendation:** Soften to "a position characteristic of the Yogacara tradition" or add "though related formulations appear in Sarvastivada Abhidharma."

## III. OVER-ANNOTATION FINDINGS

### O-1 [MINOR] — `[^dharma_phenomena]` (F27, line 480)

The note explains that *dharma* here means "any conditioned phenomenon" — this is standard Buddhist usage that the main text already makes clear through context ("only formations, only dharmas, only events, only causes, only results"). The note then gives five Sanskrit equivalents for the five terms — genuinely useful. The redundancy is minor; the Sanskrit identifications redeem the note. **No action required** but worth noting as borderline.

### O-2 [MINOR] — `[^conduct_types]` (F27, line 482)

This note explains the five character types and their purifying-conduct pairings, then refers back to "section 26.2 for the full typology." This information is already provided in `[^carita]` (F26, line 282) and `[^matching_objects]` (F26, line 556). Since F27 is a separate fascicle, some repetition for a reader starting at F27 is justified — but the three notes together create redundancy for anyone reading sequentially.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding "For full definitions, see the opening of fascicle 26 (section 26.2)" and trimming the pairing list, since `[^matching_objects]` already covers it.

### O-3 [MINOR] — `[^four_reasonings_ref]` (F27, line 462)

This note reprises the four types of reasoning already explained in `[^four_reasonings]` (F26, line 550). The F27 note is slightly shorter and adds the cross-reference "Defined in detail in section 26.5." Given that these are separate fascicles, the repetition is defensible but the two notes should be harmonized in level of detail.

**Recommendation:** The F27 note could simply say "The four types of reasoning (*yukti*) — dependence, function, proof, nature of things — are defined in detail in section 26.5, note [four_reasonings]." This avoids restating definitions.

## IV. CONSISTENCY FINDINGS

### C-1 [MODERATE] — Length and depth variation

The footnotes range from 1-sentence glosses (e.g., `[^samaya]`, `[^dukkha_vedana]`, `[^bhavagra]` — each under 30 words) to mini-essays of 150+ words (e.g., `[^sixteen_victory_practices]`, `[^three_conditions]`, `[^maitri_object]`). This variation is *mostly* appropriate — brief notes for simple glosses, longer notes for complex doctrinal points. However, there are cases where the variation seems uneven:

- `[^five_affliction_types]` (F26, line 600): 2 sentences. Lists five technical terms with Sanskrit but gives no doctrinal context or cross-references.
- `[^five_desires]` (F26, line 592): 4 sentences. Lists five items with full analysis and a claim about uniqueness.
- Both are five-item lists at the same structural level, but one gets triple the annotation depth.

**Recommendation:** Either expand `[^five_affliction_types]` to match or acknowledge the difference is justified (the affliction categories are more widely known than the five-desire scheme).

### C-2 [MINOR] — Voice variation: "Translator's voice" vs. "Reference voice"

Most notes adopt a neutral scholarly voice: "The X is Y. This differs from Z." But a few shift to a more interpretive register:

- `[^image_synonyms]` (F26, line 546): "perhaps referring to the luminous quality" — appropriately hedged.
- `[^maitri_object]` (F26, line 608): "Whether it is implied... is a question the text leaves open" — interpretive but valuable.
- `[^transformation]` (F26, line 552): "This is distinct from the ultimate *ashraya-paravritti* described in later Yogacara soteriology" — scholarly positioning.

These are fine and even desirable. The voice is consistent enough. No action required.

### C-3 [MINOR] — Cross-fascicle first-use glosses

Several terms receive glosses in F26 that are not re-glossed in F27:
- "Outflows" (*asrava*) — glossed in F26 `[^outflows]`, used without gloss in F27.
- "Transitory collection" (*satkaya*) — glossed in F26 `[^satkaya]`, used in F27 without re-glossing.

Given the design convention that each fascicle should be somewhat self-contained, a reader beginning at F27 would miss these. However, the main text does provide parenthetical Sanskrit on first use in each fascicle, so the footnote absence is less critical.

**Recommendation:** Minor. Consider adding a brief re-gloss note for *asrava* in F27 at first occurrence, as it is a key soteriological term.

### C-4 [MINOR] — Inconsistent reference library citation format

Some notes cite references with author + work + specific section (e.g., "Visuddhimagga IX.88-90," "AN 4.162," "AKBh VI.70"). Others cite only author + work (e.g., "Cf. Analayo, *Mindfulness of Breathing*, ch. on the dhyana factors" — no page or section number). The "ch. on the dhyana factors" is imprecise — Analayo's book has numbered chapters, and the relevant material is in ch. III-IV (as correctly cited elsewhere in `[^sixteen_victory_practices]`).

**Recommendation:** Standardize. Specific Nikaya references (MN, DN, SN, AN) are fine as-is. For modern secondary works, use chapter numbers consistently: "Analayo, *Mindfulness of Breathing*, ch. III" rather than "ch. on the dhyana factors."

### C-5 [MINOR] — Sanskrit equivalents: inclusion vs. omission

Some notes provide full Sanskrit for every term (e.g., `[^four_reasonings]` gives all four in Sanskrit). Others omit Sanskrit even for technical terms (e.g., `[^six_impurities]` lists six types without any Sanskrit). The inconsistency is minor but noticeable.

### C-6 [MINOR] — Identical `[^attr]` note in both fascicles

Both F26 and F27 open with identical `[^attr]` notes about the Maitreya/Asanga attribution. This is correct — each fascicle should be self-contained. No action needed; noting for completeness.

## V. REFERENCE QUALITY FINDINGS

### R-1 [MODERATE] — `[^pratipada]` (F26, line 288): AN 4.162 citation

**Current text:** "This classification appears in the Anguttara Nikaya (AN 4.162) and Abhidharmakoshabhashya (VI.70)."

**Issue:** The four *pratipada* are discussed in AN 4.162 (Padhana Sutta in some editions), but the standard Pali reference for the four courses of progress is AN 4.163-166 (the Paṭipadā Suttas). AN 4.162 may discuss effort rather than specifically the four courses. The numbering of AN suttas varies by edition (PTS vs. Burmese vs. Thai). The Abhidharmakosha reference (VI.70) should be double-checked — the four *pratipada* appear in AKBh ch. VI but the exact verse/section number varies between Pruden's translation and La Vallee Poussin's edition.

**Recommendation:** Verify AN 4.162 vs. 4.163-166 against a specific Pali canon edition. Consider citing the vagga name instead: "AN 4, Paṭipadā Vagga."

### R-2 [MINOR] — `[^birth_types]` (F26, line 292): AKBh VI.36-38 citation

**Current text:** "standard Abhidharma classification (cf. AKBh VI.36-38)"

The five types of non-returners are indeed discussed in AKBh ch. VI, but the exact verse range may be VI.35-39 or VI.38-41 depending on the edition. This is a minor precision issue. **No urgent action** but worth verifying against a specific edition.

### R-3 [MINOR] — `[^counting_cultivation]` (F27, line 498): Visuddhimagga VIII.145-190

**Current text:** "The Visuddhimagga (VIII.145-190) presents a similar counting practice"

**Issue:** This paragraph range is very broad — it covers nearly the entire mindfulness-of-breathing section of chapter VIII. The counting method specifically is at approximately VIII.189 (the eight stages of the breath path) or earlier paragraphs. The claim is not wrong but the range is so broad as to be unhelpful.

**Recommendation:** Narrow to the specific counting discussion: "Visuddhimagga VIII.189ff" or "VIII.182-190" for the counting method specifically.

### R-4 [MINOR] — `[^dhyana_anapanasati_limit]` (F27, line 516): Visuddhimagga VIII.189-190

This is a more precise citation and correctly identifies the Theravada position. The specific paragraphs VIII.189-190 discuss the fact that anapanasati can develop all four jhanas. This appears accurate based on the Visuddhimagga structure. **No correction needed.**

### R-5 [MINOR] — `[^three_sufferings]` (F27, line 546): Visuddhimagga XVI.34-38

**Current text:** "Cf. Tsongkhapa, *The Great Treatise* Vol. 1, ch. on the truth of suffering; Visuddhimagga XVI.34-38."

**Issue:** The Visuddhimagga chapter XVI discusses the truths, and XVI.34-38 should discuss the three types of suffering. This appears plausible given the chapter structure, but the Visuddhimagga's discussion of *tri-duhkhata* (Pali *ti-dukkhatā*) may be at a slightly different paragraph range depending on edition. The claim is reasonable; the citation format is acceptable.

**Recommendation:** Verify paragraph numbers against Nanamoli's 4th edition pagination.

## VI. CROSS-REFERENCE ACCURACY FINDINGS

### X-1 [MODERATE] — `[^nine_stages]` (F26, line 544): Forward reference to F30

**Current text:** "They receive their full, detailed treatment in fascicle 30 (section 30.10), where each stage is defined and the six powers and four types of attention that drive progression through them are explained."

**Verification:** Section 30.10 in fascicle_30_en.md (line 244) is indeed titled "The Nine Stages of Mental Abiding" and provides full definitions plus six powers (30.11) and four types of attention (30.12). The cross-reference is **accurate**.

The nine stage names given in the note are: (1) internal abiding, (2) continuous abiding, (3) re-settling, (4) close abiding, (5) taming, (6) pacifying, (7) completely pacifying, (8) one-pointed, (9) settling in equipoise. Checking against F30: (1) inward abiding, (2) settled abiding, (3) re-established abiding, (4) close abiding, (5) taming, (6) pacifying, (7) fully pacifying, (8) one-pointed attention, (9) equipoise.

**Issue:** The names differ slightly between the F26 footnote and the F30 main text:
- F26 note: "internal abiding" / F30 text: "inward abiding"
- F26 note: "continuous abiding" / F30 text: "settled abiding"
- F26 note: "re-settling" / F30 text: "re-established abiding"
- F26 note: "completely pacifying" / F30 text: "fully pacifying"
- F26 note: "settling in equipoise" / F30 text: "equipoise"

These are not errors in the note — the F26 note was presumably written before F30 was translated, and the F30 translation chose slightly different renderings. But now that F30 is complete, the F26 note should be harmonized.

**Recommendation:** Update the nine stage names in the F26 `[^nine_stages]` footnote to match the renderings used in section 30.10 of the final translation.

### X-2 [MINOR] — `[^three_signs]` (F26, line 558): Forward reference to F30

**Current text:** "elaborated in detail in fascicle 30"

**Verification:** The three signs appear in F30 (section 30.11-30.12 area) as part of the shamatha discussion. The cross-reference is accurate but vague — it could specify the section number.

**Recommendation:** Add a section reference: "elaborated in detail in fascicle 30 (sections 30.10-30.12)" or whichever section specifically treats the three signs.

## VIII. NOTES OF COMMENDATION

The following footnotes are exemplary and should be maintained as the benchmark:

1. **`[^image_discernment]` (F26):** Precisely explains the savikalpaka/nirvikalpaka distinction with appropriate reference to the Chinese and doctrinal function. Model of what a scholar-practitioner annotation should be.

2. **`[^transformation]` (F26):** Correctly distinguishes the Sravakabhumi's shamatha-level ashraya-paravritti from the Mahayana soteriological concept. Shows doctrinal sensitivity without over-explanation.

3. **`[^sixteen_victory_practices]` (F27):** The most complex note in the entire set. Correctly maps the Sravakabhumi's sixteen practices against MN 118, explains the counting discrepancy, notes the three-realm replacement of patinissagga, and places the practices within the five-stage path. Cross-references Analayo appropriately. This is the standard all other complex notes should aspire to.

4. **`[^dhyana_anapanasati_limit]` (F27):** Clearly states the Sravakabhumi's distinctive position, contrasts it with the Theravada view, cites the Visuddhimagga precisely, and refers to Analayo for further reading. Balanced and informative.

5. **`[^three_grades]` (F26):** Notes the Sravakabhumi's distinctive exegesis without overclaiming, and correctly contextualizes the broader commentarial tradition.

6. **`[^revata]` (F26):** Elegantly untangles the non-one-to-one mapping between the four pervasive objects and the four aspects of Revata's question. This is exactly the kind of structural insight a reader needs.

## X. OVERALL ASSESSMENT

The footnote apparatus in F26-F27 is substantive, well-informed, and serves the dual audience effectively. The annotations illuminate without overwhelming. Doctrinal claims are generally accurate and well-sourced. The main weaknesses are: (1) a handful of imprecise canonical/commentarial citations that need verification against specific editions; (2) terminology drift between the F26 footnotes and the later fascicle translations that should be harmonized now that the full translation is complete; and (3) a few forward references that can now be made specific. None of these undermine the scholarly value of the annotations.

**Verdict:** These fascicles set a strong benchmark. The 14 actionable items are refinements, not repairs.
