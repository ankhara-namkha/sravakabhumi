## 5. Taisho Source Status

**PASS with a documentation gap.**

The copyright page (00_copyright.md, line 10) correctly identifies the primary source: "English translation from the Taisho Shinshu Daizokyo, Vol. 30, No. 1579."

The bibliography (00_bibliography.md, lines 5–6) provides full bibliographic data: "edited by Takakusu Junjiro and Watanabe Kaigyoku, vol. 30, no. 1579. Tokyo: Taisho Issaikyo Kankokai, 1924-1934." Publication 1924–1934 means the Taisho text is in the public domain in virtually all jurisdictions (life + 70 years, or a fixed term from publication, well expired by 2026).

**[Minor — documentation gap] [00_copyright.md]** — The copyright page does not address whether the Taisho edition's editorial apparatus (introduction, notes, indices) carries residual editorial copyright separate from the underlying texts. Standard publishing practice for translations of public domain texts is to include a brief disclaimer such as: "The underlying Chinese text is in the public domain. The editorial apparatus of the Taisho Shinshu Daizokyo may be subject to separate copyright claims; this translation does not reproduce that apparatus." This is a belt-and-suspenders measure, not a strict legal requirement, since the translation does not reproduce Taisho editorial matter. However, it would be a standard disclosure for scholarly publications. Consider adding to the copyright page.

## 6. AI Attribution

**PASS. Attribution is complete and honest.**

The methodology (00_methodology.md, lines 37–63) is transparent and thorough about the AI-generated nature of the translation:

- Line 37: "This translation was produced computationally, using a multi-stage process..."
- Line 61: "This is a machine translation, and it would be dishonest to present it as anything else."
- The methodology explicitly names limitations: lack of direct contemplative understanding, lack of native bilingual intuition, lack of decades of immersion.

The copyright page attributes copyright to "Ankhara" without specifying whether Ankhara is a person or an entity. The methodology identifies the translator as "computational" without naming the specific AI system (the AI system). This is a design choice, not a legal defect — the copyright is asserted by the human/entity who directed and published the work, which is standard practice for AI-assisted works.

**[Note — no defect, but consider]** — AI-generated works currently occupy an uncertain copyright status in multiple jurisdictions. In the United States, the Copyright Office (as of 2024–2025) has declined to register copyright in works with substantial AI authorship without meaningful human creative selection and arrangement. The methodology's multi-stage review process, editorial synthesis, glossary development, and structural choices provide a significant basis for arguing human creative contribution, but this has not been tested in court. The CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 license requires a valid copyright holder to grant it. If copyright cannot be asserted in the work (because a court finds it is not copyrightable), the CC license cannot be granted, which means the "ShareAlike" and "NonCommercial" restrictions also cannot be enforced. **Recommendation:** Consult a copyright attorney in the publication jurisdiction before finalizing the CC license. Alternatively, consider releasing under CC0 (public domain dedication), which does not require a valid copyright to be effective and achieves the publication goal of open access.

No claim of human authorship appears anywhere in the text. No AI system is falsely presented as a human translator. Attribution is honest.

## 8. Digital Edition Reference — Consistency and Licensing Implications

**PASS with one clarification needed.**

The digital edition URL (github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi) appears in:

- **00_methodology.md** (line 65): "published at github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi under a Creative Commons license"
- **00_abbreviations.md** (line 29): "a consolidated glossary with diacritical forms is available in the [digital edition](https://github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi)"
- **00_abbreviations.md** (line 34): "a comprehensive glossary with Chinese characters is available in the [digital edition](https://github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi)"

The URL is consistent across all references.

**[Minor] [00_methodology.md, line 65]** — As noted under finding #1, the methodology describes this as published "under a Creative Commons license" without specifying which Creative Commons license. Since the digital edition is a distinct publication (potentially with additional materials — Chinese characters, diacritical glossary, review findings), its license should be explicitly stated. If the digital edition is also CC BY-NC-SA 4.0, say so. If it is a different license (e.g., CC BY for the raw data, CC BY-NC-SA for the translation text), the distinction matters for users who wish to build on the digital edition's unique materials (the glossary data, the Chinese-character annotations). Recommend: Replace "under a Creative Commons license" with "under CC BY-NC-SA 4.0 (github.com/ankhara-namkha/sravakabhumi)."

**No licensing implications arise from the URL reference itself.** Naming a URL in a print publication does not transfer any rights to GitHub or create any additional licensing obligations. The GitHub repository's own license terms will govern use of the digital files.
