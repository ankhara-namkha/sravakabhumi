## Section 26.1: Opening Questions and Summary Verse

### Finding R-01: Opening question paragraph is a dense wall of text
**Severity:** MODERATE

The opening paragraph at [T30.0424a07] packs 17 questions into a single block of prose. A practitioner reading this for the first time will lose track midway. The questions themselves are clear individually, but the sheer density makes this paragraph hard to process on first reading. Consider whether a numbered or bulleted list would better serve both audiences here, since the uddana verse that follows enumerates the same topics in a more scannable way. At minimum, a translator's note orienting the reader -- "The following seventeen questions define the scope of this entire section of the Sravakabhumi" -- would help the practitioner understand why this litany appears and what function it serves.

### Finding R-02: "Ground of renunciation (naishkramya)" -- context needed
**Severity:** MINOR

The phrase "the ground of renunciation (*naishkramya*) that was previously presented and elucidated" assumes the reader has access to and memory of the preceding text. A brief footnote indicating what the "ground of renunciation" covers (the first yoga-place of the Shravaka Ground, fascicles 22-25, dealing with the path of leaving worldly life) would help a practitioner who begins reading at fascicle 26.

### Finding R-03: "How does one set out upon what is empty and without fruit?"
**Severity:** MODERATE

This final question in the opening series is cryptic. "What is empty and without fruit" could mean many things in a Buddhist context. The Chinese 云何發趣空無有果 is itself terse, but a footnote explaining that this refers to futile or fruitless endeavors in practice (i.e., how one might pursue practice yet gain nothing from it) would prevent confusion. Without context, a practitioner might wonder if this is about emptiness (sunyata) doctrine, which it is not.

### Finding R-04: Uddana verse -- clear and well-formatted
**Severity:** OK

The verse is rendered in indented block-quote format, preserving the mnemonic character. The line breaks follow the logical groupings. The footnote on pudgala/數取趣 is well-placed and informative.

### Finding R-05: "Persons" repeated in the verse line
**Severity:** MINOR

Line 3 of the verse reads "Yogins, activities, cultivation, fruits, synonyms, persons," -- the repetition of "persons" (already in line 1) may momentarily confuse a reader into thinking this is a typo rather than a deliberate second topic-heading for a different section. The footnote partially addresses this by explaining the two Chinese terms for pudgala, but the reader might benefit from knowing that the text deliberately lists "persons" twice because it returns to the topic of person-types from a different angle later.

## Section 26.2: The Twenty-Eight Types of Persons

### Finding R-06: The numbered list of 28 types -- well-structured and scannable
**Severity:** OK

The list is clean, numbered, and easy to scan. The formatting is consistent. A practitioner can quickly identify familiar categories (stream-entry, once-returning, etc.) and flag unfamiliar ones for closer reading.

### Finding R-07: "Those of thin-dust nature" needs a gloss
**Severity:** MODERATE

Item 9, "Those of thin-dust nature," is an unusual English phrase. While the term becomes clear when the full definition is read (at entry 9 below), encountering it in the list without any parenthetical gloss is jarring. "Thin-dust" is a literal rendering of 薄塵 and evokes nothing in English. A parenthetical such as "Those of thin-dust nature (i.e., having few afflictions)" in the initial list would prevent confusion. Compare how items 12-13 provide Sanskrit in their definitions -- the same principle of first-use clarification should apply to this less-intuitive term.

### Finding R-08: "Persons of balanced disposition" -- adequately clear
**Severity:** MINOR

Item 8, "Those of balanced disposition," is reasonably clear in English -- it suggests someone without extreme tendencies. However, the full definition (entry 8) reveals a subtle point: these persons are NOT free of afflictions, they simply lack fierce or enduring ones. A reader might initially assume "balanced" means "well-adjusted" or "spiritually advanced," which is the opposite of what the text intends. The definition does clarify, but the gap between the label and its meaning could cause a brief misreading. A parenthetical note in the list -- "Those of balanced disposition (equal mixture of afflictions)" -- would close this gap.

### Finding R-09: "Path-practitioners" and "Fruit-abiders" -- clear labels
**Severity:** OK

Items 10-11 use transparent English. The four fruits are named in both entries. The structure is parallel and easy to follow.

### Finding R-10: "Those liberated by occasion" -- opaque label
**Severity:** MODERATE

Item 25, "Those liberated by occasion," is not self-explanatory. The word "occasion" in English typically means "a particular time or event," which does not convey the intended meaning. The Chinese 時解脫 and Sanskrit *samayavimukta* mean something like "liberation contingent on [favorable] conditions/times" -- i.e., an arhat whose liberation can regress if conditions deteriorate. This is one of the most technically loaded terms in the list and deserves a parenthetical in the initial enumeration: "Those liberated by occasion (i.e., whose liberation depends on favorable conditions)." Without it, a practitioner will be baffled.

### Finding R-11: "Upstream-goers" -- vivid but needs context
**Severity:** MINOR

Item 24, "Upstream-goers," is a vivid English rendering of 上流. It suggests going against the stream of samsara. The definition (entry 24) clarifies that this refers to non-returners who are reborn progressively upward through higher realms. The label itself is evocative enough, but a practitioner unfamiliar with the non-returner subtypes might wonder whether "upstream" is a metaphor or literal. The definition resolves this, so the severity is minor.

## Definitions (Entries 1-28): Detailed Readability Assessment

### Finding R-12: Entries 1-2 (dull/sharp faculties) -- parallel structure, clear
**Severity:** OK

The pair is presented in clean parallel. The two subtypes (by nature vs. by cultivation) are easy to grasp. The phrase "functions sluggishly and feebly with respect to objects of knowledge" is slightly formal but adequate.

### Finding R-13: Entries 3-7 (dominated by greed/aversion/delusion/pride/discursive thought) -- highly formulaic
**Severity:** MINOR

These five entries use an identical template: "in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of X." The repetition is faithful to the Chinese (已修已習已多修習) but creates a feeling of reading a form letter. This is a structural feature of the source text, not a translation defect. However, a brief translator's note at entry 3 -- "Entries 3-7 follow a formulaic pattern that mirrors the Chinese original, emphasizing the habitual and cumulative nature of afflictive tendencies" -- would help the practitioner understand why the repetition exists and read it as deliberate rather than lazy.

### Finding R-14: "Cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated" -- slightly awkward
**Severity:** MINOR

The phrase "cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated" renders 已修已習已多修習. While accurate, the repetition of "cultivated" bracketing "habituated" is slightly awkward in English. Consider: "practiced, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated" or "cultivated, practiced, and extensively developed." The current rendering is not wrong, but a native English reader may stumble slightly on the near-repetition.

### Finding R-15: Entry 7 -- "discursive thought (vitarka)" placement
**Severity:** MINOR

The Sanskrit *vitarka* is parenthesized at entry 7, which is good for first-use convention. However, in the glossary *vitarka* is rendered as "examination, investigation," not "discursive thought." The translation file uses "discursive thought" for 尋思, while the glossary maps 尋 (*vitarka*) to "examination." This is a terminology concern more than a readability one, but from a reader's perspective, encountering "discursive thought" here and potentially "examination" elsewhere would be confusing. Flagging for Agent T to adjudicate.

### Finding R-16: Entry 8 (balanced disposition) -- long but comprehensible
**Severity:** MINOR

This is the longest of the affliction-type entries, and its key point is subtle: the balanced person has not cultivated afflictions intensely, but has also not investigated their faults, so afflictions still arise mildly. The sentence "they have no fierce greed and no enduring greed -- yet greed still manifests in accordance with those objects" captures this well. The em-dash is effective. One minor issue: the final sentence "The same applies to aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought" compresses five parallel applications into one line. This is fine for readability but should be checked for source fidelity (Agent S concern).

### Finding R-17: Entry 9 (thin-dust nature) -- the definition is clear; the label is not
**Severity:** MODERATE (for the label; the definition itself is OK)

Once a reader reaches the full definition, "thin-dust nature" makes sense: even encountering supreme objects of desire, only feeble greed arises, and for middling/lesser objects, none at all. The image of "thin dust" (薄塵) -- a person on whom afflictions barely settle -- is actually poetic, but this metaphorical layer is invisible in English without a gloss. A footnote: "The term 'thin-dust' (薄塵, *tanurajas*) is a traditional metaphor: just as only a thin layer of dust settles on a polished surface, so afflictions arise only faintly in such a person" would make this one of the most memorable categories rather than one of the most puzzling.

### Finding R-18: Entry 10-11 (path-practitioners, fruit-abiders) -- clear, concise
**Severity:** OK

Standard Buddhist categories, well-handled. The four fruits are listed with Sanskrit, and the parallel structure mirrors entries 1-2.

### Finding R-19: Entry 12-13 (faith-followers, dharma-followers) -- clear contrast
**Severity:** OK

The contrast between following through faith in others vs. following through one's own investigation of the dharma is well-drawn. The phrase "heard, received, thoroughly investigated, thought about, measured, and examined" is a long chain but is faithful to the Chinese and conveys the thoroughness of the dharma-follower's approach.

### Finding R-20: Entry 14-15 (resolved through faith, view-attainers) -- efficient but compressed
**Severity:** MINOR

These entries define themselves by reference back to entries 12-13: "It is precisely the faith-follower who... is called a person resolved through faith." This is clear but may leave a practitioner wanting more context. What does it mean to be "resolved through faith" vs. merely "following faith"? The key difference is that the faith-follower is on the path, while the person resolved through faith has realized a fruit -- but this is only implicit. A brief parenthetical -- "that is, having now realized a fruit of the path" -- after "at the time of touching and realizing one of the shramana fruits" would make this more explicit for the practitioner.

### Finding R-21: "Touching and realizing one of the shramana fruits"
**Severity:** MINOR

The phrase "touching and realizing" (得觸證) is slightly unusual in English. "Attaining and directly realizing" might flow more naturally. "Touching" as a translation for 觸 in this context carries connotations of physical contact that could mislead. This is a borderline terminology/readability issue.

### Finding R-22: Entry 16 (body-witnesses) -- "forward and reverse order" needs context
**Severity:** MINOR

"Enters and emerges from the eight liberations in forward and reverse order" -- a practitioner will wonder what the eight liberations are and what "forward and reverse order" means. A footnote listing the eight liberations (or at minimum directing the reader to where they are explained) would be helpful. The term "body-witness" itself is evocative: the person has "witnessed through the body" the liberations but has not yet exhausted the outflows. This is clear enough.

### Finding R-23: Entry 17 (at most seven lives) -- dense but clear
**Severity:** OK

This is one of the most content-rich entries: three fetters are named, the stream-entry fruit is identified, and the "at most seven lives" mechanism is explained. The three fetters are given in both English and Sanskrit, which is excellent. "Has become one of the nature of non-falling, and is certainly destined for awakening" is a strong rendering. One potential stumble: "going back and forth between the human and celestial realms" might be clearer as "cycling between human and celestial births."

### Finding R-24: Entry 18 (clan-to-clan) -- functional
**Severity:** MINOR

"Goes from family to family" renders 從家至家, which is the etymological meaning of *kulamkula*. The term is clear enough, but a practitioner may wonder: what does going from "family to family" mean in practice? A footnote explaining that this refers to taking birth in successive families (households) within the human or celestial realms -- fewer rebirths than the seven-life maximum but still multiple -- would help contextualize this subcategory of stream-enterer.

### Finding R-25: Entry 19 (one interval) -- clear
**Severity:** OK

The definition is well-structured: it identifies the person as a once-returner, explains the affliction levels severed, and describes the single remaining rebirth. "One more existence in a desire-realm celestial state" is precise.

### Finding R-26: Entry 20 (intermediate-state parinirvana) -- similes are vivid and clear
**Severity:** OK

This is one of the strongest passages in the translation. The three subtypes are distinguished with escalating similes:
1. "Like a tiny spark from kindling that, barely rising, at once extinguishes" -- immediate.
2. "Like a spark streaming from a blazing iron ingot being hammered, extinguishing before it falls" -- brief duration.
3. "Like a spark flying from hammered hot iron that, falling but not yet reaching the ground, at once extinguishes" -- almost reaching rebirth but not quite.

The similes are vivid, the progression is clear, and the three types build logically. The practitioner can visualize each scenario. The closing summary sentence ties them together well. This passage is a model for the rest of the translation.

### Finding R-27: Entries 21-23 (rebirth/without exertion/with exertion parinirvana) -- clear parallel
**Severity:** OK

These three entries form a tight parallel set and are easy to distinguish. "Without undertaking application, without making effort, and without the path manifesting through exertion" is somewhat redundant but faithful to the source and conveys the effortlessness clearly. The contrast with entry 23 ("undertakes application and makes great effort, and with the path manifesting through extreme exertion") is sharp.

### Finding R-28: Entry 24 (upstream-goers) -- clear
**Severity:** OK

"Successively reborn upward through the various planes of rebirth, reaching as far as the Akanishtha heaven or the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" gives the reader a clear picture of the upward trajectory. The term "sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" is inherently unwieldy but standard; no improvement possible without loss of accuracy.

### Finding R-29: Entry 25 (liberated by occasion) -- several comprehension hurdles
**Severity:** MAJOR

This is the most difficult entry for a practitioner to parse on first reading. Multiple issues compound:

(a) "Pleasant abidings in the present life" -- the footnote [^samaya] helps, but the phrase itself is opaque in running text. "Meditative attainments enjoyed in this life" (the footnote's own phrasing) would be clearer in the main text with the formal term in a note.

(b) "Is liable to regress" -- regress from what? This needs to be stated explicitly: "is liable to regress from their attainment of arhatship" or "is liable to lose their realization."

(c) "They may contemplate harming themselves" -- this is jarring and potentially triggering. The footnote [^selfharm] explains the Abhidharma context (arhats of dull faculties fearing regression may consider ending their lives while still liberated), but this footnote is placed at the end of the file, far from the passage. A practitioner encountering this phrase in the running text will be confused or alarmed before reaching the footnote. The footnote itself is well-written and necessary. Consider moving the footnote call to appear immediately after "contemplate harming themselves" and ensuring footnote rendering places it nearby.

(d) "[Alternatively] guard their liberation" -- the brackets around "alternatively" suggest editorial insertion, which is correct, but the abrupt shift from self-harm to guarding liberation is disorienting. The Chinese 或思自害或守解脫 presents these as two alternative responses to the possibility of regression. Making the either/or structure more explicit would help: "They may either contemplate harming themselves[^selfharm] or [alternatively] guard their liberation..."

(e) "Through each day, night, moment, eye-blink, and instant" -- this is a graduated list of time units that emphasizes constant effort. It reads well.

(f) "Have not realized the most extremely fierce [attainment]" -- "most extremely fierce" is awkward. The Chinese 最極猛利 means the highest, most intense level of realization. "Have not yet attained the most intense [level of realization]" would be clearer.

### Finding R-30: Entry 26 (immovable dharma) -- too terse
**Severity:** MINOR

"It is a person who is the opposite of the above" is faithful to the Chinese 與上相違 but leaves the practitioner without a positive definition. A brief parenthetical -- "that is, a person of sharp-faculty lineage whose liberation is stable and not subject to regression" -- would give substance to this entry without departing from the source.

### Finding R-31: Entry 27 (liberated by prajna) -- clear
**Severity:** OK

The contrast with the body-witness (entry 16) is implicit but discernible: prajna-liberated persons have exhausted outflows but lack mastery of the eight liberations. The practitioner can grasp the distinction.

### Finding R-32: Entry 28 (liberated in both respects) -- clear with good footnote
**Severity:** OK

The definition combines both aspects (outflow-exhaustion and eight-liberation mastery) cleanly. The footnote [^ubhaya] explaining the two types of obstruction is well-written and helpful. "Affliction-obstruction portion and the liberation-obstruction portion" is technical but the footnote unpacks it adequately.

## Cross-Cutting Readability Issues

### Finding R-33: Q&A structure -- consistently maintained
**Severity:** OK

Every entry follows "What is X? It is a person who..." and closes with "This is called X." The pattern is maintained without exception across all 28 entries. This mechanical consistency actually aids readability by giving the reader a predictable rhythm.

### Finding R-34: Footnote placement -- mostly good, with one structural issue
**Severity:** MINOR

Footnotes [^samaya], [^selfharm], and [^ubhaya] are all placed at the end of the document. In long documents, this creates a gap between the reference and the note content. For a practitioner reading linearly, the footnotes on "pleasant abidings," "self-harm," and "both respects" will not be visible when the terms are encountered. This is a rendering concern (depends on how the markdown is displayed) rather than a translation defect, but it is worth noting. If possible, ensure footnotes render as hover-text or nearby pop-ups rather than endnotes.

### Finding R-35: Sanskrit parentheticals -- well-calibrated
**Severity:** OK

Sanskrit terms appear in parentheses on first use in the definitions (e.g., *srota-apanna*, *sakridagamin*, *anagamin*, *shraddhanusarin*, *dharmanusarin*, etc.). This is consistent with the NTC register and helps scholars without burdening practitioners. The italicization is consistent.

### Finding R-36: No "translation-ese" detected in most passages
**Severity:** OK

The translation generally reads as natural English. Sentence structures have been adapted from the Chinese rather than calqued. Exceptions are noted in specific findings above (R-14, R-21, R-29f).

### Finding R-37: "Discursive thought" vs. "examination" -- potential downstream confusion
**Severity:** MINOR

As noted in R-15, the translation uses "discursive thought" for 尋思 (*vitarka*) throughout entries 7-9, while the glossary maps *vitarka* to "examination." If the translation later uses "examination" for 尋 in other contexts, the reader will encounter two different English renderings for what is presented as the same Sanskrit term. This is a terminology issue but affects readability directly. Needs resolution by Agent T.

## Overall Readability Assessment

**Rating: GOOD, with targeted improvements needed**

The translation is highly readable for a practitioner with moderate Buddhist background. The Q&A structure is faithfully maintained and creates a consistent, approachable rhythm. Definitions 1-9 build logically from simple (dull/sharp) to complex (thin-dust, balanced), and the formulaic repetition in entries 3-7, while dense, accurately mirrors the source and conveys the systematic nature of the classification.

The strongest passage is entry 20 (intermediate-state parinirvana), where the three similes of sparks are vivid, graduated, and memorable. This sets a high standard for simile translation throughout the project.

**Priority fixes (MODERATE or above):**
1. **R-07 / R-17:** "Thin-dust nature" needs a gloss or footnote explaining the metaphor. Currently the most puzzling label in the list.
2. **R-10:** "Those liberated by occasion" needs a parenthetical in the initial list to prevent confusion about "occasion."
3. **R-29:** Entry 25 (liberated by occasion) has multiple comprehension hurdles: opaque phrasing, jarring self-harm reference without nearby context, and the awkward "most extremely fierce." This is the weakest entry and needs targeted revision.
4. **R-01:** The opening question block would benefit from structural reformatting or at minimum a brief orienting note.
5. **R-03:** "What is empty and without fruit" needs a footnote to prevent misreading as sunyata doctrine.

**Minor improvements (would polish but not essential):**
- R-02: Footnote on "ground of renunciation" for readers starting at fascicle 26.
- R-08: Parenthetical for "balanced disposition" in the initial list.
- R-13/R-14: Translator's note on the formulaic pattern in entries 3-7.
- R-20: Make "touching and realizing" more natural.
- R-24: Footnote on clan-to-clan meaning.
- R-30: Brief positive definition for "immovable dharma."

**No issues found with:**
- Uddana verse formatting and footnote (R-04)
