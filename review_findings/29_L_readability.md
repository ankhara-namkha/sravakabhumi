## Findings

### FINDING 1 — Formula Monotony: "Because of X… one Y" pattern repeated in §§ (1)(2)(3)
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Lines 326–330 (domains 1, 2, 3)

Each of the three domain paragraphs follows the identical structure:
> "(Domain N). Here, [X] predominates. Because of this [X], [consequence for path]."

Three times in succession, same skeleton. This is Chinese parallel structure faithfully preserved — but in English it reads as a mechanical list, not as a teaching about how different mental habits block the path in different ways.

**Original (composite):**
> **(1)** "Here, the view of the transitory collection (*satkaya-drishti*) predominates. Because of this view, one first generates fear and from the very outset does not wish to set forth toward the noble path."
> **(2)** "Here, attachment to rules and observances (*shila-vrata-paramarsha*) predominates. Because of this attachment, although one has set forth, one goes astray..."
> **(3)** "Here, doubt (*vicikitsa*) predominates. Because of this doubt, although one has set forth and does not go astray, one has not yet become habituated..."

**Suggested rewrite:** Vary the sentence structure across the three items. The first can keep the "because of" construction. The second can lead with the consequence: "Having set forth, the person gripped by attachment to rules and observances nonetheless goes astray..." The third can be made into a contrast: "In the third domain — right teaching and discipline — the barrier is subtler: doubt (*vicikitsa*). Here one has set forth and does not go astray, but has not yet settled into the correct path. Whatever has been seen and known still wavers..."

This preserves all content, removes the mechanical parallelism, and lets the reader feel each obstacle as distinct.

### FINDING 2 — Long/Run-on Sentence
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Line 336

> "A stream-entrant has at most seven further existences remaining. Because of this, many births continue in succession. When the afflictions that produce continued rebirth are severed such that births no longer repeat, the fruit of once-returning is established. That is: when one has permanently abandoned all the afflictions belonging to heavenly existence and human existence that produce continued rebirth, one will at most undergo one more heavenly birth and one more human birth. At that point the fruit of once-returning is established."

Word count of the fourth sentence alone: ~41 words — borderline, but manageable. The larger problem is structural: the paragraph defines once-returning twice ("the fruit of once-returning is established" appears at the end of sentence 3 AND sentence 5). This repetition feels like a Chinese text's internal cross-referencing, preserved accidentally.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "A stream-entrant has at most seven further existences. Over these lives, the afflictions driving continued rebirth gradually thin. When they are finally severed so that rebirth no longer repeats, the fruit of once-returning is established. Specifically: one has abandoned all afflictions belonging to heavenly and human existence that fuel rebirth, leaving at most one more birth in each realm."

### FINDING 3 — Translated-ese: "permanently abandoned" repeated four times in three paragraphs
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Lines 336, 338, 340, 344

> Line 336: "when one has permanently abandoned all the afflictions..."
> Line 338: "When one has permanently abandoned the afflictions that cause one to return..."
> Line 340: "When one has permanently abandoned all afflictions that produce any form of existence..."
> Line 344: "permanently severing the three fetters... permanently severing the five lower fetters... permanently and completely severing all afflictions..."

"Permanently abandoned" / "permanently severing" appears six times across lines 336–344. This is the text's doctrinal vocabulary (永斷), so some repetition is faithful. But six uses in eight lines dulls the prose and flattens what should feel like a rising arc — from stream-entry to arhatship.

**Suggested rewrite:** The final two paragraphs (lines 336–340) can use varied phrasing:
- Line 336: "permanently abandoned" — keep.
- Line 338: "has fully cut the bonds drawing one back to rebirth in this world" — vary.
- Line 340: "has uprooted every affliction that sustains any form of conditioned existence" — vary, and let the weight of arhatship land.

The scriptural summary at line 344 should retain "permanently severing" as it is directly citing the Blessed One's words — that register shift (scriptural citation → summary) is natural and warranted.

### FINDING 4 — Nested Qualification (moderate)
**Severity: L**
**Location:** Line 330

> "Regarding the matters that are truly seen and known, one still wavers and hesitates, obstructing the noble path and preventing it from arising."

The front-loading of "Regarding the matters that are truly seen and known" is a translated-ese construction — it mirrors the Chinese habit of establishing the topic first (至於所見所知之事). In English, this reads as awkward topic-comment syntax.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Even where matters have been truly seen and known, doubt causes wavering and hesitation — and so the noble path cannot arise."

### FINDING 5 — Register drift: closing paragraph reads as legal summary
**Severity: L**
**Location:** Lines 344–345

> "The Blessed One also speaks of permanently severing the three fetters and thinning greed, aversion, and delusion as the basis for the fruit of once-returning; of permanently severing the five lower fetters (*pancha-avarabhagiya-samyojana*) as the basis for the fruit of non-returning; and of permanently and completely severing all afflictions as the basis for the supreme fruit of arhatship. These are called the fruits of cultivation."

This sentence runs ~75 words and is structured as a legal brief — three "of X as the basis for Y" clauses strung together. It conveys information accurately but lands with no weight. "These are called the fruits of cultivation" is a flat close for a section that has just described the full arc from stream-entry to arhatship.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The Blessed One likewise identifies the scriptural grounds for each: permanently severing the three fetters and thinning greed, aversion, and delusion for once-returning; severing the five lower fetters (*pancha-avarabhagiya-samyojana*) for non-returning; and severing all afflictions without remainder for the supreme fruit of arhatship. These four are the fruits of cultivation."

Minor restructuring makes the three-part citation parallel and readable; the close remains terse without feeling deflating.

### FINDING 6 — Minor: "from the very outset" feels redundant
**Severity: L**
**Location:** Line 326

> "one first generates fear and from the very outset does not wish to set forth toward the noble path"

"First generates fear" and "from the very outset does not wish" are nearly redundant. The doubling is a Chinese expressive habit (先…即不欲…).

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "one generates fear at the outset and does not wish to set forth toward the noble path at all"

Or more directly: "fear arises at the outset, and one does not wish to enter the noble path."
