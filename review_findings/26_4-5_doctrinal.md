## Summary

The translation of sections 26.4-26.5 is doctrinally sound in its major features. The four pervasive objects framework, the Revata dialogue, the conduct-type to meditation-object mappings, the four types of reasoning, the concept of transformation of the basis, and the four purifications are all correctly represented. Cross-referencing against Tsongkhapa's presentation of the same Sravakabhumi material (Great Treatise Vol. 3, pp. 491-532) confirms the fourfold object classification, the five conduct-to-antidote matchings, the nine stages of mental abiding, and the four purifications. No claims contradicting established Yogacara/Abhidharma doctrine were found in the body text. Footnotes require targeted revisions for precision.

13 issues found: 2 moderate, 5 minor, 6 positive verifications (no action needed).

## Findings

### FINDING 1: Inconsistent rendering of 慈愍 -- "loving-kindness and compassion" vs "loving-kindness"

**Location:** Translation line 326 vs. line 376
**Category:** (1) Internal inconsistency; (4) Missing context
**Severity:** MODERATE

The Chinese term 慈愍 appears twice in section 26.5:
- Line 326 (list of knowable objects): translated as "loving-kindness and compassion"
- Line 376 (Revata dialogue, matching objects): translated as "loving-kindness"

The Chinese 慈愍 is a single compound term functioning as one category name. It is the purifying-conduct object for aversion-type practitioners. The compound combines 慈 (maitri, loving-kindness) and 愍 (karuna/anukampa, compassion/pity), but in the Yogacarabhumi it functions as a unit designating a single meditation category. This is confirmed by the source text at T30.0428c18 (beginning of the purifying-conduct section) where it lists 慈愍 as one of the five purifying-conduct objects, not two. Within that section, 悲 (karuna/compassion) and 喜 (mudita/sympathetic joy) are introduced as sub-aspects of the 慈愍 practice, not as separate top-level objects.

Rendering it as "loving-kindness and compassion" in line 326 misleadingly suggests two separate items in the list, which would change the count of knowable objects. The Revata dialogue's rendering of "loving-kindness" is closer to the intended sense.

Additionally, footnote [^maitri_karuna] is referenced on line 326 but never defined in the footnotes section. This is a broken footnote reference.

**Recommendation:** (a) Use "loving-kindness" consistently for 慈愍 in both occurrences, matching the glossary entry (慈 = maitri = loving-kindness). If the connotation of 愍 needs to be preserved, use "compassionate loving-kindness" or add a footnote explaining the compound. (b) Either define the missing [^maitri_karuna] footnote or remove the reference.

### FINDING 2: Revata footnote -- claim that four aspects of settling correspond to four pervasive objects is inaccurate

**Location:** Footnote [^revata], line 442
**Category:** (2) Potentially misleading structural interpretation
**Severity:** MODERATE

The footnote states: "The four aspects of 'settling the mind upon the object' -- matching, similar, without inversion, and not abandoning -- correspond structurally to the four pervasive objects defined earlier in this section."

This structural mapping is inaccurate on close analysis:

- "Settling upon a matching object" (於相稱緣安住其心) addresses which specific meditation object to choose based on the practitioner's character type (greed -> impurity, aversion -> loving-kindness, etc.) and confusions (confused about self -> skandhas, etc.). These map to the purifying-conduct objects, skillful objects, and affliction-purifying objects -- the OTHER three categories of the fourfold object classification, NOT the pervasive objects.
- "Settling upon a similar object" (於相似緣安住其心) describes working with the meditative image through alternating shamatha and vipashyana. This does correspond to the image with/without discernment (two of the four pervasive objects).
- "Settling without inversion" (於緣無倒安住其心) corresponds to the limit of things (knowing things as they truly are -- one pervasive object).
- "Not abandoning meditative absorption" (不捨靜慮) corresponds to the accomplishment of the purpose (one pervasive object).

The last three map to three of the four pervasive objects. But the first does NOT map to any pervasive object -- it maps to the three non-pervasive object categories. The Buddha's own framing confirms this: he introduces the answer with 或樂淨行或樂善巧。或樂令心解脫諸漏 ("whether they delight in purifying conduct, or delight in developing skill, or delight in liberating the mind from all outflows"), explicitly referencing the non-pervasive categories. Therefore the claim of a clean four-to-four structural correspondence is misleading.

**Recommendation:** Revise the footnote to: "The four aspects of 'settling the mind upon the object' encompass the entire meditation-object framework, not just the four pervasive objects. 'Settling upon a matching object' addresses which specific meditation object to choose (purifying-conduct, skillful, and affliction-purifying objects), while 'settling upon a similar object' corresponds to the two types of image, 'settling without inversion' corresponds to the limit of things, and 'not abandoning meditative absorption' corresponds to the accomplishment of the purpose."

### FINDING 3: Footnote [^four_purifications] -- "progressive stages of liberation" conflates stages with progressive liberation

**Location:** Footnote [^four_purifications], line 448
**Category:** (5) Footnote inaccuracy; (4) Missing context
**Severity:** MINOR

The footnote states: "These correspond to the progressive stages of liberation." This is vague and could mislead. The Chinese text presents the four purifications (所依清淨, 所緣清淨, 心遍清淨, 智遍清淨) as results of uninterrupted and earnest practice within the framework of not abandoning meditative absorption (不捨靜慮), not as the standard four-stage liberation path (stream-entry through arhatship). A reader unfamiliar with the Yogacara framework could misidentify "progressive stages of liberation" with the four fruits.

**Recommendation:** Replace "These correspond to the progressive stages of liberation" with: "These describe four aspects of the progressive maturation of shamatha-vipashyana practice, corresponding to the accomplishment of the purpose (*krtyanushthana*) described earlier in this section."

### FINDING 4: Footnote [^transformation] -- scope of *ashraya-paravritti* needs contextual qualification

**Location:** Footnote [^transformation], line 440
**Category:** (4) Missing context that would mislead a reader
**Severity:** MINOR

The footnote describes *ashraya-paravritti* as "a central Yogacara concept" referring to "the fundamental transformation in the practitioner's cognitive and experiential basis." This is accurate but does not distinguish the Sravakabhumi's narrower usage from the later, more expansive Yogacara usage. In the Sravakabhumi, the transformation of the basis specifically refers to the shift from the desire-realm mind to the dhyana-level mind through elimination of *dausthulya* (heaviness), resulting in the attainment of pliancy and access to dhyana. In later Yogacara texts (Mahayanasamgraha, Cheng Weishi Lun), *ashraya-paravritti* refers to the complete revolution of consciousness at buddhahood, including the transformation of the alaya-vijnana.

Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise* Vol. 3 (p. 492) confirms the narrower reading when discussing this Sravakabhumi passage: "you become free from your dysfunctional tendencies, undergoing a fundamental transformation."

**Recommendation:** Add a qualifying sentence to the footnote: "In this Sravakabhumi context, the 'transformation of the basis' refers specifically to the cognitive shift that occurs when heaviness (*dausthulya*) is eliminated and the practitioner attains access to dhyana, not to the ultimate transformation described in later Yogacara soteriology (e.g., the complete revolution of the *alaya-vijnana* at buddhahood)."

### FINDING 5: Nine stages of mental abiding -- ninth stage 等持 rendered "even settling"

**Location:** Translation line 334; footnote [^nine_stages], line 432
**Category:** (3) Minor terminology concern
**Severity:** MINOR

The ninth stage 等持 is rendered "even settling." The glossary entry for 等持 is "meditative attainment" (samapatti). Tsongkhapa (*Great Treatise* Vol. 3, p. 532) renders this as "settling in equipoise" or "balanced placement." The translation's "even settling" could be read as "settling evenly" (adjective-verb), obscuring the technical sense of equipoise/samadhi.

The Chinese full phrase is 一趣等持 (nine stages 8 and 9 combined in the source text but presented as two stages). The translation's list in the footnote: "(8) one-pointed, (9) even settling" correctly separates these. The issue is only with the rendering of 等持 itself.

**Recommendation:** Consider "settling in equipoise" for the ninth stage in both body text (line 334) and footnote (line 432) to match the glossary entry and Tsongkhapa's tradition.

### FINDING 6: Footnote [^four_purifications] item (2) -- "things as they are" is overstated

**Location:** Footnote [^four_purifications], line 448
**Category:** (5) Footnote imprecision
**Severity:** MINOR

The footnote identifies item (2) purification of the object as "direct, non-conceptual seeing of things as they are." The Chinese more precisely says 於所知事由現見故 (through direct seeing/現見 of the knowable objects). This refers to *pratyaksha* perception of the meditation object itself -- the meditator now sees the actual object directly rather than through the meditative image. The phrase "things as they are" carries an overtone of ultimate realization (seeing reality as it is, in a Madhyamaka sense) that exceeds what the text describes at this point. The purification of the object is about the quality of perception (direct vs. image-mediated), not about realization of emptiness.

**Recommendation:** Revise from "direct, non-conceptual seeing of things as they are" to "direct perception (*pratyaksha*) of the meditation objects themselves, transcending the meditative image."

### FINDING 7: Image synonyms -- 三摩地口 ("entrance of samadhi") loses distinction from 三摩地門 ("gate of samadhi")

**Location:** Translation line 338; footnote [^image_synonyms], line 434
**Category:** (4) Missing context (minor)
**Severity:** MINOR

The Chinese lists eight synonyms for the meditation image. Two of them are:
- 三摩地口 (samadhi-mukha, lit. "mouth/opening of samadhi")
- 三摩地門 (samadhi-dvara, lit. "gate/door of samadhi")

The translation renders these as "entrance of samadhi" and "gate of samadhi." Both 口 (mukha) and 門 (dvara) can carry the sense of "entrance," but the Chinese deliberately uses two distinct characters. The Sanskrit *mukha* (mouth/face/opening) has a different semantic range from *dvara* (gate/door). Tsongkhapa (*Great Treatise* Vol. 3) lists these as distinct synonyms. "Opening of samadhi" for 口 would preserve the distinction.

**Recommendation:** Render 三摩地口 as "opening of samadhi" rather than "entrance of samadhi" to maintain the distinction from 三摩地門 ("gate of samadhi"). Update footnote [^image_synonyms] accordingly.

### FINDING 8: Conduct-type to meditation-object mapping -- doctrinally correct per Yogacarabhumi

**Location:** Translation line 376; footnote [^matching_objects], line 444
**Category:** Verification (no error found)
**Severity:** None (positive finding)

The five-fold mapping is:
1. Greed (貪行) --> Impurity (不淨)
2. Aversion (瞋行) --> Loving-kindness (慈愍)
3. Delusion (癡行) --> Dependent origination (緣性緣起)
4. Pride (慢行) --> Element differentiation (界差別)
5. Discursive thought (尋思行) --> Mindfulness of breathing (阿那波那念)

Cross-referenced against:
- **Tsongkhapa** (*Great Treatise* Vol. 3, p. 496): Exact match.
- **Buddhaghosa** (*Visuddhimagga* III.74-121): Uses a different six-fold temperament system and forty-object system. Buddhaghosa maps element-meditation (*dhatuvavatthana*) to the "intelligent temperament," not pride. This is a genuine Theravada-vs-Yogacara difference, not an error.
- **Chinese source**: Exact match.

**Status:** Correct. No issues.

### FINDING 10: "The limit of things" -- twofold distinction is doctrinally precise

**Location:** Translation lines 342-348; footnote [^limit_twofold], line 436
**Category:** Verification (no error found)
**Severity:** None (positive finding)

The twofold distinction between 盡所有性 (yavad-bhavikata) and 如所有性 (yathavad-bhavikata) is correctly presented. Tsongkhapa (*Great Treatise* Vol. 3, p. 491) confirms the same twofold division. The footnote's characterization of "quantitative completeness" vs. "qualitative accuracy" is a slightly loose modern gloss, but the actual content is correct. **Status:** Essentially correct. Minor simplification in the gloss, not a doctrinal error.

### FINDING 13: "Practitioner of insight" for 修觀行者 in verse commentary

**Location:** Translation line 409
**Category:** (4) Missing context (minor)
**Severity:** MINOR (informational note)

The verse commentary at line 409 says "the practitioner of insight cultivating the signs of calming, uplifting, and equanimity." The Chinese 修觀行者 literally means "one who cultivates contemplative practice" (修 = cultivate, 觀行 = contemplative practice, 者 = one who). While 觀 can mean "insight" (vipashyana), the compound 觀行 in the Yogacarabhumi refers to the broader contemplative practice that includes both shamatha and vipashyana. The very fact that this practitioner cultivates 止相舉捨相 (signs of calming, uplifting, equanimity) shows they are practicing shamatha and vipashyana together. The rendering "practitioner of insight" could misleadingly suggest they practice only vipashyana.

This is used consistently throughout the section (lines 324, 330, 334, 352), so it functions as a term of art. The reader can infer the broader meaning from context. However, "practitioner of contemplative meditation" or simply "yogin" would be more precise.

**Recommendation:** Consider rendering 修觀行者 as "practitioner of contemplation" or "contemplative practitioner" rather than "practitioner of insight" to avoid implying vipashyana exclusivity. Alternatively, add a brief footnote on first occurrence clarifying that 觀行 encompasses both shamatha and vipashyana practice.
