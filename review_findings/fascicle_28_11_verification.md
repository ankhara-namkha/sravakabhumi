## ISSUES FOUND

### A. Minor Translation Issues (not omissions)

**A1. 中庸身 rendered as "ordinary body" (line 513)**
- Chinese: 中庸身
- The character 中庸 means "middle/average/mediocre" rather than strictly "ordinary." However, in the context of a classification alongside 劣 (inferior), this rendering is defensible. Alternative: "middling body" or "average body" -- but this would conflict with 中身 (#31) which is also "middling." The translation appropriately distinguishes the two by rendering 中庸 as "ordinary" and 中 as "middling." No error per se.

**A2. "Feeling without relish" (line 529) -- enumeration structure**
- Chinese: 無愛味受。依耽嗜受。當知亦爾。
- The Chinese actually lists 無愛味受 (without relish) and then separately 依耽嗜受 (based on indulgence) with 當知亦爾 (should be understood likewise).
- The English renders this as: "Feeling without relish likewise. Feeling based on indulgence likewise."
- This is correct, but the Chinese structure is slightly different. The 當知亦爾 ("should be understood likewise") grammatically applies to 依耽嗜受 specifically, meaning: "Feeling based on indulgence -- understand it likewise [i.e., it also comes in three kinds: pleasant, painful, neither]." The 無愛味受 stands alone in the Chinese more ambiguously. However, the overall count of 21 requires both to be understood as threefold, so the translation is functionally correct.

**A3. 相雜念住 rendered as "associated establishment of mindfulness" (line 609)**
- This is correct but could note the Sanskrit *samprayukta-smrityupasthana* for scholarly completeness, matching the treatment of the other two types (所緣/object-based, 自性/intrinsic-nature).

### B. Potential Improvements (not errors)

**B1. The word 言 in "不寂靜心者。謂從諸蓋未得解脫言" (line 543-544)**
- The Chinese has 言 (literally "word/say") at the end of the 不寂靜心 definition, just before 定心. This is likely a textual particle or a connecting word ("that is to say") rather than meaningful content. The translation omits it without consequence. Not an error.

**B2. Structural formatting in the English**
- The English effectively organizes the long continuous Chinese passage into clear subsections with bold headers (the thirty-five aspects, dwelling in contemplation, mindfulness, feelings, mind, dharmas, six frameworks, four inversions, etc.) and horizontal rules. This is an addition over the Chinese structure, which presents everything as continuous prose. This is a legitimate formatting choice that improves readability without altering content.
