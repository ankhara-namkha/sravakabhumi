## Findings

### HIGH

**H1. Three conditions vs. four conditions for consciousness (Section 27.4, Skill in the Sense Bases, line 324-326)**

The translation correctly states that the Sravakabhumi lists three conditions (dominant, object, immediately preceding) for consciousness to arise. The Chinese source explicitly confirms: 皆由三緣而得流轉。謂增上緣。所緣緣。等無間緣. However, the standard Abhidharma (Abhidharmakosha, Mipham's Gateway 5.33) lists **four** conditions (*catvari pratyaya*): hetu-pratyaya (causal condition), adhipati-pratyaya (dominant condition), alambana-pratyaya (object condition), and samanantara-pratyaya (immediately preceding condition). The omission of hetu-pratyaya is a deliberate feature of the Sravakabhumi's presentation in this context.

**Issue:** The footnote [^three_conditions] (line 530) explains the three conditions well but does not flag or explain the discrepancy with the standard four-pratyaya framework. A reader familiar with Abhidharma will wonder why hetu-pratyaya is missing. The footnote should note that the Sravakabhumi is presenting a simplified framework here (focusing on the three conditions most directly relevant to sense-base analysis), whereas the full four-pratyaya framework appears elsewhere in Yogacara literature. Without this note, a doctrinally informed reader may suspect a translation error.

**Severity: HIGH** -- Not a translation error but a significant missing context that could mislead readers with Abhidharma background.

### MEDIUM

**M1. Suffering of change definition includes afflictions -- potentially confusing (Section 27.5, line 408-412)**

The translation of the suffering of change (*viparinama-duhkhata*) faithfully renders the Chinese but the result is a definition broader than what most readers will expect. The standard understanding (per Tsongkhapa, Great Treatise Vol. 1, ch. 19 [227]) is that suffering of change is primarily about pleasant feelings and their impermanent nature. The Sravakabhumi's definition here includes not only pleasant feeling and its physical basis, but also "the afflictions that arise from painful feeling and its physical basis, and the afflictions that arise from health and other occasions for pleasant feeling" -- essentially folding afflictions themselves into the suffering of change category.

The translation is accurate to the source. However, the footnote [^three_sufferings] (line 544) presents the standard three-part classification without flagging that the Sravakabhumi's definition of suffering of change is significantly more expansive than the standard presentation (which typically restricts suffering of change to pleasant feeling and its basis). The Visuddhimagga (XVI.35) similarly includes pleasant feelings as suffering of change "because they are a cause for the arising of pain when they change," but does not include the afflictions in the way the Sravakabhumi does. This distinctive feature deserves a note.

**Severity: MEDIUM** -- The translation is correct, but the footnote should flag the Sravakabhumi's distinctive broader definition.

**M2. "Dark and bright categories" terminology (Section 27.5, line 382)**

The translation uses "dark and bright categories" (*krishna-paksha* and *shukla-paksha*) for the framework of the four truths: suffering as fruit of the dark category, origin as cause of the dark, cessation as fruit of the bright, path as cause of the bright. This is doctrinally accurate and matches the Chinese 黑品白品果因 framework. However, the term "dark category" / "bright category" may be unfamiliar to readers accustomed to the Pali tradition's "dark kamma / bright kamma" language (e.g., AN 4.232-236, in Bhikkhu Bodhi's translations). No footnote explains this terminology.

**Severity: MEDIUM** -- Missing context. A brief footnote clarifying *krishna-paksha* and *shukla-paksha* as standard Abhidharma classifications (dark = samsaric, bright = nirvanic) would help readers.

**M3. Mind consciousness dominant condition: attention vs. mind organ (Section 27.4, line 324)**

The translation states: "mind serves as the immediately preceding condition; the attention (*manaskara*) arising from this serves as the dominant condition; dharmas serve as the object condition. From these arises mind consciousness and its associated mental factors."

This rendering follows the Chinese closely (意為等無間緣。此生作意為增上緣。法為所緣緣。生起意識及相應法). The doctrinal point is that for mind consciousness, unlike the five sense consciousnesses where the physical organ is the dominant condition, *attention* (manaskara) serves as the dominant condition. The footnote [^three_conditions] correctly notes this: "For mind consciousness, the text specifies that attention (*manaskara*) serves as the dominant condition, since there is no separate physical organ for mental cognition."

However, this is a distinctively Yogacara position. The Sarvastivada Abhidharmakosha (II) generally treats the mind faculty (*manas*) as the dominant condition for mind consciousness, parallel to the five physical faculties. The Sravakabhumi's substitution of *manaskara* (attention) as the dominant condition is noteworthy and the footnote could benefit from flagging this as a specifically Yogacara interpretation.

**Severity: MEDIUM** -- Correct translation, but the footnote could note the distinctiveness of this Yogacara position more explicitly.

**M4. Twelve links enumeration completeness (Section 27.4, line 330)**

The translation lists all twelve links of dependent origination correctly: ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, six sense bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, aging-and-death. This matches the Chinese source and the standard twelve-link formula (confirmed in Mipham, Gateway 4.6; Visuddhimagga XVII). No items are missing or added.

However, the dependent origination section in 27.4 (skill in dependent origination) is presented with a different emphasis than the fuller treatment in 27.1 (purifying-conduct object for delusion). In 27.4, the text immediately moves to the three wisdoms (impermanence, suffering, non-self) without discussing the two-lifetime or three-lifetime models, the grouping into propelling/establishing links, or the affliction/karma/suffering trichotomy that Mipham presents (Gateway 4.29-4.38). This is not an error -- the Sravakabhumi is treating dependent origination here as a *skillful object* (analytical understanding) rather than as a purifying-conduct object (contemplation for delusion types) -- but a note distinguishing these two treatments would help readers.

**Severity: MEDIUM** -- Not an error, but the two appearances of dependent origination (27.1 and 27.4) serve different functions, and this is not flagged.

### LOW

**L1. Five skillful objects enumeration (Section 27.4, line 282-288)**

The five skillful objects are correctly listed: (1) skandhas, (2) elements, (3) sense bases, (4) dependent origination, (5) possible and impossible. This matches the Chinese source and standard Abhidharma. Mipham's *Gateway to Knowledge* Vol. 1 treats these same five categories across its chapters on aggregates, elements, sources, dependent origination, and correct/incorrect (chapters 1-5). The enumeration is correct and complete.

**Severity: LOW** -- Confirmed correct. No issue.

**L2. Eighteen dhatus enumeration (Section 27.4, line 316)**

The eighteen elements are listed as six triads: eye/form/eye-consciousness, ear/sound/ear-consciousness, nose/smell/nose-consciousness, tongue/taste/tongue-consciousness, body/tangible/body-consciousness, mind/dharma/mind-consciousness. This is the standard Abhidharma enumeration (6 faculties + 6 objects + 6 consciousnesses = 18). Confirmed against Chinese source and Mipham Gateway [2,1-2,39]. Count is correct.

**Severity: LOW** -- Confirmed correct.

**L3. Twelve ayatanas enumeration (Section 27.4, line 322)**

The twelve sense bases are listed as six pairs: eye/form, ear/sound, nose/smell, tongue/taste, body/tangible, mind/dharma. This matches the standard enumeration. Confirmed against Chinese and Mipham Gateway [3,1-3,2]. Count is correct.

**Severity: LOW** -- Confirmed correct.

**L4. Seven specific sufferings plus summary (Section 27.5, line 374)**

The noble truth of suffering lists seven specific sufferings (birth, aging, illness, death, association with the hateful, separation from the loved, not obtaining what one seeks) plus the summary "the five appropriated skandhas are suffering." This matches the standard Pali/Sanskrit formula (DN 22, SN 56.11, Visuddhimagga XVI.32ff). Note: the Visuddhimagga comments (XVI.32 n.7) that illness is sometimes omitted from the list because not all beings experience illness, but the Sravakabhumi includes it. The translation correctly includes illness, matching the Chinese source (生苦老苦病苦死苦).

**Severity: LOW** -- Correct. The inclusion of illness matches the Chinese source.

### INFO/NOTES

**I1. Worldly vs. supramundane path distinction (Section 27.5)**

The translation correctly and clearly distinguishes the worldly path (*samvriti-marga*, contemplation of coarseness and peacefulness, temporary suppression) from the supramundane path (*lokottara-marga*, four noble truths, permanent elimination). The footnote [^worldly_path] correctly states this achieves "only temporary suppression" and that "the underlying tendencies remain and can re-emerge." The text at line 368 states "This is a temporary abandonment, not an ultimate abandonment, because at a later time the afflictions resume their continuity." This is doctrinally precise and matches the standard Yogacara and Sarvastivada understanding. The ladder from desire realm through nothingness is correctly presented.

**Status: CORRECT** -- No issue. This is a core doctrinal distinction and it is handled well.

**I2. Three types of craving (Section 27.5, line 376, 418-420)**

The three types of craving for the truth of the origin are correctly identified: (1) craving for continued existence (*punarbhava-trishna*), (2) craving accompanied by delight and passion (*nandi-raga-sahagata-trishna*), (3) craving for this and that pleasure (*tatra-tatrabhiratika-trishna*). The expanded explanation at line 418-420 correctly maps these to: yearning for continued existence, attachment to already-obtained objects, and desire for not-yet-obtained objects. This matches the Chinese source and the standard Pali/Sanskrit threefold analysis (DN 22, SN 56.11, Visuddhimagga XVI.61). The footnote [^three_craving_types] correctly identifies the cross-tradition parallels.

**Status: CORRECT** -- No issue.

**I3. Three supernormal displays (Section 27.6, line 448-456)**

The three supernormal displays are correctly identified: (1) magical transformation (*riddhi-pratiharya*), (2) mind-reading declaration (*adesana-pratiharya*), (3) instruction (*anushasani-pratiharya*). This matches DN 11 (Kevaddha Sutta) and the Chinese source (一神境神變。二記說神變。三教誡神變). The footnote [^three_supernormal_displays] correctly notes the Buddha's preference for the miracle of instruction over the other two, referencing DN 11. The functional description -- magical display establishes authority, mind-reading provides diagnosis, instruction provides liberation -- is doctrinally sound.

**Status: CORRECT** -- No issue.

**I4. Two types of cessation and two types of path (Section 27.5, line 424)**

The text states: "Cessation is of two types: (1) cessation of afflictions, and (2) cessation of the physical basis. The path is of two types: (1) the path of the learner, and (2) the path of the non-learner."

This is a standard Abhidharma classification. Cessation of afflictions (*klesha-nirodha*) refers to the elimination of afflictions while still alive (sopadhishesha-nirvana); cessation of the physical basis (*ashrayi-nirodha*) refers to the final cessation at death (nirupadhishesha-nirvana). The learner's path (*shaiksha-marga*) includes stream-enterers through non-returners; the non-learner's path (*ashaiksha-marga*) is the arhat's path. Both are standard and correctly presented.

**Status: CORRECT** -- No issue.

**I5. Own-characteristics vs. common characteristics summary (Section 27.4, line 342)**

The reduction of five skills to two (skill in own-characteristics and skill in common characteristics) is correctly translated. Skandha skill reveals own-characteristics (each skandha has its distinct nature); the remaining four skills reveal common characteristics (impermanence, suffering, non-self, causal structure shared across phenomena). This is standard Abhidharma epistemology and matches the Chinese source. The footnote [^sva_samanya] correctly explains the distinction.

**Status: CORRECT** -- No issue.

## Overall Assessment

Sections 27.4, 27.5, and 27.6 are doctrinally sound. All major Abhidharma categories are correctly represented: the five skandhas, eighteen dhatus, twelve ayatanas, twelve links of dependent origination, four noble truths, three types of suffering, worldly vs. supramundane path, three types of craving, and three supernormal displays. Item counts are correct throughout. No claims contradict established doctrine. No anachronistic interpretations were found.

The one HIGH-severity finding concerns missing context in a footnote (the three vs. four pratyaya issue), not a translation error. The four MEDIUM findings likewise concern places where doctrinally distinctive features of the Sravakabhumi's presentation would benefit from more explicit flagging in the footnotes, particularly for readers coming from other Abhidharma traditions.

The translation demonstrates careful handling of the relationship between the Sravakabhumi's specific presentations and the broader Abhidharma tradition. The doctrinal framework of the four truths section (suffering as cause rather than essence, the three types of suffering, the dark/bright category framework) is accurately presented and well-structured.
