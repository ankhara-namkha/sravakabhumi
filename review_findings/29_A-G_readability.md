# Readability Review: Fascicle 29, Sections 29.1–29.7

**Agent R (Readability)**
**Scope:** `translation/fascicle_29_en.md`, sections 29.1 through 29.7 (up to but not including 29.8)
**Method:** Practitioner-reader (80%) + scholar-reader (20%). No Chinese source consulted. No glossary consulted.
**Standard:** Nanamoli's Visuddhimagga, Bodhi's anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee.

**Overall register assessment:** Sections 29.1–29.4 show clear signs of assembled-from-Chinese prose: heavy reliance on parallel formula repetition, subjects buried under qualification, sentences that enumerate rather than illuminate. Sections 29.5–29.7 are shorter and significantly better — tighter, more direct. The footnotes throughout are excellent: scholarly, informative, appropriately pitched. The core readability problem is in 29.1 especially, where the same structural formula ("for the purpose of... one...") repeats so many times that the cumulative effect is numbing. A practitioner trying to actually follow the instruction would get lost in the machinery. Several passages in 29.3 and 29.4 come very close to NTC standard. Agent R recommends focused revision of 29.1 and targeted fixes in 29.2–29.4.

## Flags

### R-01
**Category:** Formula Monotony (FM)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.1, "Aspiration regarding wholesome dharmas" paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> Generating aspiration for giving rise to unarisen wholesome dharmas means: regarding all wholesome dharmas not yet obtained and not yet manifest, wishing to obtain them and make them manifest, one generates the aspiration, arouses keen intensity, and with the desire to obtain and the desire to make them present, brings them into being. This is called generating aspiration for giving rise to unarisen wholesome dharmas.
>
> Generating aspiration for causing already-arisen wholesome dharmas to abide, not to be forgotten, and to be cultivated to perfection means: all wholesome dharmas already obtained and already manifest are called "already-arisen wholesome dharmas." Regarding these wholesome dharmas:

**What's wrong:** The paragraph opens with "Generating aspiration for X means..." and then closes with "This is called generating aspiration for X" — an exact circular restatement. Worse, this formula has already appeared twice in the unwholesome section. By this third use in succession, it reads like a legal template, not instruction. The practitioner's eyes slide over the content looking for what's new.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> To arouse aspiration for wholesome dharmas not yet present: one turns toward all wholesome qualities not yet obtained, not yet manifest, and generates the wish to bring them forth — intensifying that wish into a desire to obtain them, a desire to make them present, and finally the active effort that brings them into being.
>
> As for wholesome dharmas already arisen and already manifest — one generates aspiration for three things: that what has been obtained not be lost or diminished (this is "causing to abide"); that it remain vividly present without dullness (this is "not forgetting"); and that it be practiced through to full perfection (this is "cultivated to completion"). One generates this aspiration, arouses keen intensity, and acts.

### R-02
**Category:** Formula Monotony (FM)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.1, "Self-urging and vigorous effort for wholesome dharmas" passage

**Quoted passage:**
> Self-urging is for causing what has already been obtained to become manifest. Vigorous effort is for causing what has not yet been obtained to be obtained.
>
> Furthermore, self-urging is for causing already-arisen wholesome dharmas to abide and not be forgotten. Vigorous effort is for causing them to be cultivated to perfection.
>
> Furthermore, for lower and middle-grade wholesome dharmas — generating those not yet arisen, and causing those already arisen to abide and not be forgotten — this is called self-urging. For upper-grade wholesome dharmas — generating those not yet arisen, and causing those already arisen to be cultivated to perfection — this is called vigorous effort.

**What's wrong:** Three consecutive "Furthermore" paragraphs giving three parallel distinctions between self-urging and vigorous effort. Each uses the same grammatical skeleton. By the third iteration, a careful reader cannot absorb the distinctions — they blur into a single undifferentiated block. The distinctions themselves matter doctrinally; the repetitive framing buries them.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Self-urging and vigorous effort differ depending on what stage and grade of development one is working with. At the most basic level: self-urging brings forth what has already been obtained but lies latent; vigorous effort secures what has not yet been obtained. Going further: self-urging sustains already-arisen wholesome dharmas so they abide and are not forgotten; vigorous effort drives them through to full cultivation and perfection. Finally, considered by grade: self-urging applies to lower and middle-grade wholesome dharmas — generating those not yet arisen and maintaining those already present — while vigorous effort applies to upper-grade dharmas, generating the unarisen and carrying the arisen through to complete perfection.

### R-03
**Category:** Nested Qualification (NQ)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.1, "The four right strivings" paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> Regarding dharmas of the dark category — for unarisen ones, to prevent their arising; for already-arisen ones, to cause their destruction — one generates aspiration, applies self-urging and vigorous effort, and uplifts and holds the mind. These are two right strivings. Regarding dharmas of the white category — for unarisen ones, to cause their arising, and so on as broadly explained for the dark category — one should know these are also two right strivings.

**What's wrong:** The main clause ("one generates aspiration, applies self-urging and vigorous effort") is bracketed on both sides by em-dash qualifications, then followed by a second sentence that cross-references backwards ("and so on as broadly explained") instead of stating what is meant. A practitioner hits "— for unarisen ones... for already-arisen ones... —" and then has to find the verb, then the second sentence sends them back to re-read. The "and so on as broadly explained" construction is especially weak: it signals to the reader that the writer did not bother to complete the thought.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> For dharmas of the dark category — unwholesome states — one generates aspiration, applies self-urging and vigorous effort, and uplifts and holds the mind: preventing those not yet arisen from arising, and destroying those already arisen. These two tasks constitute two right strivings. The same pattern applies to dharmas of the white category — wholesome states — where one generates the unarisen and sustains the arisen. These are the remaining two right strivings.

### R-04
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.1, "Self-urging and vigorous effort by cause of arising" — long paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> Furthermore, some evil and unwholesome dharmas arise solely through the power of conceptual discrimination, not through the power of the object. Others arise through both the power of conceptual discrimination and the power of the object. Those arising solely through the power of conceptual discrimination, not through the power of the object, occur during the seated state — when one thinks about past or future objects and the unwholesome dharmas arise from that thinking. Those arising through both the power of conceptual thinking and the power of the object occur during the active state — when one relates to present objects and the unwholesome dharmas arise from that encounter. At such times, there is certainly also improper mental attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*).

**What's wrong:** Classic Chinese prose structure: define a category, re-state it in full when defining its instances, then re-state it again when explaining. The phrase "solely through the power of conceptual discrimination, not through the power of the object" appears twice in adjacent sentences. "Those arising through both the power of conceptual thinking and the power of the object" is a near-repeat of "Both the power of conceptual discrimination and the power of the object" with the word "discrimination" swapped for "thinking" — an inconsistency that introduces confusion while also being redundant. The final sentence about improper mental attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*) arrives abruptly with no connector.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Unwholesome dharmas also differ in how they arise. Some arise purely through conceptual discrimination — not triggered by an object directly before one, but by thinking about past or future objects while in a seated, relatively inactive state. Others arise through the combined force of conceptual discrimination and direct sensory encounter, occurring when one is active and relating to a present object. In the latter case, improper mental attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*) is invariably also present.

### R-07
**Category:** >50-word sentences (LG)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.1, "Summary meaning of the four right efforts" — second paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> The practitioner of yoga has precisely these as their proper tasks: to sever what should be severed, and to obtain what should be obtained. First, one should generate the aspiration and desire to do so. To sever entanglements, one should from time to time diligently cultivate the aspects of calming, uplifting, and equanimity. To sever both entanglements and latent tendencies, one should further cultivate and accumulate antidote wholesome dharmas. To reveal all of these tasks, the four right strivings and four right abandonments are taught. This is their summary meaning.

**Word count:** Individual sentences are within range (longest is ~25 words). But the paragraph is constructed as five short mechanical sentences rather than flowing prose. The result feels like a list incorrectly formatted as prose — every sentence is a separate declarative statement with no connective tissue, no subordination, no movement. This is a register issue as much as a length issue.

*Reclassify:*
**Category:** Register Drift (RD)
**Severity:** Moderate

**Suggested rewrite:**
> The practitioner's essential tasks are two: to sever what must be severed and to obtain what must be obtained. Both begin with aspiration. To sever entanglements, one periodically cultivates calming, uplifting, and equanimity. To cut both entanglements and their latent roots, one further develops and accumulates the antidote wholesome dharmas. The four right strivings and four right abandonments are taught precisely to illuminate these tasks in their full scope and sequence.

### R-08
**Category:** Nested Qualification (NQ)
**Severity:** High
**Location:** 29.2, "Mind-samadhi" passage

**Quoted passage:**
> If one further uplifts dull and sunken minds, or restrains and holds agitated minds, and from time to time cultivates superior equanimity — by these means, regarding all evil and unwholesome dharmas, what is conducive to evil and unwholesome dharmas, all wholesome dharmas, and what is conducive to all wholesome dharmas — their nature, causes, faults, virtues, antidotes, and emergence — one correctly examines and investigates, abiding in one-pointed mindfulness.

**Word count:** 80 words. Main verb ("one correctly examines and investigates") is buried under a three-level stack: (1) conditional "if one... or... and..." (2) enumeration "regarding... and... and..." (3) second enumeration "their nature, causes, faults, virtues, antidotes, and emergence."

**What's wrong:** The sentence is grammatically traceable but experientially impenetrable. A practitioner trying to follow this as instruction cannot parse the nesting while simultaneously absorbing the content. This is the most egregious long-sentence example in the section.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> When the mind becomes dull or sunken, one uplifts it; when agitated, one restrains and steadies it; from time to time one cultivates equanimity. Throughout, one correctly examines and investigates — regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas and what gives rise to them, and regarding all wholesome dharmas and what gives rise to them — their nature, causes, faults, virtues, antidotes, and emergence. One abides in one-pointed mindfulness.

### R-09
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.2, "Investigation-samadhi" — self-examination passage

**Quoted passage:**
> "At present, do I have manifesting evil and unwholesome dharmas that I am unaware of? Or have evil and unwholesome dharmas ceased to manifest, and I am unaware of that? I should now thoroughly examine and investigate everywhere."

**What's wrong:** The first question is awkward: "do I have manifesting evil and unwholesome dharmas that I am unaware of?" — the phrase "manifesting evil and unwholesome dharmas" is an English noun phrase that sounds assembled. A native English speaker would say "are there unwholesome states arising in me that I haven't noticed?" or similar. The third sentence "I should now thoroughly examine and investigate everywhere" — "everywhere" is a calque rendering of a Chinese word meaning "thoroughly/completely," but in English "examine everywhere" sounds spatial rather than thoroughgoing.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Are there unwholesome dharmas actively manifesting in me right now that I have not yet noticed? Or have they ceased, and am I unaware of that cessation? I should examine carefully and completely."

### R-11
**Category:** Formula Monotony (FM)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.3, definitions of the eight abandonment factors — items (5) and (6)

**Quoted passage:**
> **(5) Mindfulness** (*smriti*): the nine aspects — because through the nine aspects one settles the mind and is able to hold it in the category of shamatha.

> **(6) Alertness** (*samprajanya*): the wisdom belonging to the category of vipashyana.

### R-13
**Category:** Nested Qualification (NQ)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.4, "Result: capacity for supramundane attainment" paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> Thus, for one who possesses these dharmas and has brought samadhi to complete perfection, the mind becomes pure, bright-white, free from all blemish and stain, separated from secondary afflictions, abiding in rectitude, possessed of capability, stable and unshakable, able to go and return, leaping and striding with vigor — able to obtain and realize supramundane dharmas. Because supramundane dharmas are the most supreme and sovereign — this is the most supreme supernatural power; because these samadhis can realize it, they are called "bases of supernatural power."

**Word count:** First sentence: 71 words.

**What's wrong:** The sentence piles eight consecutive descriptors onto "the mind becomes" — pure, bright-white, free, separated, abiding, possessed, stable, able — before arriving at the main point. The eight descriptors are a quasi-poetic list from the Chinese; in English they read as a catalog rather than an evocation. The final clause "because supramundane dharmas are the most supreme and sovereign — this is the most supreme supernatural power; because these samadhis can realize it, they are called..." has a syllogistic structure (A→B because B is C) that is traceable but labored.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Thus, when one who has brought samadhi to complete perfection possesses these dharmas, the mind becomes pure and bright, free from blemish and stain, separated from secondary afflictions — abiding in rectitude, capable, stable, unshakable, able to go and return with vigor. Such a mind can obtain and realize supramundane dharmas. Because supramundane dharmas are the most supreme — and because these samadhis can realize them — these samadhis are called "bases of supernatural power."

### R-14
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.5, "Five Faculties" — dominance hierarchy

**Quoted passage:**
> Faith is dominant as the foremost condition for giving rise to supramundane dharmas, and is also dominant with respect to generating effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom. The remaining faculties — effort and so forth — are each dominant with respect to giving rise to supramundane dharmas, and each is also dominant with respect to generating the successive faculties up through wisdom. Wisdom, finally, is dominant solely with respect to giving rise to supramundane dharmas.

**What's wrong:** "Dominant with respect to giving rise to" and "dominant with respect to generating" — both are legal-document phrasing. The word "dominant" repeated three times in adjacent sentences, always with the preposition-heavy "with respect to X." The structure is clear but the prose is inert. More subtly: "The remaining faculties — effort and so forth" is weak — a reader wants to know which faculties, not "and so forth."

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Faith exercises dominance in two directions: as the foremost condition for the arising of supramundane dharmas, and as the condition that generates effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom in sequence. Each subsequent faculty — effort, mindfulness, samadhi — likewise dominates with respect to giving rise to supramundane dharmas, and each generates the faculties that follow it in the sequence. Wisdom alone has a single scope: it is dominant solely over the arising of supramundane dharmas.

### R-15
**Category:** Broken Logic (BL)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.6, "Five Powers" — the "indomitable" definition

**Quoted passage:**
> This pure faith — whether by gods, by maras, by shramanas, by brahmanas, or by any other beings in the world — none can properly lead one astray from it, and afflictive entanglements also cannot bend it. Therefore it is called indomitable. With this as the foremost and as the precursor, the remaining faculties — effort and so forth — are also called powers.

**What's wrong:** The logical move is: (1) faith cannot be overcome by gods/maras/etc., therefore it is indomitable, therefore (2) faith is a "power." But the sentence transitions immediately from defining faith as indomitable to saying "the remaining faculties — effort and so forth — are also called powers." This creates an implicit claim that effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom are *also* indomitable in the same way — yet the text has only defined indomitability for faith. A practitioner might reasonably ask: in what sense is, say, mindfulness indomitable? The text does not say. The phrase "with this as the foremost and as the precursor" is the only connective, and it is too thin to carry the inference.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> This pure faith is indomitable — no god, mara, shramana, brahmin, or any being in the world can properly lead one astray from it, and afflictive entanglements cannot bend it. Because faith functions as foremost and precursor for the remaining faculties, the same quality of being unvanquishable extends to effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom as well. All five are therefore called powers.

### R-16
**Category:** Register Drift (RD)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.7, "Faith faculty and faith power = the four verified purities" paragraph

**Quoted passage:**
> The faith faculty and faith power correspond to the pure faith within the four purities of verified confidence (*avetya-prasada*). Why? Because all the verified purities attained by those who have entered the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*) arise through this as cause, condition, and antecedent basis. Those verified purities are the dominant result (*adhipati-phala*) of this faith. Therefore the Blessed One, in accordance with the principle of causal relationship, states that one should regard them as being those verified purities — not that they are identical in substance, not that they are identical in characteristic.

**What's wrong:** The final sentence is particularly knotted: "states that one should regard them as being those verified purities — not that they are identical in substance, not that they are identical in characteristic." The double negation clarifying identity is philosophically necessary but reads as a legal disclaimer rather than illuminating prose. The overall paragraph stays at the level of technical correlation without ever giving the practitioner a sense of what verified confidence *feels* like or why this correlation matters for practice.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> The faith faculty and faith power correspond to the pure faith within the four verified purities (*avetya-prasada*). Why? Because every verified purity attained upon entering the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*) arises with this faith as its cause, condition, and antecedent basis — these purities are the dominant result (*adhipati-phala*) of faith. The Blessed One therefore teaches that this faith *gives rise to* those purities; he does not equate them in substance or in characteristic. The causal relationship is the point, not an identity.

### R-17
**Category:** Nested Qualification (NQ)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.7, "Effort faculty and effort power" one-liner

**Quoted passage:**
> Here, the effort faculty and effort power are the effort within the four right efforts — specifically, the expedient right effort that can permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing (*darshana-marga*). This is the right effort intended, because through it one can ultimately cut off all evil and unwholesome dharmas.

**What's wrong:** "the effort within the four right efforts — specifically, the expedient right effort that can permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing" has a self-modifying structure that requires the reader to hold three qualifiers simultaneously. The closing sentence "This is the right effort intended" is weak meta-commentary — the reader does not need to be told that this is the effort intended; they need to understand why *this* effort is the one that matters.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> The effort faculty and effort power are a specific kind of effort within the four right efforts: the expedient right effort capable of permanently cutting off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing (*darshana-marga*). This effort is singled out because it can ultimately sever all evil and unwholesome dharmas — a scope that makes it the definitive expression of the effort faculty.

### R-18
**Category:** Formula Monotony (FM)
**Severity:** Moderate
**Location:** 29.7, correlations section — five parallel entries

**Quoted passage:**
> **Effort faculty and effort power = the four right efforts.** Here, the effort faculty...
>
> **Mindfulness faculty and mindfulness power = the four foundations of mindfulness.** The mindfulness faculty...
>
> **Samadhi faculty and samadhi power = the four dhyanas.** In this context, the samadhi faculty...
>
> **Wisdom faculty and wisdom power = the four noble truths.** Finally, the wisdom faculty...

**What's wrong:** The four sub-entries after the faith entry each follow the same formula: bold header with an equals-sign correlation, then a sentence beginning "Here/The/In this context/Finally, the [faculty] faculty and [faculty] power are the [adjective] [noun] within the [group]..." This is fine for a list or table, but as continuous prose the formula monotony is noticeable. The connective words (Here / The / In this context / Finally) are doing nothing to create movement or relationship between the entries. This is inherently a taxonomic section so some list-quality is appropriate, but the variation is minimal.

**Suggested rewrite (moderate intervention — preserve structure, add connective tissue):**
> **Effort faculty and effort power = the four right efforts.** Specifically, this is the expedient right effort within the four right efforts — the effort that can permanently cut off all afflictions abandoned by the path of seeing (*darshana-marga*), and thereby ultimately sever all evil and unwholesome dharmas.
>
> **Mindfulness faculty and mindfulness power = the four foundations of mindfulness.** This is right mindfulness within the four foundations — the mindfulness capable of completely eliminating all inversions without remainder.
>
> **Samadhi faculty and samadhi power = the four dhyanas.** This is right samadhi within the four dhyanas (meditative absorptions) — the samadhi that serves as the expedient means for realizing the fruit of non-return.
>
> **Wisdom faculty and wisdom power = the four noble truths.** This is right knowledge within the four noble truths — the knowledge that realizes direct realization (*abhisamaya*) and obtains the shramana fruits.

### R-21
**Category:** Register Drift (RD)
**Severity:** Low
**Location:** 29.4, "Question/Answer" on *rddhipada* etymology

**Quoted passage:**
> **Answer:** Just as one who has feet can go and return, leap and stride with vigor, and can obtain and realize the most extraordinary dharmas in the world — the extraordinary dharmas of the world are called "supernatural" (*riddhi*); because these samadhis can reach them, they are called "bases of supernatural power" (*rddhipada*).

**What's wrong:** The analogy shifts mid-sentence from legs-as-instrument to samadhis-as-instrument without a clear bridge. "Just as one who has feet... the extraordinary dharmas of the world are called supernatural" — the subject of the analogy (feet) and the subject of the conclusion (extraordinary dharmas) are not parallel. The analogy would be clearer if it drew an explicit comparison: "just as feet enable one to travel to distant places, these samadhis enable one to reach what would otherwise be unreachable."

**Suggested rewrite:**
> **Answer:** Just as one with healthy feet can travel anywhere — going and returning, leaping and striding with vigor — so too these samadhis can reach the most extraordinary dharmas in the world. The extraordinary is called "supernatural" (*riddhi*); the samadhis that reach it are called "bases of supernatural power" (*rddhipada*).
