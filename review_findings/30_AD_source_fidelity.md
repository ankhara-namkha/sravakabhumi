## Findings

### 1. Mistranslation: 偏覆左肩 rendered as "bares the left shoulder" — **Severity: C (Critical)**

**Location:** Section 30.2, [T30.0448c01]

**Chinese:** 將請問時偏覆左肩右膝著地

**English:** "At the moment of asking, one bares the left shoulder and places the right knee on the ground"

**Issue:** 偏覆左肩 means "partially covers the left shoulder" — i.e., the left shoulder remains covered and the RIGHT shoulder is bared. This is the standard monastic gesture of respect (偏袒右肩, baring the right shoulder). The English says "bares the left shoulder," which reverses the gesture. Should read: "bares the right shoulder" or "drapes the robe over the left shoulder, leaving the right shoulder bare."

---

### 2. Mistranslation: 修斷 rendered as "cultivation and renunciation" (x2) — **Severity: M (Major)**

**Location:** Section 30.3, [T30.0448c13]; Section 30.4, [T30.0449a10]

**Chinese (30.3):** 又應稱揚修斷功德
**English (30.3):** "and should extol the merits of cultivation and renunciation"

**Chinese (30.4):** 稱揚修斷諸功德已
**English (30.4):** "having extolled the merits of cultivation and renunciation"

**Issue:** 修斷 is a compound meaning "cultivation (of wholesome qualities) and abandonment/severance (of unwholesome ones)" — the twin aspects of the path. 斷 = cut off, sever, abandon. "Renunciation" (出離, nekkhamma) is a different concept entirely — it refers to going forth or turning away from worldly life. The correct rendering is "cultivation and abandonment" or "developing [virtue] and eliminating [faults]."

---

### 3. Mistranslation: Footnote reverses acharya/upadhyaya roles — **Severity: M (Major)**

**Location:** Section 30.2, footnote [^teacher_types]

**Footnote text:** "The preceptor (*acharya*, 軌範師) is the teacher who models monastic conduct and discipline. The instructor (*upadhyaya*, 親教師) is the personal ordination teacher."

**Issue:** In standard Buddhist Vinaya terminology, the upadhyaya (親教師) is the preceptor — the ordination master who presides over ordination. The acharya (軌範師) is the instructor — the teacher who trains the student in conduct and ritual. The footnote has the English role labels ("preceptor" and "instructor") correctly paired with the Sanskrit, but the descriptions are swapped: it says the acharya "models monastic conduct" (which is correct for acharya) but calls this role "preceptor" (which conventionally designates the upadhyaya/ordination master). The confusion is in mapping English role names to the Sanskrit terms. Most precisely: acharya = instructor/teacher (軌範師, one who teaches norms of conduct), upadhyaya = preceptor/ordination master (親教師, the personal teacher who ordains). The running text itself ("preceptor (acharya), instructor (upadhyaya)") has the labels reversed from the more common convention where "preceptor" = upadhyaya.

### 4. Minor Addition: "true" in "true benefit" — **Severity: m (minor)**

**Location:** Section 30.2, [T30.0448c01]

**Chinese:** 為隨證得自義利故
**English:** "seeking to attain their own true benefit"

**Issue:** The word "true" has no counterpart in the Chinese. 自義利 = "one's own benefit/welfare." The addition doesn't distort meaning but adds a nuance not present in the source.

---

### 5. Minor Compression: 讚勵慶慰 four-term compound rendered as three — **Severity: m (minor)**

**Location:** Section 30.4, [T30.0449a10]

**Chinese:** 以如是等柔軟言詞讚勵慶慰
**English:** "Having thus praised, encouraged, and comforted the student"

**Issue:** The Chinese has four actions: 讚 (praise), 勵 (encourage), 慶 (congratulate/rejoice), 慰 (comfort). The English omits 慶 (congratulate/rejoice). This is a minor omission as the four terms form a near-synonymous cluster, but 慶 specifically connotes rejoicing in the student's aspiration, which differs from mere comfort.

---

### 6. Minor Addition: "sense" in "sense objects" — **Severity: m (minor)**

**Location:** Section 30.3, first praise statement

**Chinese:** 沈沒境界樂著境界
**English:** "who sink into sense objects and cling to them"

**Issue:** 境界 = "objects" or "realms" (visaya), not specifically "sense objects." The addition of "sense" narrows the meaning. In context, 境界 here likely does refer to sensory domains, so this is a minor interpretive addition rather than an error.

---

### 7. Structural Note: Reordering of final sentence in 30.3 — **Severity: m (minor)**

**Location:** Section 30.3, closing paragraph

**Chinese:** 汝今為欲勤修自利利他正行。汝今為欲利益安樂無量眾生。哀愍世間及諸天人阿素洛等。為令獲得義利安樂故來問爾。

**English:** "You have come to ask because you wish to diligently practice the right conduct that benefits both yourself and others, to bring benefit and happiness to immeasurable beings, and out of compassion for the world — for gods, humans, asuras, and all the rest — so that they may attain benefit and happiness."

**Issue:** The Chinese places 故來問爾 ("therefore you have come to ask") at the very end as a concluding clause. The English front-loads "You have come to ask because..." at the beginning. The meaning is fully preserved; this is a natural English restructuring. Noting for completeness only.
