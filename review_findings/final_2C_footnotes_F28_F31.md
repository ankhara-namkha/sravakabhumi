### B. Missing Footnotes — F28 Gaps

The following passages would receive a footnote in a translation at F26 density (1:229). Listed by priority.

1. **Section 28.1, gradual sequence** (shila -> no remorse -> gladness -> ease -> samadhi -> knowing -> disenchantment -> dispassion -> liberation -> nirvana): This is the *cetaso-vimutti* chain from DN 2, AN 10.1, etc. No footnote links it to its canonical parallel. PRIORITY: HIGH.

2. **Section 28.1, "three trainings exclusive to the noble teaching"**: The claim of exclusivity deserves a note: non-Buddhist paths (e.g., Jain, yoga) also have moral, meditative, and cognitive disciplines. What makes the Buddhist three "superior" is their orientation toward nirvana. No footnote addresses this.

3. **Section 28.3, three gates of liberation**: The relationship between wishlessness->signlessness->emptiness follows a specific sequence here (conditioned->unconditioned->non-existent). In Theravada, the order is often emptiness->signlessness->wishlessness (Vism XXI.66). This ordering difference deserves a footnote.

4. **Section 28.4, "perception of death" (maranasati)**: The tenth perception in the counter-list is the perception of death. This is a major meditation practice with extensive treatment in the Visuddhimagga (Vism VIII.1-41) and the Lamrim Chenmo (Vol. 1, ch. 9). No footnote links the Sravakabhumi's treatment to these parallels.

5. **Section 28.5, counterfeit dharma passage**: The vivid description of the false teacher who invents a private yoga, teaches it secretly to lay followers, and conceals it from tripitaka-holders is one of the most remarkable sociological passages in the Sravakabhumi. No footnote contextualizes it. It parallels SN 16.13.

6. **Section 28.6, four types of diligence** (hearing, reflection, meditation, obstacle-purification): This maps onto the standard threefold wisdom (shrutamayi, cintamayi, bhavanamayi) plus a fourth. The three-to-four expansion is distinctive. No footnote.

7. **Section 28.7, four aspects of attention** (object, condition, to-be-avoided, to-be-cultivated): This fourfold phenomenology of meditative attention is distinctive to the Yogacarabhumi and has no Pali parallel. Deserves a note identifying it as such.

8. **Section 28.8, "basis-extinction and basis-transformation"** (所依滅/所依轉): These are early instances of the ashraya-paravrtti concept that becomes central to later Yogacara. No footnote connects to the glossary entry for *ashraya-paravrtti*.

9. **Section 28.8, "sequential-decision persons" and warmth/summit/patience/world's-highest-dharma**: The four prerequisites for noble path entry (*nirvedha-bhagiya*) are mentioned without a footnote explaining this important Abhidharma category.

10. **Section 28.9, four念住 as antidote to four inversions**: The systematic mapping (body->purity inversion; feeling->pleasure inversion; mind->permanence inversion; dharma->self inversion) is a key Abhidharma structural claim. The existing footnote [^four_inversions] covers it, but the *second* mapping (body=basis of karma, feeling=purpose of karma, mind=agent of karma, dharma=means of karma) receives no footnote at all.

11. **Section 28.9, "listened to, reflected upon, cultivated" (shrutamayi/cintamayi/bhavanamayi)**: The threefold wisdom classification appears at the very end of the fascicle but receives no footnote despite being one of the most fundamental Buddhist epistemological categories.

12. **Section 28.9, 35 aspects of body**: The distinctive 35-fold body analysis is unique to the Yogacarabhumi. The Satipatthana Sutta (MN 10) enumerates 31 anatomical parts. This discrepancy deserves a footnote.

13. **Section 28.9, five types of internal/external**: The fivefold sub-division of internal/external body contemplation is far more elaborate than anything in the Pali canon. No footnote highlights this.

## III. Priority New Footnotes — Fascicle 28 (10 Drafts)

### F28-NEW-1: Gradual Liberation Sequence (Section 28.1)
**Insert after:** "one finally realizes the ultimate nirvana where there is nothing more to be done."
```
[^gradual_liberation]: This gradual sequence — from ethical discipline through non-remorse, gladness, ease, concentration, knowledge-and-vision, disenchantment, dispassion, liberation, to nirvana — is one of the most widely attested doctrinal formulas in early Buddhism. It appears in the Pali canon as the *sila-samadhi-panna* progression (DN 2, Samannaphala Sutta; AN 10.1, Kimatthiya Sutta; AN 11.1) and is treated systematically in Vism I.1-8. The Sravakabhumi's version follows this sequence closely, confirming the shared doctrinal inheritance across Buddhist schools.
```

### F28-NEW-2: Contested Dhyana Requirement (Section 28.1)
**Insert after:** "One cannot accomplish this by entirely forsaking all dhyanas."
```
[^dhyana_requirement]: The claim that direct realization of the truths (*satya-abhisamaya*) requires dhyana as its basis is the normative Yogacara position. It should be noted, however, that other Buddhist schools allow for stream-entry without prior dhyana attainment. Theravada commentarial tradition recognizes the *sukkha-vipassaka* (dry insight practitioner) who gains noble attainment through pure insight. The Sravakabhumi itself acknowledges this possibility implicitly in section 28.1 below, where it notes that prajna can sometimes lead to the development of the mind-training.
```

### F28-NEW-3: Agotra Controversy (Section 28.5)
**Expand existing [^agotra] or add separate note.**
```
[^agotra_context]: The doctrine of *agotra* — that certain beings permanently lack the capacity for liberation — is one of the most contested claims in Yogacara thought. The *Lankavatara Sutra* and *Lotus Sutra* explicitly reject this position, asserting that all sentient beings possess buddha-nature (*tathagatagarbha*). Later Yogacara thinkers, including Paramartha, sought to harmonize the two positions. Asanga's presentation here represents the early, unmodified Yogacara stance.
```

### F28-NEW-4: Counterfeit Dharma Passage (Section 28.5)
**Insert after:** "Counterfeit dharma (*saddharma-pratirupa*) — which is not true dharma — obstructs true dharma."
```
[^counterfeit_elaboration]: This vivid portrait of a fraudulent teacher — intellectually sharp but morally polluted, who secretly constructs a private "yoga" that contradicts the sutras and vinaya, and who teaches it covertly to devotees while hiding it from learned monks — is among the most sociologically detailed passages in the Sravakabhumi. A parallel warning appears in SN 16.13, the Saddhamma-patirupa Sutta, where the Buddha predicts that the true Dharma will decline when counterfeit teachings proliferate. The passage may reflect actual situations in the monastic community of Asanga's time.
```

### F28-NEW-5: Perception of Death (Section 28.4)
**Insert after:** "10. The perception of death"
```
[^maranasati]: The perception of death (*marana-samjna*, Pali: *maranasati*) is treated at length in the Visuddhimagga (VIII.1-41), where Buddhaghosa prescribes eight methods of reflecting on death. The Lamrim Chenmo (Vol. 1, ch. 9) similarly devotes an entire chapter to mindfulness of death. In the present context, the perception of death functions as the antidote to the tenth opposing factor: clinging to life when experiencing life-threatening pain.
```

### F28-NEW-6: Three Wisdoms (Section 28.9, end)
**Insert after:** "Heard, reflected upon, cultivated — the threefold wisdom classification."
```
[^three_wisdoms]: The threefold classification of wisdom — born of hearing (*shrutamayi-prajna*), born of reflection (*cintamayi-prajna*), and born of cultivation (*bhavanamayi-prajna*) — is fundamental to Buddhist epistemology. The first two are necessarily mundane (*sasrava*); the third can be either mundane or supramundane (*anasrava*). This classification governs the entire structure of the Sravakabhumi's training path: one hears the teaching, reflects on its meaning, then cultivates it in meditation.
```

### F28-NEW-7: Thirty-Five Aspects of Body (Section 28.9)

[^thirtyfive_body]: The Sravakabhumi's 35-fold classification of the body is considerably more elaborate than the Pali Satipatthana Sutta's enumeration of 31 anatomical parts (MN 10, DN 22). The Yogacara scheme includes not only physical parts but also categories such as "body with consciousness" vs. "body without consciousness," "male body," "female body," and "body of an indeterminate sex" — reflecting the Abhidharma tendency to exhaustive taxonomic analysis. This classification is shared between the Yogacara and Sarvastivada Abhidharma traditions.

### F31-NEW-1: Twelve Links Sanskrit and Parallel (Section 31.1)
**Insert at first mention of twelve links.**
```
[^twelve_links]: The twelve links of dependent origination (*dvadasha-nidana*) — ignorance, formations, consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, feeling, craving, grasping, becoming, birth, and aging-and-death — constitute the most comprehensive Buddhist analysis of how suffering perpetuates itself. The formula is common to all Buddhist schools (cf. SN 12.1, Paticca-samuppada Sutta; Vism XVII.1-314). The Sravakabhumi's treatment is distinctive in applying the six-aspect vipashyana framework to each link.
```

### F31-NEW-2: Three Sufferings (Section 31.1)
**Insert after "pursued by the three sufferings."**
```
[^three_sufferings]: The three sufferings (*tri-duhkhata*) are: (1) the suffering of suffering (*duhkha-duhkhata*) — outright painful experience; (2) the suffering of change (*viparinama-duhkhata*) — the impermanence inherent in pleasant experience; (3) the suffering of formations (*samskara-duhkhata*) — the pervasive unsatisfactoriness of all conditioned existence. This classification appears in SN 38.14, SN 45.165, and is discussed in Vism XVI.34-38.
```

### F31-NEW-3: House Simile Parallels (Section 31.2)
**Expand existing [^house_simile].**
```
[^house_simile_expanded]: The house simile appears in multiple Buddhist sources. The Maharahulovada Sutta (MN 62) uses the elements in a simile for non-self. The Visuddhimagga (XI.30-31) employs a similar analysis: "when boards and rafters are put together, the word 'house' is used; so too when the five aggregates are present, the term 'being' is used." The Sravakabhumi's version specifically targets pride (*mana*) by dissolving the apparent unity of the body into its constituent elements.
```

### F31-NEW-4: Breath as External Sense-Base (Section 31.3)
**Insert after "they are classified under the external sense-base."**
```
[^breath_external]: The classification of breath as belonging to the external sense-base (*bahya-ayatana*), despite being "internally obtained" (i.e., experienced within one's own body), follows the Abhidharma principle that wind (*vayu*) as tangible form is an object of tactile consciousness and therefore belongs to the *sprashtavya-ayatana* (tangible sense-base), which is external. This Abhidharma technicality grounds the subsequent contemplation of breath as simultaneously internal and external.
```

### F31-NEW-5: Conduct-Type Assignments (Section 31.5)
**Insert at the conduct-type to meditation-object mapping.**
```
[^conduct_mapping]: The assignment of specific meditation objects to specific character types (*carita*) is a shared feature of Yogacara and Theravada meditation theory. The Visuddhimagga (III.74-103) provides a parallel system: impurity for the lustful, loving-kindness for the angry, mindfulness of breathing for the deluded. However, the Sravakabhumi assigns dependent origination to the deluded type and mindfulness of breathing to the discursive-thought type — a distinctive Yogacara arrangement. See section 26.5 of this translation for the initial presentation.
```

### F31-NEW-6: Signless Beginner's Practice (Section 31.7)
**Insert after the beginner's instruction to settle without any object.**
```
[^signless_beginner]: This instruction — that a beginner should initially settle the mind without binding it to any object whatsoever, simply resolving toward signlessness and freedom from elaboration — is remarkable. It differs significantly from the graduated approach of the Visuddhimagga, where the beginner always starts with a specific meditation object (Vism III.104 ff.). The Sravakabhumi's method begins with objectless settling, then introduces objects only after initial ease is established. This "clearing-first" approach may reflect a distinctively Yogacara pedagogy.
```

In the Yogacarabhumi, uddana verses appear at major division points, confirming the text's origin in an oral teaching tradition.

### F31-NEW-9: Three Objects of Delight / Anussati (Section 31.11)
**Insert after the three objects of delight.**
```
[^three_delights]: The three objects that "moisten" the mind — recollecting the Three Jewels, recollecting pure discipline, and confidence in one's attainments — correspond to the first three of the standard Buddhist recollection (*anusmriti*) practices. The Visuddhimagga devotes chapters VII-VIII to these recollections (Vism VII.1-67 for Buddha, Dhamma, Sangha recollections; VII.68-117 for recollection of virtue). In the Sravakabhumi, they serve the specific function of counterbalancing the "disciplining" attention that turns the mind from defilements: without the "moistening" effect of recollected joy, the mind would become dry and rigid.
```

### F31-NEW-10: Goldsmith Simile — AN 3.100 Precision (Section 31.11)
**Expand existing [^goldsmith] with precise Pali reference.**
```
[^goldsmith_expanded]: The goldsmith simile appears in the Anguttara Nikaya as the Nimitta Sutta (AN 3.100 / 3.102 in some editions), where the Buddha compares mental training to the goldsmith's art of alternating between firing and cooling. In both texts, the key insight is identical: exclusive application of one technique (firing alone or calming alone) produces rigidity, not refinement. Only the alternation between opposites — heat and cold, discipline and delight, shamatha and vipashyana — produces a mind that is both pure and workable. The Sravakabhumi's version adds the specific four-attention framework as the Buddhist elaboration of this universal craft metaphor.
```
