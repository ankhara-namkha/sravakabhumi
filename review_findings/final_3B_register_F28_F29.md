# Senior Reader Review: Fascicles 28 and 29
## Prose Register, Taxonomic Clarity, and Revision Quality
### Assessment against NTC/Nanamoli/Bodhi/Lamrim Chenmo standard

---

## Overall Verdict

**Fascicle 28** is the stronger text. Its opening sections (28.1–28.5) are often genuinely good — precise, organized, alive in the right Nanamoli register. The middle sections (28.6–28.9) are adequate. Section 28.10–28.11 is the weakest: the thirty-seven factors enumeration is clear but airless, and the mindfulness-of-dharmas taxonomy descends into catalog-mode without the animating prose that keeps a reader oriented and engaged.

**Fascicle 29** shows clear evidence of revision. The opening four sections (29.1–29.4) are the best prose in the fascicle — genuinely NTC-quality. Sections 29.5–29.8 are workmanlike but thin. Sections 29.9–29.17 are mostly solid, with two standout passages (fire-drilling analogy, 29.8; four maras, 29.16) that reach toward the contemplative register. The revision held. There are no extended patches of the pre-revision mechanical Chinese-gloss style. But there are residual spots — discussed below.

## Criterion-by-Criterion Assessment

### 1. Translated-ese (prose assembled from Chinese rather than written in English)

**F28:** Low in the early sections. The Q&A structure in 28.1 (three trainings) flows naturally. The graduated-path chain at 28.1 line 49 ("Because ethical discipline is well purified, one is free from worry and remorse... from dispassion one attains liberation...") is excellent: causal chain following the English preference for "because X, Y; because Y, Z."

Translated-ese appears in the mid-fascicle. The following passage in 28.7 is the clearest example of clause-order inversion:

> "The object aspect: The image (*pratibimba*) that is similar to the knowable matter appears clearly and vividly."

And this from 28.7:

> "When vipashyana takes the lead, one concentrates the mind internally and applies vigorous observation, attending to what conditions the samadhi. When shamatha takes the lead, one first arouses discernment, then calms with equal vigor, attending to the same conditioned ground."

The second sentence here is unclear — "attending to the same conditioned ground" is a phrase assembled from Chinese conceptual vocabulary without English orientation. A reader has no context for "conditioned ground" at this point.

The thirty-five aspects of body in 28.11 are enumerated in a long, unpunctuated run that is hard to scan mentally. The parenthetical glosses [i.e., the great elements] help, but the list itself remains an inert data-dump.

**F29:** Translated-ese is mostly absent in the revised sections. One notable survival:

> "One should know: regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas that relate to non-visible objects — to prevent unarisen ones from arising and permanently sever those already arisen — one urges and exerts oneself." (29.1, self-urging paragraph)

The em-dash sandwich construction around the infinitive clauses is Chinese clause structure preserved in English dress. Compare the GOOD example in the design-invariants: subject first, action verb second, qualifications after.

### 2. Formula Monotony

**F28:** The formula monotony problem in this fascicle is not "Furthermore" (though it appears) but rather the "This is called X" closing-formula, which echoes through sections 28.2–28.4 as:

- "This is called the superior training in ethical discipline."
- "This is called the superior training in mind."
- "This is called the superior training in prajna."
- "This is called the faculty of X."
- "This is called Y."

Most individual sections do not violate the 3+ rule, but at the fascicle level the cadence is metronomic. In sections 28.7–28.9 the repetition becomes noticeable: "This is called effortful operating attention... This is called intermittent operating attention... This is called uninterrupted operating attention... This is called effortless operating attention" — four consecutive paragraphs ending with the identical closing formula.

**F29:** The revision handled "Furthermore" variation well in 29.1. But the four right abandonment subtypes (29.1, lines 89–97) use the "it is called X-abandonment" closing formula four times in succession. Additionally, the bolded subheadings in 29.1 (Aspiration regarding unwholesome dharmas / Self-urging and vigorous effort distinguished by object / Self-urging and vigorous effort by grade of affliction / etc.) create a visual monotony that amplifies the sense of formula repetition — every few paragraphs the reader hits a new bold heading followed by the same structural prose pattern.

### 3. Nested Qualification

**F28:** The most egregious instance is in the "What is feeling" taxonomy (28.11, lines 520–530):

> "It is: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. Pleasant bodily feeling, painful bodily feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant bodily feeling. As with bodily feeling, so too with mental feeling. Pleasant feeling with relish, painful feeling with relish, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling with relish. Feeling without relish likewise. Feeling based on indulgence likewise. Pleasant feeling based on renunciation, painful feeling based on renunciation, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling based on renunciation."

This is not nested qualification exactly — it is a parallelism cascade. But the effect on the reader is disorientation: by the time we reach "feeling based on indulgence likewise," the "likewise" has no clear referent in the English because the pattern has been interrupted by "feeling without relish likewise" (which belongs to a different classification axis). The passage is accurate but the reader loses the structural thread.

**F29:** The one nested-qualification passage remaining from pre-revision is in 29.1:

> "Regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas that relate to non-visible objects — to prevent unarisen ones from arising and permanently sever those already arisen — one urges and exerts oneself."

The main verb ("urges and exerts oneself") is buried behind a relative clause and two embedded infinitive phrases in em-dashes. Three qualification layers before the action.

### 4. Register Drift

**F28:** Register drift occurs most noticeably in the following three passages:

**(a) The "What is mind" taxonomy (28.11, lines 522–538):** The twenty kinds of mind — twelve consecutive "the X mind and the not-X mind" dyads — slip into pure enumeration catalog. A practitioner sitting with this passage would not find it penetrating their understanding; a scholar would have trouble remembering where they are in the list. The passage serves an Abhidharma classificatory purpose but the translation does nothing to make the logic of the classification visible to the reader. The text says "twenty kinds of mind" but does not tell the reader why twenty, or what principle organizes them, until footnote [^twenty_minds] — which a reader would have to stop and read to get oriented.

**(b) The resolute confidence taxonomy (28.7, lines 390–400):** Nine types in a single long paragraph, the distinctions within some types (narrow/vast/immeasurable) not naturally differentiated for the English reader. The prose runs without paragraph breaks, making the distinctions hard to isolate.

**(c) The six frameworks for internal/external body contemplation (28.11, lines 554–568):** Six consecutive interpretations beginning "When one takes as object..." — each following the same sentence structure with only the specific classification variable changed. By the fourth interpretation a careful reader is still tracking, but barely. By the sixth, tracking requires active effort.

**F29:** Register drift is present in 29.5–29.7 (five faculties, five powers, their correlation with other groups). These three short sections lack the animated quality of 29.1 and 29.8. The distinction between faculties and powers, in particular — essentially "faculties exercise dominance, powers are indomitable" — is telegraphed in two paragraphs each but never quite lands with weight. The reader is told the distinction but does not feel it. A practitioner would not come away from these sections with a working sense of what a "faculty" is vs. a "power."

### 5. Contemplative Quality

**F28:** The graduated path chain (28.1, lines 49–51) has genuine contemplative warmth:

> "First, because ethical discipline is well purified, one is free from worry and remorse. Being free from worry and remorse, one experiences gladness and ease. Because there is ease, the mind attains right concentration... From dispassion comes dispassion. From dispassion one attains liberation."

This is alive. The reader can feel the sequence as a living progression.

The portrait of the corrupt teacher in 28.5 (destruction of yoga through wrong conduct) is an unexpectedly vivid piece of writing — specific, psychologically observed, and pointedly relevant to any institutional religious community. The passage rises above the taxonomic surrounding material.

Conversely, the four types of yogin (28.8) and the cultivation through perceptions (28.9) are dry. They deliver information. They do not open anything in the reader.

**F29:** The fire-drilling analogy (29.8) is the best contemplative writing in either fascicle:

> "Just as fire requires sustained friction before it suddenly blazes forth, the undefiled dharmas that burn away all afflictions require steady cultivation before they arise."

And the parallel enumeration of the four stages against the analogy stages is elegant — each bullet point lands with precision and the structural parallelism works in English rather than fighting it.

The four maras passage (29.16) — specifically the "Mara's Activities" subsection (lines 498–514) — has the right tone: each activity named in a clause that would be instantly recognizable to a practitioner as a temptation they have actually encountered. "When the desire for wholesome dharmas leading to liberation arises, but through the dominant force of attachment to sensory pleasures one quickly retreats and abandons them — this is the work of mara." This lands.

### 6. Reading Arc

**F28:** The reading arc is the fascicle's primary structural problem. At the macro level:

- 28.1 (Three trainings): logical, well-organized, strong Q&A structure
- 28.2 (Three faculties): brief, clear
- 28.3 (Three gates of liberation): adequate
- 28.4 (Factors opposing/according with training): appropriately detailed
- 28.5 (Four yoga failures): vivid, unexpectedly strong
- 28.6 (Four yogas): good structure, slightly mechanical execution
- 28.7 (Types of attention): increasingly technical, reader requires prior familiarity
- 28.8 (Three yogins): clear
- 28.9 (Two types of cultivation): requires patience
- 28.10 (Thirty-seven factors): efficiently organized
- 28.11 (Four foundations): loses the reader in the taxonomic depth

A reader moving through the fascicle feels the warmth and clarity of the early sections, then a gradual thinning as the material becomes more deeply classificatory. The problem is not any single passage but the unbroken accumulation of taxonomy without periodic return to practice-ground or the animated commentary register that characterized 28.1 and 28.5. By 28.11, the reader is in pure Abhidharma index mode.

**F29:** The reading arc is better managed. The four right efforts (29.1) are dense but active — the prose moves because the distinctions being drawn (self-urging vs. vigorous effort by object, grade, cause) have a practical logic the reader can follow. The fire-drilling analogy in 29.8 provides a genuine moment of relief and illumination in the middle of what would otherwise be unbroken technical material. The shramana fruits section (29.12) and the mara activities (29.16) provide two more moments where the prose lifts above catalog level. The fascicle ends at 29.17 with a clean three-cause analysis that is almost epigrammatic in its clarity.

The weakest arc moment in F29 is sections 29.5–29.7 (faculties, powers, correlations). These three sections sit like a flat plateau between the fire-drilling analogy and the seven factors of awakening — the reader coasts through them without being engaged or oriented.

### 7. Broken Logic / Unexplained Jargon / Sentences over 50 words

**F28:**

Over-50-word sentences are frequent and occasionally problematic:

Line 504: The thirty-five-aspect body list is a single long sentence that runs approximately 120 words. It is grammatically coherent but cognitively exhausting. The parenthetical insertions help but the list cannot be retained without active note-taking.

Line 528: "Furthermore, pleasant feeling is feeling arising from contact conducive to pleasant feeling, included in the category of balanced feeling. This is called pleasant feeling. When associated with the five sense-consciousnesses, it is called bodily feeling. When associated with the mental consciousness, it is called mental feeling." — The "category of balanced feeling" phrase introduces a concept ("balanced/unbalanced/neither") without telling the reader what it means. The next sentences do not explain it; the reader would have to infer from "unbalanced feeling" that this is the hedonic categorization schema. A bracketed gloss would serve.

Line 380: The "condition aspect" passage (28.7): "Six prerequisites must be accumulated: (1) being guided by the teaching; (2) having a consistent practice; (3) having keen aspiration; (4) generating deep disenchantment regarding what should be abandoned; (5) being able to distinguish mental disturbance from mental stillness; and (6) being free from disruption by others — whether from people, non-humans, sounds, or activity." This is fine on its own. The sentence following is the logic problem: "When vipashyana takes the lead, one concentrates the mind internally and applies vigorous observation, attending to what conditions the samadhi. When shamatha takes the lead, one first arouses discernment, then calms with equal vigor, attending to the same conditioned ground." The phrase "conditioned ground" has no established meaning in the translation and cannot be reconstructed from context.

**F29:** The extended analytic passages in 29.1 (four abandonment types) each end with a version of "because... it is called X-abandonment" — none of these sentences exceeds 50 words individually, but the explanatory syntax is sometimes circular. Example:

> "Because regarding already-arisen evil and unwholesome matters one should cultivate discipline to cause their destruction, and one should not tolerate them — for this reason it is called discipline-abandonment."

The reason given for the name does not clearly explain the relationship between "discipline" and "abandonment." A reader is told the name was selected on this basis but cannot reconstruct why "cultivating discipline" makes it the "discipline-abandonment" rather than the "cultivation-abandonment." A sentence of disambiguation would help.

## F28 Taxonomic Clarity (Criterion 8)

**Could a reader reconstruct the structure of the thirty-seven factors without a diagram?**

In section 28.10, the answer is: barely yes for the seven groups and their member counts; no for the structural logic.

The enumeration in 28.10 is clear: seven groups, member counts visible from the lists. A reader would know that there are four foundations, four right efforts, four bases, five faculties, five powers, seven awakening factors, and eight noble path factors — and that these add to thirty-seven.

What a reader would not grasp from 28.10 alone:
1. That the seven groups represent a progression, not a catalog — earlier groups are more foundational, later groups more advanced
2. That there is extensive overlap among groups (mindfulness appearing in all seven groups at different levels) and that this overlap is intentional
3. Why thirty-seven and not thirty-five or forty — the organizing principle is not stated

The footnote [^thirty_seven] addresses point 2 (overlap is intentional deepening), and this information belongs in the body text.

**Could a reader reconstruct the structure of the three trainings?**

Yes. Section 28.1 is a model of how taxonomic content should be organized for a non-specialist. The three trainings are introduced, defined, sequenced (28.1 lines 47–51), justified in that sequence through the graduated path, and named ("superior") with two explanations. A reader finishing 28.1 understands the framework, the logic, and its soteriological weight. This section should be the model for the rest of the fascicle.

**Could a reader reconstruct the structure of the four foundations of mindfulness?**

For the high-level structure (body, feeling, mind, dharmas as four objects), yes.
For the twenty-one feelings taxonomy, only with effort — the classification axes shift without adequate transition signals.
For the twenty minds taxonomy, the groupings (6 active, 14 meditative; 8 hindrance-stage, 6 affliction-stage) are only explained after the list, which means the reader cannot follow the list's internal logic while reading it. Better: state the organizing principle before the list.
For the six frameworks of internal/external contemplation, no — a reader would not emerge from this with a usable understanding of why six frameworks are given or how they relate to each other.

**Structural suggestions:**

1. In 28.10, add a bridge paragraph before the full enumeration that tells the reader: "These seven groups represent a graduated path — each building on what precedes it — and they overlap intentionally, with qualities like mindfulness and wisdom appearing at every level in progressively purified form." This is currently in the footnote and should be in the body.

2. In 28.11, before the twenty-one feelings list, state the organizing principle: "Feelings are classified along three axes: hedonic tone (pleasant/painful/neutral), associated consciousness (bodily/mental), and orientation (with or without relish, indulgence-based or renunciation-based)." Then the list becomes navigable.

3. In 28.11, before the twenty minds list, state: "Of these twenty, the first six arise in daily activity; the remaining fourteen arise in meditation — eight at the stage of purifying hindrances, six at the stage of purifying afflictions." Then the list follows a visible logic.

4. In 28.11, the six frameworks for internal/external body contemplation (lines 554–568) could benefit from a single orienting sentence at the top: "The Sravakabhumi offers six distinct frameworks for understanding what 'internal,' 'external,' and 'internal-and-external' mean — each highlighting a different classificatory principle."

## F29 Revision Check (Criterion 9)

**Did the revision hold?**

Yes. The pre-revision mechanical quality — Chinese-structured sentences, no prose variation, sustained passive-voice catalog — has been substantially remediated. The sections that were weakest (29.1 in particular) now show the characteristic marks of the post-revision quality: parallel constructions compressed from Chinese mechanicism into English idiom, sub-heading organization that genuinely serves navigation, and at least occasional animation in the language.

**Where the register is strong:**

- 29.1 opening paragraph: "Having thus continuously practiced the four foundations of mindfulness, one has removed the coarser inversions..." — Clean, oriented, active.
- 29.1 aspiration and self-urging passages: The distinctions are sharply drawn and practically intelligible.
- 29.2 four samadhis: Each description reads as active practice instruction. The investigation-samadhi's inner-examination passage ("Are there evil and unwholesome dharmas actively manifesting in me right now...") is excellent — direct address register, appropriately intimate.
- 29.8 fire-drilling analogy: The best passage in either fascicle.
- 29.12–29.13: The stream-entry analysis (three fetters as three domains of obstruction) and the thin-dust person's seven qualities are well-organized and readable.
- 29.16 mara's activities: Vivid, specific, practically oriented.

**Where unremediated patches remain:**

- 29.3 eight abandonment factors: The definitions are accurate but thin. "(5) Mindfulness (*smriti*): the quality that settles the mind and holds it steady within the shamatha category, specifically through the nine aspects of mental abiding." This sentence defines mindfulness by reference to two undefined concepts (shamatha category, nine aspects) that are explained elsewhere but not here. A reader encountering this section independently is left with empty terminology.

- 29.5–29.6 (faculties and powers): Two consecutive sections that do the doctrinal work of distinguishing "dominance" from "indomitability" but do not make this distinction felt. The example of faith as "dominant over all four subsequent faculties plus supramundane dharmas" (29.5) is stated but not illustrated — there is no moment of contact with what this means in practice. The Bodhi/Nanamoli standard would here insert a sentence of practical grounding.

- 29.7 (correlation of faculties and powers): The explanatory formula "the Blessed One therefore teaches, in accordance with the principle of causal relationship, that this faith gives rise to those verified purities — he does not equate them in substance or in characteristic" (29.7 lines 197) is scholarly commentary unwoven into the translation body. This belongs in a footnote, not the running text; it interrupts the description with meta-commentary.

- 29.11 (Summary of the Thirty-Seven Factors): A single sentence section, which is appropriate — this is a summary connector. But the sentence reads: "Through the gradual cultivation of the thirty-seven factors of awakening by means of these preparatory practices — this is called the cultivation of the factors of awakening." The em-dash construction is weak. Better: "Thus — through the gradual cultivation of these thirty-seven factors — the preparatory work of the path is called the cultivation of the factors of awakening." (Or simply: "This sustained, graduated cultivation of all thirty-seven factors is what the text means by 'the cultivation of the factors of awakening.'")

## 5 Worst Passages — Fascicle 28

### 1. The "What is mind" taxonomy (28.11, lines 522–538)

**Current text:**
> "What is mind (*citta*)? It is: the mind with attachment and the mind free from attachment; the mind with aversion and the mind free from aversion; the mind with delusion and the mind free from delusion; the contracted mind and the scattered mind; the sunken mind and the uplifted mind; the agitated mind and the non-agitated mind; the tranquil mind and the non-tranquil mind; the concentrated mind and the unconcentrated mind; the well-cultivated mind and the uncultivated mind; the well-liberated mind and the not-well-liberated mind. Thus there are altogether twenty kinds of mind."

**Problems:** Inert enumeration, no organizing principle stated before the list, "the X mind and the not-X mind" repeated ten times without variation. A reader cannot tell whether the list has any internal logic.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "Mind (*citta*) appears in twenty forms, organized in three tiers. During ordinary activity, six kinds arise: the mind entangled by attachment — and the mind free from it; the mind gripped by aversion — and the mind free from it; the mind clouded by delusion — and the mind free from it.
>
> During meditation, the mind passes through stages of deepening purification. At the stage of clearing the hindrances, eight kinds arise: the contracted mind (gathered inward through right practice) and the scattered mind (dispersed outward among the objects of sense pleasure); the sunken mind and the uplifted one; the agitated mind and the non-agitated; the tranquil mind that has found freedom from the hindrances and the non-tranquil that has not.
>
> At the stage of purifying the afflictions, six kinds arise: the concentrated mind capable of entering the root dhyanas, and the unconcentrated that cannot; the well-cultivated mind that enters samadhi at will, without obstruction, with speed — and the uncultivated that still struggles; the well-liberated mind completely freed from everything, and the not-well-liberated that has not yet fully arrived."

### 2. The resolute confidence paragraph (28.7, lines 400)

**Current text:**
> "Resolute confidence with luminosity: Regarding the sign of light, one clarifies the mind, skillfully apprehends it, and has resolute confidence accompanied by luminosity. Without luminosity: One cannot skillfully apprehend the sign of light and has resolute confidence accompanied by dimness. Dull: The resolute confidence found in a person of dull faculties. Quick: The resolute confidence found in a person of sharp faculties. Narrow: Resolute confidence accompanied by narrow faith and aspiration, and resolute confidence directed at a narrow object of mental comprehension — because the attention is narrow and the object is narrow, it is called narrow. Vast: Resolute confidence accompanied by vast faith and aspiration, and directed at a vast object — because the attention is vast and the object is vast, it is called vast. Immeasurable: Resolute confidence accompanied by boundless and limitless faith and aspiration, directed at a boundless and limitless object — because the attention is immeasurable and the object is immeasurable, it is called immeasurable. Pure: Resolute confidence accompanied by what has been well cultivated, fully matured, and brought to completion. Impure: Resolute confidence accompanied by what has not been well cultivated, not fully matured, and not brought to completion."

**Problems:** Nine types strung into one paragraph without visual or logical organization; the because-clause in the narrow/vast/immeasurable entries is circular (X is called narrow because it is narrow); the entries do not tell the reader why any of these nine types matter or how they relate to each other.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "Nine types of resolute confidence are distinguished along three axes:
>
> *Luminosity*: Resolute confidence with luminosity — clear apprehension of the sign of light, the mind vivid and bright. Resolute confidence without luminosity — the sign only dimly apprehended, the mind accompanied by obscurity.
>
> *Faculty sharpness*: Dull resolute confidence, found in those of slower faculties; quick resolute confidence, found in those of sharper faculties.
>
> *Scope*: Narrow resolute confidence accompanies faith and aspiration that are themselves narrow, directed at a correspondingly narrow object. Vast accompanies broad faith and aspiration directed at a broad object. Immeasurable accompanies faith and aspiration that are boundless, directed at a limitless object.
>
> *Maturity*: Pure resolute confidence is well cultivated, fully matured, brought to completion. Impure is the reverse — not yet cultivated through or brought to completion."

### 3. The six frameworks for internal/external body contemplation (28.11, lines 554–568)

**Current text (representative excerpt):**
> "*First interpretation:* When one takes as object the bodily form belonging to one's own sentient-being class and dwells in contemplation of the body — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object form not belonging to the sentient-being class — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the bodily form of other sentient beings — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body..."
>
> (followed by five more interpretations of identical syntactic structure)

**Problems:** No introduction tells the reader why there are six frameworks, what they illuminate that one cannot, or how they relate to each other. By the fourth framework the reader has stopped tracking. The information is there but architecturally opaque.

**Proposed structural intervention:**
Insert before *First interpretation:*

> "The Sravakabhumi offers six distinct frameworks for understanding what 'internal,' 'external,' and 'internal-and-external' mean — each highlighting a different principle of classification. The first distinguishes by the class of beings whose bodies are observed. The second by whether form belongs to the sense faculties. The third by the quality of the meditator's own concentration. The fourth by whether form is creating (the great elements) or created (derived form). The fifth by the presence or absence of consciousness (living body vs. corpse). The sixth by the distinction between the core body (visible parts) and the surface body (skin and appearance). All six extend naturally from body-contemplation to feeling, mind, and dharmas."

Then the six interpretations follow with that framework in place.

### 4. The "What is feeling" taxonomy (28.11, lines 520–530)

**Current text:**
> "It is: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. Pleasant bodily feeling, painful bodily feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant bodily feeling. As with bodily feeling, so too with mental feeling. Pleasant feeling with relish, painful feeling with relish, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling with relish. Feeling without relish likewise. Feeling based on indulgence likewise. Pleasant feeling based on renunciation, painful feeling based on renunciation, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling based on renunciation."

**Problems:** Three classification axes (hedonic tone, bodily/mental, relish/renunciation) run together without labeled boundaries. The phrase "Feeling without relish likewise" and "Feeling based on indulgence likewise" have lost their referents — "likewise" to what? The structure requires knowing the classification schema before reading the list.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "Feeling (*vedana*) is classified along three axes. By hedonic tone: pleasant, painful, or neither-painful-nor-pleasant. By associated consciousness: when feeling arises with the five sense-consciousnesses, it is bodily feeling; when it arises with mental consciousness, it is mental feeling. All three hedonic tones arise in both forms. By orientation: feeling that accords with nirvana and the perfection of the holy life is called feeling without relish; feeling that falls within the realms of conditioned existence is called feeling with relish. Of the latter, feeling bound to the desire realm — not oriented toward the abandonment of desire — is called feeling based on indulgence; feeling bound to the form and formless realms or oriented toward dispassion is called feeling based on renunciation. The three hedonic tones occur within each orientation, yielding twenty-one combinations in total."

### 5. The "conditioned ground" passage (28.7, lines 378–379)

**Current text:**
> "When vipashyana takes the lead, one concentrates the mind internally and applies vigorous observation, attending to what conditions the samadhi. When shamatha takes the lead, one first arouses discernment, then calms with equal vigor, attending to the same conditioned ground."

**Problems:** "Conditioned ground" is untranslated jargon — the Chinese term (諸行) means something like "conditioned formations" or "the conditioned basis." The phrase "the same conditioned ground" has no antecedent in the English text; the reader cannot reconstruct what it refers to.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "When vipashyana leads, one turns the mind inward and applies alert investigation to examine the conditions that support samadhi. When shamatha leads, one first cultivates discernment and then settles the mind with equal care — directing attention to the same basis of conditioned formations."

## 5 Worst Passages — Fascicle 29

### 1. Self-urging nested qualification (29.1)

**Current text:**
> "One should know: regarding evil and unwholesome dharmas that relate to non-visible objects — to prevent unarisen ones from arising and permanently sever those already arisen — one urges and exerts oneself. This is called self-urging."

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "Self-urging addresses evil and unwholesome dharmas that relate to non-visible objects — past and future events held in thought. Regarding these, one prevents the unarisen from arising and permanently severs those already present."

### 2. Discipline-abandonment logic (29.1, lines 89–97)

**Current text:**
> "Because regarding already-arisen evil and unwholesome matters one should cultivate discipline to cause their destruction, and one should not tolerate them — for this reason it is called discipline-abandonment."

**Problems:** Circular. The name includes "abandonment" but "discipline" is not explained as the mechanism of abandonment.

**Proposed rewrite:**
> "Already-arisen unwholesome matters must be destroyed without tolerance. The primary instrument for this destruction is discipline — restraint imposed on the mind's tendency to dwell in or repeat the unwholesome. Because discipline is deployed as the means of abandonment, this effort is called discipline-abandonment."

### 3. Faculties and powers (29.5–29.6)

**Current text (representative):**
> "Faith exercises dominance in two directions: as the foremost condition for the arising of supramundane dharmas, and as the condition that generates effort, mindfulness, samadhi, and wisdom in sequence."

**Problems:** Accurate but inert. "Exercises dominance in two directions" is scholastic language that does not make the practitioner feel what a faculty is. The distinction between faculties and powers is stated but not animated.

**Proposed rewrite (bridging 29.5 and 29.6 with a framing sentence):**
> "Faith (*shraddha*) has two qualities that distinguish it from ordinary belief. As a faculty (*indriya*, lit. 'governing force'), it exercises dominance — it generates what follows it: effort arises in its wake, then mindfulness, then samadhi, then wisdom. Each faculty both enables supramundane dharmas to arise and sets the next faculty in motion. As a power (*bala*, lit. 'force capable of resisting'), the same faith becomes indomitable. No god, mara, shramana, brahmana, or worldly being can properly deflect it; no afflictive entanglement can bend it. The distinction is directional: faculty is generative power looking forward; power is resistant strength looking outward against opposition."

### 4. Commentary embedded in text (29.7, the Blessed One's teaching explanation)

**Current text:**
> "The Blessed One therefore teaches, in accordance with the principle of causal relationship, that this faith *gives rise to* those verified purities — he does not equate them in substance or in characteristic."

**Problems:** This is translator's meta-commentary on the Blessed One's teaching style — it does not belong in the translation body. It interrupts the description to explain why the text says what it says, rather than rendering what the text says.

**Proposed action:** Move this sentence to footnote [^correlation] where the cross-reference material already lives.
