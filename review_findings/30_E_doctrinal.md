# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 30, Section 30.5 (Four-Fold Investigation)

**Reviewer:** Agent D (Doctrinal)
**Scope:** Section 30.5 only (lines 98-150 of fascicle_30_en.md)
**Cross-references:** Tsongkhapa, *Great Treatise* Vol. 1 (Ch. 4, teacher-student); Trungpa, *PTOD1* (entering the path, teacher-student)

---

## Summary

Section 30.5 is doctrinally sound in its core framework. The four domains (aspiration, lineage, faculties, disposition) and four methods (questioning, discourse, observed conduct, knowledge of others' minds) accurately represent Yogacara teacher-assessment pedagogy as found in the Chinese source. No critical doctrinal errors were found. Two moderate issues and three minor observations are noted below.

---

## Findings

### 1. (M) "Spiritual lineage" for gotra — potentially misleading connotation

**Location:** Line 106 — "One should investigate their spiritual lineage (*gotra*)"

**Issue:** The rendering "spiritual lineage" for *gotra* (種姓) could suggest a transmission lineage (teacher-to-student succession), which is not what the Sravakabhumi means here. In Yogacara doctrine, *gotra* refers to the innate dispositional type or family — the practitioner's inherent orientation toward one of the three vehicles. This is closer to "spiritual family" or "disposition-type" than "lineage" in the English sense of a line of succession.

Tsongkhapa's *Lamrim Chenmo* Vol. 1 (line 939) uses *gotra* in the Mahayana sense: "One who abides in the lineage of the Mahayana has relied on an excellent teacher." Here "lineage" works because the context is clear. But in the Sravakabhumi's assessment framework, where the teacher is determining *which vehicle the student innately belongs to*, "spiritual lineage" risks being read as "which tradition were you trained in?" rather than "what is your innate spiritual disposition?"

The glossary entry (line 81) gives "lineage, family, disposition" with "lineage" as preferred. This is acceptable if context disambiguates, but the parenthetical gloss could be strengthened.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding a brief parenthetical or footnote on first use: "*gotra* — one's innate spiritual family or disposition, determining affinity for a particular vehicle." This would prevent confusion with transmission-lineage. Alternatively, the footnote could note that gotra in Yogacara is an inherent orientation, not a chosen or acquired affiliation.

**Severity:** M (moderate) — The term is technically defensible per the glossary, but without clarification a practitioner-reader could misunderstand the entire assessment framework as being about institutional affiliation rather than innate disposition.

---

### 2. (M) Omission of the sixth conduct type (sama-carita) — no misleading omission, but merits a note

**Location:** Lines 130-140 — "Each of the five conduct types responds to the dharma in a distinctive way"

**Issue:** The translation says "five conduct types," and the Chinese source at this point indeed uses "廣說乃至尋思行耶" ("broadly explained up to discursive-thought conduct"), abbreviating the list. However, the Sravakabhumi elsewhere (fascicle 26, fascicle 29) recognizes a sixth type: *sama-carita* (等分行, equal-part conduct / balanced disposition). The Chinese "廣說乃至" ("broadly explained up to") is a standard abbreviation formula that implicitly includes the sixth type.

The translation accurately follows the Chinese's abbreviation by listing five. However, a reader encountering section 30.5 without having read the earlier fascicles might assume the Sravakabhumi recognizes only five conduct types, which would be a doctrinal misunderstanding.

The Visuddhimagga (III.74-97) also works with six character types (raga, dosa, moha, saddha, buddhi, vitakka), which maps to the Sravakabhumi's framework with some differences.

**Recommendation:** A brief translator's note acknowledging the sixth type (equal-part conduct) and that the Chinese abbreviates here would prevent doctrinal confusion. Something like: "The Sravakabhumi recognizes six conduct types; the sixth, equal-part conduct (*sama-carita*), is omitted here by the standard abbreviation formula. See section 26.2."

**Severity:** M (moderate) — Not an error in translation, but a misleading omission for a reader approaching this section independently.

---

### 3. (m) Paracitta-jnana — the fourth investigation method needs doctrinal context

**Location:** Lines 110, 146-148 — "Investigation through knowledge of others' minds (*paracitta-jnana*)"

**Issue:** The fourth method of investigation — knowledge of others' minds — is presented straightforwardly, but this is one of the six superknowledges (*abhijna*) that requires advanced attainment. The Lamrim Chenmo (Vol. 1, note at line 8009) lists this as one of six superknowledges; Trungpa in PTOD1 does not directly address this capacity in the hinayana teacher-student context.

The translation correctly states that a teacher "accomplished in practice" who "has attained the knowledge that discerns others' minds" can use this method. This is accurate to the source. However, the text presents this method on par with the other three (questioning, discourse, observed conduct), without noting that this is an extraordinary capacity available only to highly accomplished practitioners, not a standard pedagogical tool. A practitioner reading this might wonder whether all yoga teachers are expected to have this ability.

The Chinese is clear: 善達瑜伽修瑜伽師以得知他心差別智 ("a yoga teacher accomplished in yoga who has obtained the knowledge differentiating others' minds"). The translation captures this correctly.

**Recommendation:** No change needed to the translation text itself, but a brief footnote noting that *paracitta-jnana* is one of the six *abhijna* (superknowledges) attained through advanced meditative practice would be helpful for the practitioner audience.

**Severity:** m (minor) — Accurate to source; footnote would aid reader comprehension only.

---

### 4. (m) Gotra investigation through discourse — doctrinal alignment with Yogacara vehicle theory

**Location:** Lines 124-127 — investigation of lineage through discourse

**Issue:** The passage describes the teacher expounding each vehicle's teaching in sequence (shravaka, pratyekabuddha, great vehicle) and observing which elicits the strongest response. This reflects the Yogacara doctrine of fixed *gotra* — that practitioners have an innate, determinate orientation toward one vehicle. This is a distinctive Yogacara position that differs from:

- The Madhyamaka position (as Tsongkhapa presents it in the Lamrim Chenmo) that all beings ultimately have Mahayana potential
- The tathagatagarbha doctrine (which Trungpa references in PTOD1, line 859: "we are buddha already, that we possess buddha nature")

The translation accurately presents Asanga's Yogacara framework without importing these other perspectives. This is correct — the Sravakabhumi should be translated on its own terms. No anachronism or category error.

**Recommendation:** No change. This is a note for the record: the translation correctly maintains the Yogacara framework without anachronistically importing later tathagatagarbha or Madhyamaka universalism.

**Severity:** m (minor, informational) — No action needed; confirms doctrinal fidelity.

---

### 5. (m) "Teachings of what is pure and beautiful" for greed-conduct — verify register

**Location:** Line 132 — "Those of greed-conduct (*raga-carita*), when they hear teachings of what is pure and beautiful..."

**Issue:** The Chinese reads "聞為說淨妙言論" — "hear spoken pure/sublime/excellent discourse." The word 淨妙 literally means "pure and wonderful/subtle." The translation "teachings of what is pure and beautiful" is acceptable, though "pure and beautiful" slightly shifts the register toward aesthetics. The Visuddhimagga's parallel (III.74ff.) describes the raga type as responsive to beauty and the pleasing. The Chinese 淨妙 is closer to "refined and sublime" or "pure and marvelous" than specifically "beautiful."

This is a register observation, not a doctrinal error. The greed-conduct type's responsiveness to 淨妙 discourse is doctrinally correct — those dominated by attachment respond strongly to teachings presented in an attractive, refined manner.

**Recommendation:** Consider "pure and refined discourse" or "sublime discourse" as alternatives. Not a doctrinal issue.

**Severity:** m (minor) — Translation register, not doctrinal accuracy.

## Cross-Reference Notes

### Tsongkhapa's Lamrim Chenmo (Vol. 1, Ch. 4)

Tsongkhapa's treatment of the teacher-student relationship (lines 927-1126) focuses primarily on:
- Ten qualities of the teacher (Maitreya's *Mahayana-sutralamkara* XVII.10)
- Three qualities of the student (Aryadeva's *Catuhshataka*: nonpartisan, intelligent, diligent)
- How to rely on the teacher in thought and deed

The Sravakabhumi's framework is different in kind: it describes the teacher's *active investigation* of the student, whereas the Lamrim Chenmo describes the student's qualities and attitudes. These are complementary, not contradictory. Tsongkhapa cites the Sravakabhumi directly for meditation posture (line 1403) but does not cite this specific teacher-investigation passage.

Notably, Tsongkhapa's student-quality framework (nonpartisan, intelligent, diligent) is a Madhyamaka/sutra framework, while the Sravakabhumi's four-domain investigation is a Yogacara practice-pedagogy framework. No contradiction; different purposes.

### Trungpa's PTOD1

Trungpa discusses the teacher-student relationship primarily from the student's perspective (entering the path, taking refuge, devotion). He does not present a systematic teacher-assessment framework comparable to the Sravakabhumi's four-fold investigation. His emphasis on buddha nature (line 859) and the student's own motivation to practice is consistent with the Sravakabhumi's emphasis on aspiration (*pranidhana*) as the first domain of investigation.

No contradictions with the Sravakabhumi were found. The frameworks operate at different levels: Trungpa addresses the experiential dimension of entering the path; the Sravakabhumi provides the technical-pedagogical protocol.

---

## Verdict

**No critical issues.** Two moderate findings (gotra terminology clarity, abbreviation of sixth conduct type) that merit translator's notes. Three minor observations for the record. The section is doctrinally sound and faithfully represents the Yogacara teacher-assessment framework without anachronism or category error.
