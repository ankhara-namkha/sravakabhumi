# Agent R Readability Review — Fascicle 31, Sections 31.8–31.11

**Reviewer:** Agent R (Readability)

## Overall Assessment

Sections 31.8–31.11 are substantially better than the problem areas documented in earlier fascicles. The goldsmith simile passage (31.11) and the paired-operation description (31.9) reach genuine NTC register. However, there are recurring structural problems in 31.8 and 31.11 that prevent the text from fully landing. The main defects are: (a) one-sentence encyclopedic paragraphs where the content needs to breathe, (b) translated-ese in enumeration passages, and (c) two sentences that run well over 50 words and bury their core meaning.

## Findings

### 31.8 Purification of Obstacles

---

**Issue R-1** | Severity: M | Type: Nested qualification / Translated-ese

**Location:** The *timidity obstacle* definition sentence:

> "The *timidity obstacle*: when one diligently practices renunciation and seclusion, afflicted longing, displeasure, anxious hope, and distress arise."

The colon introduces a subordinate clause structure ("when X, Y arise"), but then the passage immediately pivots to a parallel definition for *covering obstacle* without any connective tissue. The result is three definitions in a row separated only by periods, each in a different syntactic format. This is a structural assembly of Chinese-style parallel definitions, not English prose.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph-level):**

> The *timidity obstacle* arises when one diligently practices renunciation and seclusion: afflicted longing, displeasure, anxious hope, and distress surface and pull the mind away from practice. The *covering obstacle* is the five hindrances — sensual desire and the rest. The *discursive-thought obstacle* consists of afflicted discursive thoughts beginning with thoughts of sensual desire. The *self-elevation obstacle* arises from partial, inferior wisdom-views or comfortable abiding: one exalts oneself, thinking "I can attain this; others cannot."

This puts each definition in a complete, consistent form and gives *timidity* and *self-elevation* their causal structure, which is what distinguishes them from the other two.

---

**Issue R-2** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) / Nested qualification

**Location:** Causes of the covering obstacle:

> "The covering, discursive-thought, and self-elevation obstacles share a single root cause: improper attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*) habitually directed toward conditions that favor these obstacles. What is improper attention here? Not contemplating impurity, but attending to signs of purity. Not contemplating loving-kindness, but attending to signs of anger. Not contemplating the sign of brightness, but attending to the sign of darkness. Not contemplating the sign of shamatha, but attending instead to memories of relatives, homeland, past amusements and pleasures, and other such evil discursive thoughts. Not contemplating dependent origination, but conceiving 'I' and 'mine' regarding formations across the three times."

The five-part "Not X, but Y" formula repeated verbatim five times is formula monotony (Type 2). The first three iterations land cleanly. By the fourth and fifth, the reader has gone mechanical. The fourth also embeds a catalogue ("memories of relatives, homeland, past amusements and pleasures, and other such evil discursive thoughts") inside the formula, which breaks the parallel structure.

**Word count of full passage:** The five "Not X, but Y" sentences collectively run ~90 words of parallel structure — not a single >50-word sentence, but the effect is worse: five identical short sentences producing the sensation of a very long, locked-down passage.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> The covering, discursive-thought, and self-elevation obstacles share one root: improper attention (*ayonisho-manaskara*) directed toward whatever sustains them. Specifically: attending to signs of purity instead of contemplating impurity; to signs of anger instead of loving-kindness; to darkness rather than the sign of brightness; to memories of relatives, past pleasures, and distracting thought instead of the sign of shamatha; and conceiving "I" and "mine" regarding formations across the three times instead of contemplating dependent origination.

This converts five parallel short clauses into a single grammatical series — still precise, but readable as a sentence rather than as a list of mantras.

---

**Issue R-3** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) with embedded list

**Location:** The antidotes paragraph:

> "For all forms of timidity, recollection (*anusmriti*) is the general antidote — through the attention of recollection, one gladdens the mind, removing timidity already arisen and preventing its future arising. Specifically: for bodily weakness, excessive effort, and beginner status, the antidote is balanced and thorough application of effort. For insufficient effort: respectful listening and diligent questioning. For intense afflictions: the practice of appropriate objects such as impurity. For unfamiliarity with seclusion: the following reasoned reflection — 'Because in the past I was unfamiliar with seclusion, timidity now arises when I practice it. If I do not train in seclusion now, the same will be true in future lives. Therefore I should abandon my displeasure toward seclusion and cultivate delight in it.'"

Word count of final sentence: 54 words. But the larger problem is register. The passage reads as a scholastic key-value table ("For X: Y. For X: Y.") embedded in running prose. The quoted reflection at the end is vivid and alive — it is the only practitioner-voice in a passage otherwise written for a doctrinal manual.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> For all forms of timidity, recollection (*anusmriti*) is the general antidote: one gladdens the mind through remembrance, dispelling timidity that has arisen and closing the door on its future arising. The specific antidotes vary by cause. Bodily weakness, excessive effort, and beginner inexperience call for balanced, thorough effort. Insufficient effort calls for attentive listening and earnest questioning. Intense afflictions call for practice with the appropriate object — impurity contemplation, for instance. And for unfamiliarity with seclusion, one reasons: "Past unfamiliarity brought today's timidity. If I do not train now, the same will return in future lives. I should therefore release my displeasure and cultivate delight in seclusion."

This separates the structural mapping from the vivid reflective voice, giving each its own rhythm.

---

**Issue R-4** | Severity: m | Type: Broken logic / Translated-ese

**Location:** Final paragraph of 31.8:

> "Furthermore, one knows that obstacles by nature are obstructing, defiling, and belong to the dark category — they should be abandoned. To abandon them, one must know their causes. To generate urgency in abandoning them, one must know their dangers. Having abandoned them, one reflects: 'How can I prevent these obstacles from arising in future lives?' — and therefore seeks to cultivate antidotes. Through these four causes, one purifies the mind of obstacles."

The logic sequence is: nature -> know causes (to abandon) -> know dangers (urgency to abandon) -> cultivate antidotes (to prevent future arising). This is a retrospective summary of the four-part structure. But "having abandoned them, one reflects" is jarring: it suggests the obstacles have already been eliminated before one cultivates antidotes, which inverts the actual sequence (you cultivate antidotes *in order to* abandon). The sentence is a translation artifact; the Chinese likely means "reflecting on how to prevent future arising, one cultivates antidotes."

**Targeted fix:**

> "Having understood the dangers, one reflects: 'How can I prevent these obstacles from arising in future lives?' — and therefore applies antidotes."

### 31.9 Shamatha and Vipashyana: Mutual Growth, Completion Criteria, and Paired Operation

---

**Issue R-5** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) / Nested qualification

**Location:** The transformation-of-the-basis sentence:

> "As the practitioner's vipashyana grows clearer and more powerful through sustained cultivation, it generates ever-greater physical and mental pliancy. The shamatha aspect grows and expands accordingly. As pliancy increases, one-pointed attention toward the object grows in turn; as one-pointedness grows, pliancy increases yet further. Mental one-pointedness and physical-mental pliancy depend upon each other, each sustaining the other. They are perfected only when transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravrtti*) is attained. Through this transformation, direct perceptual wisdom regarding the knowable objects arises."

This passage is actually clean — no single sentence exceeds 50 words, and the mutually-sustaining rhythm is handled well. This is good prose. (No change needed.)

---

**Issue R-6** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words)

**Location:** Completion criteria paragraph for impurity:

> "In brief: through the proper cultivation of the contemplation of impurity — cultivating it closely, extensively, and repeatedly — there comes a point where, whether walking or standing, with various objects present and their many aspects observed, the aspect of impurity naturally predominates without deliberate effort, rather than aspects of purity."

Word count: 57 words. The "whether walking or standing, with various objects present and their many aspects observed" insertion breaks the sentence's momentum at exactly the wrong moment (just before the payoff: "the aspect of impurity naturally predominates").

**Targeted fix:**

> "In brief: through close, extensive, and repeated cultivation of the contemplation of impurity, there comes a point where the aspect of impurity naturally predominates without deliberate effort — whether one is walking or standing, whatever objects are present."

Moves the qualifications after the payoff clause.

---

**Issue R-7** | Severity: m | Type: Formula monotony

**Location:** The five parallel completion-criteria summaries:

> "The same principle applies to the remaining four contemplations, each with its distinctive signs: for loving-kindness, the aspect of a loving mind predominantly manifests, not anger...For dependent origination, the aspects of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and no-self predominate, not the aspects of permanence, pleasure, and the delusion accompanying self-view. For element differentiation, the perception of varied, multiple element-natures...predominates, not the perception of a unified body. For mindfulness of breathing, inner tranquility and shamatha predominate, not conceptual proliferation."

Four "X predominates, not Y" clauses in immediate succession. This is less severe than R-2 because the content genuinely varies, and the passage is brief. But the repeated "not Y" ending becomes predictable by the third iteration.

**Suggested variation (light touch):**

> "The same principle applies to the remaining four contemplations...For loving-kindness: the aspect of a loving mind predominates, displacing anger. For dependent origination: impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and no-self come to the fore, dissolving the aspects of permanence, pleasure, and self-view. For element differentiation: one perceives varied, multiple element-natures and a differentiated body, no longer a unified whole. For mindfulness of breathing: inner tranquility and shamatha predominate, leaving conceptual proliferation behind."

Minor variations ("displacing," "come to the fore," "no longer a unified whole," "leaving...behind") break the mechanical "not Y" ending without altering the meaning.

---

**Issue R-8** | Severity: m | Type: Register drift (one phrase)

**Location:** Paired operation paragraph:

> "Vipashyana becomes pure and luminous, pliantly gathered within shamatha's embrace, flowing as if on shamatha's own path."

This is the best sentence in 31.9 — genuinely alive, practitioner-facing, precise. (No change; flagging as exemplary.)

### 31.10 Intermediate Summary Verse

No issues. The verse is well-rendered: rhythmically consistent, terminologically accurate, and appropriately compact. The three stanzas scan as a genuine verse form rather than prose broken into lines. No changes recommended.

### 31.11 Cultivation of Attention: Four Types, Objects of Practice, and the Goldsmith Simile

---

**Issue R-9** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) / Nested qualification

**Location:** Definition of the third attention type:

> "(3) *Attention that generates pliancy* — through this attention, at intervals one turns the mind from disagreeable dharmas, at intervals delights it in admirable dharmas, and then abides inwardly in signless, non-conceptual serenity with one-pointed mindfulness. Through this, all physical and mental heaviness is counteracted, ease arises, and pliancy is generated throughout body and mind."

Word count of first sentence: 52 words. The "at intervals...at intervals...and then" tripartite structure is fine in principle, but by the time the reader reaches "abides inwardly in signless, non-conceptual serenity with one-pointed mindfulness," four attributes have stacked in a row. The sentence lists what the attention does rather than letting the reader feel it.

**Targeted fix:**

> "(3) *Attention that generates pliancy* — one alternates: at intervals turning the mind from disagreeable dharmas, at intervals brightening it with admirable ones, and then settling inwardly in signless, one-pointed serenity. All physical and mental heaviness falls away; ease and pliancy pervade body and mind."

---

**Issue R-10** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words)

**Location:** Definition of the fourth attention type:

> "(4) *Attention that purifies wisdom-vision* — through this attention, at intervals, using internal mental serenity as a basis, one repeatedly applies effort to cultivate higher wisdom through dharma-investigation."

Word count: 35 words — under the limit. But the phrase "using internal mental serenity as a basis, one repeatedly applies effort to cultivate higher wisdom through dharma-investigation" is bureaucratic prose, not practitioner-facing language. The four words "applies effort to cultivate" are especially flat.

**Targeted fix:**

> "(4) *Attention that purifies wisdom-vision* — at intervals, settling into inner serenity, one turns attention toward dharma-investigation, cultivating higher wisdom from that collected ground."

---

**Issue R-11** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) with register drift

**Location:** Third object of delight, "confidence in attainments":

> "One reflects: 'I have power and capacity. My discipline is pure. I am a fit vessel for the dharma. I share pure discipline with my companions and share views with the wise and virtuous. I am capable of diligently practicing this right conduct. In this very life, I can attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized.' Through this, great joy arises. Furthermore, by recollecting attainments already established through past courageous diligence, one generates confidence and delight regarding future attainments."

The first-person reflection is vivid and good ("I can attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized" is excellent). But "Furthermore, by recollecting attainments already established through past courageous diligence, one generates confidence and delight regarding future attainments" is abstract summarizing prose that follows a vivid voice-passage, flattening what it should deepen.

Word count of final sentence: 28 words — under the limit. Issue is register, not length.

**Targeted fix:**

> Remove or integrate: "Furthermore, by recollecting attainments already established through past courageous diligence, one generates confidence and delight regarding future attainments."

Could become: "Recollecting what past diligence has already established, one sees clearly: more awaits, and it is within reach." This keeps the arc without reverting to abstract-nominal mode.

---

**Issue R-12** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words) / Translated-ese

**Location:** Integration paragraph:

> "It turns from dark through the disciplining attention. It turns toward bright through the moistening attention. At intervals, it is gathered inward through shamatha by the pliancy-generating attention. At intervals, it discerns, scrutinizes, investigates, and examines dharmas through the wisdom-purifying attention. In this way, shamatha and vipashyana periodically sustain the mind, which becomes capable of embracing all aspects and all qualities as causes for further cultivation. Through days and nights, moment by moment, one achieves progressive advancement."

This passage reprises the four-attention structure using a new "It turns from / It turns toward / At intervals, X / At intervals, Y" formula. The four-clause structure is parallel Chinese enumeration translated directly into English clause-by-clause. By the fourth clause, the reader is counting items rather than following meaning.

The final sentence, "Through days and nights, moment by moment, one achieves progressive advancement," is the weakest line in the whole section: it is entirely abstract, ends on the bureaucratic noun "advancement," and gives the practitioner nothing to hold.

**Paragraph-level rewrite:**

> The mind turns from dark through disciplining attention, and toward bright through moistening attention. At intervals the pliancy-generating attention draws everything inward to shamatha's stillness; at intervals the wisdom-purifying attention spreads outward through dharma-investigation. Sustained by both movements, the mind grows capable of embracing whatever aspect is needed. Day and night, breath by breath, the practice deepens.

---

**Issue R-13** | Severity: M | Type: Long sentence (>50 words)

**Location:** The goldsmith application paragraph:

> "Just so, the diligent yogin — to make the mind shed the impurities of desire and all afflicted distress — generates deep disenchantment with repulsive dharmas. To direct the mind toward all that is pure, wholesome, and joyful, one generates delight in admirable dharmas. Then the practitioner, wishing to establish in whatever aspect — whether shamatha or vipashyana — can readily engage with each, skillfully unite with each, without wavering or disturbance. Whatever purpose one sets the mind toward, one accomplishes in accord with one's resolute confidence."

The third sentence is grammatically awkward: "Then the practitioner, wishing to establish in whatever aspect — whether shamatha or vipashyana — can readily engage with each." The phrase "wishing to establish in whatever aspect" lacks an object ("establish [the mind] in whatever aspect" is the implied meaning). The em-dash interruption further obscures the main verb.

Word count of third sentence: 38 words — under the limit. Issue is the dropped object and the awkward em-dash.

**Targeted fix:**

> "Then the practitioner, having cultivated both, can readily settle the mind in either shamatha or vipashyana — skillfully uniting with each, without wavering or disturbance."

---

**Issue R-14** | Severity: m | Type: Unexplained jargon (contextual)

**Location:** 31.8, first sentence:

> "Yogachara practitioners who properly cultivate the approaches purify their minds of obstacles through four causes..."

The spelling "Yogachara" here (with a "c") differs from the project convention "Yogacara" used elsewhere. This is inconsistent romanization, not a readability issue per se — flagging for the terminology agent. (Minor.)
