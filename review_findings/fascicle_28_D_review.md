## 1. Three Superior Trainings (Section 28.1)

### Finding D-1: Accurate presentation of the three trainings [PASS]

The translation presents the three trainings (adhishila, adhicitta, adhiprajna) accurately per Yogacara tradition. Cross-referencing with Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise* Vol. 1 (Chapter 23-24), the enumeration, sequence rationale, and mutual relationship are all consistent:

- **Enumeration:** shila -> samadhi -> prajna. Matches standard scheme across traditions (Tsongkhapa GT1, Ch. 24: "Through ethical discipline, concentration, and wisdom, Achieve nirvana"; Trungpa PTOD1: "All saddharma are shila, samadhi, and prajna").
- **Sequential causation:** shila -> no remorse -> gladness -> ease -> samadhi -> knowledge-and-vision -> disenchantment -> dispassion -> liberation -> nirvana. This graduated chain matches AN 11.1-2 exactly. PASS.
- **Reason for three (not more, not fewer):** establishing concentration, providing basis for knowledge, accomplishing the purpose. This matches the Yogacarabhumi's own framework as cited by Tsongkhapa (GT1: "The three trainings bring to completion all the tasks of yogis and yoginis... makes a distracted mind undistracted... balances an unbalanced mind... liberates an unliberated mind"). PASS.
- **"Superior" (adhika) meaning:** two senses — (1) what they lead to (each leads to the next), (2) unique to the Buddhist teaching (not shared with tirthikas). PASS.

### Finding D-2: Prajna-first path presentation [PASS]

The passage about a noble disciple who attains the path of seeing without first developing root dhyanas, then cultivating the seven factors of awakening — this correctly describes the "dry insight" (shushka-vipashyanaka) practitioner. The footnote [^prajna_first] accurately identifies this as a recognized Sarvastivada/Yogacara position.

Cross-reference: The Visuddhimagga (XXI.75-78) distinguishes seven types of noble persons by which faculty predominates (faith, concentration, or understanding), confirming that insight-first approaches are doctrinally attested. PASS.

### Finding D-3: Three persons and fruits [PASS]

The mapping of three types of persons (not-yet-abandoned-desire, partially-abandoned, fully-abandoned) to the three fruits (stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning) is standard Sarvastivada Abhidharma. The footnote [^three_fruit_types] correctly identifies this scheme and references Abhidharmakosha VI. PASS.

## 3. Three Liberation Gates (Section 28.3)

### Finding D-5: Liberation gates — Yogacara-specific ordering differs from Theravada; translation is accurate to the source but footnote cross-reference is potentially misleading [m — minor]

**Issue:** The footnote [^three_gates] says: "This threefold framework is widely attested (cf. Visuddhimagga XXI; Abhidharmakosha VII)." While the *framework itself* is indeed widely attested, the specific assignment of the three gates differs between the Sravakabhumi and the Visuddhimagga:

**Sravakabhumi (Yogacara) presentation:**
1. Faults of conditioned things -> no yearning -> **wishlessness** (apranihita)
2. Yearning for nirvana -> perceiving nirvana as free from signs -> **signlessness** (animitta)
3. Non-existent self/being -> known as non-existent -> **emptiness** (shunyata)

**Visuddhimagga (Theravada) presentation (XXI.70):**
1. Impermanence -> **signless** liberation
2. Pain/suffering -> **desireless** liberation
3. Not-self -> **void** liberation

The Sravakabhumi's scheme is distinctly Yogacara/Sarvastivada: it links signlessness to nirvana (the unconditioned, free from signs of conditioned existence), while the Visuddhimagga links signlessness to impermanence (formations without the sign of permanence). The translation accurately renders the Chinese source. The footnote's doctrinal explanation is correct for the Yogacara position. However, the parenthetical "cf. Visuddhimagga XXI; Abhidharmakosha VII" may lead a reader to expect identical treatment, when in fact the correspondences differ between traditions.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief note in the footnote acknowledging that the Theravada tradition (Visuddhimagga) assigns the three contemplations (impermanence, suffering, non-self) to the three gates differently, to avoid false impression of uniformity.

**Severity:** m (minor) — the translation itself is accurate; this concerns only a footnote's cross-reference precision.

### Finding D-6: Signlessness linked to nirvana — doctrinally correct for Yogacara [PASS]

The footnote [^signlessness_nirvana] explains that "signs" (nimitta) are the marks of conditioned existence (arising, persisting, ceasing), and nirvana is free from these. This is doctrinally accurate for the Yogacara/Sarvastivada position. The Sravakabhumi's source text (永出離 = "permanent emergence") linked to the signless gate is correctly rendered. PASS.

### Finding D-7: Emptiness gate linked to the non-existent (self) — accurate [PASS]

The text says "as for what is spoken of as a self, or as a sentient being, a life-force, a being born, and so forth — this is called 'the non-existent.'" Emptiness here is about the non-existence of self and person, not about the emptiness of all dharmas (which would be the Madhyamaka reading). This is accurately Yogacara/Sravaka-level emptiness. PASS.

## 4. Thirty-Seven Bodhipakshya-Dharmas (Section 28.10)

### Finding D-8: All seven groups correctly enumerated [PASS]

Verified against the Chinese source:
- Four foundations of mindfulness (smrityupasthana): body, feeling, mind, dharmas. CORRECT.
- Four right efforts (samyak-pradhana): abandon arisen evil, prevent unarisen evil, generate unarisen good, maintain arisen good. CORRECT.
- Four bases of supernatural power (riddhipada): aspiration (chanda), diligence (virya), mind (citta), investigation (mimamsa). CORRECT.
- Five faculties (indriya): faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, prajna. CORRECT.
- Five powers (bala): same five. CORRECT.
- Seven factors of awakening (bodhyanga): mindfulness, investigation-of-dharmas, diligence, joy, ease, concentration, equanimity. CORRECT.
- Eightfold noble path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration. CORRECT.

Total: 4+4+4+5+5+7+8 = 37. PASS.

### Finding D-9: Fourth riddhipada — translation of 觀 as "investigation" [m — minor]

**Issue:** The Chinese 觀三摩地 renders the fourth basis of supernatural power. The Sanskrit is *mimamsa-samadhi* (samadhi of investigation/inquiry). The translation renders 觀 as "investigation" which is acceptable, though 觀 more literally means "observation" or "contemplation." The Sanskrit *mimamsa* is correctly supplied in the parenthetical. This is a minor terminological question, not a doctrinal error.

**Severity:** m (minor) — the Sanskrit parenthetical resolves any ambiguity.

### Finding D-11: Twenty-one feelings correctly counted [PASS]

Verified against Chinese: 7 groups of 3 (basic hedonic tone; bodily; mental; with relish; without relish; based on indulgence; based on renunciation) = 21. The text says 如是總有二十一受或九種受. Translation correctly renders "altogether twenty-one kinds of feeling, or nine kinds of feeling." PASS.

### Finding D-12: Nine feelings — footnote hedges appropriately [m — minor]

**Issue:** The footnote [^twenty_one_feelings] says: "The 'nine kinds' likely refers to one selected threefold classification applied to the three hedonic tones (cf. MN 137, Salayatana-vibhanga Sutta, which enumerates nine feelings by combining the three tones with the three bases of indulgence, renunciation, and equanimity)."

This is indeed the most standard identification: pleasant/painful/neutral x indulgence/renunciation/equanimity = 9. However, the word "likely" introduces unnecessary uncertainty. The MN 137 identification is well-established in commentarial literature and Abhidharma. Alternatively, the nine could be: basic 3 + bodily 3 + mental 3. Both readings are defensible, but the MN 137 reading is more standard.

**Recommendation:** Consider strengthening the footnote wording from "likely refers to" to "refers to" with a note that alternative counting methods exist.

**Severity:** m (minor) — interpretive hedging, not doctrinal error.

### Finding D-13: Twenty minds correctly counted and subdivided [PASS]

Verified against Chinese: 10 pairs = 20. The subdivision into 6 "active state" (行時, walking/daily activity) and 14 "meditative state" (住時, dwelling/seated) is correctly identified in both text and footnote. The further subdivision of the 14 into 8 (purifying hindrances) and 6 (purifying afflictions) is also correct. PASS.

### Finding D-14: Footnote comparison to Satipatthana Sutta's 16 minds [m — minor]

**Issue:** Footnote [^twenty_minds] says "This classification parallels the Satipatthana Sutta's sixteen types of mind (cf. MN 10) but with Yogacara-specific additions and organization." The standard Satipatthana Sutta lists 16 types of mind in 8 pairs:
1. with lust / without lust
2. with hate / without hate
3. with delusion / without delusion
4. contracted / distracted
5. exalted / unexalted
6. surpassable / unsurpassable
7. concentrated / unconcentrated
8. liberated / unliberated

The Sravakabhumi has 20 types in 10 pairs. The additional pairs (tranquil/non-tranquil, well-cultivated/uncultivated) and the reorganization of some pairs (sunken/uplifted and agitated/non-agitated vs. exalted/unexalted and surpassable/unsurpassable) are genuine Yogacara developments. The footnote's characterization is accurate. PASS.

### Finding D-15: Four inversions (catur-viparyasa) mapping to four foundations [PASS — with one note]

The translation presents:
- Body contemplation counteracts perceiving purity in the impure
- Feeling contemplation counteracts perceiving pleasure in suffering
- Mind contemplation counteracts perceiving permanence in the impermanent
- Dharma contemplation counteracts perceiving self in the non-self

Cross-referencing with Analayo's *Satipatthana* (p. 459 in the reference text): "The Nettippakarana and the Visuddhimagga also set the four satipatthanas in opposition to the four distortions (vipallasas), which are to 'mis-take' what is unattractive, unsatisfactory, impermanent, and not-self, for being attractive, satisfactory, permanent, and a self."

The mapping is:
- Analayo/Visuddhimagga: body -> unattractive (= impure); feeling -> unsatisfactory (= suffering); mind -> impermanent; dharmas -> not-self.
- Sravakabhumi: body -> impure/pure; feeling -> suffering/pleasure; mind -> impermanent/permanent; dharmas -> non-self/self.

These are the same correspondences. PASS.

**Note:** The translation's mention of "four charnel ground paths" (應四憺怕路) for the body-impurity contemplation is consistent with the term established in the glossary and in Fascicle 26.

### Finding D-16: "World-Honored One" used instead of glossary-mandated "Blessed One" [m — minor, terminology]

**Issue:** The translation uses "World-Honored One" (line 581, 583) for 世尊 (bhagavat). The Master Glossary explicitly specifies: "Blessed One | NOT 'World-Honored One' (Chinese-register calque)."

**Location:** Lines 581 and 583 of the translation.

**Recommendation:** Replace "World-Honored One" with "Blessed One" in both occurrences.

**Severity:** m (minor) — terminology watchlist violation, not doctrinal error.

### Finding D-20: Footnote on samyaktva-niyama [PASS]

### Finding D-21: Footnote on three types of smrityupasthana [PASS]

Footnote [^three_smrityupasthana] correctly identifies the threefold analysis (object-based, intrinsic-nature, associated) as a "distinctively Abhidharma contribution, found in the Abhidharmakosha (VI.14-15)." The distinction between hearing/thinking (outflows only) and meditation (with or without outflows) is accurate. PASS.

### Finding D-22: Footnote on "four yogas" total of 16 sub-types [m — minor]

**Issue:** Footnote [^four_yogas] says: "Together they total sixteen sub-types (faith: 2 aspects + 2 bases = 4; aspiration: 4 types; diligence: 4 types; method: 4 implicit stages = 4)." The Chinese text (此四瑜伽有十六種) confirms 16 types total. However, the footnote's count for faith is questionable: faith has 2 aspects and 2 bases, but it is unclear whether these constitute 4 "types" in the same sense as the other three yogas. The text's own structure gives: faith (2+2 = presented as 2 aspects + 2 bases, not clearly 4 separate types), aspiration (4 explicitly listed), diligence (4 explicitly listed), method (4 implicit stages). The footnote's interpretation that faith = 4 sub-types achieves the correct total of 16, but the faith sub-types are aspects and bases, not parallel "kinds" like the other three. This is a minor interpretive question.

**Severity:** m (minor) — the total is correct; the internal breakdown is a legitimate but debatable interpretation.

## 7. Additional Doctrinal Checks

### Finding D-23: The gradual path chain (shila -> no remorse -> ... -> nirvana) [PASS]

The graduated chain in section 28.1 is one of the most widely attested path formulations in early Buddhist literature. Verified against AN 11.1-2 (Kimatthiya Sutta) as cited in the footnote [^gradual_path]. The chain matches: ethical purity -> freedom from remorse -> gladness -> ease -> right concentration -> knowledge-and-vision -> disenchantment -> dispassion -> liberation -> nirvana. PASS.

### Finding D-24: First dhyana description [PASS]

Section 28.1: "separated from desires and from unwholesome dharmas, possessing examination (vitarka) and analysis (vicara), entering and fully abiding in the first dhyana with its joy and ease born of separation." This matches the standard first dhyana formula found across all Buddhist schools. PASS.

### Finding D-25: Formless attainments included under adhicitta [PASS]

The text correctly includes formless attainments under the training of mind while noting that the dhyanas are "supreme" because they support entry into the noble path. This is standard Yogacara/Sarvastivada doctrine. The Visuddhimagga similarly treats the four form jhanas as the principal supports for vipassana, with the formless as supplementary. PASS.

### Finding D-26: Six interpretive frameworks for internal/external/internal-and-external [PASS]

The six frameworks for interpreting "internal," "external," and "internal-and-external" contemplation are a distinctively Yogacara-Abhidharma scholastic contribution. The translation correctly presents all six without omission:
1. Self-sentient vs. non-sentient vs. other-sentient
2. Sense-faculty form vs. non-faculty form
3. Concentrated/pliant vs. unconcentrated/coarse
4. Great elements vs. derived form
5. Body with consciousness (living) vs. body without consciousness (corpse)
6. Own inner body vs. others' inner body, with surface bodies

Each is correctly extended to feeling, mind, and dharma contemplation. PASS.

### Finding D-27: Four rationales for exactly four foundations [PASS]

The text presents four rationales:
1. Four inversions (impure/pure, suffering/pleasure, impermanent/permanent, non-self/self)
2. Four types of ignorance regarding self (self depends on body; self experiences feeling; mind/consciousness mistaken for self; dharmas enable self's defilement/purification)
3. Four dimensions of karma (body as basis, feeling as purpose, mind as agent, dharmas as means)
4. Four dimensions of defilement/purification (same four-fold pattern)

All four are accurately translated and doctrinally sound. PASS.

### Finding D-28: Adhicitta training — claim of exclusivity to Buddhist teaching [PASS]

The text says the three trainings "exist exclusively within the noble teaching and are not shared with non-Buddhist paths." Footnote [^exclusive_teaching] correctly notes this is "a standard Yogacara position" and explains the qualifier "superior" (adhika) as marking the orientation toward the four noble truths and complete severance of afflictions. This is accurate: non-Buddhists may cultivate shila and samadhi, but these are not "superior" (adhika) because they are not directed toward nirvana. PASS.
