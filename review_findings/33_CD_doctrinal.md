# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 33, Sections 33.6-33.9

**Reviewer:** Agent D (Doctrinal)
**Sections:** 33.6 (Remaining six attentions), 33.7 (Functional summary and four-category classification), 33.8 (Extension to higher attainments, second dhyana), 33.9 (Universal coarse aspects)
**Cross-references:** Chinese source T30.0465b27-0467b21; Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3 (Tsongkhapa, LRCM 555-561); Visuddhimagga IV.79-94; Abhidharmasamuccaya; MASTER_GLOSSARY.md

## Summary

Total findings: 10 (0 Critical, 2 Major, 5 minor, 3 Observation)

The doctrinal framework is handled with care overall. The seven-attention system, the suppression/eradication distinction, and the vitarka/vicara analysis are all doctrinally sound. Two major issues concern the three-grade affliction scheme and an ambiguity in the four-category classification. Several minor points and observations are noted below.

---

## Findings

### 1. [M] Three grades vs. nine grades of affliction (33.7, line 180, fn at line 184)

**Issue:** The translation and footnote [^affliction_grades] present the worldly path as severing "three grades" of affliction: upper, middle, and lower. This is accurate to the Chinese (上品, 中品, 下品). However, the standard Abhidharma/Yogacara scheme divides each ground's afflictions into **nine grades** (three main grades each subdivided into three: upper-upper, upper-middle, upper-lower, etc.), and the worldly path severs them in nine progressive stages using an uninterrupted path (anantarya-marga) and path of liberation (vimukti-marga) for each grade. The Sravakabhumi itself uses the three-grade scheme here as a simplification (the seven attentions operate at the level of three grades, not nine), but the footnote should acknowledge the relationship to the fuller nine-grade scheme. As written, a scholar could mistake this for a contradiction of the Abhidharmakosha's nine-grade system.

**Recommendation:** Add a sentence to footnote [^affliction_grades] noting that the standard Abhidharma scheme further subdivides into nine grades (each of the three grades split into three sub-grades), and that Asanga's presentation here collapses the nine into three for the purpose of mapping to the seven attentions. The third attention severs the upper three of nine, the fourth the middle three, the sixth the lower three. This is not a doctrinal error in the translation itself but a misleading omission (category 4) in the apparatus.

### 2. [M] Four-category classification: text says "six types" but lists only four (33.7, line 182)

**Issue:** The translation reads: "These four types of attention should be understood as subsumed within the framework of six types of attention: the conforming attention, the counteracting attention, the purity-conforming attention, and the investigation-conforming attention." This lists four types but says "six types." The Chinese (如是其餘四種作意當知攝入六作意中。謂隨順作意。對治作意。順清淨作意。順觀察作意) also says "six types of attention" (六作意中) but then names only four categories.

This is because the source text is saying these *four remaining types* of attention (from the seven) are subsumed into a framework of *six types* of attention. The "six types" (六作意) is a broader taxonomic framework in the Yogacarabhumi; the four named here are four of those six. The other two types in the full six-type framework are not named because they are not directly relevant at this point. The translation follows the Chinese faithfully, but the result is confusing: the reader encounters "six types of attention" followed by a list of only four.

**Recommendation:** The footnote [^four_categories] partially addresses this but says "four broader categories." It should instead explain that the Yogacarabhumi employs a taxonomy of six types of attention (六作意), of which four are relevant here. The other two types (which are not enumerated in this passage) complete the full sixfold scheme found elsewhere in the Yogacarabhumi. This prevents reader confusion about the arithmetic.

### 3. [m] "Disgust and destruction" for 厭壞對治 (33.7, line 182)

**Issue:** The translation renders 厭壞對治 as "the counteractive measure of disgust and destruction." The term 厭壞 in this Yogacara context refers to the counteractive measure that operates through making the practitioner feel revulsion/aversion toward the lower ground (厭 = aversion/disgust) and through mentally "breaking down" or deconstructing the attractiveness of the lower ground (壞 = destroying the appeal). The rendering "disgust and destruction" is technically defensible but could mislead a reader into thinking there is a destructive or aggressive quality. In Yogacara technical usage, 厭壞對治 is standardly the counteractive measure that works by rendering the object unattractive through analysis -- closer to "disenchantment and dismantling" or "revulsion and dismantling."

**Recommendation:** Consider "disenchantment and dismantling" or at minimum add a brief note clarifying that "destruction" refers to dismantling the attractiveness of the lower ground through analytical contemplation, not literal destruction.

### 4. [m] "Dark category, attending upon desires" in vitarka/vicara context (33.8, line 198)

**Issue:** The translation reads: "Therefore they belong to the dark category, attending upon desires." The Chinese is 是故皆是黑品所攝隨逐諸欲. The phrase 隨逐諸欲 means "following after / pursuing desires." In context, vitarka and vicara in the first dhyana are being analyzed from the perspective of one seeking the second dhyana. The statement that they "belong to the dark category" (黑品所攝) and "attend upon desires" is the coarse-peaceful analysis applied to the first dhyana, where from the second dhyana's perspective, the first dhyana's factors are treated as analogous to the desire realm's coarseness.

This is doctrinally delicate. Vitarka and vicara are wholesome dharmas within the first dhyana -- they are explicitly factors (branches) of the first dhyana itself. The text's claim that they "belong to the dark category" and "attend upon desires" is a *perspectival* judgment from the standpoint of seeking the second dhyana, not an absolute categorization. The translation renders this correctly from the Chinese, but could mislead a reader into thinking vitarka and vicara are inherently unwholesome. The footnote [^second_dhyana_coarse] partially addresses this by noting they "become obstacles to the deeper stillness of the second dhyana," but does not flag the perspectival nature of the "dark category" assignment.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief note (either in text or footnote) clarifying that this assignment to the "dark category" is relative and perspectival -- part of the coarse-peaceful analysis method -- not an absolute classification of vitarka and vicara as unwholesome. The first dhyana's own description (33.6, line 162) correctly presents them as branches of the dhyana, confirming the perspectival usage.

### 5. [m] Footnote [^temporary_suppression] (line 170): samuccheda-prahana Sanskrit

**Issue:** The footnote states: "Only the supramundane path -- through insight into the four noble truths -- can permanently destroy (*samuccheda-prahana*) the seeds of affliction." The term *samuccheda-prahana* means "abandonment by cutting off" or "eradication-abandonment." This is standard and correct. However, the Yogacara tradition distinguishes multiple types of prahana. The worldly path achieves *vikkhambhana-prahana* (suppression-abandonment) or in Yogacara terms what Asanga calls 暫時伏斷方便 (temporary suppression method). Providing the Sanskrit for the worldly path type (*vikkhambhana-prahana* or the Yogacara equivalent *pratipaksha-prahana*) alongside the supramundane type would make the footnote more complete.

**Recommendation:** Add the Sanskrit for temporary suppression alongside *samuccheda-prahana* to make the contrast fully parallel.

### 6. [m] Progressive joy stages: attribution numbers (33.6, fn line 172)

**Issue:** Footnote [^progressive_joy] says: "faint and intermittent during attentions 3-4." The attentions of separation and joyful engagement are attentions #3 and #4 in the seven-attention sequence. But the Chinese (又於遠離攝樂作意現在轉時) actually says "during the attentions of separation and joyful engagement" -- which the main text (line 160) correctly renders. The footnote's use of "attentions 3-4" is accurate numerically but introduces an ambiguity: the reader might count from the beginning and arrive at different numbers depending on whether they count from 0 or 1.

**Recommendation:** Use the names rather than numbers in the footnote for clarity: "faint and intermittent during the attentions of separation and joyful engagement (attentions 3-4)."

### 7. [m] "Internally generated yet classified among external bases" (33.8, line 196)

**Issue:** The translation states vitarka and vicara are "internally generated yet classified among external bases." The Chinese: 依內而生外處所攝. This is a subtle Yogacara Abhidharma point: vitarka and vicara arise internally (依內而生) but are "included in external bases" (外處所攝) -- meaning that although they are mental factors (*caitta*), their objects (*vishaya*) are categorized among the external sense-fields. This is a technical Yogacara classification point about the twelve ayatanas. The translation is accurate but compressed. Without context, a reader unfamiliar with the ayatana framework may find this incomprehensible.

**Recommendation:** This is a minor point best addressed through the footnote [^second_dhyana_coarse], which could note that the ayatana classification places the objects of vitarka and vicara under external bases. However, since the footnote is already substantial, this could also be left as-is with the understanding that scholars will recognize the reference.

### 8. [O] Suppression vs. eradication: well handled (33.6, line 156 and fn line 170)

**Observation:** The translation and footnote clearly articulate the fundamental Yogacara distinction between the worldly path's temporary suppression and the supramundane path's permanent eradication of affliction-seeds. This is doctrinally critical and is handled well. The Chinese 暫時伏斷方便非是畢竟永害種子 is rendered faithfully. Tsongkhapa's Lamrim Chenmo (LRCM 561) confirms exactly this point: "for the time being they are not activated in the slightest degree. But you have not completely destroyed the seeds of those afflictions." The translation is in perfect alignment with both source and tradition.

---

### 9. [O] Seven attentions applied universally (33.8-33.9)

**Observation:** The extension of the seven-attention framework to all eight attainments (four dhyanas plus four formless) and the identification of two universal coarse aspects (painful abiding and shorter lifespan) are doctrinally sound. Tsongkhapa confirms (LRCM 555) that "by this means you are freed from attachment to states up to Nothingness," i.e., the worldly path extends through seven transitions, each with the seven attentions. The Sravakabhumi's teaching that the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception cannot be transcended by this method (because there is no higher worldly ground) is the standard Yogacara position, implicitly present in 33.9's statement that coarse aspects extend "from the desire realm up through the sphere of nothingness" -- correctly omitting the peak of existence.

---

### 10. [O] Vitarka/vicara definitions match Yogacara standard (33.8, line 196, fn line 204)

**Observation:** The definitions of vitarka as "initial, sudden engagement with an object -- a coarse mental verbalization" and vicara as "subsequent, gradual traversal of the object -- a subtle mental verbalization" align precisely with standard Yogacara Abhidharma. The Chinese 麁意言性 / 細意言性 (coarse mental verbalization / subtle mental verbalization) corresponds to the Abhidharmasamuccaya's characterization. The Visuddhimagga (IV.88) gives a parallel Theravada account: vitakka is "the act of directing the mind onto the object" and vicara is "continued pressure on the object." While the terminological register differs (Theravada "applied thought" and "sustained thought" vs. Yogacara "examination" and "analysis"), the underlying phenomenology is consistent. The footnote's citation of Visuddhimagga IV.88-94 is appropriate and accurate.
