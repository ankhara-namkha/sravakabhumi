## Findings

### Attribution Accuracy

[Severity: m] [All fascicles, header] — Attribution is handled consistently and correctly across all eight fascicles. Each opens with "Composed by Asanga" with a footnote: "The Chinese text attributes this work to Maitreya Bodhisattva. Modern scholarly consensus attributes the Sravakabhumi to Asanga. See Deleanu." The introduction (00_introduction.md) likewise states: "Modern scholarly consensus attributes the Sravakabhumi to Asanga (c. late 4th-early 5th century CE), though the Chinese text follows the traditional attribution to Maitreya Bodhisattva." This is accurate, balanced, and consistent. No finding to act on.

### Doctrinal Accuracy

[Severity: M] [Fascicle 28, section 28.7] — The four types of attention in section 28.7 (effortful, intermittent, uninterrupted, effortless) are presented with the "attention to characteristics" (lakshana-pratisanvedana-manaskara) as a reference point. The footnote [^attention_to_characteristics] correctly identifies this as the first of the seven attentions detailed in fascicle 33. However, the body text of 28.7 states that "intermittent operating attention" spans "from after the attention to characteristics through to the attention of completed application" — this is doctrinally compressed. The seven attentions in fascicle 33 present a more nuanced mapping. The existing footnote adequately covers the cross-reference, but the body text could be read as equating these two different frameworks (the four effort-based attentions of 28.7 vs. the seven attentions of 33.3). A reader without access to fascicle 33 might conflate them. **Recommendation:** This is adequately handled by the footnote; no action needed unless a reader reports confusion.

[Severity: M] [Fascicle 28, section 28.1, footnote ^dhyana_requirement] — The footnote states: "The claim that direct realization of the truths requires dhyana as its basis is the normative Yogacara position. Other Buddhist schools, however, allow for stream-entry without prior dhyana attainment." This is accurate but somewhat simplified. The Sravakabhumi itself, in the very same section (28.1), explicitly acknowledges the "dry-insight" practitioner who enters the path of seeing without root dhyanas (relying on access concentration). The footnote [^prajna_first] covers this. The two footnotes together provide adequate coverage, but the reader must find both. No critical gap, but the proximity of two footnotes giving apparently conflicting signals could confuse. **Recommendation:** Minor. The text itself resolves the tension (prajna can lead to mind-training). No doctrinal error.

[Severity: M] [Fascicle 29, section 29.10] — The assignment of the eight noble path factors to the three trainings follows the Yogacara-Sarvastivada scheme: right view, right thought, and right effort under prajna; right speech, right action, right livelihood under shila; right mindfulness and right samadhi under samadhi. The footnote [^eightfold_path] correctly notes that this differs from the Pali tradition, where right effort belongs to the samadhi training. This is a genuine doctrinal divergence between traditions — the Yogacara placement of right effort under prajna is well-attested (cf. Abhidharmakosha VI). The footnote adequately flags it. No error, but this is the kind of passage where a removed footnote could have created a gap. The footnote survives and is accurate. **Recommendation:** No action needed.

### Category Errors

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 30, section 30.13] — The four types of vipashyana are: correct discernment (正思擇), thorough discernment (最極思擇), comprehensive investigation (周遍尋思), and comprehensive examination (周遍伺察). The footnote correctly maps the first two to the two modes of knowing (yavad-bhavikata and yathavad-bhavikata) and the latter two to vitarka/vicara. This is accurate. No category error.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 29, section 29.9] — The seven factors of awakening are classified: investigation of dharmas, effort, and joy under vipashyana; ease, samadhi, and equanimity under shamatha; mindfulness pervading both. This matches standard Yogacara-Sarvastivada classification and is accurately presented. No error.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 33, sections 33.3-33.9] — The seven attentions (sapta-manaskara) are correctly presented and correctly categorized as the worldly path method. The distinction between the worldly path (contemplating coarseness/peacefulness to suppress afflictions realm-by-realm) and the supramundane path (direct realization of the four noble truths) is maintained throughout. No category error.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 33, section 33.10ff] — The dhyana formulas are correctly distinguished: first dhyana possesses vitarka and vicara; second dhyana is free from both; third dhyana has bliss (sukha) without joy (priti); fourth dhyana has neither suffering nor pleasure, with purified equanimity and mindfulness. This follows standard Abhidharma precisely. No error.

### Context Gaps from Footnote Removal

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 26, section 26.5, "emptyfruitless" reference] — The opening question in 26.1 asks "How does one set out upon what is empty and without fruit?" with footnote [^emptyfruitless]. I verified this footnote still exists — it was not removed. No gap.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 27, section 27.3, sixteen victory practices] — The sixteen victory practices passage is dense and technical. The existing footnote [^sixteen_victory_practices] correctly explains the four tetrads and their relationship to MN 118. The footnote [^learner_seen_path] correctly identifies the prerequisite level. The footnote [^dhyana_anapanasati_limit] correctly notes the Sarvastivada position that anapanasati extends only through the third dhyana — and flags the Theravada divergence. These are all critical footnotes for a reader navigating this passage, and all survive. No gap from the audit.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 28, section 28.5, "agotra" concept] — The concept of persons without spiritual lineage (agotra-pudgala) is doctrinally controversial. Two footnotes survive: [^agotra] explaining the Yogacara position, and [^agotra_context] noting the controversy with tathagatagarbha traditions. Both are well-written and accurate. No gap.

**Potential gap identified:**

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 31, section 31.5, nine practice approaches] — The nine practice approaches (*nava-akara-prayoga*) are listed and explained. These are: (1) establishment, (2) abiding, (3) uplifting, (4) guarding, (5) investigation, (6) further endeavor, (7) equanimity, (8) non-cessation, (9) combining. The passage itself is clear. However, some of these terms overlap with the nine stages of mental abiding (fascicle 30) in potentially confusing ways — "establishment" appears in both, "equanimity" appears in both. If a footnote distinguishing the nine practice approaches from the nine stages of mental abiding was removed during the audit, this could create confusion for a careful reader. I did not find such a distinguishing footnote. **Recommendation:** Consider whether a brief footnote distinguishing the nine practice approaches from the nine stages of mental abiding would aid comprehension. This is a minor clarity concern, not a doctrinal error.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 31, section 31.6-31.7, purification of obstacles and completion criteria] — The criteria for when shamatha and vipashyana are "complete" are stated: shamatha is complete when the nine stages culminate in pliancy; vipashyana is complete when analytical meditation produces its own pliancy. The goldsmith simile then describes how to balance the two. This passage is critical doctrinally — Tsongkhapa devotes extensive commentary to it in the Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3. The existing footnotes appear adequate to contextualize the passage. No obvious gap from the audit.

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 32, section 32.16-32.17, pliancy attainment] — The description of the arising of pliancy is one of the most important passages in the text. The surviving footnote [^pliancy_process] is excellent — it explains the sequence, references Tsongkhapa's commentary, and warns against overestimation. No gap.

### Specific Doctrinal Checks

**Five skandhas:** Correctly enumerated wherever they appear (26.5 pervasive objects, 27.4 skillful objects, various). Form, feeling, perception, formations, consciousness — standard order maintained.

**Twelve links of dependent origination:** Correctly presented in 27.1 and 31.1. The twelve-link sequence (ignorance through aging-and-death) is standard and accurate.

**Four noble truths:** Correctly presented in 27.5 (affliction-purifying objects) and 29.12 (shramana fruits). Suffering, origin, cessation, path — standard formulation.

**Eight noble path factors:** All eight correctly named and correctly assigned to the three trainings per Yogacara convention (29.10).

**Thirty-seven factors of awakening:** The seven groups are correctly presented across fascicles 28-29: four foundations of mindfulness, four right efforts, four bases of supernatural power, five faculties, five powers, seven factors of awakening, noble eightfold path. The count of 37 is maintained. The progression from one group to the next is accurately represented.

**Nine stages of mental abiding:** Correctly presented in 30.10 with the six powers (30.11) and four types of attention (30.12). Names, definitions, and power-stage mappings are all accurate.

**Seven attentions (sapta-manaskara):** Correctly presented in fascicle 33. The mapping to the worldly path of ascending through grounds is accurate. The distinction between the first six attentions (worldly) and the seventh as the fruit is maintained.

**Four dhyanas:** The standard formulas are correctly rendered throughout. First dhyana: vitarka, vicara, joy, bliss born of separation. Second: inner clarity, one-pointedness, joy and bliss born of samadhi. Third: equanimity, mindfulness, alertness, bodily bliss. Fourth: neither suffering nor pleasure, purified equanimity-mindfulness. All accurate.

**Formless attainments:** Correctly presented in 33.14-33.15: infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, neither-perception-nor-non-perception. Standard sequence and descriptions.

**Two absorptions without mind:** The cessation attainment (nirodha-samapatti) and non-perception attainment (asamjni-samapatti) are correctly distinguished in 33.15. The cessation attainment is correctly identified as available only to non-returners and arhats.

**Five superknowledges (abhijna):** Listed in 33.16: divine eye, divine ear, knowledge of others' minds, recollection of past lives, magical powers. This is the standard five (the sixth, knowledge of the destruction of outflows, is sometimes added but is not part of this classification). The passage correctly develops them through twelve perceptions. Accurate.

**Rebirth mapping (33.17):** The mapping of dhyana grades to heavenly rebirths is accurate: first dhyana to the three Brahma heavens, second to the three radiance heavens, third to the three purity heavens, fourth to cloudless/merit-born/vast-fruit. The five pure abodes for non-returners are correctly listed. Formless attainments map to formless realms. All standard Abhidharma cosmology, accurately rendered.

### Shamatha vs. Vipashyana Classification

[Severity: m] [Throughout] — The text consistently maintains the shamatha-vipashyana framework as its organizing principle. Shamatha is always associated with calm abiding, the nine stages, mental one-pointedness, and the image without discernment. Vipashyana is always associated with analytical investigation, the four types of discernment, and the image with discernment. No instances found where these categories are confused or reversed.

### Introduction (00_introduction.md)

[Severity: m] [00_introduction.md, "the nine stages of mental abiding became the standard framework for shamatha instruction across both the Tibetan and East Asian traditions"] — This claim is accurate. The nine stages from the Sravakabhumi (via the Yogacarabhumi broadly) became foundational in Tibetan shamatha instruction (Tsongkhapa's Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3 relies on them centrally; Kamalashila's Bhavanakrama draws on them). The claim about East Asian traditions is somewhat broader — East Asian shamatha instruction draws on multiple sources including the Tiantai meditation manuals (Zhiyi) — but the Sravakabhumi's influence through the Yogacarabhumi is well-attested in East Asian Buddhist scholarship. The claim is defensible.

[Severity: m] [00_introduction.md, "Tsongkhapa's Lam Rim Chen Mo, Kamalashila's Stages of Meditation, and the contemplative traditions of all four major Tibetan Buddhist schools all draw substantially on this text"] — This is accurate. Tsongkhapa's shamatha and vipashyana chapters in the LRCM draw extensively and explicitly from the Sravakabhumi. Kamalashila's three Bhavanakramas are deeply influenced by the Yogacarabhumi tradition. "All four major Tibetan Buddhist schools" is a strong claim but defensible — Gelug (Tsongkhapa), Kagyu (Pointing Out the Great Way tradition draws on nine stages), Sakya, and Nyingma (Mipham's Gateway to Knowledge) all reference the Sravakabhumi material. No correction needed.

### Worldly vs. Supramundane Path Distinction

[Severity: m] [Fascicle 33] — The critical distinction between the worldly and supramundane paths is consistently maintained:

- **Worldly path** (fascicle 33, sections 33.2-33.17): Seven attentions, dhyanas, formless attainments, superknowledges. Correctly characterized as *suppressing* afflictions without permanently uprooting them (except where non-returners infuse uncontaminated practice).
- **Supramundane path** (noted in uddana 33.1 as continuing into subsequent fascicles): Direct realization of the four noble truths, permanent eradication of afflictions.

The translation correctly notes at 33.2 that "only two directions remain — the worldly and the supramundane" and at the uddana footnote that the supramundane content continues beyond fascicle 33. The separation is doctrinally accurate. The worldly path material in fascicle 33 never claims permanent affliction-removal through dhyana alone (except for the specific case of non-returners in the pure abodes). No category error.

## Overall Assessment

The translation is doctrinally sound. No critical errors were found. The three major findings are points of potential reader confusion rather than actual doctrinal errors — and in each case, existing footnotes provide adequate context. The footnote audit does not appear to have created any doctrinal gaps: all footnotes essential for understanding controversial or complex doctrinal points (agotra, dry-insight, eightfold path training assignments, shamatha-vipashyana completion criteria, pliancy attainment, anapanasati dhyana limit) survive.

The technical classifications — five skandhas, twelve links, four noble truths, eight path factors, thirty-seven factors of awakening, nine stages, seven attentions, four dhyanas, formless attainments, five superknowledges, rebirth destinations — are all accurately rendered and correctly categorized.

Attribution (Asanga vs. Maitreya) is handled consistently and correctly throughout.

The methodology and introduction make no inaccurate claims. The one observation about "first complete English rendering" is a matter of potential misreading, not actual error, and the scope section provides the necessary qualification.
