# Agent R — Readability Review
## Fascicle 33, Sections 33.10–33.18
## File: `review_findings/33_EF_readability.md`

---

## Overall Assessment

The translation is largely competent and faithful. The dhyana formula commentaries (33.10–33.14) are the strongest portions: the term-by-term structure keeps the reader oriented and the prose is generally clean. The formless attainments (33.14) and twelve perceptions (33.16) are the weakest: both develop complex progressions that get obscured by over-qualification and translated-ese. Section 33.18 (characteristics of the desire-free person) is almost there but undercuts itself with formula monotony in a passage that should be luminous.

Register calibration: The translation sits closer to Nanamoli than to Bodhi — it aims for precision and hits it, but occasionally at the cost of flow. The practitioner-facing passages (33.16's magical display sequence, 33.18's portrait) have the most room to gain from revision.

### (2) Formula Monotony

**F1 — 33.11: "born of … not born of …" echo (minor)**
Location: lines 260–261
> "hence 'born of concentration,' not 'born of separation' as in the first dhyana."

The parenthetical contrast is fine once. No repetition problem here — flagged and dismissed.

**F2 — 33.13: "cannot operate freely" repeated twice in the same paragraph (moderate)**
Location: lines 276
> "In the first dhyana, because examination and analysis are present, uninterrupted equanimity cannot operate freely. In the second dhyana, because joy is present, uninterrupted equanimity cannot operate freely."

The doubling is structurally faithful (the Chinese probably repeats), but in English these two sentences read as the same sentence said twice. Compress.

Suggested rewrite:
> "In the first dhyana, examination and analysis prevent uninterrupted equanimity from fully operating; in the second dhyana, joy does the same."

**F3 — 33.17: "One who has well cultivated … will … be reborn among …" repeated six times (moderate)**
Location: lines 410–422

This is the most significant formula-monotony problem in the batch. Six consecutive parallel sentences with identical structure across a short passage. The repetition is doctrinally accurate but deadening. The reader needs one sentence to grasp the pattern, then a compressed table or a single sentence that lists the progression.

Suggested rewrite (paragraph structure, not a table, to preserve prose register):

> Those who cultivate each dhyana or formless sphere in its lower, middle, and upper grades are reborn, degree by degree, in the corresponding heavens. The first dhyana yields the Brahma-host, Brahma-ministers, and great Brahma deities; the second, the minor-radiance, boundless-radiance, and supreme-radiance deities; the third, the minor-purity, boundless-purity, and universal-purity deities; the fourth, the cloudless, merit-born, and vast-fruit deities. Non-returners who intermittently infuse the contaminated fourth dhyana with the uncontaminated, cultivating it through five grades, are reborn in the five pure abodes: the non-afflicted, the non-heated, the clearly appearing, the clearly seeing, and the highest-form. Those who cultivate the formless spheres are reborn in their respective formless heavens, which have no spatial distinctions — their inhabitants have no material form — though modes of abiding differ. One who cultivates the attainment of non-perception is reborn among the non-perceiving deities.

This preserves all content, varies sentence rhythm, and lets the reader follow a progression rather than reading a list of clones.

**F4 — 33.18: Sentence-level formula monotony (moderate)**
Location: lines 434–438

The section begins well but then falls into a series of short declarative sentences of near-identical weight:
> "One's bodily actions are settled and one's faculties are unmoved. One's bearing and deportment are free from agitation. … One's speech is gentle and one's speech is calm. One takes no pleasure in clamor and takes no pleasure in crowds. One's words are unhurried."

"One's speech is gentle and one's speech is calm" — "one's speech" appears twice in a single sentence, which is a copyist error or an overly literal rendering of a two-part Chinese compound. Fix immediately.

The passage continues in the same staccato rhythm for ten more sentences. This portrait of the desire-free person deserves warmer, more gathered prose. The Visuddhimagga and Bodhi both write portrait-passages with longer arcs — the traits flow into one another rather than being listed.

Suggested rewrite (paragraph structure):

> One who is free from desire can be recognized by these signs. The body is settled: faculties unmoved, bearing undisturbed, posture sustained for a long time without alarm or anxiety, without frequent shifting. The speech is gentle and unhurried; there is no pleasure in noise, no pleasure in crowds.
>
> When the eyes meet a form, there is only cognition — no craving arises on its account. The same holds for sounds heard by the ears, odors known by the nose, flavors tasted by the tongue, sensations felt by the body: each is simply known, without craving following in its wake.
>
> There is no fear. The discernment is deep and subtle; pliancy is vast. Body and mind are composed and self-contained, free from greed, free from eruptions of anger. The mind is patient — not disturbed by craving-related thought, nor by any other unwholesome movement.

### (3) Nested Qualification

**F5 — 33.14: The sphere of nothingness paragraph (moderate)**
Location: lines 322
> "Seeking to advance beyond infinite consciousness, one searches for some further object beyond that consciousness — whether of form or formless nature — and finds nothing at all. Finding no object, one transcends both the preliminary and root stages of infinite consciousness and generates conviction that there is nothing whatsoever remaining. This is called a 'provisional conviction' (*parikalpita-adhimoksha*) regarding nothingness."

The logic is clear enough, but "one searches for some further object beyond that consciousness — whether of form or formless nature — and finds nothing at all" contains two embedded qualifications inside a participial clause inside the main clause. The sentence is readable but effortful. For a practitioner following the progression of how each formless attainment arises, clarity of sequence matters.

Suggested rewrite:
> "Seeking to advance beyond infinite consciousness, one looks for any further object — whether form or formless — and finds nothing whatsoever. From that finding of nothing, one transcends both the preliminary and root stages of infinite consciousness and generates conviction that nothing remains. This is called a 'provisional conviction' (*parikalpita-adhimoksha*) regarding nothingness."

**F6 — 33.15: The cessation attainment sentence (moderate)**
Location: lines 352–353
> "For a learner (*shaiksha*), this requires having been freed from desire for the sphere of nothingness. For an arhat, it requires the aspiration to abide temporarily in quiescence. When this attention takes the lead and mind and mental factors cease, this is called the cessation attainment."

"When this attention takes the lead and mind and mental factors cease" — "this attention" is ambiguous: it refers back to the attention introduced two paragraphs earlier. The reader has to track the antecedent across a paragraph break. Better to name it: "the attention of seeking upward."

Suggested fix:
> "When this upward-seeking attention takes the lead and mind and mental factors cease, this is called the cessation attainment."

### (1) Translated-Ese

**F7 — 33.10: "it is called" construction repeated throughout (minor)**
Location: lines 224–244 (throughout the dhyana formula commentaries)

Every entry in sections 33.10–33.14 ends with the pattern: "Therefore it is called X." This is structurally faithful (the Chinese formula 故名X is exactly this) and unavoidable in a term-by-term commentary. However, "Therefore it is called" repeats eleven times across sections 33.10–33.11 alone. This is not a flaw that can be fixed without restructuring the whole exegetical method, but the reviewer flags it so the editor is aware. No fix recommended — the repetition is intrinsic to the commentary genre.

**F8 — 33.10: "one has attained the attention of completed application" / "attention to the fruit of completed application" (minor)**
Location: lines 232, 242–244

The distinction between "attention of completed application" (加行究竟作意) and "attention to the fruit of completed application" (加行究竟果作意) is technically important but the two phrases are easily confused by a reader at speed. The footnote at 33.9 (not in scope) presumably handles this, but within 33.10 itself the reader sees both phrases with no reminder of the distinction. Consider a brief parenthetical: "the attention to the fruit of completed application (the root attainment itself)."

**F9 — 33.13: "deeply perceives the faults inherent in joy" (minor)**
Location: line 272
> "One deeply perceives the faults inherent in joy."

"Deeply perceives the faults" is a literal translation of a Chinese compound meaning something more like "sees through joy as a fault" or "discerns joy as flawed." As written, "perceives the faults inherent in joy" sounds like one is simply noticing that joy has drawbacks, whereas the meditative meaning is stronger: one perceives joy itself as a form of agitation, as something to be left behind. Suggested: "One discerns the faults of joy directly" or "One sees through joy as a source of disturbance."

**F10 — 33.16: "pervading villages, gatherings, assemblies of elders and townspeople and other great crowds, in open halls, homes, and chambers" (moderate)**
Location: lines 387–388
> "Through this perception, pervading villages, gatherings, assemblies of elders and townspeople and other great crowds, in open halls, homes, and chambers — one takes the signs of all the diverse and clamorous sounds they produce."

"Pervading villages, gatherings, assemblies of elders and townspeople and other great crowds, in open halls, homes, and chambers" is a string of seven nouns that the participle "pervading" struggles to carry. The meaning is that one's attention spreads to encompass all these sound-producing spaces — but "pervading" makes it sound like the practitioner physically permeates buildings. Suggested rewrite:

> "Through this perception, one extends attention across villages and gatherings — assemblies of elders, townspeople, great crowds — through open halls, homes, and chambers — and takes the signs of all the diverse, clamorous sounds that arise there."

### (4) Register Drift

**F11 — 33.12: "to that same extent" (minor)**
Location: line 278
> "to the extent that one abides in mindfulness, to that same extent the perceptions and attentions accompanied by joy cease to manifest"

"To that same extent" is mildly scholastic. Better: "as one abides in mindfulness, joy-accompanied perceptions and attentions cease to manifest." The Chinese correlative structure (幾所…爾所…) is faithfully rendered but the English can shed the correlative without loss.

**F12 — 33.15: "by this method one achieves this absorption" (minor)**
Location: lines 348, 352

This phrase closes both descriptions of the two absorptions, identically. It reads as a filing note ("this is the method for achieving this absorption") rather than prose. Omit entirely from both — the descriptions already convey the method; the summary adds nothing for a practitioner.

**F13 — 33.16: The magical display paragraph (moderate)**
Location: lines 382–384

This is the one paragraph in sections 33.10–33.18 that should feel genuinely alive — the list of magical powers is among the most vividly written passages in the sutra literature. The current translation is competent:

> "From one body one manifests many — through the conviction of manifestation. From many bodies one manifests one — through the conviction of concealment. One's body passes through walls, fences, and city ramparts without obstruction. One emerges from the earth and submerges in it as in water. One walks upon water as upon land. Seated cross-legged, one soars through the air like a bird. With one's hand one touches the sun and moon in all their vast power and brilliance. With one's body one travels freely as far as the Brahma-world."

The individual sentences are fine. The register problem is the parenthetical mechanism tags — "through the conviction of manifestation," "through the conviction of concealment" — which interrupt the flow of what should feel like wondrous, ascending movement. These could be either absorbed into the sentence or moved to a footnote. The rest of the passage builds well; do not over-fix.

Suggested light revision for the opening:
> "Through the conviction of manifestation, one body becomes many; through concealment, many become one. The body passes through walls, fences, and city ramparts without obstruction…"

### (5) Sentences Over 50 Words

**F14 — 33.13, lines 278 (57 words, moderate)**
> "Because there is equanimity: to the extent that one abides in mindfulness, to that same extent the perceptions and attentions accompanied by joy cease to manifest. But if one does not practice the third dhyana well, or sometimes loses mindfulness, those joy-accompanied perceptions and attentions may arise again. One should immediately, with swift wisdom, penetrate and correctly know them."

Actually three sentences — none individually over 50 words. No violation. Dismissed.

**F15 — 33.14 (sphere of infinite space): lines 312–313, single sentence ~52 words (minor)**
> "Having removed perceptions of form, of resistance, and of diversity, one generates conviction regarding space with its aspect of boundlessness. Therefore it is said: 'one enters infinite space.' Having transcended the preliminary attention of completed application and entered the root attention to the fruit of completed application, it is said: 'one dwells fully equipped in the sphere of infinite space.'"

Not one sentence — the punctuation breaks it. No violation.

**F16 — 33.16, line 368 (~100 words, significant)**
> "How does one develop the superknowledges? A practitioner of dhyana who has already attained a purified root dhyana takes that purified dhyana as a basis and then listens to, receives, retains, and thoroughly masters the correct dharma pertaining to the five superknowledges — namely, the superknowledge of magical display (*rddhi-abhijna*), of recollection of past lives (*purvanivasanusmrti*), of the divine ear (*divya-shrotra*), of knowledge of death and rebirth (*cyuty-upapatti-jnana*), and of knowledge of others' minds (*para-citta-jnana*). One directs attention to these, and through concentration-based attention one comes to understand their meaning and dharma."

The sentence runs from "A practitioner…" through "their meaning and dharma." It is over 100 words with no internal breathing. The embedded Sanskrit list of five superknowledges inflates it further. Break it into two.

Suggested fix:
> "A practitioner who has attained a purified root dhyana takes that purified dhyana as a basis and then listens to, receives, retains, and thoroughly masters the correct dharma pertaining to the five superknowledges. These are: magical display (*rddhi-abhijna*), recollection of past lives (*purvanivasanusmrti*), the divine ear (*divya-shrotra*), knowledge of death and rebirth (*cyuty-upapatti-jnana*), and knowledge of others' minds (*para-citta-jnana*). One directs attention to these and, through concentration-based attention, comes to understand their meaning and dharma."

**F17 — 33.16, line 392, approximately 100 words (significant)**
> "Those dominated by craving show restless faculties, agitated bearing, and constant smiling. Those dominated by anger show perpetual frowning, harsh and halting speech, and frequent changes of color. Those dominated by ignorance are mostly incoherent, dark in understanding, with unclear speech and vulgar expression. In this way, through these patterns, one extends the analysis to all the afflictions through shamelessness and recklessness."

These are all individually short. The problem is the final sentence — "In this way, through these patterns, one extends the analysis to all the afflictions through shamelessness and recklessness" — which is ambiguous: does "through shamelessness and recklessness" mean the analysis proceeds via shamelessness, or that it extends to cover shamelessness and recklessness as its endpoint? Clarify: "…extending the analysis to all the remaining afflictions, including shamelessness and recklessness."

### (6) Unexplained Jargon

**F18 — 33.10: "separated from evil and unwholesome dharmas" — "dharmas" unexplained (minor)**
Location: line 228

"Dharmas" (法) here means phenomena / mental-and-physical factors, not teachings. A practitioner unfamiliar with this usage will stumble. At minimum, a footnote. The translator may have decided that "dharmas" in this technical sense is established by this point in fascicle 33, but this is the first occurrence in the formula commentary sections and warrants a brief gloss.

Suggested inline fix: "evil and unwholesome dharmas (mental-physical factors)" — or a footnote if the editor prefers not to interrupt the formula.

**F19 — 33.15: "dutula cotton" (minor)**
Location: line 372

"*Dutula* cotton" — the Sanskrit term is left untranslated with no gloss. Most practitioners will not know this is a specific type of extremely fine, light cotton (Sanskrit: *tūla*, Chinese: 都如羅棉). Add a brief gloss: "(*dutula* — an especially fine, light cotton)."

**F20 — 33.16: "no-contention knowledge (*arana-jnana*)" and "power of aspiration (*pranidhi-jnana*)" (moderate)**
Location: line 394

These two terms appear at the end of the section with no explanation: "no-contention knowledge (*arana-jnana*), the power of aspiration (*pranidhi-jnana*), and the four analytical knowledges." All three are important dharma categories, and the reader is dropped into them without preparation. The footnote at [^liberation_mastery] (line 402) covers the liberation, mastery, and totality perceptions but does not address these. A brief parenthetical or footnote is warranted.

Suggested footnote addition to [^liberation_mastery]:

> No-contention knowledge (*arana-jnana*, 無諍智) is the ability to engage beings without generating conflict or contention in their minds — a refined social-meditative capacity. The power of aspiration (*pranidhi-jnana*, 願智) is the ability to know the aspirations and wishes of sentient beings. The four analytical knowledges (*catur-pratisam-vid*) are knowledge of dharmas (their intrinsic nature), meanings (their conventional implications), expressions (their linguistic forms), and eloquence (the capacity to teach them).

**F21 — 33.17: "cloudless, merit-born, or vast-fruit deities" (minor)**
Location: line 416

These heavens (無雲、福生、廣果) have no corresponding Sanskrit terms provided, unlike most of the cosmological vocabulary in this section. Minor inconsistency — if the translator is providing Sanskrit for the pure abodes, consistency suggests doing so here as well, or adding a footnote for all three.

### (7) Broken Logic

**F22 — 33.13: "the saints" identification (moderate)**
Location: lines 282–283
> "Therefore it is said that in the third dhyana, the saints declare: 'The person who abides here is fully equipped with equanimity, mindfulness, and alertness, and experiences bodily bliss — dwelling fully equipped in the third dhyana.' 'The saints' refers to the Blessed One and the disciples of the Buddha."

The identification of "the saints" as "the Blessed One and the disciples of the Buddha" comes after the quotation — the reader must go back and re-read the quotation knowing who the speakers are. This is minor but fixable by moving the identification to before the quotation:

Suggested fix:
> "For this reason, the saints — meaning the Blessed One and his disciples — declare of the third dhyana: 'The person who abides here is fully equipped with equanimity, mindfulness, and alertness, and experiences bodily bliss — dwelling fully equipped in the third dhyana.'"

**F23 — 33.14 (sphere of infinite space): "In the preliminary stages, one also cognizes the aggregates of the lower ground" (moderate)**
Location: lines 313–314
> "Here one should know that in the preliminary stages, up to but not yet entering the root attainment, one cognizes only space. Once the root attainment is entered, one also cognizes one's own aggregates belonging to that ground. In the preliminary stages, one also cognizes the aggregates of the lower ground."

The last sentence contradicts the first: the first sentence says "in the preliminary stages, one cognizes only space"; the last says "in the preliminary stages, one also cognizes the aggregates of the lower ground." These are not reconciled within the text. A practitioner will be confused. This may be a doctrinal accuracy issue (Source Fidelity agent's call), but from a readability standpoint the logic breaks. If the contradiction is in the source, a translator's note is required; if it is a translation error, correct it.

**F24 — 33.16: Causal gap between perceptions 1–5 and magical display (minor)**
Location: between lines 380 and 382

The text lists perceptions 1–5 and then, without a transitional bridge, moves to the magical display powers. The reader is not told that these first five perceptions, when complete, produce magical display. The footnote at [^five_superknowledges] (line 400) covers this — but the gap in the main body is abrupt. Consider adding one sentence: "Through the completion of these five perceptions, the various powers of magical display become accessible."

(Note: this sentence IS present in the translation at line 382 — "Through the completion of these five perceptions, one attains the various wonderful powers of magical display." The reviewer initially missed it. Finding F24 is dismissed.)
