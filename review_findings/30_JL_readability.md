# Readability Review — Fascicle 30, Sections 30.10–30.12
**Agent R (Readability) | Reviewer perspective: 80% practitioner, 20% scholar**
**Register standard: Nanamoli, Bodhi, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee**

---

## Overall Assessment

Sections 30.10–30.12 are in substantially better shape than earlier fascicle 29 sections. The nine-stage framework in 30.10 reads with reasonable clarity, and the footnote on Sanskrit equivalents is well-placed. However, several specific issues warrant attention: one instance of translated-ese in the opening enumeration, a few nested-qualification constructions, one >50-word sentence, and a register dip in 30.11 where the correlating paragraph reads like a diagram caption rather than instruction. Section 30.12 is clean and needs no changes.

---

## Flags by Category

### (1) Translated-ese — prose sounds assembled from Chinese rather than written in English

**30.10 Opening sentence (line 248):**
> "A bhikshu causes the mind to abide inwardly and to settle — to re-establish and abide closely — to become tamed, pacified, and fully pacified — to achieve one-pointed attention, and finally equipoise."

The chain of em-dashed infinitives reads like the Chinese list structure (心住、等住、安住…) transliterated into English typography. No English writer would naturally write "causes the mind to abide inwardly and to settle — to re-establish and abide closely." The verb "causes" applies awkwardly across all the phrases, and the em-dashes splice together what should be a more fluid preview. The sentence reads as an assembly of glossary entries, not as orientation for a reader.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The nine stages form a progressive sequence: first the mind is placed inward and allowed to settle; it is re-established and held close; it is tamed, pacified, and thoroughly pacified; and finally it achieves one-pointed absorption and equipoise."

---

### (3) Nested qualification — main verb buried

**30.11 Long correlating paragraph (line 276):**
> "Initially, through the combined power of hearing and reflection — by the dominant force of repeated hearing and repeated thinking — one first causes the mind to abide on the internal object, and through sustained and purifying methods settles it evenly throughout."

The parenthetical gloss "by the dominant force of repeated hearing and repeated thinking" is inserted mid-clause between "hearing and reflection" and the main action. The result is a clause-inside-a-clause construction that delays the verb ("causes the mind to abide") while unpacking what hearing and reflection already mean. The reader must hold two open frames simultaneously.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The first two stages arise through hearing and reflection: by repeatedly hearing and thinking about the instructions, one places the mind on its object and, through sustained refinement, settles it evenly."

---

### (5) >50-word sentences

**30.11, the full correlating paragraph (line 276)** is a single compound sentence of approximately 140 words. Counting roughly:

> "Initially, through the combined power of hearing and reflection — by the dominant force of repeated hearing and repeated thinking — one first causes the mind to abide on the internal object, and through sustained and purifying methods settles it evenly throughout. Having thus bound the mind inward, through the power of mindfulness one repeatedly directs attention, gathering the mind and preventing scattering — this is re-established abiding and close abiding. From this point forward, through the power of clear knowing, one tempers the mind, preventing it from flowing toward signs, unwholesome thoughts, and secondary afflictions — taming and pacifying. Through the power of diligence, when either of those two briefly arises, one does not tolerate them but immediately cuts them off, eliminates, expels, and vomits them out — fully pacifying the mind and achieving one-pointed attention. Through the power of habituation, equipoise is brought to completion."

This is actually broken into multiple sentences, but the first sentence is 48 words and reads fast enough. However, the fourth sentence — "Through the power of diligence, when either of those two briefly arises, one does not tolerate them but immediately cuts them off, eliminates, expels, and vomits them out — fully pacifying the mind and achieving one-pointed attention" — is approximately 47 words and ends with a dangling participial phrase that applies ambiguously (does "fully pacifying" describe the outcome of cutting off, or is it naming stage 7?). The ambiguity is a broken-logic risk (see category 7 below).

---

### (4) Register drift — scholarly catalog rather than living text

**30.11 as a whole** reads as a caption for a diagram rather than as instruction a practitioner could sit with. The paragraph maps powers to stages mechanically — "hearing and reflection → stages 1–2; mindfulness → stages 3–4" — and while this is accurate, the prose form of the paragraph does the same thing more wordily without adding any texture that would help a practitioner understand *why* mindfulness is what bridges stage 2 to stages 3–4, or what it feels like when clear knowing kicks in to restrain the mind from signs.

This is a minor flag — the section is inherently taxonomic, and some catalog-quality is appropriate. But a single orienting sentence at the start of 30.11 would help enormously: something that gives the practitioner a frame before the mapping begins.

**Suggested addition at the start of 30.11:**
> "Each power is the specific faculty that makes the next stage possible — understanding how they map to the stages clarifies what to cultivate and when."

Or more simply, reframe the opening sentence from:
> "One should know that six powers bring about the accomplishment of these nine stages:"

to:
> "The nine stages rest on six powers, each ripening in sequence:"

This is a small change but it opens the taxonomy into a progression rather than a list.

### (7) Broken logic / ambiguity

**30.10, stage (7) Fully pacifying (line 262):**
> "one does not tolerate them for even a moment but immediately cuts them off, eliminates them, expels them, and vomits them out."

The quadruple verb list (cuts off / eliminates / expels / vomits out) applied to "unwholesome thoughts or secondary afflictions" is slightly jarring. "Vomits out" is striking and vivid — arguably appropriate for the forceful ejection described — but placed at the end of a list with three synonyms, it reads as an escalation that goes slightly over the edge of decorum for the NTC register. Nanamoli and Bodhi would not use "vomits" in this context; they use "expels" or "rejects." This is a minor flag — the Chinese (嘔吐) may literally mean vomit — but if source fidelity allows any rendering latitude, "expels" or "drives out" would be more register-consistent.

**30.11, same passage (line 276):**
> "Through the power of diligence, when either of those two briefly arises, one does not tolerate them but immediately cuts them off, eliminates, expels, and vomits them out — fully pacifying the mind and achieving one-pointed attention."

"Either of those two" refers back to "unwholesome thoughts" and "secondary afflictions" (from the preceding sentence). The referent is clear enough in context, but the phrase "fully pacifying the mind and achieving one-pointed attention" conflates stages 7 and 8 as simultaneous outcomes of a single act of diligence. In 30.10 they are described as distinct: stage 7 (fully pacifying) = not tolerating even briefly; stage 8 (one-pointed attention) = effort continuing without gaps. The footnote (line 278) makes clear that diligence covers stages 7–8, so the mapping is intentional — but the phrasing suggests they occur simultaneously rather than sequentially. A small clarification would help:

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Through the power of diligence, when unwholesome thoughts or secondary afflictions briefly arise, one does not permit them to linger — cutting them off immediately. This brings about full pacification, and sustained diligence without relaxing produces one-pointed attention."
