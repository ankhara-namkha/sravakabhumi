## 1. F28.10: Thirty-Seven Factors of Awakening (4+4+4+5+5+7+8=37)

### Enumeration Verification

The translation correctly lists all seven groups:
- Four foundations of mindfulness: body, feeling, mind, dharmas -- **4 items, CORRECT**
- Four right efforts: (1) abandon arisen unwholesome, (2) prevent unarisen unwholesome, (3) generate unarisen wholesome, (4) sustain arisen wholesome -- **4 items, CORRECT**
- Four bases of supernatural power: desire, diligence, mind, investigation -- **4 items, CORRECT**
- Five faculties: faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, prajna -- **5 items, CORRECT**
- Five powers: faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, prajna -- **5 items, CORRECT**
- Seven factors of awakening: mindfulness, investigation-of-dharmas, diligence, joy, ease, concentration, equanimity -- **7 items, CORRECT**
- Eightfold noble path: right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration -- **8 items, CORRECT**

**Total: 4+4+4+5+5+7+8 = 37. VERIFIED CORRECT.**

### Doctrinal Accuracy

**[O — Observation]** The translation of the four right efforts (line 486) lists the aims in the order: (1) abandoning arisen unwholesome, (2) preventing unarisen unwholesome, (3) generating unarisen wholesome, (4) sustaining arisen wholesome. The Chinese source at T30.0439c22 lists them in the same order found in the standard Sarvastivada enumerations (四正斷者.一於已生惡不善法為令斷故...二於未生惡不善法為不生故...三於未生善法為令生故...四於已生善法為欲令住). The translation follows the source faithfully. Some Pali sources reverse the first two (prevent unarisen first, then abandon arisen), but the Yogacarabhumi's order is standard for the Sarvastivada tradition. No issue.

**[O — Observation]** The eightfold path order matches the standard: view, thought, speech, action, livelihood, effort, mindfulness, concentration. Correct.

**[O — Observation]** The four bases of supernatural power are correctly identified as four samadhis named by their predominant force (desire, diligence, mind, investigation) rather than four separate powers. This matches the Chinese (欲三摩地斷行成就神足 etc.) and the standard Sarvastivada-Yogacara formulation.

**No issues found. All 37 factors correctly enumerated and doctrinally sound.**

## 2. F28.11: Four Foundations of Mindfulness

### Body/Feeling/Mind/Dharma Structure

**CORRECT.** The translation preserves the standard fourfold structure:
- Body (身): 35 aspects enumerated
- Feeling (受): 21 kinds / 9 kinds
- Mind (心): 20 kinds (6 active + 14 meditative)
- Dharmas (法): 20 kinds (black/white, defiled/pure)

### Compatibility with Analayo Framework

The Sravakabhumi's treatment is distinctively Yogacara-Abhidharma (scholastic analysis of categories) rather than the practice-oriented Satipatthana Sutta approach that Analayo analyzes. Key points of compatibility and divergence:

**[O — Observation]** Compatible: The fourfold structure (body, feeling, mind, dharmas) is identical. The translation correctly preserves the internal/external/internal-external three-way contemplation found in both Pali and Chinese traditions. The four inversions (impure-as-pure, suffering-as-pleasure, impermanent-as-permanent, non-self-as-self) mapped to the four foundations matches the standard framework found in Analayo and Visuddhimagga XXII.

**[O — Observation]** Divergent but doctrinally correct: The Sravakabhumi's 35 aspects of body is a Yogacara-specific elaboration not found in the Pali Satipatthana Sutta or Analayo's analysis. However, it is internally consistent and represents legitimate Abhidharma classification. The six interpretive frameworks for "internal/external/internal-external" are likewise a Yogacara scholastic contribution.

**[m — minor]** The translation lists 20 kinds of mind and then says "these fourteen kinds of mind should all be understood as arising during the meditative state" (line 538). The Chinese text also makes this distinction (如是十四種心當知皆是住時所起). However, the reader may be confused by the transition from 20 to 14 without explicit arithmetic: the total of 20 = 6 (active state) + 14 (meditative state). The translation does explain this at line 538 but a cross-check confirms the arithmetic is correct. The note at [^twenty_minds] provides the full explanation. **No error, just noting the complexity is handled correctly.**

**[O — Observation]** The 21 feelings are correctly structured as 7 threefold classifications (basic hedonic tone; bodily/mental x 3; with/without relish x 3; indulgence/renunciation x 3). The note [^twenty_one_feelings] explains the scheme clearly. The "nine kinds" is left somewhat open (the note suggests the MN 137 framework), which is appropriate given that the Chinese source itself is not fully explicit (或九種受).

**[m — minor]** The translation renders 法念住 as "foundation of mindfulness of dharmas" (line 484, 524, etc.). The mapping to the four inversions says "the foundation of mindfulness of dharmas was established" to counteract the self-inversion (line 580). This is correct. The dharma-mindfulness counteracting the self-inversion is standard across all traditions (cf. Visuddhimagga XXII.60-61; Analayo, Satipatthana pp. 181-200).

**No substantive issues found. Fourfold structure correct. Analayo-compatible where traditions overlap; divergences are legitimate Yogacara-Abhidharma contributions, clearly marked.**

## 3. F29.12: Four Shramana Fruits

### Attainment Criteria

**[CORRECT]** The translation correctly identifies:
- **Stream-entry (srota-apanna):** Severance of the three fetters (satkaya-drishti, shila-vrata-paramarsha, vicikitsa). Maximum seven more existences. Guaranteed no rebirth in evil destinies.
- **Once-returning (sakridagamin):** Thinning of greed, aversion, and delusion. At most one heavenly birth + one human birth remaining.
- **Non-returning (anagamin):** Severance of the five lower fetters. No return to this world; reborn only in heavenly realms.
- **Arhatship:** Complete severance of all afflictions without remainder.

### Abandonment Factors

**[CORRECT]** The three fetters for stream-entry are correctly mapped to three domains of obstruction:
1. Householder domain -> satkaya-drishti (identity view) -> fear prevents setting forth
2. Wrong teaching/discipline -> shila-vrata-paramarsha (attachment to rules) -> goes astray after setting forth
3. Right teaching/discipline -> vicikitsa (doubt) -> stalls despite correct orientation

This three-domain mapping is a distinctive Yogacara analysis (as the footnote correctly notes). Cross-checked against Abhidharmakosha VI standard treatment: the three fetters are standard; the three-domain interpretation is specific to the Yogacarabhumi.

**[M — Moderate]** The translation states for once-returning (line 336): "one has permanently abandoned all afflictions belonging to heavenly and human existence that drive continued rebirth." The Chinese says: 若斷再生相續煩惱生無重續 (if one has severed the afflictions of repeated rebirth so that births no longer recur). Then: 謂若永斷天有所攝人有所攝再生相續所有煩惱 (permanently severing all afflictions belonging to heavenly and human existence that drive continued rebirth). The translation is acceptable, but the phrase "permanently abandoned all afflictions belonging to heavenly and human existence that drive continued rebirth" could be read as suggesting total elimination of desire-realm afflictions -- which would be non-returning, not once-returning. The once-returner has *thinned* (薄/tanu) desire-realm afflictions, not eliminated them. The Chinese at this point is describing the specific subset of afflictions that produce *rebirth repetition* (再生相續) beyond one more cycle, not all desire-realm afflictions. The footnote [^thin_or_bound] correctly addresses the textual variant about "thinning" vs. "binding." The translation's phrasing is faithful to the Chinese but could lead a reader to conflate once-returning with non-returning. **Suggest adding a brief clarifying phrase or adjusting to: "the afflictions that drive repeated rebirth beyond one further cycle."**

**[O — Observation]** The definition "the noble path is called shramana; the severance of afflictions is called fruit" (line 316) is the standard Sarvastivada-Yogacara formulation (cf. AKB VI.50). Correct.

**[O — Observation]** The text correctly notes the causal chain: "a later-arising path -- whether intermediate or superior -- is the fruit produced by an earlier-arising path" (line 316). This establishes the progressive character of the four fruits. Doctrinally standard.

## 4. F29.13-14: Person Typology

### F29.13: Behavioral Types and the Thin-Dust Person

**[CORRECT]** The five conduct types (greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive thought) plus equal-part and thin-dust are standard Yogacara person typology, found in the Yogacarabhumi's earlier fascicles (26.1-26.2) and in the Visuddhimagga III.74-97.

**[CORRECT]** The seven qualities of the thin-dust person (manda-rajas):
1. Freedom from heavy obstructions (three: karmic, afflictive, maturation)
2. Initial purity (pure discipline + upright view)
3. Complete accumulations (four: merit, wisdom, prior-life, present-life)
4. Abundant pure faith
5. Endowment with discernment
6. Endowment with merit
7. Endowment with virtue

Cross-checked against Chinese: matches exactly. The three obstructions (業障, 煩惱障, 異熟障) are standard Yogacara-Sarvastivada categories. The five heinous deeds (五無間業) are correctly identified. The four accumulations (福德資糧, 智慧資糧, 先世資糧, 現法資糧) are correctly rendered.

**[m — minor]** The translation of 正直見 as "upright view" (line 372) is acceptable. The Chinese explains four qualities of this view: (1) 淨信相應 associated with pure faith, (2) 勝解相應 associated with resolute conviction, (3) 遠離誑諂 free from deception and flattery, (4) 善思法義無惑無疑加行出離 well-considered reflection oriented toward liberation without confusion or doubt. All four are correctly translated. However, "upright view" could also be rendered as "straight view" or "correct view" — "upright" works well for 正直 and avoids confusion with "right view" (正見, samyag-drishti), which is a different concept.

### F29.14: Six Categories of Persons

**[CORRECT]** The six categories are:
1. Shramana (4 subtypes: mastered path, teaches path, lives by path, corrupts path)
2. Brahmin (3 subtypes: by birth, by designation, by right conduct)
3. Pure conduct (3 subtypes: accepted renunciation, temporary severance, ultimate severance)
4. Bhikshu (5 subtypes: by begging, self-proclaimed, by designation, destroys afflictions, formally ordained)
5. Diligent (3 subtypes: ceasing transgression, ceasing engagement, ceasing afflictions)
6. Renunciant (2 subtypes: well-taught dharma/vinaya, wrongly-taught)

**Total subtypes: 4+3+3+5+3+2 = 20. Verified against Chinese.**

**[CORRECT]** The four shramana types match canonical sources (MN 40, AN 4.241). The characterization of learners and beyond-learners as "shramanas who have mastered the path" (勝道沙門) is standard Yogacara. The passage about the Blessed One saying these four exist only where the eightfold path is found matches standard canonical sources (DN 16, MN 11).

**[M — Moderate]** The first alternative characterization of the "shramana who has mastered the path" (line 402) says: "The well-gone ones (sugata) -- those who have realized the complete and permanent exhaustion of greed, aversion, and delusion -- are shramanas who have mastered the path." The Chinese: 諸善逝名勝道沙門, and then 諸善逝者謂已證得貪瞋癡等無餘永盡. This first reading identifies sugatas (Buddhas) as the supreme-path shramana. But the second reading (line 404) identifies 學無學 (learners and beyond-learners) as the supreme-path shramana: "Alternatively: learners and beyond-learners are called shramanas who have mastered the path." The Chinese has 又學無學名勝道沙門. The translation correctly preserves both readings. However, the first reading equating sugata exclusively with the exhaustion of greed/aversion/delusion is standard, while the second reading extends "mastered the path" to all noble persons (learners + beyond-learners). Both are attested in the Yogacarabhumi. **No error; correctly translating two complementary interpretations.**

**[O — Observation]** The three types of pure conduct (brahmacarya) present an interesting gradation: (1) undertaking celibacy (formal vow), (2) ordinary beings who have separated from desire-realm desire through worldly paths (temporary, since they can regress), (3) noble ones who have attained non-returning or arhatship (permanent). This correctly maps to the Yogacara understanding of brahmacarya as progressive: vow-based, meditation-based, and realization-based. Doctrinally standard.

**[O — Observation]** The five types of bhikshu correspond to standard Vinaya classifications. The inclusion of "bhikshu who destroys afflictions" (破壞煩惱苾芻) as the fourth type -- representing the arhat -- alongside the formally ordained bhikshu as the fifth correctly represents the Yogacara position that spiritual attainment and institutional ordination are distinct qualifications. Standard.

## 5. F29.16: Four Maras

### Identification

**[CORRECT]** The four maras are:
1. Skandha-mara (蘊魔) -- the five appropriating skandhas
2. Klesha-mara (煩惱魔) -- all afflictions of the three realms
3. Mrtyu-mara (死魔) -- death/perishing of beings
4. Devaputra-mara (天魔) -- the celestial being who obstructs practitioners

This is the standard four-mara classification found across all Buddhist traditions (SN 23.1; Visuddhimagga XVII; Abhidharmakosha VI.58).

### Fourfold Relational Analysis

**[CORRECT]** The text presents the four maras through their relation to death:
- Skandha-mara = what death rests upon (substrate)
- Klesha-mara = what brings death about (cause of rebirth -> death)
- Mrtyu-mara = death itself (cessation of life faculty)
- Devaputra-mara = what obstructs transcending death

This relational analysis is found in the Chinese (若死所依.若能令死.若正是死.若於其死作障礙事不令超越) and the translation renders it correctly. This systematic four-part analysis is a distinctive Yogacarabhumi contribution, not merely listing the four maras but showing their structural interrelation.

### Mara's Activities

**[CORRECT]** The catalog of mara's activities is correctly organized around the foundational practices: (1) arising of wholesome desire for liberation, (2) guarding sense gates, (3) moderation in food, (4) wakefulness practice, (5) full awareness during activities, (6) doubt about Triple Gem and truths, (7) frightening apparitions in solitude, (8) attachment to gains/praise/status. Each is identified as "the work of mara." This structure maps to the practices outlined in earlier fascicles, confirming the Sravakabhumi's practical orientation.

**[m — minor]** The translation of 天魔 as "celestial mara" (line 478) rather than "devaputra-mara" in the running text is acceptable -- the Sanskrit is given in the header and the glossary. The Chinese 天魔 literally means "heavenly mara" and refers to a son of the gods (devaputra) in the highest desire-realm heaven who has attained great autonomous power. The description "born at the highest level of the desire realm who has attained great power" (line 480) is correct -- this refers to the Paranirmita-vashavartin (他化自在天) heaven, though the text does not name the heaven explicitly. The Chinese (有生欲界最上天子得大自在) confirms: "a heavenly being born at the peak of the desire realm, possessing great autonomous power."

**[O — Observation]** The passage about the limits of mara's power (lines 488-492) correctly distinguishes three categories: (1) those who have separated from desire through worldly paths -- mara temporarily loses power over them but they remain "bound by mara's bonds" since they will return to this realm; (2) those who have not separated from desire -- entirely under mara's power; (3) implicitly, those who have attained the supramundane path -- they escape mara's net entirely. This three-way distinction is doctrinally standard. The point that worldly-path dispassion does not free one from mara's net (because regression is possible) is an important Yogacara teaching correctly rendered.

**No substantive issues found.**

## 6. F29.17: Three Causes of Fruitless Practice

### Identification

**[CORRECT]** The three causes are:
1. Faculties not sufficiently developed (諸根未積集)
2. Instructions unsuitable (教授不隨順)
3. Power of samadhi weak (等持力微劣)

Cross-checked against Chinese at T30.0448b04: 一由諸根未積集故.二由教授不隨順故.三由等持力微劣故. The translation matches exactly.

### Doctrinal Soundness

**[CORRECT]** The three-factor analysis is doctrinally sound. It reflects the standard Yogacara teaching that progress requires the convergence of internal capacity (faculties), external guidance (teaching), and meditative attainment (samadhi). The text correctly specifies that any one factor alone -- or even two of three -- is insufficient, and only the conjunction of all three produces "certain fruit" (決定有果).

**[m — minor]** The translation renders 諸根未積集 as "one's faculties have not been sufficiently developed" (line 524). The Chinese 積集 literally means "accumulated" or "gathered together" -- suggesting that the faculties need to be brought together and matured. "Sufficiently developed" captures the sense but loses the nuance of 積集 as accumulation/convergence. "Not yet accumulated" or "not yet converged" would be more literal. This is minor: the doctrinal content is correctly communicated. The Chinese at line 208 uses the same phrase and gives the combinatorial logic (all three needed) which the translation renders faithfully.

**[O — Observation]** The six-permutation proof (lines 526-528) -- showing that each possible pair without the third fails, and only the complete triad succeeds -- is a standard Yogacara argumentative structure (also used for the three gates of liberation, etc.). The translation handles this clearly and concisely.

**No substantive issues found.**

### Overall Assessment

The translation of these path-factor sections is doctrinally sound. All 37 factors are correctly enumerated (confirmed by counting). The four foundations of mindfulness structure is correct and well-analyzed. The four shramana fruits correctly present the standard Sarvastivada-Yogacara attainment framework with the three-fetter/domain analysis that is distinctive to the Yogacarabhumi. The person typology across sections 29.13-29.15 is standard Yogacara and internally consistent. The four maras are correctly presented with the distinctive relational analysis. The three causes of fruitless practice are doctrinally standard and correctly rendered.

The one moderate finding concerns the once-returner description, which could benefit from a small clarification to prevent confusion with the non-returner's complete severance. All other findings are minor or observational.

The translator's notes throughout are of high quality -- they correctly identify standard cross-traditional parallels (Visuddhimagga, Abhidharmakosha, Pali canonical sources) as well as features distinctive to the Yogacara-Sarvastivada tradition. The note on the 縛/薄 textual variant is particularly well-handled.
