## Issue 1 — Corpse-stage enumeration: order vs. Satipatthana standard

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.6, line 459 (external impurity of putrefaction list)
- **Problem:** The translation lists the external corpse stages as: "the livid, the festering, the decayed, the bloated, the gnawed, the blood-red, the scattered, bones, sinew-connected bones, and the skeleton." This faithfully follows the Chinese source order (青瘀、膿爛、變壞、膖脹、食噉、變赤、散壞、骨、鎖、骨鎖). However, footnote [^corpse_stages] (line 582) claims these "correspond to the cemetery contemplations (*shitavana-bhavana*) taught in the sutras (cf. MN 10, the Satipatthana Sutta)" and the Visuddhimagga (VI.1-95). The correspondence is only partial and the note risks misleading. The Satipatthana Sutta (MN 10) has nine progressive stages of decomposition (bloated-livid-festering through bones-to-dust; see Analayo p. 1241). The Visuddhimagga (VI.1) enumerates ten types of foulness in a non-progressive order (bloated, livid, festering, cut up, gnawed, scattered, hacked-and-scattered, bleeding, worm-infested, skeleton). The Sravakabhumi list has its own distinct order and includes items absent from the Visuddhimagga list (decayed/變壞, blood-red/變赤) while omitting others present in Visuddhimagga (cut up, hacked-and-scattered, bleeding, worm-infested). Saying these "correspond" without qualification could mislead a reader into thinking the lists are identical.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga VI.1 (ten foulnesses); MN 10 / Analayo p.1089, 1239 (nine stages of decomposition); Analayo note 152 (Madhyama Agama has five stages, Ekottara Agama has eight).
- **Recommendation:** Footnote [^corpse_stages] should clarify that the Sravakabhumi list is distinctive and only partially overlaps with the Satipatthana and Visuddhimagga enumerations. Note the major differences: the Sravakabhumi has a different count (10 items listed, but grouped differently from the Visuddhimagga's 10), includes blood-red and decayed as stages not found in the Visuddhimagga's tenfold asubha, and lacks the Visuddhimagga's cut-up, hacked-and-scattered, bleeding, and worm-infested. The phrase "an extensive parallel treatment" overstates the parallelism.

## Issue 2 — Maitri defined for beings "without suffering and without happiness"

- **Severity:** Moderate
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 528
- **Problem:** The translation reads: "With respect to the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral -- whether beings are without suffering and without happiness: wishing equally to give them happiness is loving-kindness (*maitri*)." The Chinese says: 若於無苦無樂親怨中三品有情。平等欲與其樂。當知是慈。 This is doctrinally significant. The text says maitri operates specifically with respect to beings who are *without suffering and without happiness* (無苦無樂), i.e., beings in a neutral-feeling state. This is a precise Yogacara distinction: maitri addresses beings in a neutral state by wishing them happiness; karuna addresses beings with suffering (有苦) by wishing to remove it; mudita addresses beings with happiness (有樂) by rejoicing. The translation's phrasing "whether beings are without suffering and without happiness" could be read as a casual aside or a conditional clause, weakening the force of what is actually a definitional criterion. The Visuddhimagga (IX.93) defines maitri/metta differently -- its characteristic is "promoting the aspect of welfare" without this restriction to neutral-state beings. The Sravakabhumi's definition is distinctive and more technically precise; the translation should make this unmistakably clear rather than leaving it ambiguous.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Chinese source: 若於無苦無樂親怨中三品有情。平等欲與其樂。當知是慈。 Visuddhimagga IX.93-96 for the standard Theravada definitions by contrast.
- **Recommendation:** Restructure the sentence to make the definitional force clear. For example: "With respect to those among the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral who are without suffering and without happiness -- wishing equally to give them happiness: this should be understood as loving-kindness (*maitri*)." A translator's note should flag that this tripartite definition (maitri for neutral-state beings, karuna for suffering beings, mudita for happy beings) is a distinctive Yogacara formulation that differs from the Pali commentarial tradition where maitri is defined more broadly as wishing welfare regardless of the object-being's current affective state.

## Issue 3 — Compassion and sympathetic joy conflated in presentation

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 528
- **Problem:** The translation reads: "wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion (*karuna*); wishing to rejoice in their happiness is sympathetic joy (*mudita*)." The Chinese says: 平等欲拔其苦欲慶其樂。當知是悲是喜。 The translation handles this adequately but omits the structural signal 當知 ("one should understand") that brackets the definition. More importantly, the sentence runs compassion and joy together in a way that could blur the doctrinal distinction between karuna's object (beings with suffering) and mudita's object (beings with happiness). The source text immediately clarifies: 有苦有情是悲所緣。有樂有情是喜所緣 -- "Beings with suffering are the object of compassion. Beings with happiness are the object of sympathetic joy." The translation does include this clarification but as a continuous run-on, making it easy to miss that these are two sharply distinct practices with distinct objects. This matters doctrinally because the Yogacara tradition here is more precise than the Pali commentarial tradition about the object-domain of each brahmavihara.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga IX.93-96 (characteristic definitions of each brahmavihara); source text 有苦有情是悲所緣。有樂有情是喜所緣.
- **Recommendation:** Consider formatting the maitri/karuna/mudita definitions as a numbered or bulleted list to visually reinforce the tripartite structure. Each brahmavihara has (a) a specific object-domain defined by the affective state of beings and (b) a specific wish. This tripartite schema is a key doctrinal contribution of this passage.

## Issue 4 — "Sentient beings" in aversion-purification sentence (terminology violation)

- **Severity:** Moderate
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 530
- **Problem:** The translation reads: "If a person of aversion-conduct practices loving-kindness and compassion toward all sentient beings, their aversion becomes attenuated." The Chinese says: 若有瞋行補特伽羅。於諸有情修習慈愍令瞋微薄。名於瞋恚心得清淨。The Chinese 有情 (*sattva*) is correctly rendered as "sentient beings" here -- this is NOT a case of confusing 補特伽羅 (*pudgala*) with *sattva*. However, the terminology watchlist in `the terminology watchlist` has the entry: "sentient being (for 補特伽羅) | person / individual" with the note "補特伽羅 = pudgala, not sattva." This line does NOT translate 補特伽羅 as "sentient being" -- it translates 有情 as "sentient being," which is correct. So this is actually NOT a terminology violation upon close examination. However, the phrasing "toward all sentient beings" may create a minor register inconsistency, since elsewhere the translation uses "beings" or "persons" for the objects of maitri practice (line 514: "toward those in the category of friends, the category of enemies, or the category of the neutral"). A footnote or parenthetical noting that 有情 (*sattva*) here refers to sentient beings as the objects of brahmavihara practice would maintain register consistency.
- **Doctrinal reference:** The distinction between pudgala and sattva is doctrinally significant in Yogacara; see glossary entries for both.
- **Recommendation:** Downgraded from initial assessment. The rendering is technically correct (有情 = sattva = sentient being). Consider adding "(*sattva*)" after "sentient beings" for terminological consistency with the project's Sanskrit-annotation practice. The more significant issue is the second half of the sentence -- see Issue 5.

## Issue 5 — Incomplete rendering of the concluding formula

- **Severity:** Moderate
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 530
- **Problem:** The translation renders the conclusion as: "their aversion becomes attenuated. This is called the purification of the mind from aversion." The Chinese is: 令瞋微薄。名於瞋恚心得清淨。 The phrase 令瞋微薄 literally means "causes aversion to become thin/attenuated." The phrase 名於瞋恚心得清淨 literally means "this is called obtaining purification of the mind with respect to aversion." The translation splits this into two sentences but renders the content accurately. However, the phrase "their aversion becomes attenuated" could mislead a reader into thinking this is a temporary suppression rather than a purificatory practice. The Chinese 清淨 (purification/becoming pure) carries a stronger connotation of genuine cleansing. The word 微薄 (thin, attenuated) does suggest a gradual process rather than complete elimination, which is doctrinally correct -- brahmavihara practice at the shamatha level suppresses afflictions without permanently uprooting them (that requires vipashyana). The translation conveys this correctly, but a note would help readers understand that "attenuated" and "purification" operate at different levels: attenuation describes the experiential effect, while "purification" is the formal category name for this class of meditation object. This is not an error but a potential source of confusion.
- **Doctrinal reference:** The Visuddhimagga (IX.1-2) similarly distinguishes the suppression function of brahmaviharas (suppressing ill will) from the uprooting function of insight. The Sravakabhumi maintains this same distinction with the term 微薄 (attenuated, not eliminated).
- **Recommendation:** Minor. No change to the translation text is needed. Consider a brief translator's note clarifying that "attenuated" reflects the shamatha-level function of brahmavihara practice -- it suppresses aversion without permanently removing it.

## Issue 6 — Equanimity (upeksha) as the fourth brahmavihara: footnote claim

- **Severity:** Moderate
- **Location:** Footnote [^maitri_object], line 592
- **Problem:** The footnote states: "Equanimity (*upeksha*), the fourth brahmavihara, is not explicitly named here but is implied in the 'equally established' intention." This is a defensible interpretive claim but requires careful scrutiny. The Chinese 平等安住利益意樂 ("equally established in an intention of benefit") uses 平等 ("equal, impartial"), which is related to the quality of equanimity (upeksha) but is not the same as naming upeksha as a brahmavihara. The text explicitly names maitri (慈), karuna (悲), and mudita (喜) but does not name upeksha (捨). The footnote's claim that equanimity is "implied" could be: (a) correct, if the equal/impartial regard across friend/enemy/neutral is understood as the equanimous basis for the other three; or (b) overreaching, since the text may deliberately subsume the four brahmaviharas under the heading of 慈愍 (maitri-karuna) without separately naming upeksha because it is treated as the mental posture that underlies the practice rather than a distinct object. The Visuddhimagga (IX.88-90) treats equanimity as requiring prior development of the first three brahmaviharas and as arising only at the fourth dhyana -- it is doctrinally subsequent, not simultaneous. Saying upeksha is "implied" here risks anachronistically importing the fourfold schema into a passage that may intentionally present a threefold schema under the 慈愍 heading.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga IX.88-90, 104 (equanimity as the "outcome" of the first three divine abidings, requiring the third dhyana as prerequisite); IX.111 (only the first three are of triple/quadruple dhyana; equanimity belongs to the remaining single dhyana).
- **Recommendation:** Revise the footnote to be more cautious. Rather than asserting upeksha is "implied," note that the text presents three of the four brahmaviharas explicitly (maitri, karuna, mudita) under the unified heading 慈愍. Whether upeksha is implied in the 平等 (equal/impartial) quality or is simply not treated in this section is a question the text leaves open. The Visuddhimagga treats equanimity as doctrinally subsequent to and dependent upon the first three, which may explain its absence here.

## Issue 7 — "Vast" for desire-realm happiness: questionable mapping

- **Severity:** Moderate
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 522; footnote [^three_grades], line 594
- **Problem:** The translation reads: "The happiness of the desire realm is called 'vast.' The happiness of the first and second dhyanas is called 'great.' The happiness of the third dhyana is called 'immeasurable.'" The Chinese says: 欲界快樂名廣。初二靜慮地快樂名大。第三靜慮地快樂名無量。This is an accurate rendering. The footnote explains: "'vast' (廣) for the desire realm, 'great' (大) for the first and second dhyanas, and 'immeasurable' (無量) for the third dhyana." The doctrinal issue is whether this mapping is being correctly understood. The sutra formula being glossed says 廣大無量 (*vipula-apramana*, "vast and immeasurable"). The Sravakabhumi is parsing these three characters (廣, 大, 無量) as referring to three distinct grades of happiness in ascending order. This is a distinctive exegetical move. Standard Abhidharma and Pali commentarial treatments (e.g., Visuddhimagga IX.1-130) treat 廣大無量 as a compound describing the all-encompassing scope of brahmavihara practice (i.e., "vast, great, and immeasurable" as predicates of the practice's reach toward all beings), not as three distinct grades of happiness mapped to cosmological levels. The Sravakabhumi's interpretation is a legitimate Yogacara reading but is non-standard. The footnote acknowledges the third-dhyana mapping but does not flag that this entire parsing is a distinctive Yogacara exegesis, not the mainstream reading of the sutra formula.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga IX.103-110 (the "measureless" scope of brahmaviharas refers to the unlimited number of beings, not grades of happiness); MN 99: 廣大無量 as description of the reach of metta across beings.
- **Recommendation:** Add a note to footnote [^three_grades] explicitly stating that this parsing of 廣大無量 into three cosmological grades is a distinctive Yogacara exegesis. In the broader Buddhist commentarial tradition, these terms typically describe the boundless scope of the practice's reach, not three kinds of happiness at different cosmological levels. Both readings are legitimate, but readers should be aware of the interpretive distinctiveness.

## Issue 8 — "Without enmity" as the supreme intention of benefit: structural analysis

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.7, lines 520-521
- **Problem:** The translation reads: "'Without enmity' names the supreme intention of benefit. This freedom from enmity is made clear by two phrases: 'without hostility' means not wishing to oppose or contend; 'without harm' means not wishing to cause injury." The Chinese says: 由無怨故名為增上利益意樂。此無怨性二句所顯。謂無敵對故無損惱故。不欲相違諍義是無敵對。不欲不饒益義是無損害。The translation accurately renders the structural analysis. The issue is that the source text identifies three terms in the sutra formula (無怨 = without enmity, 無敵 = without hostility, 無損害 = without harm) and explains the relationship: 無怨 (without enmity) is the overarching quality, and the other two are its two aspects. The translation renders this correctly. No doctrinal error, but the rendering "not wishing to oppose or contend" for 不欲相違諍 could be more precise. The Chinese 相違諍 specifically means "contradicting and quarreling" -- active hostility in disputation. "Not wishing to oppose or contend" is adequate but slightly weak.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Standard sutra formula: mettasahagatena cetasa avera abyapajjha (Pali). The Sanskrit equivalents are avairin, asapatna, avyabadha.
- **Recommendation:** No change to translation text needed. The rendering is adequate. Optionally, a parenthetical could note the Sanskrit: "without hostility (*asapatna*) means not wishing to oppose or contend; without harm (*avyabadha*) means not wishing to cause injury."

## Issue 9 — Five types of desire: doctrinal distinctiveness unannounced

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.6, lines 475-482; footnote [^five_desires], line 586
- **Problem:** The footnote correctly states: "This fivefold scheme is unique to the Sravakabhumi's presentation." This is an important scholarly note. However, the footnote could mislead in describing item (4) as "craving for forms and appearances (attachment to the form realm)." The Chinese 色欲色貪 means "desire for forms, craving for forms" -- but the mapping in the main text (line 506) purifies this through "impurity by comparison" (觀待不淨), which operates by showing that the form realm appears impure when compared to a higher purity. This suggests that "form" here (色) does refer to rupa-dhatu (the form realm) specifically, not to visual forms or physical appearances in a general sense. The footnote's parenthetical "(attachment to the form realm)" is correct on the doctrinal reading, but the main text rendering "desire and craving for forms" (line 480) could be misread as referring to visual forms (*rupa* in the sense of visible objects) rather than to the form realm (*rupa-dhatu*). A reader unfamiliar with the mapping would not know that "forms" here means the entire form realm.
- **Doctrinal reference:** The mapping at line 506: "Through the impurity by comparison as object, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for forms." The impurity-by-comparison specifically compares form-realm purity to formless-realm purity.
- **Recommendation:** Consider rendering item (4) as "desire and craving for the form realm (*rupa-dhatu*)" rather than "desire and craving for forms," or at minimum add a parenthetical "(i.e., attachment to form-realm existence)" in the main text. The current rendering is ambiguous between *rupa* as visible form and *rupa* as the cosmological form realm.

## Issue 10 — Missing context: no mention of the "equally-disposed" and "thin-dust" types

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.6, line 508
- **Problem:** The closing paragraph states: "the primary intention in this context is specifically the impurity of putrefaction. The remaining types of impurity also serve as purifying-conduct objects in their own right." The Chinese says: 今此義中本意。唯取朽穢不淨。所餘不淨亦是其餘淨行所緣。 This is accurately rendered. However, a doctrinal note could clarify what "also serve as purifying-conduct objects in their own right" means. The Sravakabhumi's framework (established in section 26.5) matches five behavioral types to five purifying-conduct objects. The text is here noting that while the primary impurity practice for greed types is the impurity of putrefaction (cemetery contemplation), the other five impurities (suffering, inferiority, comparison, affliction, rapid disintegration) also function as purifying-conduct objects -- but for different purposes or combinations. The reader who has not internalized the fivefold schema may not understand what this sentence means. No error, but missing context.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Section 26.5 (the five-fold matching); Visuddhimagga III.121 (suitability of meditation subjects to temperament).
- **Recommendation:** Consider a brief translator's note stating that the remaining impurities (suffering through rapid disintegration) extend beyond the strict cemetery-contemplation framework and address desire at progressively higher cosmological levels, culminating in the desire for satkaya itself. This is what makes the Sravakabhumi's impurity scheme broader than the standard charnel-ground contemplation.

## Issue 11 — Body-parts list: count discrepancy with the dvattimsa-kaya

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.6, line 457; footnote [^body_parts], line 580
- **Problem:** Footnote [^body_parts] states this list "closely parallels the thirty-two parts of the body (*dvattimsatikaya*) enumerated in the Pali Satipatthana Sutta (MN 10)." The traditional Pali list has 31 or 32 items (varying by inclusion of brain). The Chinese list in the source is: 髮毛爪齒、塵垢皮肉、骸骨筋脈、心膽肝肺、大腸小腸、生藏熟藏、肚胃脾腎、膿血熱痰、肪膏肌髓、腦膜洟唾、淚汗屎尿. The translation enumerates: "hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, dust and grime, skin, flesh, bones, sinews, veins, heart, gallbladder, liver, lungs, large intestine, small intestine, the upper stomach, the lower stomach, belly, spleen, kidneys, pus, blood, hot phlegm, fat, grease, muscle, marrow, brain membrane, mucus, saliva, tears, sweat, excrement, and urine." This totals approximately 34-35 items. The list includes items not in the standard Pali 32 (e.g., "dust and grime"/塵垢, "the upper stomach"/生藏 and "the lower stomach"/熟藏 as separate from "belly"/肚, "muscle"/肌 alongside "flesh"/肉). The footnote's claim of "close parallel" is correct in a general sense, but the specific enumeration differs. Furthermore, "hot phlegm" (熱痰) is not the same as the Pali "phlegm" (*semha*) -- 熱痰 specifically means "heated phlegm" or possibly "bile-heated mucus," which may correspond to *pitta* (bile) rather than *semha* (phlegm) in some Abhidharma enumerations.
- **Doctrinal reference:** MN 10 (31 parts without brain; 32 with brain per later tradition); Visuddhimagga VIII.42ff (32 parts grouped in six pentads and a pair); Analayo pp. 5039-5040 (anatomical contemplation).
- **Recommendation:** Footnote [^body_parts] should note that while the list parallels the *dvattimsatikaya*, the Sravakabhumi version has its own distinct enumeration with several items not found in the Pali standard list. "Closely parallels" is acceptable but could note the divergences.

## Issue 12 — Third-dhyana happiness called "immeasurable": needs doctrinal anchor

- **Severity:** Minor
- **Location:** Section 26.7, line 522; footnote [^three_grades], line 594
- **Problem:** The footnote states: "The third dhyana is traditionally considered the peak of experiential bliss in Buddhist cosmology -- hence its happiness is called 'immeasurable.'" This is correct and well-stated. However, it would benefit from noting the specific doctrinal basis: the third dhyana is described in the standard formula as the state where "the noble ones declare: one who has equanimity and mindfulness dwells in happiness" (有捨有念安住樂住). The Visuddhimagga (IV.175-177) explains that the third-dhyana bliss is the most refined bodily-felt pleasure, surpassed in the fourth dhyana by equanimity which transcends pleasure/pain entirely. The footnote's subsequent clause -- "The fourth dhyana and formless attainments transcend pleasure/pain duality" -- is accurate but should note this is specifically because the fourth dhyana replaces sukha-vedana with upekkha-vedana.
- **Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga IV.175-177 (third dhyana as peak of sukha); DN 9 (standard dhyana formula).
- **Recommendation:** Minor enhancement to footnote. No change to translation text needed.
