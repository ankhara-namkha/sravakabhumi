# Readability Review — Fascicle 31, Sections 31.5–31.7
## Nine Practice Approaches (Introduction through Conclusion)
## Agent R — Practitioner/Scholar Perspective

**Read as:** 80% practitioner / 20% scholar. Register standard: Nanamoli, Bodhi, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee.

**Severity codes:** C = critical (structural breakdown), M = moderate (hinders reading), m = minor (noticeable but tolerable)

---

## Overall Assessment

Sections 31.5 and 31.7 are strong. The prose is largely alive, the nine practices are vivid and readable, and the painter simile in 31.7 is excellent — one of the best passages in the fascicle. The main weakness is concentrated in **31.6 (Timely Practice)**, which degrades into a scholastic Q&A catalog that loses the practitioner entirely. There are also scattered formula-monotony and nested-qualification problems throughout.

## Section 31.5 — Introduction and Approaches 1–4

### Finding 1 [M] — Formula monotony in approach (1)
**Location:** Approach (1) Appropriate practice paragraph

**Problem:** The phrase "If of the X-conduct type, on Y" repeats six times in succession with no variation, creating a mechanical list feel. The Chinese formula is justified, but rendered this way in English it sounds like a menu, not instruction. A practitioner would not feel they are being taught; they would feel they are reading a reference table.

**Current text:**
> If one is of the desire-conduct type, one settles the mind on impurity. If of the aversion-conduct type, on loving-kindness. If of the delusion-conduct type, on dependent origination. If of the pride-conduct type, on element differentiation. If of the discursive-thought-conduct type, on mindfulness of breathing. If of the equal-part type or the thin-dust type, one settles the mind on whatever single object one finds pleasing.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> The object must match the practitioner's character-type. Those of desire-conduct type settle on impurity; those of aversion-conduct type, on loving-kindness; those prone to delusion, on dependent origination; those prone to pride, on element differentiation; those prone to discursive thought, on mindfulness of breathing. For the equal-part type or the thin-dust type, any single pleasing object will serve. In each case, one practices diligently.

The rewrite breaks the mechanical "If of the X-conduct type, on Y" chain by introducing a framing sentence, shifts to a tighter parallel list, and ends with the action.

### Finding 3 [M] — Nested qualification
**Location:** Approach (4) Unslackened practice, countdown meditation paragraph

**Current text:**
> "If my life should last seven more days — six, five, four, three, two, one day — one hour, half an hour, a moment, half a moment, the time of a single meal, a single in-breath to out-breath, a single out-breath to in-breath — for however long I remain alive, I will attend to the Buddha's sacred teaching with diligent effort, cultivating yoga. In whatever time remains, I will surely accomplish much."

**Word count:** 76 words. Two em-dash interruptions fragment the sentence structure, and the main verb ("I will attend") is buried at word 42. However, this is direct speech rendering a meditation instruction, and the fragmentation may be intentional — it enacts the countdown. The content lands well for a practitioner despite the structural complexity.

**Verdict:** The sentence is long but the content is alive and the fragmentation serves the countdown. This is a judgment call: leave as-is, or split at the second em-dash:

> "If my life should last seven more days — six, five, four, three, two, one — or one hour, half an hour, a moment, half a moment, a single meal, a single breath — for however long I remain alive, I will attend to the Buddha's teaching with diligent effort. In whatever time remains, I will surely accomplish much."

Minor restructure only. The original is not broken.

## Section 31.6 — Approach 5: Timely Practice

### Finding 5 [C] — Register drift: scholastic catalog, not living instruction
**Location:** The entire 31.6 section

**Problem:** This section is structured as a long sequence of "What is X? ... What is the sign of X? ... When is the time for X?" repeated four times (calming, observation, uplifting, equanimity). In English, this Q&A pattern rapidly becomes mechanical and deadening. A practitioner who enters this section expecting to learn *how* to balance calming and observation will instead encounter a dry definitional catalog. The register drops from Bodhi/Nanamoli to reference manual.

The four-part repetition of "What is X / What is the sign of X / When is the time for X" creates monotony by the second cycle. By the third (uplifting), the reader has stopped absorbing and is just parsing.

**Current structure (schematic):**
```
What is calming? [definition]
What is the sign of calming? [two sub-types]
When is the time for calming? [conditions]

What is observation? [definition]
What is the sign of observation? [two sub-types]
When is the time for observation? [conditions]

What is uplifting? [single sentence]
What is the sign of uplifting? [single sentence]
When is the time for uplifting? [single sentence]

What is equanimity? [definition]
What is the sign of equanimity? [two sentences]
When is the time for equanimity? [single sentence]
```

This is a Q&A skeleton presented as prose. The information is there; the pedagogy is dead.

**Suggested rewrite approach (paragraph-level restructure):**

Rather than four separate Q&A blocks, integrate each element — nature, sign, and timing — into a coherent paragraph describing how the practitioner *uses* it. The Nanamoli model: explain the concept, give its operative sign, then state the condition of application — all in flowing prose. Reserve the bold headings for the four terms only, not for the sub-questions.

**Draft rewrite:**

> **Calming** is the nine stages of mental abiding — the mind becoming signless, free from conceptual elaboration, peaceful, evenly abiding, purely unified. Its signs are two: the object-sign (the cognitive image corresponding to shamatha objects, through which the mind stills) and the causal sign (a mind already habituated to shamatha now practicing vipashyana, so that future shamatha is purified). Calming is called for when the mind is agitated or in danger of agitation — or when a mind long-habituated to vipashyana has been disturbed by discursive thought or the press of daily demands.
>
> **Observation** is the contemplative investigation that proceeds through four modes, three gates, and the six aspects of differentiated objects. Its signs parallel those of calming: the object-sign (the cognitive image corresponding to vipashyana objects, through which wisdom investigates) and the causal sign (a mind habituated to vipashyana now practicing internal shamatha, so that future vipashyana is purified). Observation is called for when the mind sinks or is in danger of sinking — or when a mind trained in shamatha needs now to know its objects as they truly are.
>
> **Uplifting** is the taking up of a pure, excellent object to encourage, guide, and gladden the mind. Its sign is that energizing object itself, in accord with the arousal of diligent effort. The time for uplifting is whenever the mind has sunk low or is in danger of doing so.
>
> **Equanimity** is the mind's freedom from affliction toward its object — the quality of mental evenness. When both the calming and observation aspects are established, the mind possesses pliancy, uprightness, and spontaneous functioning: it is a trained mind that operates freely and naturally. The sign of equanimity is the letting go of striving — one does not generate excessive effort toward the object. This is the time for equanimity: when the mind has been freed from agitation in both the shamatha and vipashyana aspects.

This rewrite maintains all content, eliminates the triple Q&A scaffold, and flows as instruction rather than definition. Each paragraph can be read by a practitioner as guidance on *when and how* to apply each quality.

### Finding 7 [m] — Unexplained jargon: "three gates"
**Location:** 31.6, observation definition

**Current text:**
> It is the contemplation that operates through four modes, three gates, and the six aspects of differentiated objects.

**Problem:** "Four modes," "three gates," and "six aspects of differentiated objects" are all technical terms from earlier in the text. A practitioner reading 31.6 in isolation will not know what "three gates" refers to. There is no gloss or footnote. The six aspects were defined in 31.1–31.4. The four modes and three gates would benefit from either a brief gloss or a footnote.

**Fix:** Add a footnote: "The four modes, three gates, and six aspects were introduced in [earlier section reference]." Or add a brief parenthetical: "four modes (examination, investigation, full investigation, and thorough investigation) ... three gates ..." if those definitions have appeared earlier in the fascicle.

### Finding 9 [M] — Nested qualification / sentence overload
**Location:** Approach (9) Correct practice, description of the five types of clearing

**Current text:**
> *Internally collecting the mind* means: through the nine stages of mental abiding, with vipashyana as the leading force, one draws the mind inward. *Non-attending attention* means: from the very beginning, one turns away from all signs and abides without disturbance. *Redirected attention* means: one takes up the objects of another concentrative level, contemplating a different ground of concentration. *Antidote attention* means: one contemplates impurity to counteract attachment to purity, mindfulness of breathing to counteract discursive thought, the space element to counteract material form — and so on for each pairing. *Signless-realm attention* means: one ceases attending to all signs and turns attention to the signless realm.

**Assessment:** This passage is clear and well-executed. Each italicized term is defined cleanly in one sentence. The antidote-attention definition uses a well-constructed list. No structural issue.

### Finding 10 [M] — 50+ word sentence
**Location:** Approach (9), alternating-confidence paragraph

**Current text:**
> For this reason, the practitioner at that time neither abandons nor grasps the object. Because calming continues to operate toward the object, it is not abandoned. Because one does not form signs or engage in conceptualization regarding the object, it is not grasped. Through this internal collection of the mind, one clears the object — yet because one does not take up the observation aspect, the mind is undisturbed; and because one takes up the calming aspect, the mind still engages with the knowable object.

**Last sentence word count:** 47 words. Under threshold. But the sentence has four subordinate "because" clauses that create significant cognitive load. A practitioner may lose the thread.

**Fix:** Split the last sentence:
> Through this internal collection of the mind, one clears the object. The mind remains undisturbed because one does not take up the observation aspect; and because one takes up the calming aspect, the mind still engages with the knowable object.

### Finding 13 [m] — Translated-ese: "narrow scope / vast / immeasurable" paragraph
**Location:** Approach (9), scope-shifting paragraph

**Current text:**
> When confidence is generated at a narrow scope, one clears it at the narrow scope; the same applies to vast and immeasurable scopes. After clearing a narrow-scope object, one may generate confidence again at the narrow scope, or shift to the vast, or to the immeasurable. The same holds for vast and immeasurable objects.

**Problem:** This passage reads as close paraphrase of Chinese parallel construction — the repetition of "narrow scope / vast / immeasurable" three times in succession mirrors a Chinese listing pattern. In English, it sounds slightly mechanical. The final sentence ("The same holds for vast and immeasurable objects") is implied by what precedes it and adds little.

**Fix:**
> When confidence is generated at a narrow scope, it is cleared at the same scope. After clearing, one may generate confidence again at the narrow scope, or shift to the vast or immeasurable. The same principle applies throughout.
