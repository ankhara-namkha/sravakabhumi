## Findings

### 1. [m] Footnote imprecision: seven attentions and affliction-grades

**Location:** Footnote [^seven_attentions], line 78
**Text:** "the third through sixth progressively sever afflictions of upper, middle, and lower grades"
**Issue:** This is slightly inaccurate. The Chinese source (later in fascicle 33) specifies:
- 3rd (遠離作意, separation) severs **upper-grade** afflictions
- 4th (攝樂作意, joyful engagement) severs **middle-grade** afflictions
- 5th (觀察作意, investigation) is a **verification check** -- the practitioner tests whether afflictions are truly abandoned by deliberately attending to attractive objects; it does NOT itself sever afflictions
- 6th (加行究竟作意, culmination of application) severs **lower-grade** afflictions

The footnote collapses four attentions into "progressively sever," obscuring the distinct verificatory function of the 5th attention. Since the footnote is providing a scholarly overview of the entire framework, this imprecision could mislead readers about the structure.

**Recommendation:** Revise to: "the third and fourth sever upper and middle-grade afflictions; the fifth verifies whether latent tendencies remain; the sixth severs lower-grade afflictions; and the seventh is the actual entry into the higher attainment."

### 2. [m] Terminology: "inner defilement" in footnote [^own_char]

**Location:** Footnote [^own_char], line 128
**Text:** "Affliction-desire (*klesha-kama*, 煩惱欲) is the inner defilement of craving"
**Issue:** The project's terminology watchlist explicitly prohibits "defilements" for 煩惱, requiring "afflictions" instead (NTC/Tibetan register). While "defilement" here modifies klesha-kama rather than directly translating 煩惱, it introduces the Theravada register term that the project has deliberately excluded. A reader encountering "defilement" in a footnote may assume it is an accepted rendering.

**Recommendation:** Change to "the inner affliction of craving" or "the afflictive aspect of craving."

### 3. [O] Three viparyasas: doctrinally accurate assignment to feeling-bases

**Location:** Line 92 (own-characteristic passage) and footnote [^own_char]
**Observation:** The text correctly assigns:
- Pleasant feeling --> craving + inversions of perception and mind (想心倒, samjna-citta-viparyasa)
- Painful feeling --> anger and resentment (瞋恚, 忿恨)
- Neutral feeling --> ignorance + secondary afflictions (覆惱誑諂無慚無愧) + inversion of views (見倒, drsti-viparyasa)

This is doctrinally accurate per the Abhidharma tradition. The three types of viparyasa (perception, mind, view) are standardly distinguished, with view-inversion being the subtlest and most deeply rooted, hence its association with the obscurity of neutral feeling and ignorance. The Yogacarabhumi-sastra (Bendale section) and Abhidharmasamuccaya both support this assignment. No issue.

### 4. [O] Eight similes footnote: sutta references are substantively correct

**Location:** Footnote [^eight_similes], line 130
**Observation:** The footnote cites both MN 22 (Alagaddupama Sutta) and MN 54 (Potaliya Sutta). This dual citation is justified because:
- MN 54 contains seven of the eight similes (bone, flesh, torch, coals, serpent, dream, borrowed goods) as a systematic set.
- MN 22 contains overlapping similes (skeleton, flesh, torch, coals, dream, borrowed goods, fruit of a tree) plus additional ones (butcher's block, swords/spears, snake's head).
- The Sravakabhumi's set of eight appears to draw from both sources, combining MN 54's serpent simile with MN 22's tree-fruit simile.

The brief descriptions of each simile's meaning in the footnote are accurate and match the sutta explanations. No correction needed.

However, a minor enhancement: the footnote could note that the Chinese 凝血肉 ("clotted blood-flesh") corresponds to Pali *mamsapesi* ("piece of flesh" / "lump of meat") -- the Chinese adds the "blood" element, possibly reflecting Xuanzang's translation style or an alternate Indic recension.

### 5. [O] Four grounds of certainty: pramana framework correctly identified

**Location:** Line 114 and footnote [^reasoning_desires], line 134
**Observation:** The footnote correctly identifies:
1. 至教量 (agama-pramana) = scriptural authority
2. 現智見 = direct personal perception (corresponding to pratyaksha-pramana, though using different terminology than the glossary's 現證量)
3. 比度量 (anumana) = inference
4. 法性 (dharmata) = nature of dharmas (not a pramana)

The footnote's claim that "the first three are the Yogacara tradition's three valid cognitions (*pramana*)" is doctrinally accurate. The fourth item (dharmata) is correctly identified as a separate appeal beyond the three pramanas. This matches standard Yogacara epistemology as found in Dignaga and the Yogacarabhumi itself. No correction needed.

One note: the glossary entry for the three pramanas lists them as: (1) 至教量 agama-pramana, (2) 比度量 anumana-pramana, (3) 現證量 pratyaksha-pramana. The order in the text (scriptural authority first, then direct perception, then inference) differs from the standard logical order (perception, inference, testimony), but this reflects the Chinese source's own ordering and is not an error.

### 6. [O] No anachronisms detected

The sections are free of modern psychological or scientific terminology. The translation consistently uses traditional Buddhist categories: vedana (feeling), klesha (affliction), viparyasa (inversion), upaklesha (secondary affliction), pramana (valid cognition). No modern imports.

### 7. [O] Doctrinal framework: hearing-reflection-cultivation correctly applied

**Location:** Line 120 and footnote [^hearing_reflection], line 136
**Observation:** The text and footnote correctly note that the attention to characteristics remains at the level of hearing (shruta) and reflection (cinta), not yet reaching meditative cultivation (bhavana). The footnote accurately states that the transition to pure cultivation occurs with the next attention (conviction). This matches the Chinese 聞思間雜 and the standard Buddhist epistemological progression. No issue.
