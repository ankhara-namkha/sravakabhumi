### FLAG R-01 | H | Translated-ese / Nested Qualification (line 352)

**Passage:**
> "Among these practitioners, those of greed, aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive-thought conduct must first purify their conduct through the appropriate purifying-conduct objects before they can attain correct mental abiding."

**Problem:** "Purifying-conduct objects" is an untranslated compound standing in for a Chinese technical phrase. The sentence front-loads five character types, tucks the method ("purifying-conduct objects") inside a qualification, and buries the actual activity ("attain correct mental abiding") at the very end. A reader has to hold five items in working memory to reach the main verb. This is Chinese clause order, not English.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Practitioners whose predominant disposition is greed, aversion, delusion, pride, or discursive thinking must first work through a suitable purification exercise for that disposition before the mind can settle into correct abiding."

---

### FLAG R-02 | H | Unexplained Jargon (line 352 and throughout 29.13)

**Passage:**
> "purifying-conduct objects"

**Problem:** This phrase — used five times across 29.13 without ever being glossed — means "the meditation objects prescribed to purify each conduct type." A practitioner reader has no anchor for this compound. The word "objects" suggests things in the world; the intended meaning is "prescribed antidote practices." This jargon should be glossed on first use.

**Suggested fix at first use (line 352):**
> "…must first work through the appropriate antidote practice (purifying-conduct object) for their disposition…"

Then use whichever form thereafter: "antidote practice" or "purifying-conduct object," consistently.

### FLAG R-03 | M | Formula Monotony / Register Drift (lines 362–386 — the sevenfold list)

**Passage:** Items (3) through (7) of the thin-dust person's characteristics follow an identical template: bold number → noun phrase → colon → one sentence. Read consecutively, the list is taxonomically correct but inert. The practitioner has no sense of *why* these qualities matter or how they connect. The last two items in particular — (6) Endowment with merit and (7) Endowment with virtue — are simply enumerations of sub-qualities with no interpretive thread.

**Problem:** The list reads as a scholastic catalog. Items (1) and (2) are handled with nuance; by (5)–(7), each item collapses into a single sentence with no texture.

**Suggested approach:** No structural change needed, but items (5)–(7) need at least one sentence each that tells the reader *what this quality does for the practitioner*, not merely what it is. The current (5), for example, is serviceable; (6) and (7) are bare.

**Draft for (6):**
> "**(6) Endowment with merit**: one's appearance inspires confidence in others — dignified, approachable, clearly at ease in one's body. One enjoys health, longevity, a good reputation, and the respect of rulers and officials. These outward conditions ease the path: one receives what is needed and is not harassed or overlooked.

**Draft for (7):**
> "**(7) Endowment with virtue**: one is naturally inclined toward few wants and contentment — the shramana adornments described earlier — without having to strain for them. They are simply one's nature.

### FLAG R-04 | H | >50-word sentence / Nested Qualification (line 374)

**Passage:**
> "Because one reflects well on the meaning of the dharma and undertakes practice toward liberation without confusion or doubt, one properly reflects, deliberates, and investigates the meaning of impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self in all dharmas. Through this, confusion and doubt are eliminated, the two extreme views are abandoned, and one attains advancement. By these four qualities, the view is called upright."

**Word count:** The first sentence alone is 57 words. Combine with the two immediately following and the whole paragraph is a single cognitive load with three different "through this / by this" closures stacked at the end. The structure is:
- Because X → one does Y (sentence 1)
- Through this → Z (sentence 2)
- By these → conclusion (sentence 3)

This is the Chinese argumentative form (cause-effect-summary) translated directly. In English it reads as circular.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Reflecting well on the dharma's meaning — on impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self — one enters practice free from confusion and doubt. This reflection clears confusion, loosens the grip of extreme views, and opens the way forward. These four qualities together make the view upright."

### FLAG R-05 | M | Translated-ese / Long sentence (line 374, the full upright-view paragraph)

**Passage (lines 372–374, the whole upright-view explanation):**
> "In detail: because upright view is associated with pure faith, one cannot be drawn away from the Buddha's true dharma and vinaya. Because it is associated with resolute conviction, one develops deep confidence regarding the inconceivable powers of the tathagatas and noble disciples, the varieties of birth, the profound dharma teachings, and the indeterminate matters — without alarm, terror, or fear. Because it is free from deception and flattery, one's view is genuine: one practices in accord with the teaching and presents oneself truthfully. Because one reflects well on the meaning of the dharma and undertakes practice toward liberation without confusion or doubt…"

**Problem:** Four consecutive "because X → one does Y" structures. The Chinese parallel is clear; the English is mechanical. Each clause is individually correct but the accumulation numbs the reader. This is formula monotony of grammatical structure, not just vocabulary.

**Suggested approach:** Break the parallelism after the second clause. The four qualities of upright view can be presented as a short list, then unpacked in prose with varied sentence openers:

> "Upright view has four characteristics. Because it is grounded in pure faith, one holds firm in the Buddha's dharma and vinaya and is not pulled away by other teachings. Resolute conviction deepens this: one rests in confidence about the inconceivable powers of the tathagatas, the varieties of birth, the profound dharma teachings, and the indeterminate matters — without alarm, without fear. Free from deception and flattery, one's view is honest: one practices as taught and presents oneself as one is. And through sustained reflection on impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, confusion and doubt dissolve, the two extreme views fall away, and one advances."

### FLAG R-06 | H | Translated-ese / Register drift (line 376 — "complete accumulations" paragraph)

**Passage:**
> "**(3) Complete accumulations** (*sambhara*) are of four kinds, as broadly explained previously. In brief: *merit accumulations* provide suitable resources and favorable conditions in this life — abundant provisions, genuine fields of merit as spiritual friends, freedom from obstructions, and the ability to practice diligently. *Wisdom accumulations* furnish discernment and the capacity to understand the meaning of what is well-taught and poorly taught, and to obtain suitable instruction, guidance, and admonishment. *Prior-life accumulations* are wholesome roots gathered in past lives, through which one attains perfect faculties in this life. *Present-life accumulations* include the desire for wholesome dharma, mature faculties, and the discipline of moral training and sense-faculty restraint, as explained previously."

**Problem:** "As broadly explained previously" appears twice in close succession (here and in line 376). More critically, "genuine fields of merit as spiritual friends" is cryptic: what are "fields of merit"? And the phrase "furnish discernment and the capacity to understand the meaning of what is well-taught and poorly taught" sounds like a translation of a translation — no practitioner would write "the capacity to understand the meaning of what is well-taught and poorly taught." It's a calque of a Chinese phrase structure.

**Suggested rewrite (partial):**
> "*Wisdom accumulations* give one the discernment to distinguish sound teaching from flawed teaching, and the capacity to receive instruction, correction, and guidance and actually use it."

And gloss "fields of merit" at first use:
> "…genuine teachers and companions — fields of merit (*punya-kshetra*) — as spiritual friends…"

### FLAG R-07 | M | Formula monotony — "as explained previously" (lines 372, 376, 384, 426)

**Occurrences (partial list in this passage):**
- Line 372: "as explained previously"
- Line 376: "as broadly explained previously" (twice)
- Line 384: "whose characteristics should be understood as the shramana adornments previously described"
- Line 426: "as explained previously"

**Problem:** Five near-identical backward references in 60 lines. Each is individually defensible but collectively they give the impression that the text is gestural and incomplete — the author keeps pointing elsewhere rather than delivering. In a practice-context text this undercuts trust.

**Suggested approach:** Either consolidate into one reference with a section citation, or replace with a brief re-statement where the content is short enough (e.g., item 7 on virtue). Only keep the cross-reference if the referenced content is genuinely lengthy and essential to understanding.

### FLAG R-08 | M | Unexplained jargon (line 404 — "turns the true dharma eye")

**Passage:**
> "…they sustain the conventional dharma and vinaya and turn the true dharma eye so that it does not cease."

**Problem:** "Turn the true dharma eye" is either an idiom meaning "keep the dharma alive in the world" or a calque of a Chinese metaphor that doesn't fully translate. A practitioner reader without this background will find it opaque. If it means "keep the dharma wheel turning" or "preserve the capacity for awakening in the world," say so, or gloss it.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "…they sustain the conventional dharma and vinaya and keep the true dharma eye alive — preserving the lineage of transmission so it does not cease."

### FLAG R-09 | H | >50-word sentence / Nested qualification (line 406)

**Passage:**
> "An ordinary person (*prthag-jana*) of trainable nature who practices right conduct for self-benefit, possessing shame and remorse, delighting in proper training — striving to obtain the unobtained, realize the unrealized, and witness the unwitnessed, with the power and ability to do so — is a shramana who lives by the path."

**Word count:** 57 words. The main subject ("ordinary person") is loaded with four modifying clauses before the predicate ("is a shramana who lives by the path") appears. This is a relative-clause stack — fine in Chinese, which allows this front-loading; awkward in English.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "An ordinary person (*prthag-jana*) of trainable nature — one who practices right conduct for self-benefit, who has shame and remorse, who delights in proper training, who strives to obtain what is not yet obtained, realize what is not yet realized, and witness what is not yet witnessed, with the genuine capacity to do so — is a shramana who lives by the path."

The em-dash pair separates the defining cluster from the predicate, which reduces the stacking slightly, but a more thorough rewrite would front the predicate:

> "A shramana who lives by the path is an ordinary person (*prthag-jana*) of trainable nature: practicing right conduct for self-benefit, with shame and remorse, delighting in training, and genuinely capable of obtaining what is unobtained, realizing what is unrealized, and witnessing what is unwitnessed."

### FLAG R-10 | M | Register drift — the closing sentence of the shramana-who-lives-by-the-path (line 406)

**Passage:**
> "Such a person possesses the warmth of wholesome dharmas and can generate the life-faculty of noble wisdom: being alive and not dead, one is therefore called a shramana who lives by the path."

**Problem:** "Being alive and not dead" is a startlingly blunt phrase following the doctrinal explanation. It may be a gloss on "life-faculty" (*jivita-indriya*) or a direct translation of a Chinese phrase about vitality. As written, it reads as sarcastic or redundant rather than doctrinal. The reader does not know whether this is literal (as opposed to spiritually dead) or technical. A gloss is needed.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Such a person possesses the warmth of wholesome dharmas and can generate the life-faculty of noble wisdom. Being alive to the path in this sense — not spiritually inert — one is called a shramana who lives by the path."

### FLAG R-11 | M | Formula monotony — repeated enumeration structure in 29.14 (lines 398–433)

**Problem:** Section 29.14 presents six person-categories, each introduced by a bold ordinal header ("First: The Shramana — Four Types," "Second: The Brahmin — Three Types," etc.) and then defined by subtypes. The structure is consistent, which serves clarity, but the *introductory sentences* for each category follow an identical template:

> "[Term] has [N] subtypes: (1) X, (2) Y, (3) Z."

This pattern appears six times in approximately 40 lines. The content differs but the sentence frame never does. In a scholastic reference work this is acceptable; in a living translation it becomes numbing.

**Suggested approach:** No need to change every header, but vary the opening move occasionally. For example:

- "The brahmin, in this teaching, is not merely a matter of birth." (then: three subtypes.)
- "Pure conduct (*brahmacarya*) takes three forms, corresponding to the depth of the renunciation."
- These variant openers signal that the translator has digested the material, not merely transmitted its structure.

### FLAG R-12 | L | Translated-ese — "the task has been accomplished" (line 414)

**Passage:**
> "…whose task is certain and complete, who has driven out all evil and unwholesome dharmas — as stated: 'A brahmin has nothing more to be done; the task has been accomplished. This is called a brahmin.'"

**Problem:** "The task has been accomplished" is a direct translation idiom. In English this phrase reads as bureaucratic. The meaning — that nothing more needs to be done for liberation — would land better as:

> "…'A brahmin has nothing left to do; the work is complete. This is what is meant by brahmin.'"

Or, to preserve the slightly elevated register of a quoted saying:
> "…'For this brahmin, nothing remains to be done; all has been accomplished.'"

### FLAG R-14 | L | Register drift — "greed, aversion, or wrong views; anger, resentment, concealment, vexation, deception, flattery" (line 428)

**Passage:**
> "…whenever there arise thoughts of desire, thoughts of ill-will, or thoughts of harm; greed, aversion, or wrong views; anger, resentment, concealment, vexation, deception, flattery, and the like — states contrary to the shramana dharma that lead to the hells and other evil destinations — one does not tolerate them but immediately severs, destroys, removes, and expels them."

**Problem:** The four-verb climax ("severs, destroys, removes, and expels") is effective — it lands with force. But the preceding enumeration is exhausting. Two semicolon-separated clusters of afflictions, followed by an em-dash gloss, followed by the resolution clause, gives the reader too much to track before reaching the verbs. The practitioner's felt sense of urgency gets buried.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Whenever thoughts of desire, ill-will, or harm arise — greed, aversion, wrong views, anger, resentment, concealment, vexation, deception, flattery, and the like, all of them contrary to the shramana dharma and leading toward the lower realms — one does not tolerate them. One severs, destroys, removes, and expels them at once."

Breaking the final clause into its own sentence lets the four verbs land as instruction rather than conclusion.

### FLAG R-15 | M | Unexplained jargon — "latent tendencies (*anushaya*) and active entanglements (*paryavasthana*)" (line 428)

**Passage:**
> "This ceasing of afflictions is of two kinds in brief: ceasing the latent tendencies (*anushaya*) and ceasing the active entanglements (*paryavasthana*)."

**Problem:** Both terms are introduced with Sanskrit but no English gloss. A practitioner reader who hasn't encountered Abhidharma categories will not understand the distinction between a "latent tendency" and an "active entanglement." A one-phrase gloss on each would resolve this.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "This ceasing of afflictions is of two kinds: ceasing the latent tendencies (*anushaya*) — afflictions dormant in the mind-stream — and ceasing the active entanglements (*paryavasthana*) — afflictions that have surfaced and seized the mind."

### FLAG R-17 | M | The closing sentence of 29.14 (line 434) — anti-climactic summary

**Passage:**
> "Therefore it is said: whether shramanas, brahmins, practitioners of pure conduct, bhikshus, the diligent, or renunciants — all of these are alternative designations for the person (*pudgala*)."

**Problem:** The section closes on a definitional summary that does no interpretive work. The practitioner reader might reasonably ask: "So what?" The taxonomic point — that all these are names for the pudgala — is technically correct, but the sentence does not help the reader understand *why* this taxonomy matters. This could simply be how the source closes; if so, accept it. But if there is any room for a translator's note or a slight expansion of the closing, it would help the reader land.

**Suggested alternative closer:**
> "Therefore it is taught: whether addressed as shramanas, brahmins, practitioners of pure conduct, bhikshus, the diligent, or renunciants — all of these are names for the person (*pudgala*). The variety of designations reflects the different frames through which the path is entered and the practitioner is recognized; the underlying referent is always the same."

(Only if this reading is supported by the source — otherwise retain the current form and note that the source closes in summary.)

## Overall Assessment

Sections 29.13–29.14 are **structurally sound** and **accurate in register** in most places. The prose is clearly better than the mid-fascicle drift identified in earlier review passes. However, several high-priority issues remain:

1. The "purifying-conduct objects" compound needs a gloss and the terminology normalized.
2. The upright-view paragraph (line 374) has stacked "because X → Y" structures that need to be broken.
3. The ordinary-person / shramana-who-lives-by-the-path sentence (line 406) exceeds 50 words and buries the main predicate.
4. "Complete accumulations" paragraph uses translated-ese idioms and double "as explained previously."
5. The closing enumeration in the Diligence section (line 428) would land harder with the resolution clause separated as its own sentence.

The sevenfold list structure in 29.13 works well overall but items (6) and (7) are thin — one-sentence entries that function as placeholders rather than explanations.
