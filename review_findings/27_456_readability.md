## Detailed Findings

### Section 27.4: Skillful Objects

---

**FLAG R-27.4-01**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Section header and opening paragraph, line 282

> "What are the skillful objects?"

The word "skillful" is never defined here. A reader unfamiliar with the technical sense of *kaushala* will interpret "skillful" as meaning "done with skill" (i.e., competent practice), not "analytical knowledge of the categories of experience." The opening sentence gives no indication that this is about knowing Abhidharma categories. The difference matters: the reader may expect a section on meditation technique, but the section is actually about doctrinal analysis.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief orientation sentence after the list. Example: "These five skills constitute analytical knowledge of the basic categories of experience — not practical techniques, but clear understanding of what phenomena are."

---

**FLAG R-27.4-02**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 292

> "The skandhas are five: (1) the form skandha, (2) the feeling skandha, (3) the perception skandha, (4) the formations skandha, and (5) the consciousness skandha."

"Skandha" is used here without any definition or gloss. The first-use standard for the project is "the five skandhas (aggregates)" with "skandhas" thereafter. Here the word "skandhas" appears in the section header at line 290 and in the text at line 292 with no gloss at all. A reader who knows the word "aggregates" but not "skandha" will be lost immediately.

**Suggestion:** On first use in this section (line 292), write: "The skandhas (aggregates) are five:..." This one addition orients the reader for all subsequent uses in 27.4.

---

**FLAG R-27.4-03**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 298

> "Perception with a sign and perception without a sign; narrow perception and broad perception; immeasurable perception and the perception of the base of nothingness."

"With a sign / without a sign" is opaque to a general reader. "Sign" has no antecedent definition in the section. In contemporary English "sign" most naturally means a visual symbol or indicator. The Abhidharma sense (an object feature that anchors cognition) is completely different and is not explained.

Similarly "the base of nothingness" appears here as a level of perception with no explanation. A reader has not been told what "bases" are or that there are formless levels of meditative absorption.

**Suggestion:** Add a parenthetical gloss on first use: "perception with a sign (an object of focus) and perception without a sign" and "the perception corresponding to the formless attainment of nothingness." Alternatively, add a footnote — the footnote infrastructure is already established in this translation.

---

**FLAG R-27.4-04**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 300

> "Furthermore, all remaining mental factors apart from feeling and perception are collectively called the formations skandha."

The phrase "all remaining mental factors apart from feeling and perception" is technically precise but reads awkwardly. The logic (feeling has its own skandha, perception has its own skandha, everything else gets grouped here) is clear once you understand the system, but a reader who does not already know the skandha system will find the negative definition confusing. This is a catch-all category defined entirely by exclusion.

**Suggestion:** Add an orienting clause: "Furthermore, all remaining mental factors — volitions, intentions, attention, and other mental events — apart from feeling and perception are collectively called the formations skandha." Naming two or three examples makes the category concrete.

---

**FLAG R-27.4-05**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 302

> "Heart (*citta*), mind (*manas*), and consciousness (*vijnana*)."

Three Sanskrit terms appear together with English glosses. The glosses ("heart," "mind," "consciousness") are reasonable, but a reader will immediately ask: what is the difference between heart, mind, and consciousness? They appear to be synonyms in English, and the text does not explain why three words are needed. The footnote [^citta_manas_vijnana] is excellent and explains this — but the body text gives no signal that the threefold distinction is meaningful rather than stylistic variation.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief parenthetical in the body text: "Heart (*citta*), mind (*manas*), and consciousness (*vijnana*) — three terms that can all mean 'mind,' here used to indicate that the consciousness skandha encompasses all aspects of mental activity." This prevents the reader from skipping the footnote as if the distinction were trivial.

---

**FLAG R-27.4-06**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 304

> "The preceding feeling, perception, and formations skandhas, as well as this consciousness skandha, all have the distinctions of past, future, present, internal, external, and so forth, as previously explained in detail."

"And so forth, as previously explained in detail" is a backward reference that works for a reader of a continuous text but is mildly disorienting when reading in isolation. The phrase "as previously explained in detail" points to earlier content (the form skandha definition at line 294 lists these distinctions explicitly) — this is fine, but the reader may feel uncertain whether "and so forth" means they have missed something.

**Suggestion:** Minor fix: "as explained for the form skandha above" is more precise than "as previously explained in detail."

---

**FLAG R-27.4-07**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 312

> "Apart from the skandhas, no self can be apprehended — no permanent, eternal, unchanging dharma is to be found. Nor is there the slightest dharma that is 'mine.'"

"No permanent, eternal, unchanging dharma is to be found" — the word "dharma" here means "phenomenon" (not "the Dharma" = Buddhist teachings). This is footnoted for the first occurrence in the document [^dharma_phenomena], but in this section the dual meaning of "dharma" is active simultaneously (the five skillful objects are dharma teachings about dharmas). A reader who has absorbed the earlier footnote may manage, but a reader encountering this usage fresh will be confused.

**Suggestion:** At line 312, use "phenomenon" in place of "dharma" since the scholarly precision is not needed at this specific moment and the context is already familiar: "no permanent, eternal, unchanging phenomenon is to be found."

---

**FLAG R-27.4-08**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Logical flow break
**Location:** Line 318

> "If one truly knows, accepts, and carefully investigates that these eighteen dharmas arise and appear each from its own distinct element, its own distinct seed, and its own distinct lineage — this is called skill in the elements. Truly knowing that these eighteen dharmas each operate from its own distinct element is to be skilled in causation. Therefore this is called skill in the elements."

The last two sentences nearly repeat the first: skill in elements = knowing they arise from distinct elements = being skilled in causation. The repetition, rather than building the thought, creates momentary confusion. Why is "causation" introduced in the second sentence but not the first? The logical step being made — that knowing distinct origins means understanding causation — is not spelled out.

**Suggestion:** Clarify the movement: "...this is called skill in the elements. By knowing that each of the eighteen arises from its own distinct origin, one understands the law of causation — that like conditions produce like effects. Therefore this is called skill in the elements."

---

**FLAG R-27.4-09**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 324

> "Regarding skill in the sense bases: the eye serves as the dominant condition (*adhipati-pratyaya*); form serves as the object condition (*alambana-pratyaya*); the just-ceased preceding mind serves as the immediately preceding condition (*samanantara-pratyaya*). From these three conditions arises eye consciousness and its associated mental factors."

The sentence ending at "associated mental factors" is 47 words (borderline), but what follows — the ear-to-mind chain that extends the same pattern — is the real concern. The cumulative effect of listing all six sense-doors in the same grammatical template creates a passage of relentless technical parallelism without any pause for the reader.

More critically, "dominant condition," "object condition," and "immediately preceding condition" — three new technical terms — appear without any intuitive gloss. "Dominant condition" as the sense organ is counterintuitive: the eye does not seem to "dominate" vision in ordinary English usage.

**Suggestion:** Before the list begins, add one sentence: "Each sense consciousness arises through three factors working together: the sense organ (which governs or 'dominates' the cognition), the object (which the mind latches onto), and the just-preceding moment of mind (which makes way for the next)." Then the technical terms follow with the reader already oriented.

---

**FLAG R-27.4-10**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 332

> "If one further knows that only dharmas nourish dharmas, only dharmas equally moisten dharmas, only formations give rise to formations — and those formations, being born from causes and born from conditions, were originally non-existent and then came into being, and having come into being they disperse and cease, their nature being impermanent — because they are impermanent, they are subject to birth, aging, illness, and death, to grief, withering, sorrow, lamentation, anguish, suffering, and distress."

This is approximately 90 words. The sentence spans dependent origination, impermanence, and the full enumeration of suffering. It is grammatically coherent but requires multiple readings to parse. The parenthetical dash-clause in the middle ("and those formations...being impermanent") suspends the main clause for so long that the reader may lose the thread.

**Suggestion:** Break after the dash-clause: "...only formations give rise to formations. Those formations, being born from causes and born from conditions, were originally non-existent and then came into being. Having come into being, they disperse and cease — their nature is impermanent. Because they are impermanent, they are subject to birth, aging, illness, and death, to grief, withering, sorrow, lamentation, anguish, suffering, and distress."

**FLAG R-27.4-11**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 338

> "...wholesome and unwholesome dharmas have their matured results (*vipaka*)."

"Matured results" (*vipaka*) is introduced here for the first time. The concept of karma ripening into results is widely known in Buddhist circles, but the specific terminology "matured results" as a technical category distinct from immediate effects is not self-evident. The term "vipaka" also appears italicized in parentheses as if it is glossing "matured results," but the relationship between the two is unexplained.

**Suggestion:** Gloss briefly: "wholesome and unwholesome dharmas produce their matured results (*vipaka* — the ripened karmic fruits of actions), each according to its kind."

**FLAG R-27.4-12**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 342

> "These five skills are, in brief, reduced to two: (1) skill in own-characteristics (*svalakshana*), and (2) skill in common characteristics (*samanya-lakshana*)."

"Own-characteristics" and "common characteristics" are important Buddhist analytical concepts but the English compounds are abstract to a reader who has never encountered them. "Own-characteristics" could be read as "characteristics belonging to oneself" (autobiographical) rather than "the distinctive property specific to each type of thing." Similarly "common characteristics" sounds like "shared traits between people" rather than "features — like impermanence — shared by all phenomena."

**Suggestion:** Add a one-sentence gloss: "Skill in own-characteristics means knowing what makes each phenomenon distinctively what it is (form is form, not feeling); skill in common characteristics means knowing what all phenomena share — impermanence, suffering, non-self, and causal dependence."

### Section 27.5: Affliction-Purifying Objects

---

**FLAG R-27.5-01**
**Severity:** Critical
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 350

> "What are the affliction-purifying objects (*klesha-vishodhana-alambana*)?"

The section opens with an italicized Sanskrit compound of three elements (klesha + vishodhana + alambana) that has not been introduced before. The English "affliction-purifying objects" does carry the meaning, but "objects" in Buddhist meditation context means "meditation objects" — things the mind focuses on — which is not standard English usage. A general reader will wonder: objects of what? What is purifying what?

**Suggestion:** After the opening question, add a sentence of orientation: "These are meditation objects — things to focus the mind on — that work to eliminate (*vishodhana*, purify) the afflictions (*kleshas*) that keep beings in suffering."

---

**FLAG R-27.5-02**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 350

> "One contemplates the coarseness of the lower realm and the peacefulness of the upper realm — as the desire realm compared to the first dhyana, and so on up to the base of nothingness compared to the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

"The desire realm," "the first dhyana," "the base of nothingness," and "the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" are four technical terms presented in quick succession with no explanation. A reader who has not already read section 27.3 or the dhyana section will have no idea what cosmological/meditative schema is being assumed. The phrase "and so on up to" implies a known sequence that the reader is supposed to be tracking.

The term "base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" is particularly difficult — it is a double negative with an ambiguous referent.

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote reference or a brief parenthetical: "the desire realm (the world of ordinary sense experience) compared to the first dhyana (the first meditative absorption, free from sense desire), and so on up through the four dhyanas and four formless attainments, until the highest — the base of neither-perception-nor-non-perception — which has no higher realm above it." This also appears to be the section's first real use of the cosmological vertical scheme and the reader needs orientation.

---

**FLAG R-27.5-03**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 354–356

> "What is coarseness? Coarseness is twofold: (1) substantial coarseness, and (2) numerical coarseness."
> "What is substantial coarseness? The desire realm compared to the first dhyana — although both possess the five skandhas, the desire realm has grave dangers and afflictions, with suffering and misery predominant, being most base and loathsome."

"Numerical coarseness" is an unusual compound in English. "Numerical" typically refers to numbers/quantities; the sense here ("more varieties of things to be abandoned") is closer to "multiplicity" or "greater variety." The contrast between "substantial" (quality-based) and "numerical" (quantity-based) coarseness is a real analytical distinction, but "numerical" does not convey it naturally.

**Suggestion:** Replace "numerical coarseness" with "coarseness by multiplicity" or "quantitative coarseness," and gloss it: "coarseness by multiplicity — more types of suffering and affliction requiring abandonment."

---

**FLAG R-27.5-04**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 368

> "Because one observes the many dangers of the lower realm — like illness, like a boil, like a poisoned arrow — regarding its unsettled nature as coarseness. One observes the upper realm, which is the opposite of this, as peacefulness. One thereby abandons all the afflictions of the lower realm. This proceeds from the desire realm up to the base of nothingness. This is a temporary abandonment, not an ultimate abandonment, because at a later time the afflictions resume their continuity."

The first sentence is grammatically awkward: "Because one observes... regarding its unsettled nature as coarseness" — the main clause is missing. "Because" introduces a subordinate clause but there is no main clause for it to depend on. This reads as a sentence fragment.

**Suggestion:** Revise: "This is the worldly affliction-purifying object: one observes the many dangers of the lower realm — like illness, like a boil, like a poisoned arrow — taking its unsettled nature as coarseness, and one observes the upper realm, which is the opposite, as peacefulness." Then continue with the remaining sentences.

**FLAG R-27.5-05**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 382

> "One should know that herein the four truths are established on the basis of the fruit and cause of the dark and bright categories (*krishna-paksha* and *shukla-paksha*): the truth of suffering is the fruit of the dark category; the truth of origin is the cause of the dark category; the truth of cessation is the fruit of the bright category; the truth of the path is the cause of the bright category, because it can be attained and realized."

This sentence is approximately 75 words. The schema "dark = suffering/origin, bright = cessation/path" is a useful mnemonic but appears suddenly without introduction. "Dark category" and "bright category" evoke moral valence but need a brief gloss: these are not moral judgments but a standard Abhidharma framework grouping the truth of suffering and origin (the way things go wrong) versus cessation and path (the way out).

**Suggestion:** Break after the colon and add a brief orientation: "The four truths are arranged in two pairs: the dark category (*krishna-paksha*, the side of suffering and its causes) and the bright category (*shukla-paksha*, the side of liberation and its causes). The truth of suffering is the fruit of the dark category; the truth of origin is its cause. The truth of cessation is the fruit of the bright category; the truth of the path is its cause, since it can be cultivated and realized."

**FLAG R-27.5-06**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 386

> "Furthermore, their own-characteristics are without deception, and by seeing them, non-distorted awareness operates. Therefore they are called 'truths.'"

"Non-distorted awareness operates" is unusual phrasing. "Operates" is correct but mechanical. English would more naturally say "gives rise to" or "arises." Also "without deception" is an unusual way to say "they do not deceive." The sentence means: these truths are exactly what they appear to be, and seeing them produces clear understanding. But the current phrasing is abstract.

**Suggestion:** "Their characteristics are genuine — they do not deceive — and seeing them produces clear, undistorted awareness. Therefore they are called 'truths.'"

**FLAG R-27.5-07**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 390

> "Only the noble ones (*arya*) regarding these truths equally acknowledge them as truths. They truly know and truly see. All ordinary beings (*prithagjana*) do not truly know and do not truly see."

"Noble ones" (*arya*) is introduced without prior definition in this section. In the context of 27.5, a reader who has not read the earlier fascicles does not know that "noble ones" means "those who have attained one of the four stages of realization" as opposed to "ordinary beings" who have not. The contrast is doing crucial work here — it explains why the truths are called "noble" truths — but the terms are undefined.

The phrase "equally acknowledge them as truths" is slightly odd. "Equally" implies comparison to something, but it is not clear to what. Does it mean "all noble ones equally acknowledge" (i.e., unanimously), or does it mean "they acknowledge them as truths in the same way as they actually are"?

**Suggestion:** Gloss on first use: "Only those who have attained direct realization — the noble ones (*arya*), as distinguished from ordinary beings (*prithagjana*) who have not yet penetrated the truths — truly know and truly see these truths as truths." Then "equally acknowledge" can be dropped or replaced with "genuinely acknowledge."

**FLAG R-27.5-08**
**Severity:** Critical
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 394

> "Furthermore, regarding 'birth is suffering': at the time of birth, various bodily and mental painful feelings arise. It is not that birth itself is suffering; it is the cause and condition of suffering. Therefore it is called 'suffering.'"

This paragraph makes a genuinely surprising philosophical point — that "birth is suffering" does not mean birth IS the pain but that birth CAUSES the pain. This is an important doctrinal clarification. But the passage then reads: "Stated in full through to 'not obtaining what one seeks is suffering': because of the condition of not obtaining what one seeks, various bodily and mental painful feelings arise. It is not that not-obtaining itself is suffering; it is the cause and condition of suffering."

The phrase "Stated in full through to" is a contracted reference to earlier content ("birth... aging... illness..."). A reader who has just read those items (at line 374) can follow. But the phrasing "stated in full through to" is a bureaucratic shorthand that breaks the flow and is unusual in English prose. It makes the paragraph feel like an internal note rather than a translation intended for readers.

**Suggestion:** Replace "Stated in full through to 'not obtaining what one seeks is suffering'" with: "The same reasoning applies to each of the seven: aging, illness, death, association with what is hateful, separation from the loved, and not obtaining what one seeks."

**FLAG R-27.5-09**
**Severity:** Critical (long + guesswork)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words; passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 414

> "**(3) The suffering of conditioned existence.** The nature of the suffering of conditioned existence pervades all five appropriated skandhas. In summary: excluding the suffering of suffering, excluding the affliction-comprised suffering of change, and excluding the pleasant-feeling-comprised suffering of change and its physical basis — all remaining skandhas that are accompanied by neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling, whether born from those feelings, whether conditions giving rise to those, whether the skandhas that serve as the container for arising and abiding — this is the nature of the suffering of conditioned existence."

This single sentence (from "In summary:" to "conditioned existence.") is approximately 85 words and is defined entirely by exclusion: exclude this, exclude that, exclude the other — what remains is the suffering of conditioned existence. A reader who has not fully internalized the taxonomy being built (suffering of suffering, two types of suffering of change) cannot follow this at all.

The phrase "the skandhas that serve as the container for arising and abiding" is particularly opaque. "Container" is an unusual metaphor for the skandhas. What is being "contained"? The reader has no way to know.

**Suggestion:** Break the sentence significantly and add one positive statement:
"The suffering of conditioned existence is the most pervasive and subtle. It pervades all five appropriated skandhas. It is not the outright pain of the suffering of suffering, nor the deterioration of pleasure described as the suffering of change. Rather, it is the baseline unsatisfactoriness of all conditioned experience: even neutral feelings, along with the skandhas that underlie all arising and ceasing, carry this pervasive quality of being unreliable, beyond our control, and subject to impermanence. This is the suffering of conditioned existence."

**FLAG R-27.5-10**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 416

> "Because those skandhas are impermanent by nature, associated with arising and ceasing, having all clinging, followed by the heaviness of the three types of feeling, included within insecurity, not free from the suffering of suffering and the suffering of change, and operating without autonomy — through the suffering of conditioned existence, they are said to be 'suffering.'"

This is approximately 70 words. The list of participial modifiers ("impermanent by nature, associated with... having... followed by... included within... not free from... operating without") stacks seven descriptors before reaching the main clause. The phrase "having all clinging" is also grammatically unusual — does it mean "accompanied by all forms of clinging" or "possessing all clinging tendencies"?

**Suggestion:** Break after the fourth modifier and use parallel shorter sentences: "Those skandhas are impermanent by nature, arising and ceasing moment by moment. They are attended by all forms of clinging, weighed down by all three types of feeling, and shot through with insecurity. They are not free from the suffering of suffering and the suffering of change, and they operate without autonomy. For all these reasons, through the suffering of conditioned existence, they are said to be 'suffering.'"

**FLAG R-27.5-11**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 420

> "This yearning operates through three modes: yearning for continued existence, and yearning for objects."

"Three modes" is promised, but only two are listed here: "yearning for continued existence" and "yearning for objects." **FLAG R-27.5-12**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Logical flow break
**Location:** Line 424

> "Cessation is of two types: (1) cessation of afflictions, and (2) cessation of the physical basis. The path is of two types: (1) the path of the learner, and (2) the path of the non-learner."

### Section 27.6: Four Types of Instruction

---

**FLAG R-27.6-01**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 434

> "What is instruction (*avavada*)?"

"Instruction" is a plain English word but is used here as a technical term for a specific pedagogical relationship between a qualified teacher and a student on the path. The Sanskrit *avavada* (personal instruction, counsel) is not the same as general "teaching" — it implies a personalized, directed relationship. The body text does not distinguish "instruction" in this technical sense from ordinary teaching.

**Suggestion:** Gloss at first use: "What is instruction (*avavada* — personal guidance given by a qualified teacher to a student on the path)?"

---

**FLAG R-27.6-02**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 438

> "Furthermore, to bring about the initial direct realization of the truths, one first teaches the truth of suffering and afterward the truths of origin, cessation, and the path. Furthermore, to bring about the attainment of the dhyana absorptions, one first teaches the initial dhyana absorption and afterward teaches the remaining dhyana absorptions. Such and similar patterns should be known as gradual instruction."

The passage is clear, but "Such and similar patterns should be known as gradual instruction" is formulaic in a way that feels slightly bureaucratic. Also, "dhyana absorptions" is redundant — "dhyana" already means absorption. The compound has been used earlier in the translation, so this is a consistency flag rather than a new error.

**Suggestion:** For "Such and similar patterns," replace with "These are the patterns of gradual instruction." For "dhyana absorptions," verify consistency with glossary — if the established term is "dhyanas," use that.

---

**FLAG R-27.6-03**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Awkward / unnatural English
**Location:** Line 440

> "It is instruction received from a venerable one, or from one resembling a venerable — one who understands yoga, its rules, and personal instruction — or from the Tathagatas or the Buddha's disciples."

"One resembling a venerable" is unusual English. "Venerable" used as a noun (rather than a title like "the Venerable Ananda") sounds odd without context. The reader does not know if "a venerable one" means a senior monk, an enlightened being, or simply a wise elder.

Also, "yoga, its rules, and personal instruction" — the word "yoga" here has a specific Yogacara-tradition sense (meditative practice/spiritual discipline) that differs from the physical practice familiar to Western readers. This is the first use of "yoga" in section 27.6 without a gloss.

**Suggestion:** "It is instruction received from an elder of great attainment, or from one who resembles such an elder — one who understands meditative practice (*yoga*), its proper procedure, and the art of personal guidance — or from the Tathagatas or the Buddha's disciples."

---

**FLAG R-27.6-04**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Logical flow break
**Location:** Line 448–456 (Supernormal Displays subsection)

> "Furthermore, there is instruction replete with all marks. What does this involve? It is instruction through the three supernormal displays (*pratiharya*)."

The subsection on "instruction replete with all marks" introduces a new concept — supernormal displays — without connecting it to the four types of instruction just described. A reader expects section 27.6 to continue analyzing those four types or close the section. Instead, a fifth element ("instruction replete with all marks") appears.

The footnote [^three_supernormal_displays] is excellent and explains the logical connection (the Buddha prized the miracle of instruction above magical display). But the body text does not make clear whether this is a fifth category, a synthesis, or a coda. The reader cannot tell where this fits.

**Suggestion:** Add a transitional sentence: "Beyond these four types, there is an ideal form of instruction — instruction replete with all marks — in which all three of a teacher's supernormal capacities work together."

---

**FLAG R-27.6-05**
**Severity:** Minor
**Category:** Unexplained jargon
**Location:** Line 448

> "...the three supernormal displays (*pratiharya*)."

"Supernormal displays" is a defensible translation, but "display" has a performative or theatrical connotation in English ("a display of fireworks") that may not align well with the sense of miraculous demonstration as proof of awakening. "Miracles" or "wondrous demonstrations" might be more immediately intelligible, though the tradition itself was cautious about these terms.

**Suggestion:** Consider "the three miraculous demonstrations" or "the three wonders (*pratiharya*)" — then the text reads naturally and the Sanskrit is preserved.

> "Through the supernormal display of mind-reading declaration, one can investigate the differences in others' mental states and tendencies."

**FLAG R-CROSS-02**
**Severity:** Moderate (long sentence)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words

> "Whatever serves as the antidote to those forms of suffering — youth is the antidote to aging; health is the antidote to illness; life is the antidote to death; meeting with the beloved is the antidote to association with the hateful; non-separation from the loved is the antidote to separation from the loved; obtaining what one seeks is the antidote to not-obtaining — as well as the afflictions that arise from painful feeling and its physical basis, and the afflictions that arise from health and other occasions for pleasant feeling and the feelings generated by those — all of this collectively constitutes the nature of the suffering of change."
