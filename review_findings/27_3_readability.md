# Readability Review: Fascicle 27, Section 27.3

## Summary

27 issues found (5 critical, 11 moderate, 11 minor)

---

## Findings

### R-1: Opening sentence — no context for "anapanasmriti" — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Para 1: "What is the object of mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*)?"
- **Issue:** *Anapanasmriti* appears as a parenthetical gloss — but the section heading already uses "Mindfulness of Breathing," so a reader from a general English background will be puzzled by the italicized Sanskrit term appearing immediately after a perfectly good English equivalent. The term gets no explanation on first use. Readers who know no Sanskrit will wonder whether "anapanasmriti" means something different from "mindfulness of breathing" or is simply an alternative label.
- **Recommendation:** Either drop the parenthetical here (the heading already introduces the practice) or add a brief explanatory phrase: "...mindfulness of breathing (*anapanasmriti*, literally 'mindfulness of in-and-out-breath')." Alternatively, introduce the Sanskrit once in the heading and not again in the body.

---

### R-2: "Intermediate inbreath / intermediate outbreath" — concept not grounded for general readers — Severity: CRITICAL

- **Location:** "Types of Breath" subsection, paras 2–3
- **Issue:** The idea that there are four breath types (inbreath, intermediate inbreath, outbreath, intermediate outbreath) is counterintuitive and alien to any ordinary English-speaking reader's experience. A practitioner sitting down to meditate has no felt sense of an "intermediate inbreath." The text says it is "a brief, similar, subtle wind" that arises in the interval when one breath has stopped and the next has not yet begun — but this description is very technical and abstract. The reader has no way to check whether they are perceiving this correctly.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief grounding clause, such as: "This brief pause — the momentary stillness between one breath ending and the next beginning — is what the text calls the intermediate inbreath." The word "wind" for a subtle internal breath sensation also needs brief flagging for Western readers; the text uses "wind" (*vayu*) several more times in the same sense.

---

### R-3: "Impelling karma" — unexplained jargon — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** "Causes, Supports, Movements, and Channels," para 1: "Impelling karma"
- **Issue:** "Impelling karma" appears with no explanation in the running text. The footnote explains it, but readers who do not read footnotes (a large proportion of practitioner-readers) will encounter this term without any handle. The phrase "karmic force from previous lives that projects the present existence, sustaining the biological processes including breathing" is precisely the kind of explanation that should appear at least partially in the running text.
- **Recommendation:** Expand to: "Impelling karma — the karmic force from prior lives that projects and sustains the present existence, including its biological processes." This is two extra clauses and removes the dependency on the footnote for basic comprehension.

---

### R-4: Long sentence — embryonic stages argument — Severity: CRITICAL

- **Location:** Para beginning "If breathing depended only on the body..." (the entire philosophical counter-example paragraph)
- **Issue:** This paragraph contains a single sentence of approximately 120 words presenting three counter-examples in one sustained period. Specifically: "If breathing depended only on the body, then beings who enter the attainment of non-perception... If breathing depended only on the mind, then those who enter formless attainments... And if breathing depended merely on body and mind without the qualification 'each according to its proper function'..." The reader must hold three conditional arguments and five technical terms in mind simultaneously before reaching the conclusion.
- **Recommendation:** Break into three separate sentences with an explicit summary: (1) State the first counter-example and its conclusion. (2) State the second. (3) State the third. Then add a summary sentence: "None of these cases is true; therefore breathing depends on both body and mind, each functioning in its proper mode." Also: "attainment of non-perception" and "attainment of cessation" are introduced here without context — a general reader does not know what these attainments are. At minimum, a brief parenthetical is needed: "the attainment of cessation (a deep meditative state in which mental activity temporarily ceases)."

---

### R-5: Embryonic stages — untranslated Sanskrit terms mid-sentence — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** "...beings in the embryonic stages of *kalala*, *arbuda*, *peshi*, and so forth"
- **Issue:** Three Sanskrit embryonic stage terms are used in the running text with no English glossing. A general reader sees three opaque italicized words and cannot reconstruct what they signify. The "and so forth" further obscures that there are five stages total. The footnote explains this, but the running text should carry enough meaning to be followable without footnotes.
- **Recommendation:** Replace with: "beings in the earliest embryonic stages (the tiny embryo of the first weeks, before breathing begins)." The specific Sanskrit terms can remain in a footnote for scholarly purposes.

---

### R-6: "Anapana" — unexplained Sanskrit term in a list of names — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** "Four Names and Two Faults," para 1: "(2) *anapana*"
- **Issue:** The text lists four names for the breath: wind, *anapana*, inbreath and outbreath, and body formations. *Anapana* is used here as if it were a settled English term — but it has not been explained in the running text (only as part of the compound *anapanasmriti* in the first paragraph). A practitioner reader will not know whether *anapana* refers to the breath itself, a specific type of breath, or the practice of attending to breath.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief gloss: "*anapana* (the Indic term specifically for the in-and-out breath as a meditation object)." This distinguishes it from "wind" as a generic term for moving air.

---

### R-7: "Styana-middha" — unexplained Sanskrit compound — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** "Two Faults" section: "torpor and drowsiness (*styana-middha*)"
- **Issue:** The English translation "torpor and drowsiness" is serviceable and clear. However, *styana-middha* appears in italics as if it is a technical term the reader should recognise, when in fact the English sufficiently conveys the meaning. The italicized Sanskrit here adds no value for a practitioner reader.
- **Recommendation:** Either remove the Sanskrit parenthetical (the English is self-sufficient) or retain it and add a brief note that this is one of the five classical hindrances. Currently neither explanation nor context is given for why this phrase is elevated to the status of a cited Sanskrit technical term.

---

### R-8: Advanced counting section — logical flow breaks — Severity: CRITICAL

- **Location:** "(v) Advanced counting" and "(vi) Transcending the counting stage"
- **Issue:** The text introduces five subtypes (i–iv plus "advanced") but the section header says "four types of counting cultivation." Then items (v), (vi), and (vii) are added. The reader is told at the outset "there are, in brief, four types" but then receives seven numbered items. This is a direct logical contradiction from the reader's perspective. No bridging sentence explains why additional items appear after the announced four are complete. This will confuse any attentive reader.
- **Recommendation:** Either: (a) revise the opening to say "four basic types, with additional advanced stages," or (b) add a transitional sentence before item (v): "Beyond these four foundational methods, the text continues with advanced stages that build upon them." The current text leaves the reader wondering whether they miscounted.

---

### R-9: "Twofold grasped and grasper" — abrupt technical introduction — Severity: CRITICAL

- **Location:** Opening of "(2) Cultivation of Comprehending the Skandhas": "One further attends to and contemplates the twofold grasped and grasper"
- **Issue:** "The twofold grasped and grasper" is introduced without any prior preparation in the running text. A practitioner-reader with no Yogacara background has no idea what the "grasped and grasper" distinction means or why attending to it is the next logical step after counting. The footnote explains it, but the running text reads as if the reader is expected to already know this technical dyad.
- **Recommendation:** Replace with something like: "One further attends to and contemplates both sides of the breathing experience — the breath and body as what is known (the grasped), and the mind and mental factors as what knows (the grasper)." This grounds the technical term in concrete experiential language before the formal analysis of the five skandhas follows.

---

### R-10: Skandha analysis — "skandha" not yet glossed in this section — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** "(2) Cultivation of Comprehending the Skandhas," para 2
- **Issue:** "Form skandha," "feeling skandha," "perception skandha," "formations skandha," and "consciousness skandha" are listed sequentially. The term "skandha" first appeared in the section heading without a gloss. A practitioner with basic familiarity will know this term, but per the readability brief, a reader with "no Abhidharma background" will not. The footnote does not address this.
- **Recommendation:** On first use add a brief parenthetical: "the form skandha (the bodily component of experience)" — or add a single sentence before the list: "The five skandhas — the five components that constitute a person's experience: body, feeling, perception, volitional formations, and consciousness — are comprehended as follows."

---

### R-11: Noble truths section — 60+ word sentence — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** "(4) Cultivation of Comprehending the Noble Truths," para 1: "Whatever was originally non-existent and then came into being, and having come into being disperses and ceases — this is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death. Whatever is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death — this is suffering. Whatever is suffering is without self, not autonomous, and devoid of a sovereign."
- **Issue:** Not a single sentence over 50 words, but the three-clause chain "Whatever... Whatever... Whatever..." creates a logic chain of approximately 80 words with anaphora. Reading this aloud, the cumulative "whatever" repetition is slightly awkward in natural English, though the logic is clear.
- **Recommendation:** The structure is largely acceptable. The phrase "devoid of a sovereign" is the weak point — "sovereign" sounds regal and does not immediately signal what is meant (that no self or agent controls the process). Replace with: "lacking any controlling self."

### R-12: "Direct realization (abhisamaya)" — Sanskrit mid-sentence — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** "(4) Cultivation of Comprehending the Noble Truths," final sentence: "one attains direct realization (*abhisamaya*) of the truths"
- **Issue:** *Abhisamaya* is introduced with "direct realization" as an English gloss, which is adequate. However, the significance of this attainment — that it marks stream-entry — is not stated in the running text. A practitioner reader will not know whether "direct realization" means insight, awakening, or something in between. The footnote explains it fully, but this is a milestone event in the path description and deserves at least a brief marker in the text.
- **Recommendation:** Expand to: "one attains direct realization (*abhisamaya*) of the truths — the non-conceptual breakthrough that marks entry into the noble path."

### R-13: The sixteen victory practices listed in one dense paragraph — Severity: CRITICAL

- **Location:** The numbered list of sixteen practices (para beginning "(1) 'I now train in being mindful of the inbreath...'")
- **Issue:** All sixteen practices are presented in a single unpunctuated run-on block of approximately 350 words. Practices 1–16 are embedded in continuous prose, each separated only by a number and quotation mark. There is no visual or structural break between the four tetrads (body, feeling, mind, dharmas), even though the commentary that follows is explicitly organized around those tetrads. A reader trying to follow or use this text for practice cannot easily identify which tetrad a given practice belongs to.
- **Recommendation:** Break the sixteen practices into four labeled groups matching the commentary structure:

  **First tetrad (body):** practices 1–4
  **Second tetrad (feeling):** practices 5–8
  **Third tetrad (mind):** practices 9–12
  **Fourth tetrad (dharmas):** practices 13–16

  This structural alignment between the enumeration and the commentary is essential for usability.

### R-14: Practice 2 — "Whether long or short" — too cryptic — Severity: CRITICAL (sixth criterion: clarity of each of the sixteen practices)

- **Location:** Practice 2 in the sixteen-practice list: "(2) Whether long or short."
- **Issue:** Practice 2 reads simply: "Whether long or short." This is a bare fragment with no subject, no verb, and no self-contained meaning. Without reading the commentary, a practitioner has no idea what is meant. The other fifteen practices are phrased as complete training vows ("I now train in..."); practice 2 breaks the pattern entirely and is incomprehensible in isolation.
- **Recommendation:** Rephrase to match the pattern of the other practices: "(2) 'I now train in knowing whether the breath is long or short while breathing in and breathing out.'" This is what the commentary explains it to mean; the formula should state it explicitly.

### R-15: "Resolute confidence (adhimoksha)" — jargon mid-sentence — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Commentary on the first tetrad, practice 3: "generates resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*) regarding this"
- **Issue:** "Resolute confidence" is a reasonable gloss, but *adhimoksha* is a technical Yogacara term for a specific mental factor — an act of determined ascription rather than ordinary "confidence." Describing it as "resolute confidence" suggests an emotional state, whereas the text is describing a specific meditative act: deliberately ascribing the breath-experience to the fine channels pervading the entire body. The meaning is that one mentally resolves or decides to take this as the object, whether or not one can literally feel every pore.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "generates resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*) regarding this" with "mentally resolves (*adhimoksha*, a determined act of ascription) to take this as the object." This makes the meditative operation clear without requiring the reader to import meaning from elsewhere.

---

### R-16: Practice 4 interpretation — the breath-interval reading is counterintuitive — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Commentary on practice 4 ("calming body formations"), first explanation (the breath-interval reading)
- **Issue:** The text gives two explanations of what "calming body formations" means. The first — taking as object the empty interval between breaths — is highly counterintuitive. A practitioner reading this will understand "calming body formations" to mean calming the breath; the notion that the practice consists in attending to the absence of breath is unexpected and unexplained. No bridging sentence helps the reader understand why attending to the gap between breaths would constitute "calming."
- **Recommendation:** Add an explanatory clause: "...at that time one resolves: 'I now train in calming body formations' — for at this interval the breath (which is the body formation) has stilled of itself, and one trains to know that stilling." This helps the reader understand the interpretive logic.

---

### R-17: Second explanation of practice 4 — relationship to first explanation unclear — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Commentary on practice 4, second paragraph (beginning "Furthermore, through cultivating...")
- **Issue:** The text gives a second, quite different explanation of "calming body formations" — now referring to the calming of harsh breath sensations that comes from habituation to the practice. The word "Furthermore" at the start suggests this is an addition, not an alternative interpretation, but the two explanations are in tension: the first says the practice is attending to the breath-gap; the second says the practice is the calming of harsh sensations through habituation. A practitioner reader cannot tell whether these are two separate occasions for the vow or two readings of the same thing.
- **Recommendation:** Add a transitional sentence: "A second application of the same vow arises from a different situation: through sustained practice, the initially harsh sensations of the breath gradually calm..." This signals that both readings are valid applications of the same practice-vow in different circumstances.

---

### R-18: Second tetrad commentary — "preparatory practice extending only through the third dhyana" — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Commentary on the second tetrad: "breath-mindfulness is taught as the preparatory practice extending only through the third dhyana"
- **Issue:** The phrase "preparatory practice" is potentially misleading. It could imply that breath-mindfulness is merely a beginner's technique, whereas the context is that it is being practiced by an advanced learner (*shaiksha*) working toward arhatship. The limitation is about the physiological fact that breathing stops in the fourth dhyana — not about the sophistication of the practice.
- **Recommendation:** Replace "preparatory practice" with "the cultivation of mindfulness of breathing, which is practiced through the third dhyana only" — and add a brief parenthetical explaining why: "since breathing ceases in the fourth dhyana."

---

### R-19: Long sentence — mental formations list — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Commentary on practices 7–8 (second tetrad): "Such deluded thoughts arise — various agitations of conceit accompanying perception and intention, proliferations, fabrications, and craving."
- **Issue:** The sentence beginning "But as soon as these arise, one is able swiftly to penetrate them with wisdom without becoming deeply attached, and to skillfully eliminate, remove, and expel them" is clear enough individually. But it follows a sentence of approximately 90 words listing the deluded thoughts. The combination creates a very dense passage for a practitioner trying to understand what is actually happening.
- **Recommendation:** Insert a paragraph break before "But as soon as these arise..." to separate the listing of mental agitations from the prescription for working with them. The contrast (agitations arise / one penetrates them with wisdom) is the key teaching and deserves its own breath.

### R-20: Third tetrad — "access concentration (anagamya-samapatti)" — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** Commentary on the third tetrad: "the access concentration (*anagamya-samapatti*) of the first dhyana"
- **Issue:** "Access concentration" is a technical term that many practitioner-readers will not recognize. The footnote explains it well, but the running text would benefit from a brief gloss. The term implies that there is a specific, well-defined concentration state below the threshold of full dhyana absorption — a concept that requires explanation for those without Abhidharma training.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief gloss in the running text: "the access concentration (*anagamya-samapatti*) — the stable preliminary concentration just below the threshold of full first dhyana absorption."

---

### R-21: The mind-states list — grammatical issue — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** Third tetrad commentary: the list beginning "Whether there is a mind with desire or a mind free from desire..."
- **Issue:** The list of paired mind-states is grammatically structured as a single run-on noun clause of approximately 80 words. In English this creates a breathless quality. Each paired state is a potential stopping point, and a practitioner reading this aloud in a retreat context would find it very hard to deliver.
- **Recommendation:** Replace the run-on with a formatted list:

  > Whether there is a mind with desire or a mind free from desire; a mind with aversion or a mind free from aversion; a mind with delusion or a mind free from delusion; a contracted mind or a scattered mind...

  The semicolons are already present, but the entire passage should either be set as a block-quoted list or reformatted with line breaks between each pair to aid reading.

### R-22: "Showing, guiding, praising, encouraging, gladdening, and energizing the mind" — meaning unclear — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Third tetrad, practice 10 commentary: "attending outward to a pure and excellent object — showing, guiding, praising, encouraging, gladdening, and energizing the mind"
- **Issue:** The six verbs — "showing, guiding, praising, encouraging, gladdening, and energizing" — are all transitive without stated objects. What exactly is being shown to whom? What is being praised? A practitioner reader will not be able to use this instruction. The six verbs appear to be technical terms for specific acts of attention-management, but they are presented as if their meaning is self-evident.
- **Recommendation:** Expand with at least a brief gloss: "attending outward to an uplifting and pure object in order to arouse the mind — showing it (what is worth attending to), guiding it (toward that object), praising it (its capacity for alertness), encouraging, gladdening, and energizing it." Alternatively, the footnote should explain what the six verbs describe in practice.

### R-23: "Latent tendencies (anushaya)" — jargon — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** Fourth tetrad commentary: "latent tendencies (*anushaya*) that must still be abandoned"
- **Issue:** *Anushaya* is introduced with "latent tendencies" as a gloss. This is adequate, but for a practitioner reader, the contrast between "active hindrances" (just mentioned) and "latent tendencies" needs a sentence of explanation: what is the difference? One can temporarily suppress an active hindrance while a latent tendency remains as a dormant seed. Without this distinction the fourth tetrad's progression (liberating from active hindrances → then addressing latent tendencies) loses its logic.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief explanatory clause: "latent tendencies (*anushaya*) — the dormant seeds of affliction that remain even after the active hindrances have been removed."

---

### R-24: The fourth tetrad — the "three realms" terminological collision — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Fourth tetrad, para 2: "They are the three realms"
- **Issue:** The phrase "three realms" is used here to mean abandonment, dispassion, and cessation — but any Buddhist practitioner will immediately associate "three realms" with the desire realm, form realm, and formless realm (the realms of existence). This is the identical phrase reused for a completely different meaning. The footnote addresses this, but the running text provides no warning and simply lists the new three realms. A practitioner reader will almost certainly misread this on first pass.
- **Recommendation:** Avoid "three realms" in the running text for this usage. Replace with "three spheres" or "three dimensions" and add a parenthetical: "what the text here calls 'three realms' (*dhatu*) in the sense of three aspects of liberation, not the three realms of existence." This collision of terminology is significant enough to warrant a clear in-text flag.

### R-25: The fourth tetrad — collapsing of practices 14–16 into one paragraph — Severity: MODERATE

- **Location:** Fourth tetrad, final paragraph: "Therefore one resolves: 'I now train in contemplating abandonment, contemplating dispassion, and contemplating cessation while breathing in and breathing out.'"
- **Issue:** Practices 14, 15, and 16 (abandonment, dispassion, cessation) are collapsed into a single combined vow at the end. The commentary gave individual treatment to practices 13 (impermanence) and then collapsed 14–16. This asymmetry is confusing — a reader expecting sixteen distinct practices receives only fourteen distinct commentary units. The three-realm framework is explained, but no transitional sentence explains why practices 14–16 are grouped together and what distinguishes them from each other.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief transition: "Because these three practices correspond to the three aspects of liberation described above — the abandonment of what the path of seeing removes, the dispassion that removes what the path of cultivation removes, and the final cessation of all conditioned existence — they are cultivated together and may be vowed together." This makes the grouping intelligible.

### R-26: "Outflows (ashrava) are forever exhausted" — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** Closing of sixteen victory practices: "The outflows (*ashrava*) are forever exhausted."
- **Issue:** "Outflows" is a translation of *ashrava* (also rendered "taints," "cankers," or "effluents"). It is one of the most common technical terms for the afflictions that sustain samsara, but it has not appeared before in this section. A practitioner with no Abhidharma background will not know what "outflows" refers to — the term is cryptic without context. This is the culminating moment of the entire section (arhatship), and the reader deserves to know what exactly has been exhausted.
- **Recommendation:** Expand to: "The outflows (*ashrava*) — the deep-seated afflictions of sensual desire, existence, and ignorance that sustain the cycle of rebirth — are forever exhausted."

---

### R-27: Closing paragraph — "discursive-thought conduct" introduced without reminder — Severity: MINOR

- **Location:** "Closing: Application to Discursive-Thought Conduct": "Persons of discursive-thought conduct (*vitarka-carita*) should diligently train in this..."
- **Issue:** "Discursive-thought conduct" (*vitarka-carita*) is the character-type for which mindfulness of breathing is prescribed. This was stated in section 27.1 (via the general framework of purifying-conduct objects) and in the footnote to the section heading, but it is not restated anywhere in section 27.3's running text before this closing paragraph. A reader who has worked through the entire section (which is lengthy and detailed) may have lost track of why this practice is being taught. The closing paragraph is the natural place to tie this back, but the current text does so abruptly.
- **Recommendation:** Add a brief orienting clause: "Persons of discursive-thought conduct (*vitarka-carita*) — those whose minds tend to restless mental proliferation — should diligently train in this and delight in employing it."
