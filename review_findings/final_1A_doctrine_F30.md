**Reviewer:** Doctrine Holder (Shamatha specialist)

**Cross-references:** Tsongkhapa, *Great Treatise* Vol. 3 (LRCM); Kamalashila, *Stages of Meditation*; Buddhaghosa/Nanamoli, *Visuddhimagga* Ch. IV; Chinese source T30.0449a17--0453a29

## 1. Nine Stages of Mental Abiding (30.10)

### 1a. Stage names and sequence -- CORRECT

The nine stages as given in translation (line 248):

1. Inward abiding (内住, *citta-sthapana*)
2. Settled abiding (等住, *samsthapana*)
3. Re-established abiding (安住, *avasthapana*)
4. Close abiding (近住, *upasthapana*)
5. Taming (調順, *damana*)
6. Pacifying (寂靜, *shamana*)
7. Fully pacifying (最極寂靜, *vyupashamana*)
8. One-pointed attention (專注一趣, *ekotikarana*)
9. Equipoise (等持, *samadhana*)

Cross-check against Tsongkhapa LRCM Vol. 3 (lines 1197--1217): Tsongkhapa gives: (1) mental placement, (2) continuous placement, (3) patched placement, (4) close placement, (5) taming, (6) pacification, (7) complete pacification, (8) one-pointed attention / single channeling, (9) balanced placement / concentrated awareness. The translation's names are different in English gloss but doctrinally equivalent. Tsongkhapa explicitly cites the Sravaka Levels as his source, confirming this is the passage he relies upon.

**Verdict:** No doctrinal error. The sequence and logic of progression match the standard Yogacara list exactly. (Observation only)

### 1b. Stage 2 description -- CORRECT with nuance

**[O]** The translation of stage 2 (line 252) renders 相續方便澄淨方便 as "sustained and purifying methods." The Chinese is more literally "through the method of continuity and the method of clarification/purification." This is doctrinally adequate -- the sense is that one refines and settles the mind through sustained effort. Tsongkhapa (LRCM line 1201) says: "Its continuity is not distracted." The translation captures the essential meaning. No action needed.

### 1c. Stage 5 vs. 6 distinction -- CORRECT

The Chinese distinguishes taming (stage 5) as dealing with external *signs* (色聲香味觸相, signs of form, sound, etc.) while pacifying (stage 6) deals with internal *thoughts and secondary afflictions* (欲恚害等諸惡尋思, 貪欲蓋等諸隨煩惱). The translation (lines 258--261) preserves this distinction clearly. Tsongkhapa (LRCM lines 1207--1209) confirms this exact mapping: stage 5 = perceiving the ten signs as disadvantageous; stage 6 = regarding thoughts and secondary afflictions as disadvantageous. Correct.

### 1d. Footnote [^nine_stages] -- ACCURATE

**[O]** The footnote (line 268) correctly lists all nine Sanskrit names and accurately states that Tsongkhapa's commentary in *The Great Treatise* Vol. 3, chs. 2--5 became the standard presentation. Cross-confirmed against LRCM Vol. 3, lines 547--549, where Tsongkhapa explicitly names "the six powers, the four types of attention, and the nine mental states which Asanga's Sravaka Levels explains" as the core framework. The chapter reference "2-5" is appropriate.

## 2. Six Powers (30.11)

### 2a. Mapping of powers to stages

Translation (lines 274--278) and footnote [^six_powers]:

| Power | Stages |
|-------|--------|
| Hearing | 1 |
| Reflection | 2 |
| Mindfulness | 3--4 |
| Clear knowing | 5--6 |
| Diligence | 7--8 |
| Habituation | 9 |

**[m] Minor precision issue.** The footnote says "hearing and reflection -> stages 1--2" as if both powers jointly produce both stages. However, the LRCM Vol. 3 (lines 1225--1227) explicitly separates them: "With the force of hearing, you accomplish mental placement" (stage 1); "With the force of reflection, you accomplish the mental state of continual placement" (stage 2). The Chinese source is slightly ambiguous because it groups them together: 初由聽聞思惟二力...最初令心於內境住及即於此相續方便澄淨方便等遍安住 -- "Initially through the two powers of hearing and reflection...first causes the mind to abide inwardly and then settles it evenly."

Tsongkhapa reads this as hearing -> stage 1, reflection -> stage 2 (one power per stage). The translation's footnote collapses them ("hearing and reflection -> stages 1--2"), which obscures Tsongkhapa's careful distinction. However, the running text at line 276 handles this reasonably: "Initially, through repeated hearing and reflection, one places the mind on the internal object, then through sustained and purifying methods settles it evenly throughout." This does not explicitly map each power to each stage one-to-one.

**Recommendation:** Revise footnote to: "hearing -> stage 1; reflection -> stage 2; mindfulness -> stages 3--4; clear knowing -> stages 5--6; diligence -> stages 7--8; habituation -> stage 9." This matches both Tsongkhapa's reading and the most natural reading of the Chinese.

### 2b. Fourth power: 正知力 rendered as "clear knowing"

**[m] Terminological inconsistency.** The fourth power is 正知力 in the Chinese. The glossary entry for 正知 (samprajanya) gives the English rendering as "alertness, clear comprehension" with preferred form "alertness." Tsongkhapa (LRCM) consistently renders this as "vigilance" (Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee convention). The translation here uses "clear knowing" (lines 274, 276, 278), which is not in the glossary as an approved rendering for 正知.

"Clear knowing" is a legitimate rendering (used by some translators for samprajanya), but the project glossary authorizes "alertness." This creates an inconsistency: the same term (正知/samprajanya) appears as "alertness" at line 166 (section 30.7) and as "clear knowing" at lines 274/276/278 (section 30.11).

**Recommendation:** Replace "clear knowing" with "alertness" throughout section 30.11, or update the glossary to authorize "clear knowing" as an alternative. Consistency is the primary concern. The Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee uses "vigilance" for this term.

### 2c. Power-to-stage logic -- CORRECT

The progression logic is sound: hearing/reflection produce initial placement (one acts on external instructions); mindfulness recollects and prevents scattering (stages 3-4); alertness/vigilance detects faults from signs and afflictions (stages 5-6); diligence applies effort to cut off remaining subtle disturbances (stages 7-8); habituation makes the process effortless (stage 9). Tsongkhapa's exposition (LRCM lines 1221--1237) confirms this mapping exactly.

## 3. Four Types of Attention (30.12)

### 3a. Names and mapping

Translation (lines 284--290):

| Attention | Stages |
|-----------|--------|
| Forceful | 1--2 |
| Interrupted | 3--7 |
| Uninterrupted | 8 |
| Effortless | 9 |

Chinese: 力勵運轉作意 (forceful), 有間缺運轉作意 (interrupted/intermittent), 無間缺運轉作意 (uninterrupted), 無功用運轉作意 (effortless/spontaneous).

Cross-check: Tsongkhapa LRCM Vol. 3 (lines 1255--1263) quotes the Sravaka Levels directly and gives: (1) tight focus -> stages 1--2, (2) intermittent focus -> stages 3--7, (3) uninterrupted focus -> stage 8, (4) spontaneous/effortless focus -> stage 9. This is an exact match.

**Verdict:** Correct. The mapping is standard and matches Tsongkhapa's citation precisely.

### 3b. Distinction between types -- CORRECT

**[O]** The translation's description (lines 284--288) is brief but doctrinally sound. Tsongkhapa (LRCM lines 1259--1263) provides additional nuance: in stages 1--2, there is more time not concentrated than concentrated, so the designation "interrupted" is reserved for stages 3--7 where concentration duration is longer but still broken by laxity/excitement. The translation does not reproduce this important Tsongkhapa gloss, but this is an editorial choice, not a doctrinal error. The footnote [^four_attentions] (line 290) correctly identifies the Chinese terms and cross-references Tsongkhapa.

### 3c. Application to vipashyana -- CORRECT

Translation (line 288): "These four types of attention, as they operate within the nine stages, belong to the shamatha class. Furthermore, one who has attained inner shamatha and then diligently cultivates vipashyana employs these same four types of attention. Therefore they also belong to the vipashyana class."

This accurately renders 當知如是四種作意。於九種心住中是奢摩他品。又即如是獲得內心奢摩他者。於毘鉢舍那勤修習時。復即由是四種作意。方能修習毘鉢舍那。故此亦是毘鉢舍那品. The doctrinal point -- that the same four attentional modes operate in both shamatha and vipashyana cultivation -- is correctly rendered.

## 4. Four Types of Vipashyana (30.13)

### 4a. Identification -- CORRECT

The four types (line 298): correct discernment (正思擇), thorough discernment (最極思擇), comprehensive investigation (周遍尋思), comprehensive examination (周遍伺察). The first two correspond to the two fundamental modes of knowing: yavad-bhavikata (full extent of what exists) and yathavad-bhavikata (nature of things as they are). The latter two correspond to vitarka and vicara.

Cross-check: Tsongkhapa (LRCM Vol. 3 passim) does not elaborate on these four in the shamatha chapters (they belong to the insight discussion). The identification is standard Yogacara doctrine per the Sravakabhumi.

### 4b. Footnote [^four_vipashyana] -- ACCURATE

**[O]** The footnote (line 308) correctly identifies the Chinese terms, correctly maps the first two to yavad-bhavikata and yathavad-bhavikata, and correctly maps the latter two to vitarka and vicara. One small observation: the footnote says the latter two "correspond to the Abhidharma pair of investigation (*vitarka*) and examination (*vicara*)." In the glossary, 尋 (vitarka) is rendered "examination" and 伺 (vicara) is "analysis." The footnote uses "investigation" and "examination" -- the running text uses "comprehensive investigation" and "comprehensive examination." This is not wrong per se (the running text terms are translations of the compounds 周遍尋思 and 周遍伺察, not bare 尋 and 伺), but the mapping should be clear. No doctrinal error.

### 4c. Objects of the four types -- CORRECT

The Chinese specifies that all four operate on 淨行所緣, 善巧所緣, and 淨惑所緣 (purifying-conduct, skillful, and affliction-purifying objects). The translation (lines 300--302) correctly preserves this scope.

## 5. Six-Aspect Framework (30.14)

### 5a. The six aspects

Translation (line 330): meaning (義), things (事), characteristics (相), categories (品), time (時), reasoning (理). The Chinese is identical: 一義。二事。三相。四品。五時。六理.

### 5b. Three doors -- CORRECT

The three doors of vipashyana (lines 318--326): (1) following appearances (隨相行), (2) following investigation (隨尋思行), (3) following examination (隨伺察行). These match the Chinese exactly and are doctrinally standard.

### 5c. Mapping to three understandings -- CORRECT

Translation (lines 356--358): investigating meaning -> understanding of linguistic meaning (語義覺); investigating things and own-characteristics -> understanding of the limits of things (事邊際覺); investigating common characteristics, categories, time, and reasoning -> understanding of reality (如實覺).

Cross-check against Chinese: 尋思義故起語義覺。尋思其事及自相故起事邊際覺。尋思共相品時理故起如實覺. Exact match.

### 5d. Cross-fascicle consistency with Fascicle 31

Fascicle 31 applies the six-aspect framework to dependent origination (31.1), element differentiation (31.2), and mindfulness of breathing (31.3). The six aspects are: meaning, things, own-characteristic, common characteristic, categories, time, and reasoning -- seven display items when "characteristics" is split into own-characteristic and common characteristic.

**[O]** The framework in 30.14 lists "characteristics" (相) as one aspect, which in context splits into own-characteristic (自相) and common characteristic (共相). This is how it is applied in both 30.15/30.16 and throughout fascicle 31. Consistent.

### 5e. Four types of reasoning -- CORRECT

The four reasonings (lines 342--350): (a) dependence (觀待道理), (b) function (作用道理), (c) proof (證成道理), (d) nature of things (法爾道理). These match the glossary entries and are standard Yogacara epistemology.

**[O]** The rendering of the three means of valid cognition within reasoning from proof: scriptural authority (*agama-pramana*), inference (*anumana-pramana*), and direct realization (*pratyaksha-pramana*). The Chinese 比度量 is rendered "inference (*anumana-pramana*)" -- this is standard. The Chinese 現證量 is rendered "direct realization (*pratyaksha-pramana*)." In strict epistemological terms, pratyaksha-pramana is "direct perception," but in this Yogacara context, 現證量 emphasizes the meditator's direct experiential realization. The rendering "direct realization" is acceptable but slightly interpretive. The glossary entry for 現量 (pratyaksha) gives "direct perception" as the rendering. The inconsistency is minor and arguably justified by context.

## 6. Vipashyana Applied to Impurity (30.15)

### 6a. Seven sub-aspects

The six-aspect framework is correctly applied through seven display headings: Meaning, Things, Own-Characteristic (internal), Own-Characteristic (external), Common Characteristic, Categories, Time, Reasoning. This matches the Chinese structure precisely.

### 6b. Nine stages of decomposition

The translation (lines 384--406) lists nine stages of external impurity:

1. Lividity (青瘀) -- dead one day
2. Putrefaction (膿爛) -- dead two days
3. Decay and bloating (爛壞[月*逄]脹) -- dead seven days
4. Being devoured (食噉) -- by foxes, wolves, etc.
5. Stripped to sinew and bone (異赤) -- after devouring
6. Scattering (分散) -- limbs scattered
7. Bare bones (骨) -- bones scattered
8. Linked skeleton (鎖) -- connected skeleton
9. Bone-chain (骨鎖) -- detailed articulation

**[m] Stage 5 rendering.** The Chinese 異赤勝解 is rendered "stripped to sinew and bone." The term 異赤 literally suggests "different-red" or "reddish discoloration" -- this is the stage where skin and flesh are gone, blood dried, leaving sinews and exposed bone with a reddish appearance. The Visuddhimagga's corresponding stage is somewhat different in its Theravada enumeration (the Pali tradition uses a different set of cemetery contemplations). The translation's interpretive rendering "stripped to sinew and bone" captures the content accurately but loses the literal Chinese meaning of the reddish/discolored appearance. This is a minor point -- the doctrinal content is correct.

### 6c. Three types of deterioration -- CORRECT

Natural (自然變壞: lividity through bloating), caused by others (他所變壞: devouring through scattering), combined (俱品變壞: bare bones, linked skeleton, bone-chain). Translation lines 406 match the Chinese exactly.

### 6d. Two types of linked skeleton -- CORRECT

Trunk chain (形骸鎖) vs. limb chain (支節鎖), and two ways of taking up the skeleton-sign (from depictions vs. actual remains). Doctrinally standard.

### 6e. Reasoning from dependence passage -- DOCTRINALLY RICH

**[O]** The passage at line 416 contains the key Yogacara-inflected argument: "Neither internally nor externally is there any self or being who can be called pure or impure. There are only forms, only bodily shapes. Within these, purely by convention and designation, we speak of 'pure' or 'impure.'" This is important Yogacara doctrine -- impurity contemplation is grounded in conventional designation (假想施設) and dependent arising, not mere revulsion. The footnote [^dependence_impurity] correctly highlights this philosophical grounding. No doctrinal issues.

### 6f. Function of impurity meditation -- CORRECT

The text correctly identifies the specific function: "eradicate sensual desire" (能斷欲貪). This matches the standard Abhidharma teaching that ashubha meditation is the specific antidote to raga (sensual desire), not to afflictions in general.

## 7. Vipashyana Applied to Loving-Kindness (30.16)

### 7a. Defining characteristic of maitri -- CORRECT

Translation (line 438): "moved by the intention to benefit and bring happiness, one directs attention toward giving happiness to all sentient beings, generating resolute confidence. This is the defining mark of loving-kindness." The Chinese: 由欲利益安樂意樂。於諸有情作意與樂發起勝解。是慈愍相. Accurate.

### 7b. Own-characteristic: Three kinds of happiness -- CORRECT

Translation (line 442): (1) pleasures of the desire realm, (2) joyful/delightful pleasures of the form realm, (3) pleasures beyond joy. The Chinese: 一者欲求欲界諸樂。二者欲求色界有喜勇悅諸樂。三者欲求離喜諸樂. This is standard Yogacara doctrine: desire-realm pleasures, form-realm pleasures accompanied by joy (*priti*), and pleasures of the third and fourth dhyanas where joy has been transcended. Correct.

### 7c. Common characteristic: Equality argument -- CORRECT

**[O]** The extended argument for equanimity toward dear ones, enemies, and neutral ones (lines 444--460) marshals multiple reasons:

1. No real insulter/insulted (conventional unreality of agent and patient)
2. Body is material and subject to contact of two kinds
3. Both body and contacts are impermanent; the harm-doer is impermanent
4. All beings are inherently suffering -- one should not add suffering
5. One should be a spiritual friend even to enemies
6. Beginningless samsara: all beings have been one's relatives
7. Friend/enemy designations are not fixed
8. Therefore: equal mind toward all

The footnote [^equality_argument] claims "eight distinct reasons." Counting the arguments above, there are indeed approximately eight (the exact enumeration depends on how one segments, but the count is reasonable). The cross-reference to SN 15.14--19 is appropriate; this passage in the Samyutta Nikaya is indeed where the Buddha teaches that in beginningless samsara, every being has been one's relative.

**[m] Doctrinal nuance on samsara argument.** The translation attributes the samsaric-kinship argument directly to the Blessed One (line 456): "The Blessed One has said: 'I do not see any class of being...'" The Chinese 又世尊言 confirms this is a Buddha-quote. However, the SN 15.14--19 reference in the footnote is to the Pali canon. Since this is a Chinese text of the Yogacara tradition, the actual scriptural source Asanga had in mind would have been the corresponding Sanskrit/Agama source, not the Pali. The footnote says the argument "appears in the Samyutta Nikaya (SN 15.14--19)," which is correct as a parallel but could be more precise by noting that the Chinese Agama parallel is SA 940--956 (Samyuktagama). However, this is a scholarly nicety, not a doctrinal error.

### 7d. Function of loving-kindness meditation -- CORRECT

"One can eradicate anger" (能斷瞋恚, line 470). This is the standard Abhidharma teaching: maitri is the specific antidote to dvesha (aversion/anger). Correct pairing with ashubha -> raga and maitri -> dvesha.

### 7e. Reasoning from dependence -- CORRECT

"There is no self, no sentient being, no seeker of happiness, no giver of happiness. There are only skandhas, only formations" (line 468). Same Yogacara philosophical grounding as in the impurity section. Consistent and correct.

## 8. Samadhi Definition (30.9)

### 8a. Is this the passage Tsongkhapa cites?

**YES.** Tsongkhapa LRCM Vol. 3 (lines 878--881) quotes the Sravaka Levels:

> "Any continuum of attention that remembers again and again, focuses on a consistently similar object, and is continuous, free of misdeeds, and possessed of delight is called 'concentration,' as well as 'a one-pointed virtuous mind.'"

This is unmistakably the same passage as the translation's section 30.9 (line 228): "Repeated mindfulness of a homogeneous object-field, flowing without fault, conjoined with ease, causing the mind to continue -- this is called samadhi, and it is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness."

The Chinese: 數數隨念同分所緣流注無罪適悅相應令心相續。名三摩地。亦名為善心一境性.

### 8b. Accuracy of rendering

**[O]** The Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee renders 同分所緣 as "consistently similar object." The Sravakabhumi translation renders it "homogeneous object-field." Both are legitimate renderings of the same Chinese/Sanskrit. The LRCM rendering emphasizes the similarity/consistency of the object; the Sravakabhumi rendering emphasizes the field character. Neither is doctrinally wrong.

**[O]** The key elements are all present in the translation: (1) repeated (數數), (2) mindfulness/remembrance (隨念), (3) homogeneous/similar object (同分所緣), (4) flowing (流注), (5) without fault (無罪), (6) conjoined with ease (適悅相應), (7) causing continuity (令心相續). Cross-checked against both the Chinese and Tsongkhapa's citation -- all elements accounted for.

### 8c. Footnote [^samadhi_def] -- ACCURATE

The footnote (line 240) correctly identifies this as the passage Tsongkhapa cites in *The Great Treatise* Vol. 3 and correctly lists the five key elements. The claim "one of the most important passages in the Sravakabhumi" is well-supported by its extensive use in Tibetan commentarial literature.

## 10. Passages Needing a Footnote That Lack One

### 10a. Counter-mindfulness (背念) -- section 30.8

**[m]** The term 背念 (line 208) is rendered "counter-mindfulness" and briefly explained. However, this is a technical Yogacara term that does not appear in standard Buddhist terminology reference works. A footnote explaining its relationship to standard mindfulness (*smriti*) and its specifically Sravakabhumi usage would help scholars. The translation does explain the meaning in the running text, so this is not urgent.

### 10b. "Four modes of wisdom-practice" -- section 30.9

**[m]** At line 238, the text mentions "Mental one-pointedness within the four modes of wisdom-practice belongs to the vipashyana class." The Chinese is 四種慧行. This is a reference forward to the four types of vipashyana detailed in 30.13. A brief footnote or cross-reference would clarify for readers who encounter this before reading 30.13.

### 10c. Three types of happiness in loving-kindness own-characteristic -- section 30.16

**[O]** The three types of happiness (line 442) -- desire-realm pleasure, form-realm joy-pleasure, and pleasure beyond joy -- correspond to the three successive levels of meditative bliss associated with the dhyana progression. A footnote explaining that these map to the first/second dhyana (with joy), third dhyana (happiness without joy), and the equanimity beyond both would aid practitioners unfamiliar with the Yogacara dhyana taxonomy.

### 10d. "Sacrum" (血鑊) in skeleton chain -- section 30.15

**[O]** The translation (line 402) renders 血鑊 as "sacrum." The Chinese literally means "blood-cauldron" and refers to the pelvic basin or sacral area. A note clarifying this unusual term would be helpful for scholars cross-referencing the Chinese.

**Overall assessment:** The doctrinal content of sections 30.5--30.16 is accurate and sound. The nine stages of mental abiding, six powers, four attentions, and four types of vipashyana all match the standard Yogacara presentation. Cross-checking against Tsongkhapa's *Great Treatise* Vol. 3 confirms that this translation accurately renders the passage Tsongkhapa cites as authoritative. The six-aspect vipashyana framework is correctly established and consistently applied to impurity and loving-kindness contemplations. The samadhi definition is the canonical passage and is accurately rendered. No critical or major doctrinal errors were found. The four minor issues are primarily terminological consistency (glossary alignment) and footnote precision, not doctrinal inaccuracy.
