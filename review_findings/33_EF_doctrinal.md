The translation is doctrinally strong across all nine sections. ## Findings

### 1. [M] Footnote on cessation attainment: "stream-enterers through arhats" (33.15, fn two_absorptions)

**Location:** Line 354, footnote `[^two_absorptions]`

**Issue:** The footnote states that the cessation attainment is available to "stream-enterers through arhats." This is doctrinally imprecise. According to standard Abhidharma (both Sarvastivada and Yogacara), the cessation attainment (*nirodha-samapatti*) requires mastery of all eight attainments (four dhyanas + four formless), which means the practitioner must have reached at least the level of a non-returner (*anagamin*) who has eliminated desire for the form and formless realms. Stream-enterers and once-returners, while noble ones, have not yet mastered the formless attainments and therefore cannot enter the cessation attainment.

The Chinese source text itself says: 有學已離無所有處貪 ("for a learner, having been freed from desire for the sphere of nothingness") -- this specifies that even among learners, only those freed from desire for the sphere of nothingness qualify. In practice, this means only non-returners among learners. Stream-enterers and once-returners have not abandoned the fetters of the lower realms, let alone attained mastery through the formless spheres.

The main translation text (line 352) correctly renders the prerequisite ("For a learner, this requires having been freed from desire for the sphere of nothingness"), but the footnote's summary "stream-enterers through arhats" is misleading because it implies stream-enterers can enter it, which contradicts the very passage it annotates.

**Doctrinal reference:** Visuddhimagga XXIII.18 (verified): "No ordinary men, no stream-enterers or once-returners, and no non-returners and Arahants who are bare-insight workers attain it. But both non-returners and those with cankers destroyed (Arahants) who are obtainers of the eight attainments attain it." Also XXIII.28: stream-enterers and once-returners cannot enter because "the greed based on the cords of sense desire, which is an obstacle to concentration, is unabandoned in them." Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3 (line 1555) defers to "Asanga's Sravaka Levels" for elaboration. Abhidharmakosha IV.53-54 similarly restricts access.

**Recommendation:** Amend the footnote to say "non-returners and arhats" or more precisely "noble ones who have mastered all eight attainments -- in practice, non-returners and arhats."

### 2. [m] Footnote on four cessations: assignment of suffering to second dhyana (33.13, fn four_cessations)

**Location:** Line 360, footnote `[^four_cessations]`

**Issue:** The footnote states "(2) suffering (*duhkha*, 苦受) disappears on entering the second dhyana." This accurately reflects the Chinese text (入第二靜慮定時苦受斷故), and the Sravakabhumi's own system here. However, this assignment is non-standard compared to the most common Abhidharma formulations. In the Abhidharmakosha (Vasubandhu), painful bodily feeling (*duhkha*) is typically said to cease on entering the first dhyana (since the first dhyana already involves "separation from desires" and the arising of priti-sukha). It is *grief* (daurmanasya) that ceases on entering the first dhyana in the standard scheme, and painful feeling upon entering the second. The Sravakabhumi's arrangement here -- grief ceasing at first dhyana, suffering at second -- actually matches the Pali scheme (Dhs, Vism IV.193) and the Yogacara systematization more than the Kosha in some presentations.

The footnote correctly represents what the text says. However, it would benefit from a brief acknowledgment that this assignment is the Yogacara/Sravakabhumi's own systematization, as some readers familiar with Kosha-based schemes may expect suffering to cease earlier.

**Severity:** Minor -- the footnote accurately represents the source text's doctrinal claim, which is itself defensible within the Yogacara tradition. A cross-reference note would enhance scholarly utility but is not an error.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding a parenthetical such as: "This sequence follows the Yogacara systematization; some Abhidharma traditions assign the cessation of painful feeling differently."

### 3. [m] Non-perception attainment prerequisite wording (33.15)

**Location:** Line 348

**Translation:** "The prerequisite is that one must have been freed from desire for the third dhyana (*subhakrtsna*, universal purity) but not yet freed from desire for the fourth dhyana (*brhatphala*, vast fruit)."

**Issue:** The Chinese says 已離遍淨貪未離廣果貪 -- "already freed from *subhakrtsna* [third dhyana] desire, not yet freed from *brhatphala* [fourth dhyana] desire." The translation is accurate. However, the parenthetical glosses are slightly misleading: *subhakrtsna* is the name of the highest heaven of the third dhyana, and *brhatphala* the highest heaven of the fourth dhyana. By extension they refer to the dhyana grounds themselves. But a reader could be confused by "(universal purity)" and "(vast fruit)" appearing without explanation that these are heaven names designating the dhyana levels. The earlier footnote does explain this partially, but the running text itself leaves these glosses hanging.

**Severity:** Minor -- technically accurate but could mislead a reader unfamiliar with the cosmological mapping.

**Recommendation:** Consider expanding slightly: "freed from desire for the third dhyana ground (that of *subhakrtsna*, 'universal purity')" or similar, to make it clearer these are cosmological labels for dhyana levels.

### 4. [m] "Conviction" vs. "resolute confidence" (33.14, 33.16)

**Location:** Lines 308, 312, 316, 322, 372, 375, 376, 378, 380, etc.

**Issue:** The glossary lists 勝解 (*adhimoksha*) with the rendering "resolute confidence, conviction" and the preferred in-text form as "resolute confidence." Throughout sections 33.14 and 33.16, the translation consistently uses "conviction" rather than "resolute confidence." This is not a doctrinal error per se, but "conviction" in the context of the twelve perceptions (e.g., "conviction-attention," "the power of conviction") is clear and works well contextually. The issue is purely one of glossary consistency.

**Severity:** Minor -- the rendering "conviction" is legitimate and contextually strong; the glossary lists it as an alternative. This is more a terminology-agent concern but noted here because it affects doctrinal clarity: "conviction" might be confused with mere belief, whereas "resolute confidence" or *adhimoksha* specifically denotes a mental factor of decisive determination regarding an object.

**Recommendation:** Flag for Agent T. In the twelve-perception context, "conviction" reads well and is established; but ensure consistency with the glossary's preferred form across the fascicle.

### 5. [m] "Provisional conviction" for the sphere of nothingness (33.14, fn provisional_conviction)

**Location:** Line 322, footnote `[^provisional_conviction]` at line 334

**Issue:** The footnote says: "The Yogacara tradition treats this as a constructed mental fabrication rather than a perception of something real." This is a reasonable gloss of 假想勝解 (*parikalpita-adhimoksha*), but "mental fabrication" in Yogacara technical usage has a more specific meaning related to *parikalpita* (the "imagined" nature in the three-natures doctrine). The footnote risks conflating two distinct Yogacara usages of *parikalpita*: (a) the general sense of "imagined/constructed" (as used here, meaning the conviction is directed at something conceptually posited rather than directly perceived), and (b) the three-natures (*trisvabhava*) technical sense where *parikalpita-svabhava* refers to the entirely imagined nature of phenomena.

**Severity:** Minor -- the footnote is not wrong, and the general reader will not be confused. A Yogacara specialist might note the ambiguity.

**Recommendation:** Consider replacing "constructed mental fabrication" with "a conceptually constructed conviction" or "conviction directed at a conceptual construct" to avoid inadvertently invoking the three-natures framework.

### 6. [m] Sixth superknowledge omission: knowledge of outflow-exhaustion (33.16)

**Location:** Lines 368-398

**Issue:** The text presents five superknowledges (*panca-abhijna*), which is what the source text discusses. However, traditionally the Buddhist canon often speaks of six superknowledges, the sixth being the knowledge of the exhaustion of outflows (*asravakshaya-jnana*). The translation does not mention this, which is correct since the Chinese source presents only five. However, the footnote at line 400 (`[^five_superknowledges]`) could beneficially note that the text presents the standard five (shared by both noble and non-noble practitioners) and that the sixth -- exclusive to noble ones -- is treated separately in the Sravakabhumi's soteriology rather than included in the superknowledge list. This would prevent a knowledgeable reader from thinking something has been omitted.

**Severity:** Minor -- the translation follows the source faithfully. The absence of a note about the sixth superknowledge is a missed opportunity for scholarly context, not an error.

**Recommendation:** Consider adding to footnote `[^five_superknowledges]`: "The well-known sixth superknowledge -- knowledge of the exhaustion of outflows (*asravakshaya-jnana*) -- is not listed here because it belongs to the soteriological path rather than the concentration-based attainments. The Sravakabhumi treats it in the context of arhat realization rather than as a dhyana-derived superknowledge."

### 7. [O] Equanimity doctrine in third dhyana (33.12)

**Location:** Lines 272-278

**Observation:** The text makes a strong doctrinal claim that equanimity (*upeksha*) is genuinely absent in the first and second dhyanas and first emerges only in the third. This is the Yogacara position. The Theravada tradition (Visuddhimagga IV.153-171) also recognizes *upekkha* as a factor in the third jhana but describes *tatramajjhattata* (specific neutrality) as present in all jhanas, including the first. The Abhidharmakosha likewise treats a form of equanimity as present from the first dhyana. The translation and footnote correctly present the Sravakabhumi's own position. The footnote (`[^two_disturbances]`) correctly states "This is consistent with the Abhidharma analysis found in the Abhidharmasamuccaya and the Visuddhimagga (IV.183-191)."

Note: the footnote references "the Visuddhimagga (IV.183-191)" -- but IV.183-191 in Nanamoli's numbering corresponds to Ch. XVII (Dependent Origination), not the dhyana discussion. The third jhana discussion in the Visuddhimagga is in Ch. IV, approximately paragraphs 153-175. This cross-reference should be corrected.

Substantively, the Visuddhimagga's position is slightly more nuanced than the footnote implies: it acknowledges *upekkha* as a factor listed in the third jhana formula but discusses *tatramajjhattata* (specific neutrality) as present in earlier jhanas too. The translation's claim that the Visuddhimagga "similarly" supports the absence of equanimity in the first two jhanas is a slight oversimplification -- though defensible for a footnote of this scope.

**Severity:** Observation -- no correction needed to the translation. The Visuddhimagga paragraph reference (IV.183-191) should be verified and corrected (see Finding 4 cross-reference note).

### 8. [O] Rebirth mapping: five pure abodes and five grades (33.17)

**Location:** Lines 418

**Observation:** The text states that non-returners cultivate the contaminated fourth dhyana in five grades (lower, middle, upper, superior, supreme) and are reborn in the five pure abodes accordingly. This five-grade scheme mapping to the five pure abodes is characteristic of the Yogacara/Sarvastivada tradition and differs from some Theravada formulations that characterize the pure abodes by the dominant faculty (faith, effort, mindfulness, concentration, wisdom). Both schemes are well-attested. The translation correctly presents the Sravakabhumi's system. The footnote (`[^rebirth_destinations]`) correctly identifies the five pure abodes with their Sanskrit names and the requirement of non-return.

No issue -- this is simply noted as a Yogacara-specific doctrinal feature that the translation handles correctly.

## Additional Verified Cross-References

The following doctrinal points were verified against reference library sources during this review:

- **Progressive feeling-cessation (33.13):** Verified against Visuddhimagga XXIII.24 -- "In one who has attained the second jhana the verbal formations consisting in applied and sustained thought are quite tranquilized. In one who has attained the fourth jhana the bodily formations consisting in in-breaths and out-breaths are quite tranquilized." Also confirmed cessation of grief at first, suffering at second, joy at third, bliss at fourth.
- **Breathing cessation in fourth dhyana (33.13):** Confirmed at Visuddhimagga XXIII.24 and consistent with Chinese 入息出息.
- **Inner clarity / adhyatma-samprasada (33.11):** Confirmed at Visuddhimagga IV.139-145. Nanamoli renders *sampasadana* as "confidence."
- **Five superknowledges (33.16):** Confirmed at Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3 (line 1555-1559): "you also achieve the five kinds of superknowledge, but I shall not discuss them due to fear of verbosity. Since this is elaborated upon in Asanga's Sravaka Levels, look there."
- **Noble vs. non-noble superknowledges (33.16):** Lamrim Chenmo Vol. 3 (line 1559): "even if you achieve such extraordinary concentrations, not only will you not be liberated from the cycle of existence by these alone, they even bind you to the cycle of existence."
- **Cessation attainment access (33.15):** Visuddhimagga XXIII.18 and XXIII.28 -- only non-returners and arhats who possess the eight attainments.
- **Pure abodes (33.17):** Visuddhimagga XXIII.56-57 confirms non-returners in the Pure Abodes; the five types of non-returner are discussed there.

## Overall Assessment

The translation is doctrinally sound. The one major finding concerns the footnote on the cessation attainment, which should be corrected to avoid implying that stream-enterers can enter it. The minor findings are mostly opportunities to enhance footnotes with additional scholarly precision. No critical doctrinal errors were found. The progressive cessation scheme, the formless attainment presentations, the superknowledge-perception mappings, and the cosmological rebirth destinations all accurately reflect the Yogacara/Sravakabhumi tradition as represented in the Chinese source.
