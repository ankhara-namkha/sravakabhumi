**Text:** "This position is characteristic of early Yogacara thought and is explicitly rejected by other major Buddhist traditions — notably the *Lotus Sutra*, the *tathagatagarbha* literature, and the Madhyamaka tradition — which hold that all beings possess the potential for full awakening. The Madhyamaka tradition, especially Chandrakirti's Madhyamakavatara, rejects fixed gotra. The claim that the Sravakabhumi position is "early Yogacara thought" is accurate — later Yogacara (e.g., Paramartha's reception) moved toward reconciliation.

**Cross-reference:** The note directs readers to section 28.5's [^agotra] note. Verified: [^agotra] at fascicle 28 line 634 covers the same topic in greater detail. The cross-reference is valid and coherent.

### [^selfharm] (line 129) — EXPANDED

**Text:** "This is a descriptive observation about a specific doctrinal category, not a recommendation. The Abhidharma literature discusses this as a philosophical point about the nature of certain arhat attainments; the Vinaya literature, by contrast, strictly prohibits encouraging or facilitating the taking of life, including one's own."

**Factual accuracy:** CORRECT with minor nuance. The Vinaya prohibition on taking life is well-established (Parajika 3). However, the Vinaya's treatment of self-killing among arhats is more complex than a blanket "strictly prohibits." The case of Vakkali (SN 22.87) and Channa (SN 35.87) in the Pali canon involve monks praised by the Buddha after suicide precisely because they were arahants (or attained arahantship at the moment of death). The note says "encouraging or facilitating the taking of life" which is more precisely correct — the Vinaya prohibits *encouraging* another to die (Parajika 3), not necessarily all self-killing by arhats. The note's framing as "descriptive observation, not recommendation" is exactly right. The general claim about Vinaya prohibition is directionally accurate and appropriate for a footnote.

**Typos/grammar:** None found. Register: scholarly and restrained, matching the surrounding notes well.

**Verdict: PASS — no issues requiring correction. The Vinaya claim is slightly simplified but appropriate for a translator's footnote.**

## Fascicle 28

### [^agotra] (line 634) — MERGED

**Text:** "The concept of a 'person without lineage' (*agotra-pudgala*) — one who permanently lacks the potential for nirvana — is among the most controversial claims in Yogacara thought. The *Lankavatara Sutra* and *Lotus Sutra* explicitly reject this position, asserting that all sentient beings possess buddha-nature (*tathagatagarbha*). The Madhyamaka tradition and virtually all of East Asian Buddhist thought likewise reject it. Later Yogacara thinkers, including Paramartha, sought to harmonize the two positions. The vast majority of Buddhist traditions today — including all Tibetan schools, all East Asian schools, and the Theravada tradition — affirm that all beings possess the capacity for liberation. Asanga's presentation here represents the early, unmodified Yogacara stance, not the consensus of the Buddhist tradition."

**Check (1): "all Tibetan schools, all East Asian schools, and the Theravada tradition" affirming universal capacity.**

- **Tibetan schools:** CORRECT. All four major Tibetan schools (Gelug, Kagyu, Nyingma, Sakya) accept buddha-nature / tathagatagarbha as doctrinally normative, even if they interpret it differently (Madhyamaka-inflected vs. more literal readings). None accept permanent agotra.
- **East Asian schools:** CORRECT. Chan/Zen, Tiantai, Huayan, Pure Land, and Japanese schools universally accept universal buddha-nature. This is in fact the dominant soteriological framework in East Asia.
- **Theravada tradition:** ACCURATE with nuance. The note acknowledges this implicitly by saying "the capacity for liberation" rather than "tathagatagarbha" or "buddha-nature." The Theravada tradition does not use tathagatagarbha doctrine, but it does affirm that all beings can attain nibbana — there is no permanent agotra class in Theravada Abhidhamma. The traditional Theravada position is that all beings who encounter the Dhamma and practice properly can attain liberation. The note's careful wording ("capacity for liberation" rather than "buddha-nature") is precisely correct.

**Check (2): Paramartha harmonization claim.** CORRECT. Paramartha (6th century), who translated numerous Yogacara texts into Chinese, is well known for his attempts to reconcile the tathagatagarbha and Yogacara positions, notably through his concept of *amalavijnana* (immaculate consciousness) and his interpretation of Vasubandhu's works.

**Check (3): Does it read as one coherent paragraph?** YES. The note flows logically: (a) states the concept, (b) lists the rejections, (c) notes the harmonization attempt, (d) summarizes the modern consensus, (e) contextualizes Asanga's position. It reads as a unified scholarly footnote, not as two stitched notes.

**Typos/grammar:** None found.

**Register:** Matches the scholarly register of the surrounding fascicle 28 footnotes.

**Verdict: PASS — all claims accurate, reads as a single coherent note.**

### [^liberation_mastery] (line 394) — MODIFIED

**Text:** "The three perceptions of liberation (*vimoksha*), bases of mastery (*abhibhu-ayatana*), and totality (*krtsna-ayatana*)..."

**Check: Is *abhibhu-ayatana* the correct Sanskrit?** YES. *Abhibhu-ayatana* (lit. "bases of overcoming/mastery") is the standard Sanskrit term for the eight bases of mastery (ashta-abhibhv-ayatana). The stem form is *abhibhu* (one who overpowers/masters). The previous form *abhibhv-ayatana* would represent a sandhi form where the -u of abhibhu elides before the a- of ayatana, giving *abhibhv-ayatana*. Both forms are attested in the literature:
- *abhibhv-ayatana* — sandhi/compound form (technically correct in compound)
- *abhibhu-ayatana* — citation/dictionary form (more transparent to readers)

The change from *abhibhv-ayatana* to *abhibhu-ayatana* is defensible as a readability improvement. Both are technically correct Sanskrit. The unsandhi'd form *abhibhu-ayatana* is more commonly found in English-language Buddhist studies and reference works (e.g., Bodhi's translations, Gethin's *The Buddhist Path to Awakening*).

**Typos/grammar:** None found.

**Verdict: PASS — *abhibhu-ayatana* is correct and arguably more reader-friendly than the sandhi form.**
