# Sensitivity Review — Reader Safety and Responsible Publication

Reviewer: Senior dharma teacher and editor
Scope: All eight fascicles (26-33), methodology, introduction
Date: 2026-03-03
Purpose: Identify passages that could cause harm if read without proper context; evaluate adequacy of existing safeguards; suggest specific mitigations.

---

## 1. Agotra — Persons Without Spiritual Lineage

### [Risk level: HIGH] [fascicle_26_en.md: Section 26.3, para (4) By Aspiration]

**The passage:** "Among these, the aspirations of all persons can be shifted and can be abandoned. But lineage definitely cannot be shifted or abandoned." This presents lineage (*gotra*) as fixed and deterministic — a person of shravaka lineage who aspires to the mahayana "will subsequently and certainly abandon that aspiration."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^aspiration_lineage] states: "The Sravakabhumi presents a deterministic view: lineage cannot be shifted or abandoned." This accurately reports the text's position but does NOT contextualize it within the broader Buddhist tradition. A reader encountering this passage could conclude that Buddhism teaches spiritual predetermination — that some people are inherently incapable of certain paths.

**Suggested mitigation:** Expand footnote [^aspiration_lineage] to include a sentence such as: "This deterministic view of lineage is characteristic of early Yogacara thought and is explicitly rejected by other major Buddhist traditions — notably the Lotus Sutra and the tathagatagarbha literature — which hold that all beings possess the potential for full awakening. See the note on *agotra* at section 28.5."

---

### [Risk level: HIGH] [fascicle_28_en.md: Section 28.5, line 254]

**The passage:** "Permanent destruction of yoga refers to a person without lineage (*gotra*). Why? Because within their being there is no dharma capable of progressing toward nirvana; their supramundane yoga is permanently destroyed."

This is the most doctrinally dangerous passage in the entire translation. It states flatly that certain persons are permanently incapable of liberation — they possess "no dharma capable of progressing toward nirvana." Read without context, this could be used to categorize and exclude people, or to cause despair in a reader who identifies with the description.

**Existing mitigation:** Two footnotes exist:
- [^agotra]: "The concept of a 'person without lineage' (*agotra-pudgala*) — one who permanently lacks the potential for nirvana — is a distinctive and controversial Yogacara position... Other Buddhist traditions, including much of the Madhyamaka and East Asian Buddhist thought, reject this position, holding that all beings possess buddha-nature (*tathagatagarbha*)."
- [^agotra_context]: "The doctrine of *agotra* — that certain beings permanently lack the capacity for liberation — is one of the most contested claims in Yogacara thought. The *Lankavatara Sutra* and *Lotus Sutra* explicitly reject this position..."

**Assessment:** The existing footnotes are good — they identify the position as controversial and name the opposing traditions. However, there are two gaps:
1. Neither footnote states clearly that the *majority* of the Buddhist world today rejects this position. A reader might conclude this is a live 50/50 debate rather than a minority position within contemporary Buddhism.
2. Neither footnote addresses the pastoral concern directly: a reader who is struggling with doubt about their own capacity for spiritual growth should not walk away from this passage believing Buddhism has written them off.

**Suggested mitigation:** Add one sentence to footnote [^agotra_context]: "The vast majority of Buddhist traditions today — including all Tibetan schools, all East Asian schools, and the Theravada tradition — affirm that all beings possess the capacity for liberation. A reader should understand this passage as reflecting one strand of early Yogacara thought, not the consensus of the Buddhist tradition."

## 2. Corpse Contemplation

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [fascicle_26_en.md: Section 26.6]

**The passage:** The impurity contemplation lists "the livid, the festering, the decayed, the bloated, the gnawed, the blood-red, the scattered, bones, sinew-connected bones, and the skeleton." It then maps each stage to a specific type of desire it counteracts.

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^corpse_stages] contextualizes: "These external impurity stages correspond broadly to the cemetery contemplations (*shitavana-bhavana*) taught in the sutras... The progression — from the freshly dead to the fully disintegrated — systematically dismantles attachment to the body's appearance." Footnote [^charnel_ground] identifies this as a traditional practice with sutra parallels.

**Assessment:** The framing in fascicle 26 is adequate. The descriptions are presented within a clear doctrinal framework (six types of impurity, five types of desire, specific antidote relationships), and the tone is analytical rather than gratuitous. The footnotes establish the practice as traditional. Risk is moderate because the descriptions are relatively brief and taxonomic at this stage. No additional mitigation needed here.

---

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [fascicle_32_en.md: Sections 32.5, 32.8-32.10]

**The passage (32.5):** "Having apprehended the sign of luminosity, go to the charnel ground and apprehend the sign of the livid, and so on through to the skeleton. If you cannot go to the charnel ground, apprehend these signs from paintings or from sculptures of wood and stone."

**The passage (32.8):** The graduated corpse contemplation instructs the beginner to: "generate imaginative attention regarding a single livid corpse... then expand: to two, three, four, five, ten, twenty, thirty, forty, fifty, a hundred, a thousand, and onward until the livid fill every cardinal and intermediate direction." And then: "turn to your own beloved body and generate imaginative attention: after death, carried out to the charnel ground and left upon the earth, reaching the livid stage, the festering stage, and so forth through to the skeleton."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^teacher_frame] (at 32.1) clearly states: "From this point forward, the entire fascicle is framed as a skilled yoga teacher instructing a beginner... The instructional voice is maintained throughout." Footnote [^adhimoksha_practice] carefully explains imaginative attention as a deliberate meditative technique, not literal instruction to go find corpses. Footnote [^two_attentions] distinguishes imaginative from real contemplation.

**Assessment:** The teacher-frame footnote at the beginning of fascicle 32 is the single most important safeguard for this material. It establishes that the entire section is a supervised instruction from a qualified teacher to a beginner — not a DIY exercise. The footnotes on imaginative attention provide further context. However, the distance between the teacher-frame footnote (32.1) and the most graphic material (32.8, 32.10) means a reader who opens to the middle of fascicle 32 would miss it.

**Suggested mitigation:** Add a brief footnote at section 32.8 itself: "The contemplation instructions throughout this section presuppose the guidance of a qualified yoga teacher, as established at the opening of this fascicle (section 32.1). Traditional commentarial sources uniformly insist that cemetery contemplation be undertaken only under the supervision of an experienced teacher, as unsupervised practice can produce adverse psychological reactions including pathological fear or revulsion. See Visuddhimagga VI.1-4."

---

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_32_en.md: Section 32.10]

**The passage:** "Turn to your own beloved body and generate imaginative attention: after death, carried out to the charnel ground and left upon the earth, reaching the livid stage, the festering stage, and so forth through to the skeleton."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^body_threefold] explains: "The third mode is striking: the practitioner generates a vivid sequence of their own body being carried to the charnel ground and decaying through each asubha stage."

**Assessment:** This is the most psychologically intense instruction — imagining one's own body decomposing. For a reader with health anxiety, grief, or trauma related to death, this could be distressing. However, footnote [^body_threefold] acknowledges the intensity ("striking"), the teacher frame has been established, and the instruction is clearly presented as a meditative technique within a supervised context. The risk is manageable with the suggested mitigation at 32.8 above. No separate mitigation needed.

## 3. Self-Harm Reference

### [Risk level: HIGH] [fascicle_26_en.md: Section 26.2, type (25)]

**The passage:** "Out of fear of regression, they may contemplate harming themselves."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^selfharm]: "'Contemplate harming themselves' (思自害) refers to certain arhats of dull faculties who, fearing regression from their attainment, may consider ending their lives while still in the state of liberation. This is a well-known topic in Abhidharma literature."

**Assessment:** The footnote correctly identifies this as a doctrinal topic about arhats (not a recommendation), and the phrasing "contemplate harming themselves" is somewhat euphemistic. However, the footnote could more explicitly note that this is descriptive — reporting a doctrinal position about what certain realized beings might contemplate — and is in no way prescriptive. A vulnerable reader could interpret this as normalizing suicidal ideation as a response to spiritual regression.

**Suggested mitigation:** Expand the footnote: "This is a descriptive observation about a specific doctrinal category, not a recommendation. The Abhidharma literature discusses this as a philosophical point about the nature of certain arhat attainments; the Vinaya literature, by contrast, strictly prohibits encouraging or facilitating the taking of life, including one's own."

## 4. Gender Language

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [fascicle_28_en.md: Section 28.4, line 135]

**The passage:** "The youthful, flourishing, and attractive forms and appearances of women (*matrigrama*) — these are the supreme obstruction for good men who are properly engaged in training."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^matrigrama]: "The text identifies this as the 'supreme obstruction' specifically for male renunciants engaged in the training. This reflects the monastic context of the Sravakabhumi, where the audience is primarily bhikshus."

**Assessment:** The footnote correctly identifies the monastic context (male celibate practitioners) and does not universalize the claim. This is well handled. The passage accurately represents the source text and the footnote adequately frames it. No additional mitigation needed.

---

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_30_en.md: Section 30.10, line 260]

**The passage:** "Various signs cause the mind to scatter: signs of form, sound, smell, taste, and touch, as well as signs of desire, aversion, delusion, male and female, and so forth."

**Existing mitigation:** None — no footnote on "male and female."

**Assessment:** Low risk. "Signs of male and female" is part of a standard Abhidharma enumeration of objects that distract the meditation mind. In context, it is one item in a list of ten signs. It is not making a claim about gender itself but about the meditator's response to perception of gender characteristics. No mitigation needed.

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_30_en.md: Section 30.13, line 380]

**The passage:** "This corpse may be male, female, or of indeterminate sex; a dear one, an enemy, or a stranger; of low, middle, or high status; young, middle-aged, or old."

**Assessment:** This is a thorough and non-judgmental enumeration. The inclusion of "indeterminate sex" actually reflects the Abhidharma's recognition of non-binary categories. No risk; no mitigation needed.

## 5. Breathing Practice — Physical Harm Warning

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [fascicle_27_en.md: Section 27.3, lines 110-114]

**The passage:** "Through excessively intense effort, one produces imbalance in the body, or imbalance in the mind. How does bodily imbalance arise? When one forcefully holds the inbreath and outbreath, because the breathing is constrained, uneven wind circulates within the body. First, trembling and agitation arise throughout all the limbs and joints — this is called 'trembling agitation.' When this trembling-agitation wind increases, it can produce illness."

**Existing mitigation:** None — no footnote on this passage.

**Assessment:** This passage is actually a safety feature of the source text itself — Asanga is warning against a specific practice error. However, a reader unfamiliar with traditional meditation instruction might not realize that this warning is describing a well-known and avoidable hazard. The text helpfully identifies the problem (forceful breath retention) but does not say what to do about it.

**Suggested mitigation:** Add a footnote: "This warning about the physical consequences of forced breathing is consistent with guidance across Buddhist contemplative traditions. The Visuddhimagga (VIII.172-177) gives similar cautions. Modern meditation teachers universally advise that mindfulness of breathing should observe the natural breath without controlling or manipulating it. A practitioner who experiences trembling, pain, or agitation during breath meditation should immediately relax the effort and consult a qualified meditation instructor."

## 6. Person Typology and Hierarchical Language

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_26_en.md: Sections 26.2-26.3]

**The passage:** The twenty-eight types of persons and eleven criteria for their establishment constitute a detailed typological system for classifying practitioners. The descriptions of conduct types (greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive thought) include unflattering character portraits.

**Existing mitigation:** The footnotes explain the diagnostic purpose (matching personality type to meditation object) and cite parallels in the Visuddhimagga. The balanced-conduct and thin-dust types provide a wider range of human disposition.

**Assessment:** Low risk. These are diagnostic categories, not value judgments — the text explicitly prescribes specific meditation objects to address each disposition. The five affliction-dominated types are presented as tendencies to be worked with, not as permanent labels. The balanced-conduct and thin-dust types show that not all practitioners are affliction-dominated. The text's own structure (linking each type to its antidote practice) makes the therapeutic intent clear. No mitigation needed.

## 7. Fruitless Practice and Despair

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_29_en.md: Section 29.17]

**The passage:** "Through three causes, one who practices correctly and strives diligently may yet produce no fruit whatsoever." The three causes are undeveloped faculties, unsuitable instructions, and weak samadhi.

**Assessment:** Low risk. This passage could theoretically cause discouragement in a struggling practitioner, but its actual effect is constructive — it identifies specific, addressable causes of stagnation. It does not say practice is hopeless; it says three conditions must converge. The very next sentence affirms: "Only when all three converge... does diligent striving produce certain fruit." The passage actually functions as diagnostic guidance. No mitigation needed.

## 8. Four Maras — Terrifying Apparitions

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_29_en.md: Section 29.16]

**The passage:** "When dwelling in forest wilderness, under trees, among tombs, or in secluded rooms — if one encounters great and terrifying apparitions that cause the hair to stand on end; or if figures resembling shramanas, brahmins, humans, or non-humans appear suddenly and against all reason urge one to abandon the wholesome and adopt the unwholesome — these apparitions are mara's manufacture."

**Assessment:** Low risk. The passage describes frightening meditation experiences but frames them clearly as mara's work — not as real dangers. The framing is actually protective: it tells the practitioner that terrifying visions during solitary retreat are a known phenomenon, have a name, and are to be recognized and rejected. No mitigation needed.

## 9. Attainment of Non-Perception as Spiritual Dead-End

### [Risk level: LOW] [fascicle_33_en.md: Section 33.15]

**The passage:** The ordinary being "thinks: 'Perceptions are like an illness, like a tumor, like an arrow. Only non-perception is peaceful and subtle' (this is the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning)."

**Existing mitigation:** Footnote [^two_absorptions] provides extensive doctrinal context: "The Yogacara tradition emphasizes that these are functionally identical (mind ceases) but soteriologically opposite: one is based on wrong view, the other on right realization." The parenthetical "(this is the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning)" in the translation text itself is clear.

**Assessment:** Low risk. The text explicitly labels this as "the ordinary being's mistaken reasoning," and the footnote thoroughly explains why. A reader would have to ignore both the in-text parenthetical and the footnote to be misled. No mitigation needed.

## 10. Methodology: Limitations Section

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [00_methodology.md: "Limitations and Hopes"]

**The passage:** The limitations section addresses the translation's machine-generated nature, its lack of contemplative understanding, and its aspirations. It does not include any note about seeking qualified guidance for practice passages.

**Assessment:** The methodology section is honest and well-written. It acknowledges limitations clearly. However, it does not include any advisory about the practice material — specifically, that the detailed meditation instructions in fascicles 30-32 describe supervised contemplative training and should not be treated as a self-study manual.

**Suggested mitigation:** Add a short paragraph to the "Limitations and Hopes" section, after the paragraph beginning "What this process does offer...":

"A note on practice passages: The Sravakabhumi contains detailed meditation instructions — graduated contemplation exercises, breathing practices, and attentional training methods — that were designed to be taught by an experienced teacher to a student whose temperament, capacity, and readiness the teacher has assessed. The text itself makes this explicit: fascicle 32 frames its entire instruction as a 'teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga' guiding 'a beginner who relies on yogic practice.' Readers interested in undertaking any of the contemplative practices described here are strongly encouraged to seek the guidance of a qualified meditation teacher within an established Buddhist lineage."

## 11. Introduction: Missing Guidance Note

### [Risk level: MEDIUM] [00_introduction.md]

**Assessment:** The introduction provides excellent scholarly context — the text's historical significance, its influence on later traditions, the scope of the translation. It does not include any note about the nature of the practice material or the advisability of qualified guidance. Since this is the first substantive text a reader encounters, it is the natural place for such a note.

**Suggested mitigation:** Add one paragraph at the end of the introduction:

"A word about practice: While the Sravakabhumi is studied as a historical and doctrinal text, it is also a practice manual — and its meditation instructions were designed for transmission from a qualified teacher to a prepared student. The graduated contemplations of fascicle 32, the breathing practices of fascicle 27, and the attentional training methods throughout presuppose a teacher-student relationship that this translation cannot replicate. Readers who wish to undertake the practices described here are encouraged to do so under the guidance of an experienced meditation teacher. The text's own framework — which opens every major practice section with the instruction of 'a teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga' — underscores this point."

## What Is NOT a Concern

Several passages were examined and found to carry no genuine reader-safety risk:

- **Person typology (26.2-26.3):** The character portraits of affliction-dominated types are diagnostic, not derogatory. The text's own structure (each type matched to its antidote practice) makes the therapeutic intent transparent.
- **Corpse contemplation in fascicle 26:** Presented as taxonomic analysis within a clear doctrinal framework. Not gratuitous.
- **Four maras (29.16):** Frightening experiences are named and demystified. The passage is protective, not harmful.
- **Fruitless practice (29.17):** Identifies addressable causes. Constructive, not demoralizing.
- **Non-perception dead-end (33.15):** Explicitly labeled as mistaken reasoning. Thoroughly footnoted.
- **Gender language (30.10, 30.13):** Standard Abhidharma enumeration. Non-judgmental. The inclusion of "indeterminate sex" is actually progressive.
- **Cosmic suffering imagery (32.14):** Vivid descriptions of beginningless samsaric suffering (severed limbs, flowing blood, tears exceeding the oceans) are standard Buddhist contemplative rhetoric with clear canonical parallels (SN 15.3-4). They serve a specific doctrinal purpose within element differentiation practice. Though intense, they are no more graphic than standard Pali canon passages widely available in English.
