# Readability Review — Fascicle 32, Sections 32.1–32.4

Agent R (Readability) — read as practitioner (80%) / scholar (20%).
Register standard: Nanamoli's Visuddhimagga, Bodhi's anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee.

---

## Overall Assessment

The translation is substantially better than the worst passages in fascicles 29–31. The instructional frame is preserved, the teacher's voice is maintained, and there are genuine moments where the prose penetrates. However, several structural problems remain: one very long catalogue sentence that stalls all forward motion (32.2), formula monotony in the six-aspect list (32.4), and a handful of nested-qualification passages where the main verb gets lost. Section 32.3 is the strongest — the non-regret → gladness → joy sequence has natural momentum and the practitioner can follow it without effort. Section 32.1 is the thinnest — the opening question-answer unit answers its own question with a bare list and gives the reader nothing to grip.

Total findings: 11 (2 structural, 3 formula/monotony, 4 nested qualification, 2 mechanical)

## Section 32.1 — The Five Signs: Framework for the Beginner

### R1 — Register drift / structural issue [MODERATE]

**Location:** Opening paragraph (lines 23–29)

**Problem:** The section opens with a Q&A exchange that promises to answer "how does a beginner properly establish the cultivation of attention?" and "what does one first touch and realize?" The answer to the second question — "the joy of abandonment and mental one-pointedness" — appears as a bare phrase dropped at the end of the opening sentence, with no development, no framing, and no preparation for the reader. The teacher's welcome then immediately pivots to the five signs. The effect is that the framework sentence ("the joy of abandonment and mental one-pointedness") floats without any anchor — the reader who has no background will not know whether this is the goal, the first attainment, or a prerequisite. A scholar may trace it; a practitioner will be confused or simply skip it.

This is a register-drift problem: the passage reads as a chapter-header outline rather than as a living instruction.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph level):**

> How does a beginner first establish the proper cultivation of attention? And when practicing in accord with what has been established, what does one first touch and realize?
>
> The answer is the joy of abandonment — a mind that has let go — together with the quality of one-pointed attention. These are not distant attainments; they arise naturally when the beginner, guided by a skilled teacher, learns to apprehend five signs with discerning care.
>
> A teacher skilled and well-versed in yoga addresses the beginner who relies on yogic practice:
>
> "Welcome, Worthy One. Drawing on three sources — what you have seen, what you have heard, and what you can infer through reflection — you should learn to apprehend five signs: the sign of disenchantment, the sign of delight, the sign of danger, the sign of luminosity, and the sign of discerning the object."

*Note: This rewrite answers the question before pivoting to the instruction, orienting the practitioner and making the "joy of abandonment" a meaningful destination rather than an isolated phrase.*

## Section 32.2 — The Sign of Disenchantment

### R2 — Nested qualification / translated-ese [MAJOR]

**Location:** Long teacher-speech paragraph beginning "Wherever you dwell among the various settlements and villages" (lines 45–47)

**Problem:** The sentence beginning "Wherever you dwell..." runs for approximately 100 words across three levels of embedded enumeration before reaching the main clause — and the main clause never arrives in this sentence at all. What follows (line 47: "Or if you have directly witnessed...") is a continuation that adds yet more conditions. The reader must hold an open conditional frame ("if you hear of men or women...") through seven parallel catastrophe-types (enemies, fire, flood, wrongdoing, mismanagement, poor judgment, ill-disposed partners, household fire) before getting any grounding. This is translated-ese at its worst: the Chinese front-loads all conditions, and the translation has reproduced that structure without adaptation.

A practitioner reading this in a contemplative context — for whom this instruction is meant to be received and acted upon — will lose the thread by the third item in the list.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph level):**

> "Welcome, Worthy One. As you move through the villages and settlements where you dwell, let your attention rest on the signs of decline around you. You may have heard of men and women who once lived in happiness and later fell into misfortune — through serious illness, through death, through the ruin of those they loved. You may have heard of villages where wealth was destroyed: seized by enemies, burned, swept away by flood, squandered through wrongdoing or poor judgment, lost through failed enterprises or the schemes of dishonest partners, consumed in a household fire. Or perhaps you have seen these things directly, in your own settlement, or in your own life — sharp pleasures you once knew that later faded.
>
> "Whatever source brings these things to your attention — hearing, seeing, or your own experience — let them give rise to a deep disenchantment. Samsara is grievous suffering.

*Note: The list of catastrophes is compressed into a readable sequence; the practitioner hears the instruction and can follow it. The direct address is maintained throughout.*

### R3 — Nested qualification [MINOR]

**Problem:** This is actually a clean pair of sentences and reads well. No action needed — noting for completeness that this is the kind of parallel structure that WORKS in English.

*(No fix needed — flagged to confirm it is not a problem.)*

### R4 — >50-word sentence [MECHANICAL]

**Location:** "While I cycle through samsara, having not yet reached nirvana, not yet liberated in mind, nothing can be guaranteed. May these two — decline and prosperity — not appear before me. May they not cause me to fall into circumstances that give rise to fierce, harsh, bitter suffering. Through the force of this reflection, I shall sincerely take joy in renunciation and practice without heedlessness. By abiding often in this way, I shall put an end to what is without meaning." (lines 55–56)

**Problem:** The first extended sentence ("While I cycle through samsara...nothing can be guaranteed") is fine — 23 words. The issue is that "What is without meaning" as the final phrase is too compressed and abstract for the contemplative register. The phrase should land with weight; instead it is a Taisho Chinese formula (*無義* = "without meaning/purpose/benefit") that drops flat in English.

**Targeted fix:**
> "By abiding often in this way, I shall put an end to all that brings no lasting benefit."

### R5 — Formula: closing formula repetition [MINOR]

**Location:** Lines 55–57: The teacher's speech ends with a vow/reflection, then the text adds: "Thus you should attend well and receive this properly." This echoes the earlier "Attend carefully and receive this well" from line 55. The double closing — one inside the speech, one outside — creates a slightly awkward bracket. The external closing frame ("Thus you should attend well...") is formulaic and adds nothing.

**Targeted fix:** Delete line 57 entirely ("Thus you should attend well and receive this properly.") The teacher's voice already closes the speech adequately on line 55–56. If a transitional marker is needed for the next section, let 32.3 open directly.

## Section 32.3 — The Sign of Delight

### R6 — Nested qualification [MINOR]

**Location:** "the first basis of gladness... the further basis of obstacle-removing joy" (line 71)

**Problem:** "Obstacle-removing joy" is a gloss-style translation of a compound that the practitioner cannot easily receive. The phrase "the further basis of obstacle-removing joy" sounds like an academic label, not a living description. The practitioner needs to know what obstacle and what joy — the phrase should point to something felt.

**Targeted fix:**
> "Then, at the further basis of joy — the joy that clears what has been blocking the way..."

Or more directly:
> "Then, generating the joy that removes what stood in the way..."

### R7 — >50-word sentence [MECHANICAL]

**Location:** "'My discipline is pure. I have the power and capacity to abide in the training rules established by the Blessed One. In this very life, I can attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized.' From this, joyful delight arises." (lines 71–72)

**Problem:** The sentence containing "I have the power and capacity to abide in the training rules established by the Blessed One" is wordy but under 50 words. However, "attain the unattained, touch the untouched, realize the unrealized" is a triple formula that works — it has rhythmic momentum. No change needed here; noting for completeness.

*(No fix needed.)*

### R8 — Formula monotony [MINOR]

**Location:** "These forms of pleasant mental states are distinguished as follows: the first — arising from clean conscience — is called 'gladness'; the second — arising from confidence in attainment — is called 'joy.' Together they are called 'delight.'" (line 75)

**Problem:** The explanatory summary at the close of 32.3 drops from the teacher's voice into a taxonomic register. "These forms of pleasant mental states are distinguished as follows" sounds like a subheading in a textbook, not a meditation manual. The em-dash structure ("the first — arising from clean conscience — is called 'gladness'") is fine structurally, but the sentence frame kills the momentum just as the section reaches its summary.

**Targeted fix:**
> "Two qualities of pleasant mind emerge here. The first — gladness — arises from a clean conscience. The second — joy — arises from confidence in what can be attained. Together they constitute delight. This is apprehending the sign of delight."

## Section 32.4 — The Sign of Danger and Entry into Shamatha

### R9 — Nested qualification [MODERATE]

**Location:** "You should dwell often in the state of having abandoned worldly desire and grief. Whatever the object, diligently practice — whether in the shamatha mode or the vipashyana mode — and regarding those objects, cause the mind to abide, abide inwardly, and abide evenly." (lines 87–88)

**Problem:** The phrase "cause the mind to abide, abide inwardly, and abide evenly" comes at the end of a sentence that has already qualified itself twice ("Whatever the object," "whether in the shamatha mode or the vipashyana mode"). The triple abiding instruction (abide / abide inwardly / abide evenly) is correct and meaningful — it alludes to the nine stages — but it arrives buried. The bracketed footnote reference to the nine stages reinforces what the main text does not convey: that these three are a specific technical progression.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph level):**

> "Worthy One, you should dwell often in the state of having let go of worldly desire and grief. Whatever the object of your practice — whether you are working in the shamatha mode or the vipashyana mode — bring the mind to rest, settle it inward, and allow it to become even. From this will arise physical and mental pliancy, and the quality of one-pointed mind."

*Note: The three-stage abiding sequence is now a natural instruction sequence, not a list appended to a compound sentence.*

### R10 — Formula monotony: six-aspect list [MODERATE]

**Location:** The six perceptions listed at lines 95–97:
> "(1) The perception of signlessness. (2) Within signlessness, the perception of non-activity. (3) The perception of non-discrimination. (4) Within non-discrimination, the perception of being free from longing and agitation. (5) The perception of quiescence. (6) Within quiescence, the perception of peaceful bliss free from all torment."

**Problem:** The six perceptions are listed mechanically. The structural pair-pattern (X, then "within X, Y") is the right shape, and the footnote correctly identifies the three pairs. But as formatted, the six items read as six separate entries rather than three deepening pairs. A practitioner trying to understand and practice these will not see the three-tier structure. The present format looks like a glossary entry, not an instruction.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> "You should apprehend the sign in six dimensions — three deepening pairs:
>
> First, the perception of signlessness — and within signlessness, the perception of non-activity: that nothing here is pulling you into doing.
>
> Second, the perception of non-discrimination — and within non-discrimination, the perception of being free from longing and agitation: that nothing here is drawing you away.
>
> Third, the perception of quiescence — and within quiescence, the perception of peaceful bliss free from all torment: that what is present is not merely the absence of disturbance, but a living peace."

*Note: This makes the three-pair structure visible and gives each pair a brief orientation so the practitioner knows what they are perceiving and why.*

### R11 — Unexplained jargon [MINOR]

**Location:** "the dark category" and "the bright category" (line 89)

**Problem:** "Turned from the dark category toward the bright" appears without any prior explanation in section 32.4 and the reference is not self-evident. A practitioner encountering this phrase for the first time in this section will not know what "categories" are being referred to. (The terms come from earlier sections of the Sravakabhumi — the dark category being states that obstruct practice, the bright being states conducive to it.) A brief gloss in the text or a footnote would resolve this.

**Targeted fix:** Add a footnote: "The 'dark category' (*krtsna-tamas*) refers to mental states that obstruct meditation; the 'bright category' to mental states that support it. The terms were established in Fascicle 31."

Or add a brief parenthetical: "...you should then turn from the dark category (states that obstruct) toward the bright (states that support practice)..."
