## Findings

### 1. [M] 32.12 -- Translation of 壞緣念住 as "foundations with comprehensive referent" is misleading

**Location:** Line 309, footnote [^comprehensive] at line 327

**Issue:** The term 壞緣念住 is rendered as "foundations with comprehensive referent." The character 壞 does not mean "comprehensive" -- it means "to break down, destroy, analyze into parts." The compound 壞緣 means "deconstructed/analyzed referent" -- i.e., mindfulness whose referent has been broken down into its constituent skandhas, rather than taken as a whole person (friend/enemy/neutral). The rendering "comprehensive" misleadingly suggests the opposite: that the referent is taken as a totality rather than analyzed.

**Doctrinal significance:** The term captures a specific Yogacara analytical move: when the practitioner shifts from seeing "a friend" (a pudgala-concept) to seeing the five skandhas that constitute that apparent person, the meditation referent has been 壞 (deconstructed). The footnote's explanation of the concept is actually correct -- "person-categories are analyzed into their constituent skandhas" -- but the English rendering "comprehensive" directly contradicts this analytical meaning.

**Recommendation:** Render as "foundations with deconstructed referent" or "foundations with analyzed referent" (壞緣念住). Update the footnote to explain that 壞 here means "broken down/analyzed" -- the person-concept is deconstructed into skandhas. The current explanation of the concept in the footnote is accurate; only the English label needs correction.

### 2. [m] 32.12 -- Footnote [^three_happinesses] could clarify the dhyana correlation more precisely

**Location:** Line 323

**Issue:** The footnote says: "(2) blameless happiness with joy (無罪有喜樂) -- happiness of the form realm accompanied by *priti*; (3) blameless happiness without joy (無罪無喜樂) -- happiness of the higher form realm where *priti* has subsided." This is correct but slightly imprecise. *Priti* accompanies the first and second dhyanas specifically. In the third dhyana, *priti* has subsided but *sukha* (bodily/mental ease) remains. The fourth dhyana has *upeksha* (equanimity) rather than happiness per se. The footnote could be clearer about which dhyanas correspond to which type.

**Recommendation:** Optionally refine to: "(2) happiness of the first and second dhyanas, where *priti* (rapture) is present alongside *sukha*; (3) happiness of the third dhyana and above, where *priti* has subsided but *sukha* or equanimity remains." Minor -- current formulation is not wrong, just slightly vague.

### 3. [m] 32.13 -- Footnote [^all_real] statement that inversions are "directly observable in experience" needs qualification

**Location:** Line 351

**Issue:** The footnote states that dependent origination is entirely real contemplation because "the four inversions, craving, karma, and the resulting mass of suffering are not mentally constructed but directly observable in experience." This is doctrinally imprecise. The four inversions are precisely what is *not* directly observed by ordinary beings -- they are the cognitive errors that beings operate under unknowingly. What is directly observable is the *suffering* that results from them. The practitioner uses the wisdom of hearing and reflection (聞思慧) to trace the causal chain -- this is inferential analysis (a form of vicara/investigation), not direct perception. The Chinese text itself says 由聞思慧增上力故分別取相 -- "through the force of the wisdom of hearing and reflection, discriminate and apprehend signs."

The key doctrinal point the text is making is that dependent origination contemplation does not involve a constructed/imagined phase (勝解作意) -- everything contemplated is real rather than imagined. This is different from saying it is "directly observable." The contrast is between imagined (勝解) and real (真實), not between inferred and directly perceived.

**Recommendation:** Revise the footnote's explanation. Instead of "directly observable in experience," say something like: "because the objects of contemplation -- inversions, craving, karma, and their resultant suffering -- are real features of samsaric existence rather than mentally constructed scenarios (as in the imagined corpses of impurity contemplation or the visualized friends/enemies of loving-kindness)."

### 4. [m] 32.14 -- Footnote [^element_mapping] claim about "standard Pali commentarial understanding" could mislead

**Location:** Line 397

**Issue:** The footnote says the element differentiation mapping "differs from... the standard Pali commentarial understanding (own / others / alternating)." This is a defensible but simplified characterization. The Pali commentaries (Visuddhimagga, Papancasudani) do gloss internal/external/internal-and-external as own/others/alternating, but this is not the only Pali commentarial position, and the Sarvastivada tradition (from which the Sravakabhumi draws) had its own established understandings. The contrast is accurate in broad strokes but risks suggesting the Pali position is monolithic when it is not.

**Recommendation:** Minor. Consider softening to "differs from the common Pali commentarial understanding" rather than "the standard." Alternatively, no change needed if precision is not a priority here.

### 5. [m] 32.15 -- Footnote [^breath_modes] speculation about "deep absorption states" for 法爾

**Location:** Line 413

**Issue:** The footnote says regarding breath cessation before death "by the natural order of things" (法爾, *dharmata*): "perhaps referring to the deep absorption states where breathing becomes imperceptible, or simply to the inevitability of breath's cessation." The first suggestion (deep absorption states) is plausible but doctrinally speculative in this context. The text says 於未死入息出息無有流轉而起勝解。由法爾故 -- "generate imaginative attention regarding the cessation of breathing even before death, by the natural order of things." In context, this is more likely about contemplating the fact that as a natural law (*dharmata*), breath will inevitably cease, rather than a reference to the fourth dhyana's cessation of breathing (which belongs to a much more advanced stage than the beginner's practice described here). The speculation about absorption states could confuse a practitioner at this level.

**Recommendation:** Consider removing the "deep absorption states" suggestion, or at least reordering so that the more likely interpretation ("the inevitability of breath's cessation as a natural law") comes first and the absorption-state interpretation is clearly secondary: "most likely referring to the natural inevitability of breath's cessation; possibly also alluding to advanced states where breathing becomes imperceptible."

### 6. [C-verified: CORRECT] 32.16 -- Pliancy attainment sequence is doctrinally accurate

**Location:** Lines 427-431

**Verification:** The pliancy attainment sequence was compared point-by-point against:
- The Chinese source text (T30.0464c01)
- Tsongkhapa's *Lamrim Chenmo* Vol. 3, which directly quotes and comments on this Sravakabhumi passage (LCTC translation, pp. 539-543)
- Kamalashila's *Stages of Meditation* (Dalai Lama edition, p. 701)

The translation accurately preserves all 13 steps of the sequence:
1. Crown heaviness (precursor sign)
2. Mental heaviness eliminated
3. Mental suppleness and pliancy arise
4. Bodily pliancy follows
5. Wind element predominantly increases, great elements enter body
6. Bodily heaviness eliminated
7. Bodily pliancy pervades body "as if filling it to overflowing"
8. Mind leaps with joy and delight
9. Meditative object appears vividly
10. Pliancy force gradually softens
11. Subtle pliancy moves with body
12. Leaping exuberance subsides
13. Held by shamatha, mind moves serenely on its object

Tsongkhapa (LCTC Vol. 3, line 1353): "when physical pliancy initially occurs, due to the power of energy there arises a great sense of well-being in your body... Thereafter, the force of this initial occurrence of pliancy gradually subsides... this pliancy is coarse and excessively agitates your mind; so with its disappearance, there occurs a pliancy, tenuous like a shadow, that is compatible with steady concentration."

The translation's "A subtle pliancy moves with the body" accurately renders 有妙輕安隨身而行, which Tsongkhapa paraphrases as "shadow-like pliancy." Kamalashila (p. 701) confirms the same sequence: "the practitioner initially actualizes mental pliancy. This is preceded by a kind of heaviness of the brain... After generating mental pliancy, physical pliancy is actualized."

The footnote [^pliancy_process] correctly identifies this as "one of the most important [passages] in the entire Sravakabhumi" and rightly references Tsongkhapa's commentary. No doctrinal errors found.

### 7. [C-verified: CORRECT] 32.17 -- "Possessed of attention" as shamatha criterion is doctrinally accurate

**Location:** Lines 443-451, footnote [^possessed_attention]

**Verification:** The footnote states that 有作意 (*samanaskara*) "marks a critical threshold: the practitioner has for the first time attained a level of mental concentration belonging to the form realm (*rupa-dhatu*) rather than the desire realm. This is the formal criterion for the attainment of genuine shamatha."

Tsongkhapa (LCTC Vol. 3, line 1357-1359): "Once such things happen, 'You are said to have attention,' you achieve serenity and you are included among the ranks of 'those who have achieved attention.' For, by achieving serenity which is included in the access to the first stabilization, you achieve the smallest type of attention on the level of meditative equipoise."

And (line 1289): "Bhagavan, when bodhisattvas direct their attention inward and focus it upon their minds, what is this attention called for as long as physical pliancy and mental pliancy are not achieved? Maitreya, this is not meditative serenity."

The footnote is doctrinally precise. The connection to the Fourth Yoga Place (fascicle 33) is also accurate -- Tsongkhapa confirms that from this point the practitioner proceeds to the mundane or supramundane path (LCTC Vol. 3, line 1453-1455).

### 8. [m/observation] 32.16 -- Footnote [^pliancy_process] could note Tsongkhapa's warning about overestimating this attainment

**Location:** Line 435

**Issue:** This is not an error but a potentially useful doctrinal addition. Tsongkhapa devotes significant space (LCTC Vol. 3, lines 1397-1399) to warning that this attainment -- despite the remarkable experiential qualities of bliss, clarity, and non-discursiveness -- is "common to both non-Buddhists and Buddhists" and cannot be placed even on the Hinayana path. He notes that "a great number of people" have mistakenly equated this with high-level tantric attainment. Given that this footnote is the primary annotation on the most important practice passage in the fascicle, a brief note about this common misidentification could serve practitioners.

**Recommendation:** Optional. Consider adding one sentence: "Tsongkhapa warns that despite the remarkable qualities of bliss, clarity, and non-conceptuality, this attainment is common to Buddhist and non-Buddhist meditators alike and should not be overestimated as a high spiritual realization."
