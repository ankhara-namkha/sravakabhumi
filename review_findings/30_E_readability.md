# Agent R — Readability Review
## Fascicle 30, Section 30.5: The Four-Fold Investigation

---

### Overall Assessment

Section 30.5 is structurally sound and largely readable. The bold subheadings create a clear navigation structure. The five conduct-type portraits (greed, aversion, etc.) are vivid and alive. However, several passages suffer from translated-ese clause order, unexplained jargon on first use, a few nested qualification problems, and one instance of formula monotony. The section is close to NTC register but needs targeted revision in about eight places.

---

### Finding R-01 — Translated-ese / Nested Qualification
**Severity: H**
**Location:** Line 118 — "Investigating Lineage, Faculties, and Disposition Through Questioning" paragraph

**Problem:** The quoted question piles three distinct sub-questions into a single block, with each introduced by a dash-fragment. The clause order follows Chinese rhetorical structure (statement → rhetorical question → enumeration) rather than English analytical prose. A practitioner reading this aloud would stumble.

**Original:**
> "Elder, can you examine your own lineage, faculties, and disposition? What is your innate lineage — that of the shravaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, or the great vehicle? What are your faculties — dull, middling, or sharp? What is your disposition — greed-conduct, aversion-conduct, and so forth through discursive-thought conduct?"

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Elder, can you examine yourself honestly? Consider three things: first, your innate lineage — whether it inclines toward the shravaka vehicle, the pratyekabuddha vehicle, or the great vehicle. Second, your faculties — whether they are dull, middling, or sharp. Third, your disposition — whether it is rooted in greed, aversion, or one of the other conduct types."

This turns the Chinese dash-enumeration into sequenced questions with natural English signposting.

### Finding R-02 — Nested Qualification / Awkward Clause Order
**Severity: H**
**Location:** Lines 120–121 — the discernment passage

**Problem:** "If the student is discerning and able to know for themselves the distinctions among these, they skillfully apprehend the characteristics and answer accordingly. If by nature they are dull and cannot know these distinctions — cannot skillfully apprehend the characteristics — they are unable to answer as asked." The second sentence buries the main clause ("they are unable to answer as asked") after a doubled condition that repeats words from the first clause, producing a wooden, overstuffed structure.

**Original:**
> If by nature they are dull and cannot know these distinctions — cannot skillfully apprehend the characteristics — they are unable to answer as asked.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> If they are dull by nature, without the self-knowledge to discern these distinctions, the questioning will yield nothing — they simply cannot answer.

---

### Finding R-03 — Formula Monotony
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Lines 104–110 — the "What are the four X?" pattern

**Problem:** The section opens with two consecutive identically-structured Q&A blocks ("What are the four domains? ... What are the four methods?"), both formatted as numbered lists with the same syntactic skeleton. Individually fine; together, the repetition signals mechanical prose. A second instance follows in line 114 ("How does one investigate aspiration through direct questioning?"), then again at line 122 ("From this point..."). The subheading + "How does one investigate X through Y?" formula appears four times in succession. This is the Chinese 云何...謂 pattern reproduced literally rather than varied for English flow.

**Suggested approach:** Merge the two definitional blocks (four domains + four methods) into a single running paragraph before the practical examples begin. Then vary the subheading introductions: use the Q&A form for the first method, shift to a different transition for later ones ("The discourse method works differently..."; "Observed conduct offers a third angle...").

**Example revised opening:**
> The teacher examines the student across four domains — aspiration, lineage, faculties, and disposition — using four distinct methods: direct questioning, discourse, observed conduct, and (for teachers with higher attainments) direct knowledge of another's mind. Each method and domain is treated in turn.

### Finding R-04 — Unexplained Jargon on First Use
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Line 106 — *pranidhana*, *gotra*, *indriya*, *carita* all introduced in a single sentence

**Problem:** Four Sanskrit terms appear simultaneously in the domain-list sentence, each followed by a parenthetical English gloss. This works for reference but reads as a term-dump. A practitioner encounters the wall of Sanskrit before the section has established what these categories mean or why they matter for teacher-student assessment.

**Note:** The glosses themselves are correct and helpful — the issue is pacing. This is a borderline finding; if the parenthetical form is the project's standard first-use convention, it is acceptable. Flag for editor judgment rather than mandatory fix.

**Suggested light revision:**
> (1) One should investigate the student's aspiration — what vehicle they are drawn toward (*pranidhana*). (2) One should investigate their spiritual lineage — the vehicle for which they are innately suited (*gotra*). (3) One should investigate their faculties — whether sharp, middling, or dull (*indriya*). (4) One should investigate their conduct disposition — what afflictive pattern predominates (*carita*).

This distributes the gloss into the enumeration itself, so each term is defined when named rather than tagged in a lump.

### Finding R-05 — Translated-ese / Register Drift
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Line 136 — the delusion-conduct passage

**Problem:** "Those of delusion-conduct (*moha-carita*), when they hear teachings of definitive penetration to nirvana and dispassion, generate extreme alarm and dread. Like those with dull faculties, they show the same pattern of difficulty." The phrase "generate extreme alarm and dread" is translated-ese — Chinese compounds like 驚怖/驚恐 yield this word-pair, but English would not phrase it this way in living prose. "Show the same pattern of difficulty" is vague and academic; it does not tell the practitioner what they will actually observe.

**Original:**
> Those of delusion-conduct (*moha-carita*), when they hear teachings of definitive penetration to nirvana and dispassion, generate extreme alarm and dread. Like those with dull faculties, they show the same pattern of difficulty.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Those of delusion-conduct (*moha-carita*) are startled and frightened when they hear teachings aimed squarely at nirvana and dispassion. Like students with dull faculties, they struggle to comprehend and absorb what they hear.

---

### Finding R-06 — Translated-ese / Awkward Phrasing
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Line 138 — the pride-conduct passage

**Problem:** "Even when skillful means are employed to draw them toward right transformation, they do not clearly speak words of approval." This is translated-ese: "do not clearly speak words of approval" renders a Chinese verbal phrase (不明言印可 or similar) literally. In English, the natural idiom is simpler and more direct.

**Original:**
> Even when skillful means are employed to draw them toward right transformation, they do not clearly speak words of approval.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Even when the teacher uses skillful means to bring them around, they give no open acknowledgment — no "yes, I see."

---

### Finding R-07 — Unexplained Jargon
**Severity: M**
**Location:** Line 110 — *paracitta-jnana* introduced as a method

**Problem:** The fourth investigation method is introduced as "knowledge of others' minds (*paracitta-jnana*)." The parenthetical gloss is helpful, but a practitioner might wonder whether this is a rare attainment or an ordinary skill. The subsequent paragraph (lines 148–149) clarifies that it requires "accomplished in practice" and specifically "attained this knowledge." The clarification comes too late; the reader carries the ambiguity for a full section.

**Suggested fix:** Add a brief qualifier at first introduction:
> (4) Investigation through direct knowledge of others' minds (*paracitta-jnana*) — available only to teachers who have cultivated this attainment.

This resolves the ambiguity immediately and prevents the practitioner from wondering whether ordinary teachers are expected to use this method.
