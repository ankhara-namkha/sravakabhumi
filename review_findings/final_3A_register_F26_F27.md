# Senior Reader Report: Prose Register Review
## Fascicles 26 and 27 — End-to-End Reading Assessment

**Role:** Senior Reader, Nalanda Translation Committee (simulation)
**Standard:** Nanamoli's Visuddhimagga, Bodhi's anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee
**Audience calibration:** 80% practitioner (wants penetration), 20% scholar (wants traceability)
**Date:** 2026-03-02

---

## Executive Summary

Fascicle 26 is substantially stronger than Fascicle 27. The opening sections (26.1–26.3) represent the translation's weakest prose — the person-typology catalogue runs long stretches of near-identical sentence architecture without variation. Sections 26.5–26.7 represent the translation's strongest prose — the Revata dialogue in particular reads as living instruction. Fascicle 27 maintains adequate clarity throughout but suffers from a structural problem: the material is genuinely taxonomic and technical, and the translation often follows the Chinese clause-by-clause rather than finding a natural English rhythm for it. The practice-oriented sections in 27.3 (breathing) are the brightest spots in Fascicle 27. The final section (27.6, four instructions) feels rushed and thin compared to the rest.

**Overall register verdict:** B+ for Fascicle 26; B- for Fascicle 27. No passages fail outright. The problems are subtler: formula monotony, translated-ese clause structure, and rare moments of nested qualification. Both fascicles clear the NTC bar at the macro level but fall short of the Nanamoli/Bodhi standard at the sentence level in specific passages identified below.

## 1. Translated-ese

### Definition applied:
Would a native English speaker who is NOT a translator write this sentence? Test: read the sentence aloud without looking at surrounding context. Does it land?

### Fascicle 26 findings:

**F26-TE-01** (§26.2, persons #3–#8, repeated across six definitions)
> "It is a person who in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of greed. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of desire, they have fierce greed and enduring greed."

The triple construction "cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated" is a Chinese triad (修習，多修習，殷重修習 or similar) flattened into English as three synonyms. "Cultivated" and "repeatedly cultivated" are redundant in English. "Due to this cause and condition" is directly calqued from 由此因緣 — a Chinese transitional that English writers would render as simply "and so" or "therefore." The phrase "with regard to objects of desire, they have fierce greed and enduring greed" front-loads the prepositional phrase Chinese-style; English would follow subject-verb-object naturally.

*Severity: Moderate. Repeated six times in succession, which compounds the problem.*

**F26-TE-02** (§26.3, portrait of greed-conduct person)
> "With relaxed countenance, level gaze, and smiling face, they speak first to others."

This is a literal Chinese clause inversion. Chinese places the manner phrase before the main verb habitually. English wants the action first: "They greet others readily, their face open and smiling." The current phrasing sounds like stage directions, not description of a person.

**F26-TE-03** (§26.3, portrait of delusion-conduct person)
> "Whatever they think, they think it poorly; whatever they say, they say it poorly; whatever they do, they do it poorly."

This triple-doublet is a direct mirror of the Chinese parallelism (凡所思惟皆不善巧，凡所言說皆不善巧，凡所造作皆不善巧). It is not ungrammatical, but no English writer would write this. They would write: "Their thinking, speech, and action are all marked by the same impoverishment" or "They reason badly, speak badly, and act badly."

**F26-TE-04** (§26.5, image with discernment)
> "They observe, investigate, thoroughly investigate, comprehensively examine, and comprehensively analyze."

Five verbs in a row, all near-synonymous: this is a Chinese rhetorical list (伺察，尋思，審慮) expanded into five English glosses. No English author would chain five near-synonyms here. A revision to "they examine and probe the object with sustained attention" would capture the sense without the robotic enumeration.

**F26-TE-05** (§26.5, accomplishment of the purpose)
> "For one who enters the first dhyana, at the time of attaining the first dhyana, this direct perception arises with respect to the domain of the first dhyana. For one who enters the second, third, or fourth dhyana, at the time of attaining the second, third, or fourth dhyana, this direct perception arises with respect to those domains."

The repetition of "dhyana" five times in two sentences, and "at the time of attaining X, this arises with respect to X" mirroring Chinese clause parallelism, makes this sound machine-translated. English would say: "When the practitioner attains each dhyana in turn — first through fourth — direct perception arises corresponding to that level's domain. The same holds for the formless attainments through to neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

**F26-TE-06** (§26.6, charnel ground path descriptions)
> "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse...'"

This is a fine scholarly rendering of the Chinese quotation formula. However it recurs four times in three paragraphs (lines 496–502). The fourth repetition especially feels mechanical. After the first occurrence, a more natural approach is to integrate the subsequent stages in prose rather than using the "when it is said" frame each time.

### Fascicle 27 findings:

**F27-TE-01** (§27.2, elements framework, repeated across all six elements)
> "The [X] element is twofold: internal and external. The internal [X] element is the specific quality of [Y] within one's own body — included in the category of the [Z] and [W] — which is personally appropriated."

This formula appears (with minor variation) eight times across the six elements subsections. Each element gets two rounds of this: once for internal, once for external. The formula is fine once. By the third iteration the reader has absorbed the pattern and the repetition becomes deadening rather than structurally clarifying. The Chinese text uses this formula mechanically (X界，一者內，二者外。內X界者...); the English can compress after establishing the template.

**F27-TE-02** (§27.3, reverse counting)
> "Counting backward in sequence starting from ten — then nine, then eight, then seven, then six, then five, then four, then three, then two, then one — is called reverse counting."

This is the Chinese numeral sequence (十、九、八...一) fully expanded. No English explanation of reverse counting would expand every digit. It could simply read: "counting backward from ten to one in sequence."

**F27-TE-03** (§27.3, advanced counting)
> "This ten-as-one counting, when in the process the mind is free from distraction, this is called having become well-practiced."

Doubled subject: "this... this is called" mirrors a Chinese pointing-back construction. Should read: "When the mind remains undistracted throughout this counting, one is said to have become well-practiced."

**F27-TE-04** (§27.4, formations skandha definition)
> "Furthermore, all remaining mental factors apart from feeling and perception are collectively called the formations skandha."

"Collectively called" — the adverb is a Chinese formula 總名 (collectively called/designated). It appears so frequently in the skillful objects section that it becomes a verbal tic. "Are" or "constitute" would suffice in most places.

**F27-TE-05** (§27.5, suffering of conditioned existence)
> "Because those skandhas are impermanent by nature, associated with arising and ceasing, having all clinging, followed by the heaviness of the three types of feeling, included within insecurity, not free from the suffering of suffering and the suffering of change, and operating without autonomy — through the suffering of conditioned existence, they are said to be 'suffering.'"

This is the worst instance of translated-ese in Fascicle 27. The main clause — "they are said to be 'suffering'" — is buried under seven nested participial phrases, each a Chinese clause converted to an English participial. This is a severe nested-qualification failure as well (see section 3 below).

## 2. Formula Monotony

### Fascicle 26 findings:

**F26-FM-01** (§26.2, person definitions #3–#7, five iterations)
The template:
> "It is a person who in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of [X]. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of [Y], they have fierce [X] and enduring [X]. This is called a person dominated by [X]."

This formula is repeated identically five times (persons #3 through #7 — greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive thought). The variation in what fills [X] is not enough to prevent monotony. By the third occurrence (delusion-conduct, line 81) the formula has become background noise. The Chinese expects this parallelism; English can adopt it once and compress thereafter.

**F26-FM-02** (§26.3, person portraits — conclusion formula)
> "Such and similar are to be known as the characteristics of a person of [X]-conduct."

Repeated five times in succession (lines 185, 189, 193, 197, 201). The fifth repetition is the problem; it has lost all rhetorical force by then. Variation: "This portrait marks the person dominated by discursive thought" or simply ending the final description without the closing formula.

**F26-FM-03** (§26.3, establishment criteria — recurring frame)
> "Such is what is called the establishment of persons by differentiation of [X]."

Repeated eleven times (once per establishment criterion, §26.3 sub-sections). Eleven is too many. The frame is helpful for orientation the first several times; after that it becomes tedious. Possibilities: drop it for the shorter criteria (preparatory practice, meditative concentration) where it adds nothing; or vary with "This completes criterion (7)," "Criterion (8) — birth — was treated above," etc.

**F26-FM-04** (§26.5, verse commentary sections)
> "Here, '[phrase]' refers to [element]."

This formula for commenting on each verse phrase is used five times in a row in the First Verse commentary and four times in the Second Verse commentary (lines 413, 426–432). It is appropriate for scholastic verse commentary and need not be eliminated, but the mechanical uniformity — always the same verb "refers to" — could be varied: "designates," "corresponds to," "points toward."

### Fascicle 27 findings:

**F27-FM-01** (§27.2, element subsections)
The template: "[Element] is twofold: internal and external... What are its instances? They are: [list]. These are called the internal [element] element... What are its instances? They are: [list]. Such and similar things are called the external [element] element."

Repeated across earth (§27.2.1), water (§27.2.2), fire (§27.2.3), and wind (§27.2.4). This is necessary to some degree — the text is being systematic — but the internal/external split template plus the "What are its instances?" sub-question frame repeats eighteen times across four elements without structural variation. After water element, the reader knows the pattern. The translation could compress the later elements: introduce the template fully for earth, slightly abbreviated for water, and compressed to a single paragraph for fire and wind, with a note that the pattern follows.

**F27-FM-02** (§27.4, skandha definitions conclusion formula)
> "All of this is collectively called the [X] skandha."

Repeated four times in five paragraphs (feeling, perception, formations, consciousness). Effective once; cumulatively dulls by the fourth.

**F27-FM-03** (§27.5, truth definitions)
> "What is the noble truth of [X]? [Definition]. This is called the noble truth of [X]."

Used four times in sequence (lines 374–381). This is structurally appropriate for listing the four truths. The variation "What is the noble truth of the path? It is the eightfold and related noble path." is notably thin compared to the others — the brevity creates a jarring contrast rather than graceful compression.

## 3. Nested Qualification

### Fascicle 26 findings:

**F26-NQ-01** (§26.3, greed-conduct portrait) — moderate
> "A person of greed-conduct, even with regard to inferior objects of desire, can give rise to the most extremely heavy, upper-grade entanglement (*paryavasthana*) [the active, manifest form of an affliction] of greed — how much more so with middling and upper-grade objects."

The parenthetical gloss [the active, manifest form of an affliction] inserted mid-sentence creates a stutter. Either move the gloss to a footnote or restructure so the term "entanglement" is introduced cleanly once. The footnote apparatus is extensive and well-used elsewhere; this belongs there.

**F26-NQ-02** (§26.5, image with discernment — resolute confidence passage)
> "Although the knowable objects are not actually present through direct encounter, nor has some unrelated substitute appeared, nevertheless — through the resolute confidence of the ground of meditative equipoise, through an apprehension by attention that is similar to the actual objects — the knowable objects appear in a similar fashion."

Two prepositional "through" phrases stacked between the "nevertheless" and the main verb "appear." The main point — that the meditation image appears through meditative conviction even without the actual object — is buried. Restructure: "Nevertheless, through meditative conviction and an attention that resembles direct encounter, the knowable objects appear — not as they are in actuality, but in a likeness. This is the meditation image."

**F26-NQ-03** (§26.6, four sensual craving subtypes)
> "Sensual craving has four subtypes:[^four_sensual] (1) desire for color, (2) desire for shape, (3) desire for fine touch, and (4) desire for attendance."

No nesting problem here — the footnote [^four_sensual] explains further. This is handled well.

### Fascicle 27 findings:

**F27-NQ-01** (§27.3.2, supporting conditions for breath)
> "If breathing depended only on the body, then beings who enter the attainment of non-perception, who enter the attainment of cessation, or who are born in the non-perception heavens — their breathing should still operate. If breathing depended only on the mind, then those who enter formless attainments or who are born in the formless realm — their breathing should still operate. And if breathing depended merely on body and mind without the qualification 'each according to its proper function,' then beings who enter the fourth dhyana or who are born in that realm, as well as beings in the embryonic stages of *kalala*, *arbuda*, *peshi*, and so forth[^embryonic_stages] — their breathing should operate."

Three parallel conditional arguments, each branching into a mid-sentence relative clause with em-dash isolation. The logic is clear but the structure is heavy — the reader must hold each "if" condition suspended across a relative clause before reaching the consequence. This could be restructured into a cleaner if/then format:

*"Three counter-examples demonstrate this: if breathing depended on body alone, beings in the non-perception attainment or born in the non-perception heavens would still breathe — but they do not. If it depended on mind alone, those in the formless attainments or formless realms would breathe — but they do not. And if it required only body-and-mind without proper coordination, beings in the fourth dhyana or in the earliest embryonic stages (*kalala*, *arbuda*, *peshi*) would breathe — yet they do not. Therefore the breath requires both body and mind, each functioning in its proper mode."*

**F27-NQ-02** (§27.5, suffering of conditioned existence — the worst passage)
> "Because those skandhas are impermanent by nature, associated with arising and ceasing, having all clinging, followed by the heaviness of the three types of feeling, included within insecurity, not free from the suffering of suffering and the suffering of change, and operating without autonomy — through the suffering of conditioned existence, they are said to be 'suffering.'"

Seven participial/adjectival clauses stacked before the main verb. The reader has entirely lost the subject ("those skandhas") by the time the predicate arrives. This is the single most structurally failed sentence in the two fascicles. See Rewrite #5 below.

**F27-NQ-03** (§27.4, elements — skill definition)
> "If one truly knows, accepts, and carefully investigates that these eighteen dharmas arise and appear each from its own distinct element, its own distinct seed, and its own distinct lineage — this is called skill in the elements."

The "if... this is called" pattern, combined with three nested "its own distinct" phrases, creates a clause that must be parsed twice to be understood. Better: "Skill in the elements means knowing that these eighteen dharmas each arise from their own distinct element, seed, and lineage — from their own causal matrix. To know this is to be skilled in causation."

## 4. Register Drift Within Each Fascicle

### Fascicle 26:

**Opening register (26.1):** Appropriately formal and elevated. The opening question-list is handled well — a reader picks up the scope immediately.

**26.2 (person typology):** Register drops to functional-scholastic. This is partly inevitable given the content, but the formula monotony (see §2) makes this the least alive section of either fascicle. No contemplative warmth. The brief simile passages within the intermediate state descriptions (lines 113–117) are the brightest moments — "like a tiny spark from kindling" — but they are isolated.

**26.3 (establishment criteria):** Register improves in the conduct portraits. The portrait of the aversion-conduct person (line 189) is genuinely vivid: "Their dispositions are harsh and fierce. They are perverse and malicious, fault-finding and contentious." This achieves the register of description that could be recognized by a meditator from their own observation.

**26.4–26.5 (objects, Revata dialogue):** The strongest register in the entire two-fascicle reading. The Revata dialogue is handled with genuine authority. The Buddha's instructions read as instructions — the voice is alive. "Excellent, excellent! You are now skillfully able to ask about this matter" has the right weight. The verse commentaries are scholastic but cleanly so.

**26.6–26.7 (impurity, loving-kindness):** Register remains good. The charnel ground passage is appropriately vivid without being gratuitous. The loving-kindness section (26.7) is the warmest passage in Fascicle 26 — "with an equally established intention of benefit" has the quiet authority of someone who has cultivated this.

**Overall arc (F26):** Register rises from pedestrian (26.2–26.3) to genuinely strong (26.5–26.7). The fascicle ends better than it begins.

---

### Fascicle 27:

**Opening register (27.1):** Appropriately brief and pointed. The single-paragraph treatment of dependent origination as a purifying-conduct object is well-judged.

**27.2 (element differentiation):** Register dips to systematic inventory. This is the flattest section of Fascicle 27. The six elements subsections are accurate but feel like a taxonomic list being worked through rather than a teaching being received. The wind element subsection (external winds, line 88) has a brief moment of vivid list-writing — "break trees, topple walls, crumble mountains, and churn the seas" — but it's the exception.

**27.3 (mindfulness of breathing):** The register recovers here. The description of transcending the counting stage (lines 168–169) is genuinely good: "in unbroken continuity — first apprehending the inbreath as it turns; when the inbreath has ceased, apprehending the empty interval between breaths" — this reads as practice instruction, not procedure description. The description of applying dull vs. sharp faculties (lines 170–172) reads with appropriate specificity.

**27.4 (skillful objects):** Register falls again. The five-skills taxonomy is inherently technical, and the translation does not find any warmth or movement within the structure. The summary (lines 341–343) is clean and efficient but the section as a whole reads as catalogue.

**27.5 (affliction-purifying objects):** Register improves. The four noble truths treatment — particularly "like illness, like a boil, like a poisoned arrow" (line 368) and the doctor metaphor (line 384) — is the most teaching-register passage in Fascicle 27. The analysis of the three types of suffering (27.5.3) is genuinely careful and would serve a practitioner who wants to penetrate the teaching.

**27.6 (four instructions):** Register is flat. This short section closes the fascicle without much energy. The descriptions of the four types are accurate but sparse. "Realization-based instruction" receives the most engaging treatment (line 442: "what one has personally... attained, touched, and realized") but even this is formulaic. The three supernormal displays section (lines 448–456) feels like a footnote elevated to body text.

**Overall arc (F27):** Register is uneven — rises and falls with the taxonomy density of the content. The transition from the end of 27.3 (anapanasati, with real contemplative energy) to 27.4 (pure Abhidharma taxonomy) is the sharpest downshift. The fascicle ends quietly rather than landing with weight.

## 5. Contemplative Quality (Sections 26.5–26.7, 27.1–27.3)

### 26.5 (pervasive objects, Revata dialogue):
**Assessment: Alive.** The Revata dialogue achieves a genuine teaching register. The movement from "settling upon a matching object" through "settling without inversion" to "not abandoning dhyana" maps onto a practitioner's actual experience of meditation instruction. The verse passages function as summation rather than decoration. The footnote on the image synonyms (^image_synonyms) is exemplary — the note on "light-image" and Tsongkhapa's rendering is exactly the kind of scholarly footnoting that serves rather than distracts.

Minor concern: "They observe, investigate, thoroughly investigate, comprehensively examine, and comprehensively analyze" (line 324) — this five-verb chain appears twice in this section and should be collapsed. See TE-04.

### 26.6 (impurity contemplation):
**Assessment: Mostly alive, with one flat stretch.** The corpse-stage progression is well handled. The charnel ground quotation passages (lines 496–502) are slightly flat due to the "When it is said" repetition (see FM-03 concern above), but the material itself is powerful and the translation does not bowdlerize it.

The analytic section on the five types of desire and their antidotes (lines 485–508) drops register significantly — it becomes a mapping exercise. This is partly inevitable but could be written with slightly more warmth: instead of "Through the internal impurity of putrefaction as object, one purifies the mind from desire and craving for one's own body" (purely functional), one might write "The internal impurity object — contemplating the gross matter of one's own body — works against the attachment one feels to that body."

### 26.7 (loving-kindness and compassion):
**Assessment: Alive, with room for more warmth.** The sub-distinction of loving-kindness (beings without suffering), compassion (beings with suffering), and sympathetic joy (beings with happiness) is precise and useful. The footnote on equanimity (^maitri_object) is excellent. The brahmavihara register in this section is more scholastic than contemplative — Buddhaghosa and Analayo both write about loving-kindness with more warmth and directness. The passage could benefit from one or two sentences that invite the practitioner in rather than just categorizing.

### 27.1 (dependent origination):
**Assessment: Adequate but thin.** The single-paragraph treatment is accurate but too compressed to feel alive. The line "Only dharmas give rise to dharmas; there is no agent and no experiencer" is the most pointed line in the passage and would benefit from being its own paragraph, given the weight it carries as the essential insight of dependent origination.

### 27.2 (element differentiation):
**Assessment: Flat.** This section reads as inventory. The brief conclusion (lines 100–101) — "They no longer exalt themselves; their arrogance and pride become attenuated" — has the right register but comes after five dense pages of listing. A practitioner cannot travel from the element list to the liberating insight in one sentence.

### 27.3 (mindfulness of breathing):
**Assessment: Strong.** The best section of Fascicle 27 for contemplative register. The description of the intermediate inbreath (lines 112–114) is precise without being dry. The counter-examples for breath and consciousness (lines 122–123) are logical and grounding. The "transcending the counting stage" passage (line 168) is the single finest practice-instruction passage in both fascicles: "alternating in continuous flow, without movement, without wavering, without the activity of distraction, turning with delight" — this is language a practitioner can use.

The sixteen victory practices commentary (lines 226–266) maintains good register throughout. The comment on the third tetrad — "If at times one sees that the mind is covered and obstructed by the hindrance of torpor and drowsiness... then one attends outward to a pure and excellent object" (line 250) — reads like direct instruction and lands well.

## 6. Reading Arc

### Fascicle 26 as a continuous reading experience:
The fascicle has three clear movements: (1) taxonomy (26.1–26.3), (2) theory of meditation objects (26.4–26.5), (3) specific practice objects (26.6–26.7). The transitions between these movements are clean. The insertion of the Revata dialogue in 26.5 is particularly well-placed — it provides a narrative break within a long technical exposition and restores a voice to the text.

The primary obstacle to reading flow is the formula monotony in 26.2–26.3. A reader who stays with those sections arrives at 26.5 somewhat worn down. The Revata dialogue rewards that perseverance. The fascicle ends strongly.

**Verdict:** Works as a reading experience. The first third tests the reader's patience; the final two-thirds reward it.

### Fascicle 27 as a continuous reading experience:
Fascicle 27 does not resolve as cleanly as Fascicle 26. The progression — purifying-conduct objects (brief), element differentiation (systematic), mindfulness of breathing (rich), skillful objects (heavy taxonomy), affliction-purifying objects (good teaching), instruction types (thin) — creates a reading experience of uneven density. The transition from the breathing section into the skillful objects section is the most difficult: the reader has just been in genuine practice space and is dropped into a classification grid.

The four noble truths section within 27.5 is a natural high point that the fascicle should arguably end on. Instead it continues into the brief instruction-types section (27.6), which closes with three supernormal displays — an odd note to end on after the depth of 27.5.

**Verdict:** Does not work as strongly as a continuous reading experience. The ending is weak relative to the material in 27.5.

## 7. Five Worst Passages — Fascicle 26 (with rewrites)

### Worst Passage F26-1: The five-formula person definitions (§26.2)

**Current text (persons 3–7, representative excerpt):**
> "(3) Persons dominated by greed. What is a person dominated by greed? It is a person who in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of greed. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of desire, they have fierce greed and enduring greed. This is called a person dominated by greed.
>
> (4) Persons dominated by aversion. What is a person dominated by aversion? It is a person who in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of aversion. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of hatred, they have fierce aversion and enduring aversion. This is called a person dominated by aversion.
>
> (5) Persons dominated by delusion..." [pattern continues identically]

**Problems:** Formula monotony (FM-01); translated-ese triple (TE-01); "due to this cause and condition" calque.

**Rewrite:**
> **(3) Persons dominated by greed.** A greed-dominated person is one who has long cultivated and reinforced greed in previous lives. That habitual force carries forward: in this life, even slight occasions for desire arouse strong and persistent craving. This is called a person dominated by greed.
>
> **(4)–**(7).** For persons dominated by aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought, the pattern is the same — each tendency entrenched through repeated past cultivation and expressing in this life as a strong, persistent version of that affliction when its characteristic objects are encountered. The detailed portraits appear in section 26.3.

---

### Worst Passage F26-2: The resolute-confidence meditation-image passage (§26.5)

**Current text:**
> "Although the knowable objects are not actually present through direct encounter, nor has some unrelated substitute appeared, nevertheless — through the resolute confidence of the ground of meditative equipoise, through an apprehension by attention that is similar to the actual objects — the knowable objects appear in a similar fashion. For this reason, the mental representation is called the image corresponding to the knowable object."

**Problems:** Nested qualification (NQ-02); translated-ese clause order.

**Rewrite:**
> The meditator does not encounter the knowable object as it actually is, nor does some unrelated image arise. What appears is something in between: the object's likeness, conjured through the conviction-power of meditative equipoise. This likeness — appearing because the mind, in meditative steadiness, holds the object as if present — is called the meditation image, the image corresponding to the knowable object.

### Worst Passage F26-3: The closing conduct-type sentence formula (§26.3)

**Current text (five consecutive occurrences):**
> "Such and similar are to be known as the characteristics of a person of greed-conduct."
> "Such and similar are to be known as the characteristics of a person of aversion-conduct."
> [three more identical closings]

**Problems:** Formula monotony (FM-02); "such and similar" is a calque of 如是等.

**Rewrite:**
> [End the greed-conduct portrait simply with the last observation, no closing formula — let the portrait stand on its own. Then:]
>
> The portrait of aversion-conduct is the mirror opposite in almost every respect: [text continues].
>
> [For the final portrait, discursive-thought conduct:] These are the marks by which a person dominated by discursive thought is recognized.

### Worst Passage F26-4: The dhyana progression in accomplishment of purpose (§26.5)

**Current text:**
> "For one who enters the first dhyana, at the time of attaining the first dhyana, this direct perception arises with respect to the domain of the first dhyana. For one who enters the second, third, or fourth dhyana, at the time of attaining the second, third, or fourth dhyana, this direct perception arises with respect to those domains. For one who enters the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, or the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, at the time of attaining those samadhis, this direct perception arises with respect to those domains."

**Problems:** Translated-ese repetition (TE-05); formula monotony.

**Rewrite:**
> At the moment of attaining each level of meditative absorption, direct perception arises corresponding to that level's domain. For the first dhyana, the domain of the first dhyana becomes directly perceived. For the second through fourth, each dhyana's domain in turn. For the formless attainments — infinite space, infinite consciousness, nothingness, and neither-perception-nor-non-perception — the same principle holds throughout.

### Worst Passage F26-5: The charnel-ground "when it is said" repetitions (§26.6)

**Current text:**
> "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse — dead for one day, two days, or seven days — being devoured by crows, magpies, hungry dogs, vultures, foxes, jackals, and various beasts...' — this indicates the stages from the livid through the gnawed.
>
> When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse stripped of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins' — this indicates the blood-red.
>
> When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such bones, sinew-connected bones, or skeleton' — this indicates bones, sinew-connected bones, or the skeleton.
>
> When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such bones...'"

**Problems:** Formula monotony (FM-03); "when it is said" repeated four times in three paragraphs.

**Rewrite:**
> The sutra's charnel ground instructions map onto these external impurity stages. "Seeing a corpse — dead one day, two days, seven days — being devoured by crows, vultures, dogs, jackals, and beasts": this covers the stages from the livid corpse through the gnawed. "Seeing a corpse stripped of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins": this is the blood-red stage. "Seeing bones, sinew-connected bones, or a skeleton": this covers the third category. And the detailed description of scattered bones — hand bones in one place, foot bones in another, bleached white or mixed with dust, after one to seven years — corresponds to the scattered stage.

## 8. Five Worst Passages — Fascicle 27 (with rewrites)

### Worst Passage F27-1: The element-definition formula (§27.2, compressed example)

**Current text (earth element pattern, then repeated):**
> "The earth element is twofold: internal and external.
> The internal earth element is the specific quality of solidity within one's own body — included in the category of the solid and hard, included in the category of earth — which is personally appropriated...
> The external earth element is the quality of solidity outside the body — included in the category of the solid and hard, included in the category of earth — which is not personally appropriated.
> [same structure then for water, fire, wind]"

**Problems:** Formula monotony (FM-01); "included in the category of X, included in the category of Y" — calqued from Chinese parallelism.

**Rewrite (earth element as template, then compressing later elements):**
> **The earth element.** Earth is solidity — the quality of hardness and resistance. It appears internally, in one's own body: bones, flesh, nails, teeth, skin, and the rest of the solid bodily components. These are "personally appropriated" — experienced from the inside as one's own. It appears externally in tiles, clods of earth, gravel, stone, mountains. Both are earth; the distinction is only between what one calls "mine" and what one does not.
>
> **The water element** is moisture: internally, the fluids of the body — tears, sweat, blood, lymph, pus, urine, and the rest; externally, wells, springs, rivers, and oceans.
>
> **The fire element** is warmth: internally, the metabolic heat that digests food and sustains the body's processes, and feverish heat when it exceeds balance; externally, fire produced by friction or fuel, capable of burning forests, villages, and whole regions.
>
> **The wind element** is movement and lightness: internally, the twelve winds that circulate through the body's cavities and channels; externally, directional winds, seasonal storms, and the cosmic wind-wheel at the base of the world-system.

### Worst Passage F27-4: The dependent origination chain in §27.4

**Current text:**
> "If one further knows that only dharmas nourish dharmas, only dharmas equally moisten dharmas, only formations give rise to formations — and those formations, being born from causes and born from conditions, were originally non-existent and then came into being, and having come into being they disperse and cease, their nature being impermanent — because they are impermanent, they are subject to birth, aging, illness, and death, to grief, withering, sorrow, lamentation, anguish, suffering, and distress. Because they are subject to birth and so forth up to distress, they are called suffering. Because they are suffering, one has no autonomy; one's power is feeble and weak. For this reason, there is definitely no self."

**Problems:** Nested qualification; "nourish... moisten" triple; "born from causes and born from conditions" redundancy; "grief, withering, sorrow, lamentation, anguish, suffering, and distress" — seven near-synonyms calqued from Chinese.

**Rewrite:**
> To know dependent origination skillfully is to know this: only dharmas produce dharmas, only conditions give rise to conditions. No formation is self-caused. Whatever arises from causes and conditions was not there before, comes into being, and then disperses — and precisely because it arises and disperses, it is impermanent. Because it is impermanent, it is subject to birth, aging, illness, and death — to the full range of suffering. And because it is suffering, it cannot be a self: no one is the sovereign of what causes them pain. In dependent origination, therefore, there is no agent, no self.

### Worst Passage F27-5: The suffering of conditioned existence definition (§27.5)

**Current text:**
> "Because those skandhas are impermanent by nature, associated with arising and ceasing, having all clinging, followed by the heaviness of the three types of feeling, included within insecurity, not free from the suffering of suffering and the suffering of change, and operating without autonomy — through the suffering of conditioned existence, they are said to be 'suffering.'"

**Problems:** Nested qualification (NQ-02; worst instance in both fascicles); translated-ese; main verb buried under seven participials.

**Rewrite:**
> The skandhas of conditioned existence are "suffering" in a third and subtler sense. They are impermanent — arising and ceasing. They carry the seeds of clinging. They are shadowed by the heaviness of all three types of feeling. They offer no security, no refuge free from the first two types of suffering. And they cannot be commanded or controlled — no one is their sovereign. All of this is what it means to say they are suffering by the very fact of being conditioned. This is the suffering of conditioned existence.
