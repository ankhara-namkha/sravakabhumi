## Findings

### 29.15 — Eight Types of Persons by Capacity

**m-1. "Capacity" versus "capability" (根已成就/未成就)**

The translation renders 有堪能者/無堪能者 as "those with capacity" and "those without capacity." The Chinese 堪能 means "capability" or "suitability" — the potential to accomplish. The term 根已成就 (faculties already matured) is the underlying cause. The translation correctly identifies this link in the explanation paragraph. The word "capacity" is doctrinally acceptable as a rendering of 堪能, though "capability" would be slightly more precise since 堪能 implies active suitability rather than passive capacity. This is terminological rather than doctrinal — no action needed unless Agent T flags a glossary inconsistency.

**Severity:** m (minor)

---

**m-2. Four-factor convergence doctrine — correct and well-represented**

The passage on how all four factors must converge (faculties + method knowledge + continuous practice + long duration) is doctrinally sound. This teaching appears in multiple Yogacara sources. The hierarchical logic — that matured faculties are the sine qua non, then method, then intensity, then duration — accurately reflects the Chinese 根未成就...皆不能辨. The translation correctly renders this progressive dependency. The phrase "nothing can be achieved in accordance with principle, dharma, or skill" (如理如法如其善巧皆不能辨) is a reasonable rendering, though 如理如法如其善巧 could also be read as three aspects of accomplishment (according to principle, according to dharma, according to skill) rather than a single phrase. The translation's reading is defensible.

**Severity:** m (minor — interpretive, not doctrinal error)

### 29.16 — Four Maras and Mara's Activities

**m-3. Death-mara definition: "premature perishing" narrows the Chinese**

The Chinese defines death-mara (死魔) as: 彼彼有情從彼彼有情眾殀喪殞歿 — "various beings among various classes of beings undergo 殀喪殞歿." The translation renders this as "the premature perishing and passing away of beings among their various kinds."

The Chinese 殀喪殞歿 is a four-character compound where 殀 (premature death) is only one of four near-synonyms — 殀 (premature death), 喪 (loss/death), 殞 (perishing), 歿 (death/dying). Together they form a comprehensive designation of death in all its forms, not specifically premature death. The word "premature" in the translation risks suggesting that only untimely death counts as the death-mara, which would be doctrinally incorrect — death-mara encompasses ALL death of beings, not just premature death. The Visuddhimagga (Ch. VIII.2-3) distinguishes timely and untimely death but includes both under death (marana).

However, later in the same section, the translation correctly identifies death-mara's nature as "the exhaustion and cessation of the life-faculty — premature perishing" (命根盡滅殀喪殞歿), where the Chinese does pair 命根盡滅 (exhaustion of the life-faculty) with 殀喪殞歿. Here the term functions as a descriptor of the event, not a limitation to premature death only. The slight risk is that a reader encountering "premature perishing" twice may develop the impression that death-mara is specifically about premature death rather than death as such.

Cross-reference: Trungpa (PTOD1) discusses yama-mara as death broadly — fear of death and the corrosion of the body — not limited to premature death. The Abhidharmakosha similarly defines death-mara as the interruption of the life-faculty without qualification.

**Severity:** m (minor — the word "premature" slightly narrows the scope but does not constitute a doctrinal error since the fuller explanation compensates)

---

**m-4. Celestial mara: "born at the highest level of the desire realm"**

The translation says the celestial mara is "a celestial being born at the highest level of the desire realm, having attained great power." The Chinese 生欲界最上天子得大自在 says "born as the highest celestial being of the desire realm, having attained great sovereignty."

This is doctrinally correct. In standard Abhidharma, the devaputra-mara is identified with a being in the Paranirmitavashavartin heaven (他化自在天), the sixth and highest of the desire-realm heavens. Trungpa (PTOD1) identifies devaputra-mara as "son of the gods" (devaputra = celestial being). The glossary entry correctly identifies this as "the sovereign being of the highest desire-realm heaven who obstructs practitioners."

One minor note: 天子 literally means "celestial son" (devaputra), which the translation renders as "celestial being." This is acceptable — "celestial being" captures the meaning without the potentially confusing "son of the gods" phrasing. No doctrinal issue.

**Severity:** m (minor — no correction needed; noted for completeness)

---

**m-5. Mara's power over worldly-path practitioners: doctrinal nuance well preserved**

The passage distinguishing mara's power over those who have separated from desire via the worldly path versus those who have not is doctrinally significant and correctly rendered. The key distinction — that worldly-path practitioners who achieve separation from desire temporarily escape mara's command but remain "bound by mara's bonds" because they will inevitably return to this realm — is a standard Sarvastivada-Yogacara teaching. It correctly implies that only supramundane attainment (the noble path) truly transcends mara.

The translation handles this well: "mara temporarily loses command" for those on the worldly path, but they "remain bound by mara's bonds and have not escaped mara's net." This accurately captures the Chinese 魔縛所縛未脫魔罥 and the doctrinal point that worldly-path attainments are reversible.

**Severity:** m (minor — noted as a positive finding; no correction needed)

### 29.17 — Three Causes of Fruitless Practice

No doctrinal findings. The three factors — (1) undeveloped faculties (諸根未積集), (2) unsuitable instructions (教授不隨順), (3) weak samadhi (等持力微劣) — are accurately translated and doctrinally sound.

The rendering of 諸根未積集 as "faculties have not been sufficiently developed" is good. The Chinese 積集 means "accumulated/gathered" — implying faculties that have not yet been built up through prior cultivation. "Sufficiently developed" captures the functional meaning.

The three-factor model with its logical permutations (all combinations of presence/absence) is correctly preserved. The concluding statement that only convergence of all three produces fruit matches the Chinese exactly.

## Doctrinal Cross-Reference Notes

**Relationship between 29.15 and 29.17:** These two sections present overlapping but distinct frameworks. Section 29.15 identifies four factors for person-type classification (faculties, method knowledge, practice intensity, duration), while 29.17 identifies three prerequisites for fruitful practice (faculties, suitable instruction, samadhi power). The first is a taxonomic scheme; the second is a causal analysis of practice failure. The overlap on "faculties" (根) as the foundational factor is consistent — both sections treat matured/developed faculties as the indispensable prerequisite. This internal consistency is sound.

**Four maras and the path framework:** The mara section correctly positions the four maras within the broader path structure. The key doctrinal assertion — that worldly-path separation from desire does not constitute escape from mara — is consistent with the Sravakabhumi's treatment elsewhere of the distinction between removing entanglements (paryavasthana) and destroying latent tendencies (anushaya). Only the supramundane path achieves the latter. This echoes the discussion in sections 29.4-29.8 on the four samadhis and eight abandonment factors.
