### (1) Impurity of Putrefaction — Internal

**Chinese:** 云何名為朽穢不淨。謂此不淨略依二種。一者依內二者依外。

**English:** "This impurity is based on two things: first, the internal; second, the external."

- PASS. Content accurate.

**Chinese:** 云何依內朽穢不淨。謂內身中髮毛爪齒。塵垢皮肉。骸骨筋脈。心膽肝肺。大腸小腸。生藏熟藏。肚胃脾腎。膿血熱痰。肪膏肌髓。腦膜洟唾。淚汗屎尿。如是等類名為依內朽穢不淨。

**English:** "What is the internal impurity of putrefaction? It is the impurities within one's own body: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, dust and grime, skin, flesh, bones, sinews, veins, heart, gallbladder, liver, lungs, large intestine, small intestine, the upper stomach, the lower stomach, belly, spleen, kidneys, pus, blood, hot phlegm, fat, grease, muscle, marrow, brain membrane, mucus, saliva, tears, sweat, excrement, and urine. Such and similar things are called the internal impurity of putrefaction."

**Finding 1 — MODERATE: Body part enumeration: minor discrepancy in 骸骨**
- Chinese 骸骨 = skeleton/bones (as a unit). English renders "bones." This is acceptable but slightly compresses the meaning. 骸 specifically means "skeleton" or "body frame" — 骸骨 as a compound means "the skeletal frame/bones." The English "bones" is not wrong, but a more literal rendering would be "skeletal bones" to distinguish from the later occurrence of 骨 in the corpse stages.
- Severity: MODERATE (minor precision issue; no doctrinal impact).

### (1) Impurity of Putrefaction — External

**Chinese:** 云何依外朽穢不淨。謂或青瘀或復膿爛。或復變壞或復膖脹。或復食噉或復變赤。或復散壞或骨。或鎖或復骨鎖。或屎所作或尿所作。或唾所作或洟所作。或血所塗或膿所塗。或便穢處。如是等類名為依外朽穢不淨。

**English:** "What is the external impurity of putrefaction? It is the stages of a corpse: the livid, the festering, the decayed, the bloated, the gnawed, the blood-red, the scattered, bones, sinew-connected bones, and the skeleton; or what is made of excrement, made of urine, made of saliva, made of mucus, smeared with blood, smeared with pus, or a place of filth. Such and similar things are called the external impurity of putrefaction."

**Finding 2 — MODERATE: 鎖 rendered as "sinew-connected bones"**
- Chinese lists three separate items: 骨 (bones), 鎖 (chain/connected), 骨鎖 (bone-chain/skeleton). English renders them as "bones, sinew-connected bones, and the skeleton."
- The Chinese 鎖 alone literally means "chain" or "connected [bones]." Rendering it as "sinew-connected bones" is an interpretive expansion. The standard Abhidharma reading is: 骨 = individual bones, 鎖 = bones still connected by sinews, 骨鎖 = the full skeleton with sinew connections. The English captures the intended meaning but adds "sinew" which is not in the Chinese character itself — it is inferred from context and parallels.
- Severity: MODERATE (interpretive addition, but doctrinally correct inference).

**Finding 3 — MODERATE: Translation adds "stages of a corpse" as framing**
- Chinese simply says 謂或青瘀或復膿爛... ("that is, the livid, the festering...") with no framing phrase.
- English adds "It is the stages of a corpse:" as introductory framing before the list.
- This is an addition — the Chinese does not explicitly say "stages of a corpse." While the corpse stages are indeed the primary external impurity, the Chinese list also includes non-corpse items (excrement, urine, saliva, etc.). The added phrase could mislead readers into thinking only corpse stages are listed.
- Severity: MODERATE (minor addition; slightly narrows the scope of what the Chinese presents).

### (2) Impurity of Suffering

**Chinese:** 云何名為苦惱不淨。謂順苦受觸為緣所生。若身若心不平等受受所攝。如是名為苦惱不淨。

**English:** "It is the unpleasant and unequal feeling — whether of body or mind — that arises conditioned by contact with what is painful. This is called the impurity of suffering."

**Finding 4 — MODERATE: 不平等受受所攝 — translation slightly restructures**
- Chinese: 順苦受觸為緣所生 = "born from the condition of contact conducive to painful feeling" (順苦受觸 is a compound: contact that accords with painful feeling).
- 若身若心不平等受受所攝 = "included in the unequal feeling, whether of body or mind" (不平等受 = unequal/unpleasant feeling; 受所攝 = "included in [the category of] feeling").
- English: "the unpleasant and unequal feeling... that arises conditioned by contact with what is painful." This merges two distinct technical phrases. The Chinese has "contact conducive to painful feeling" (順苦受觸) as the condition, and "unequal feeling" (不平等受) as what is produced. The English conflates them into one continuous phrase. Not wrong, but less precise.
- The phrase 受所攝 ("included in [the category of] feeling") is dropped in the English.
- Severity: MODERATE (technical compression; no doctrinal error).

### (4) Impurity by Comparison

**Chinese:** 云何名為觀待不淨。謂如有一劣清淨事。觀待其餘勝清淨事便似不淨。如待無色勝清淨事。色界諸法便似不淨。待薩迦耶寂滅涅槃。乃至有頂皆似不淨。如是等類一切名為觀待不淨。

**English:** "Suppose there is something of lesser purity: when compared to something of greater purity, it appears impure. Just as the form realm, when compared to the superior purity of the formless realm, appears impure. And everything up to the summit of existence appears impure when compared to the peace of nirvana, which is the cessation of the transitory collection. All such things are called impurity by comparison."

**Finding 5 — MODERATE: 薩迦耶寂滅涅槃 — rendering adds interpretive content**
- Chinese: 待薩迦耶寂滅涅槃 = "in comparison to the nirvana of the cessation of the satkaya" or "compared to nirvana [which is] the cessation of the transitory collection."
- English: "compared to the peace of nirvana, which is the cessation of the transitory collection."
- The word "peace" (寂滅 = cessation/extinction, lit. "quiescence-extinction") is rendered loosely. 寂滅 specifically means "cessation" or "extinction" — it is a technical term, not merely "peace." The English adds "the peace of" as a stylistic softener that dilutes the force of 寂滅.
- Severity: MODERATE (stylistic looseness; 寂滅 should be rendered more precisely as "cessation" or "extinction").

### Five Types of Desire

**Chinese:** 如是不淨是能清淨貪行所緣。貪有五種。一於內身欲欲欲貪。二於外身婬欲婬貪。三境欲境貪。四色欲色貪。五薩迦耶欲薩迦耶貪。是名五貪。

**English:** "These impurities serve as the objects for purifying greed-conduct. There are five types of desire: 1. Desire and craving for one's own body 2. Sensual desire and craving for others' bodies 3. Desire and craving for objects 4. Desire and craving for forms 5. Desire and craving for the transitory collection"

**Finding 6 — MODERATE: Doubled structure 欲...欲貪 not rendered**
- Chinese uses a distinctive doubled construction: 於內身**欲**欲**貪** (desire for the internal body, craving for the internal body). The pattern repeats for each type, giving both 欲 (desire/longing) and 貪 (craving/greed) separately.
- English renders all five as "Desire and craving for X" — collapsing the doubled Chinese structure into a simple pair. The Chinese actually says something like "the desire [that is] desire for the internal body, the craving [that is] craving for the internal body" — emphasizing that each type has both a desire-aspect and a craving-aspect.
- Specifically: 於內身欲欲欲貪 seems to be 於內身 + 欲欲 + 欲貪, where 欲欲 = "desire-desire" (desire of/for the desire) and 欲貪 = "desire-craving." The doubled phrasing is a hallmark of Xuanzang's style. The English simplification is acceptable for readability but loses the emphatic doubling.
- Severity: MODERATE (stylistic compression of Xuanzang's distinctive doubled construction).

### Charnel Ground Passages

**Chinese:** 是故世尊乃至所有依外朽穢不淨差別。皆依四種憺怕路而正建立。

**English:** "For this reason, all the various external impurities of putrefaction that the Blessed One taught are established in terms of four charnel ground paths."

- PASS. 憺怕路 = charnel ground path correctly rendered.

**Chinese (first charnel ground):** 謂若說言由憺怕路見彼彼屍死。經一日或經二日或經七日。烏鵲餓狗。鵄鷲狐狼。野干禽獸之所食噉。便取其相以譬彼身。亦如是性亦如是類。不能超過如是法性。此即顯示始從青瘀乃至食噉。

**English:** "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse — dead for one day, two days, or seven days — being devoured by crows, magpies, hungry dogs, vultures, foxes, jackals, and various beasts. One then takes up this sign and applies it to one's own body: This body too is of such a nature, of such a type, and cannot transcend this condition' — this indicates the stages from the livid through the gnawed."

**Finding 7 — MINOR: Animal list translation**
- Chinese: 烏鵲餓狗鵄鷲狐狼野干禽獸
- English: "crows, magpies, hungry dogs, vultures, foxes, jackals, and various beasts"
- 鵄 = kite (a bird of prey), not simply "vultures." The Chinese lists 鵄 and 鷲 as separate items: 鵄 = kite/owl-like raptor, 鷲 = vulture. English merges them into "vultures."
- 禽獸 = birds and beasts (禽 = birds, 獸 = beasts). English renders as "various beasts," omitting the "birds" component.
- Severity: MINOR (trivial enumeration compression; no doctrinal impact).

**Chinese (second charnel ground):** 若復說言由憺怕路見彼彼屍。離皮肉血筋脈纏裹。此即顯示所有變赤。

**English:** "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse stripped of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins' — this indicates the blood-red."

**Finding 8 — MINOR: 纏裹 omitted**
- Chinese: 離皮肉血筋脈纏裹 = "separated from the wrapping (纏裹) of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins."
- English: "stripped of skin, flesh, blood, sinews, and veins" — captures the meaning but omits 纏裹 (wrapping/binding). The Chinese specifically says the corpse is "separated from the *wrapping* of skin, flesh..." — the word 纏裹 emphasizes that these tissues had *wrapped* the body. The English "stripped of" partially captures this but loses the specific image.
- Severity: MINOR (nuance lost; meaning substantially preserved).

**Chinese (third charnel ground):** 若復說言由憺怕路見彼彼骨或骨或鎖。此即顯示或骨或鎖或復骨鎖。

**English:** "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such bones, sinew-connected bones, or skeleton' — this indicates bones, sinew-connected bones, or the skeleton."

- PASS (same interpretive note as Finding 2).

**Chinese (fourth charnel ground):** 若復說言由憺怕路見彼彼骨。手骨異處。足骨異處。臗骨異處膝骨異處。臂骨異處肘骨異處。脊骨異處髆骨異處。肋骨異處。頷輪齒鬘頂髑髏等各各分散。或經一年或二或三乃至七年。其色鮮白猶如螺貝或如鴿色。或見彼骨和雜塵土。此即顯示所有散壞。

**English:** "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such bones — hand bones in one place, foot bones in another, hip bones, knee bones, arm bones, elbow bones, spine, shoulder bones, rib bones, jawbone, teeth, and skull, each scattered in different places; or after one, two, three, or up to seven years, white as conch shell or dove-colored; or bones mixed with dust and earth' — this indicates the scattered."

**Finding 9 — MINOR: 頷輪齒鬘頂髑髏 rendering**

- English: "jawbone, teeth, and skull" — compresses 齒鬘 (tooth-garland/row of teeth) to just "teeth" and 頂髑髏 to "skull." Minor loss of vivid imagery (鬘 = garland; 頂 = crown/top; 髑髏 = skull), but acceptable.
- Severity: MINOR.

### Object and Subject Distinguished

**Chinese:** 當知此中親品怨品及以中品。是為所緣。利益意樂能引快樂定地勝解。是為能緣。所緣能緣總略為一。說名慈愍所緣。

**English:** "In this context, the categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral are the objects. The intention of benefit that can bring about happiness, and the resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi, are the subjective aspect. **Finding 10 — MINOR: 能緣 rendered as "subjective aspect"**

English "subjective aspect" captures this but uses modern epistemological language not in the Chinese. A more literal rendering would be "the apprehending [mind]" or "the aspect that cognizes the object."
- Severity: MINOR (mild modernization; meaning correct).

### Three Characteristics of Benevolent Intention

**Chinese:** 若復說言無怨無敵無損害者。此則顯示利益意樂有三種相。由無怨故名為增上利益意樂。此無怨性二句所顯。謂無敵對故無損惱故。不欲相違諍義是無敵對。不欲不饒益義是無損害。

**English:** "'Without enmity, without hostility, without harm,' this indicates that the intention of benefit has three characteristics. 'Without enmity' names the supreme intention of benefit. This freedom from enmity is made clear by two phrases: 'without hostility' means not wishing to oppose or contend; 'without harm' means not wishing to cause injury."

**Finding 12 — MODERATE: Structural analysis of 無怨/無敵/無損害**
- Chinese structure: 無怨 is the overarching quality (= 增上利益意樂, the supreme intention of benefit). This quality of being "without enmity" (無怨性) is then *explained by* two sub-phrases (二句所顯): 無敵對 and 無損惱.
- 無敵對 = absence of hostility/opposition; defined as 不欲相違諍 = "not wishing to oppose or contend."
- 無損惱 = absence of harm; defined as 不欲不饒益 = "not wishing to not benefit" (i.e., not wishing to deprive of benefit).
- English renders 不欲不饒益 as "not wishing to cause injury." This is a significant simplification. The Chinese 不欲不饒益 is a double negative: "not wishing to *not* benefit" — meaning the practitioner does not wish to withhold benefit. This is different from actively "not wishing to cause injury." The original captures a more subtle intention: not merely refraining from harm, but not even wishing to withhold good.
- Severity: MODERATE (the double-negative structure of 不欲不饒益 is flattened into a simpler single-negative statement, losing a nuance of the benevolent intention).

### Resolute Confidence

**Chinese:** 若復說言勝解遍滿具足住者。此則顯示能引快樂定地勝解。又此勝解。即是能引快樂利益。增上意樂所攝勝解作意俱行。

**English:** "When it says 'with resolute confidence, pervading and dwelling fully,' this indicates the resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi that can bring about happiness. Furthermore, this resolute confidence is the resolute confidence and attention that accompany the supreme intention to bring about beneficial happiness."

**Finding 13 — MINOR: 增上意樂所攝勝解作意俱行 — compressed rendering**
- Chinese: 增上意樂所攝勝解作意俱行 = "the resolute confidence included in (所攝) the supreme intention (增上意樂), co-operating with attention (作意俱行)."
- English: "the resolute confidence and attention that accompany the supreme intention to bring about beneficial happiness."
- The Chinese says the resolute confidence is *included in* (所攝) the supreme intention and *co-operates with* attention. The English reverses the relationship, saying they "accompany the supreme intention." Also, 能引快樂利益 ("that can bring about beneficial happiness") is rendered as "to bring about beneficial happiness" — slightly different syntactic role but meaning preserved.
- Severity: MINOR (structural relationship slightly altered; meaning substantially correct).

### Maitri, Karuna, Mudita Distinction

**Chinese:** 若於無苦無樂親怨中三品有情。平等欲與其樂。當知是慈。若於有苦或於有樂親怨中三品有情。平等欲拔其苦欲慶其樂。當知是悲是喜。有苦有情是悲所緣。有樂有情是喜所緣。是名慈愍所緣。

**English:** "With respect to the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral — whether beings are without suffering and without happiness: wishing equally to give them happiness is loving-kindness. With respect to beings who have suffering or beings who have happiness among the three categories: wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion; wishing to rejoice in their happiness is sympathetic joy. Beings with suffering are the object of compassion. Beings with happiness are the object of sympathetic joy. This is called the loving-kindness and compassion object of meditation."

- PASS. The tripartite distinction is accurately rendered:
  - 慈 (maitri) → beings without suffering or happiness → wish to give happiness
  - 悲 (karuna) → beings with suffering → wish to remove suffering
  - 喜 (mudita) → beings with happiness → wish to rejoice in happiness
- Note: 平等欲拔其苦 — the 平等 (equally) in the Chinese applies to both removing suffering and rejoicing in happiness. The English places "equally" only with "remove their suffering." This is a very minor syntactic shift; the "equally" from the Chinese applies across both actions.

### Application to Aversion

**Chinese:** 若有瞋行補特伽羅。於諸有情修習慈愍令瞋微薄。名於瞋恚心得清淨。

**English:** "If a person of aversion-conduct practices loving-kindness and compassion toward all sentient beings, their aversion becomes attenuated. This is called the purification of the mind from aversion."

**Finding 14 — MINOR: "sentient beings" for 有情**
- Chinese 有情 = sentient beings. English "sentient beings" is the standard rendering. However, per the terminology watchlist, 補特伽羅 should be "person/individual" (not "sentient being"), and the glossary distinguishes 有情 from 補特伽羅. Here the Chinese uses 有情, so "sentient beings" is actually correct. No issue.
- 微薄 = "attenuated" / "made thin" — good rendering.
- PASS on this point.
