# Readability Review: Fascicle 26, Sections 26.4–26.5

**Reviewer perspective:** Educated English-speaking practitioner. No Sanskrit, Chinese, or Abhidharma background. Reading as printed, including footnotes at point of reference.

**Scope:** Section 26.4 "Objects of Meditation: Overview" (line 298) through footnote `[^four_purifications]` (line 448).

**Date:** 2026-03-01

Total issues: **31**

## Issue Catalog

### CRITICAL ISSUES

---

**C-1. Unexplained jargon on first use: "alambana-vastu" (§26.4, line 302)**

> "What are the objects of meditation? There are four types of objects of meditation (*alambana-vastu*)."

The Sanskrit term appears in parentheses immediately after the English gloss "objects of meditation." A reader with no Sanskrit background will not know whether this term is being used *instead of* the English, or whether it names a specific technical distinction from the plain English phrase. The footnote `[^four_objects]` is helpful but does not appear until much later. The issue is that the term arrives before any context establishes why a Sanskrit term is being added.

**Suggestion:** Either move the Sanskrit term to the footnote entirely, or add a brief inline signal: "objects of meditation (*alambana-vastu*; the Sanskrit is used throughout because no single English phrase covers the full technical meaning)." Alternatively, trust the footnote and remove the Sanskrit from the body text at line 302.

---

**C-2. Passage requiring guesswork: "savikalpaka-pratibimba" / "nirvikalpaka-pratibimba" in the list (lines 317–318) — Sanskrit before any English explanation**

> 1. The image with discernment (*savikalpaka-pratibimba*)
> 2. The image without discernment (*nirvikalpaka-pratibimba*)

The reader encounters both terms and their Sanskrit forms with no prior context. The English glosses ("with discernment" / "without discernment") are opaque at this stage — discernment of *what*? The footnote `[^image_discernment]` is only triggered at line 324, two paragraphs later, and only covers the first term. The reader must carry undefined terms through several paragraphs before they are explained.

**Suggestion:** Insert a one-sentence orientation before or immediately after the list: "These four objects differ in how the practitioner relates to the meditation object — through active analysis, restful attention, comprehensive knowledge, or realized fruition. Each is explained in turn below."

---

**C-3. Passage requiring guesswork: "vastu-paryanta" / "krtyanushthana" in the list (lines 319–320)**

> 3. The limit of things (*vastu-paryanta*)
> 4. The accomplishment of the purpose (*krtyanushthana*)

"The limit of things" is ambiguous in plain English — it could mean a boundary not to be crossed, the furthest extent of something, or the endpoint of things. Without context, a reader cannot resolve which. "Accomplishment of the purpose" is somewhat clearer but still vague — the purpose of *what*? These terms are introduced as a list and then explained section by section, but the gap between the list and the explanation (which spans multiple pages) leaves the reader stranded.

**Suggestion:** Same as C-2 — a brief orienting sentence after the list would reduce the cognitive load of holding four undefined terms simultaneously.

**C-4. Sentence over 50 words — and requires guesswork (line 324)**

> "Suppose there is a person who, relying on either hearing the correct dharma, or on instruction and admonition, or on what is seen, heard, or discerned, [forms] an image corresponding to the knowable object. Through the vipashyana practice of the ground of meditative equipoise (*samahita-bhumi*), they observe, investigate, thoroughly investigate, comprehensively examine, and comprehensively analyze."

Two issues here. First, "hearing the correct dharma" — "dharma" appears here for the first time in this section without explanation. Is this a teaching? A truth? A Buddhist scripture? Second, "the ground of meditative equipoise (*samahita-bhumi*)" introduces another Sanskrit term. "Ground" in the context of this text has a specific Yogacara meaning (it is the name of the broader treatise's organizing structure), but the reader does not know if "ground" here is technical or ordinary.

Word count of the first sentence: 38 words — acceptable. Word count of the combined passage as the reader experiences it (effectively one continuous instruction): the bracketed "[forms]" signals an editorial insertion that is confusing. Why is "forms" bracketed? Has something been omitted from the original? This requires guesswork.

**Suggestion:** Replace "[forms]" with an unmarked verb or add an inline translator's note: "[Translator's note: the Chinese implies the forming of an image; a verb is supplied for readability.]" Also define "dharma" in its first use in body text across the whole document, or add a footnote here.

**C-5. Sentence over 50 words and logical flow break (line 328)**

> "At that time, with respect to the knowable objects, one's resolute confidence operates as if one were directly perceiving them. Although the knowable objects are not actually present through direct encounter, nor are they other things of a similar class, nevertheless — through the resolute confidence of the ground of meditative equipoise, through an apprehension by attention that is similar to the actual objects — the knowable objects appear in a similar fashion."

Word count: 79 words. This sentence attempts to explain a subtle philosophical distinction (the image is not the real object but resembles it) but the construction "nor are they other things of a similar class" is unclear. A reader will not understand what "other things of a similar class" means. Does it mean the image is not an unrelated hallucination? Not a substitute object? The logic requires Yogacara background to decode.

**Suggestion:** Break into two sentences and rephrase the opaque clause: "Although the knowable objects are not actually present before you, nor has some unrelated substitute appeared, the objects do arise vividly in the mind, similar to their actual appearance." Then a new sentence: "This appearance — produced by the force of meditative confidence — is called the image corresponding to the knowable object."

**C-6. Unexplained jargon: "ashraya-paravritti" and "pratyaksha" introduced together (line 354)**

> "Having attained the transformation of the basis, they transcend the image. With respect to the knowable objects, non-conceptual direct-perception (*pratyaksha*) knowledge and vision arise."

The footnote for *ashraya-paravritti* (`[^transformation]`) is at line 440 — many pages away. The term "transformation of the basis" is used in body text at line 352 and again at line 354 before any footnote link appears. Meanwhile, "non-conceptual direct-perception (*pratyaksha*)" appears without a footnote at all. "Pratyaksha" is introduced as if the Sanskrit adds precision, but the reader has no frame for what makes this perception "direct" in contrast to what came before. The phrase "knowledge and vision" (two words for apparently one event) is also unexplained.

**Suggestion:** The footnote reference for `[^transformation]` should appear at the first use of the phrase (line 352), not delayed. For *pratyaksha*, either add a brief parenthetical ("non-conceptual direct-perception — knowing the object without the mediation of a mental image") or add a footnote. "Knowledge and vision" is a stock phrase that should be glossed on first appearance.

**C-7. Sentence over 50 words and logical flow break (line 356)**

> "For one who enters the first dhyana, at the time of attaining the first dhyana, this applies with respect to the domain of the first dhyana. For one who enters the second, third, or fourth dhyana, at the time of attaining the second, third, or fourth dhyana, this applies with respect to those domains. For one who enters the sphere of infinite space, the sphere of infinite consciousness, the sphere of nothingness, or the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception, at the time of attaining those concentrations, this applies with respect to those domains."

Word count of the full passage: 94 words. The passage is structurally repetitive and the terms "dhyana," "sphere of infinite space," "sphere of infinite consciousness," "sphere of nothingness," and "sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" are all introduced at once. "Dhyana" has been used earlier in the text (in the list at line 307 it is implied; it appears without definition). A reader without Abhidharma background will not know that "dhyana" refers to four levels of meditative absorption or that the "spheres" are four additional levels beyond them.

More critically, the logical flow breaks: what is "this" that applies? The antecedent is the "accomplishment of the purpose" — but after a section header and intervening text, the reader must trace back to understand what "this" refers to.

**Suggestion:** Replace the pronoun "this applies" with a brief clause: "the accomplishment of the purpose applies" or "this direct-perception knowledge and vision arise." Add a footnote or parenthetical on first use of "dhyana" in this passage, and a single footnote covering all four formless spheres.

**C-8. Passage requiring guesswork: "confused about the reality of self, sentient being, life-force, born being, sustainer, and person" (line 378)**

> "if the bhikshu is confused about the individual characteristics of formations — confused about the reality of self, sentient being, life-force, born being, sustainer, and person — they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the skandhas."

This list (self, sentient being, life-force, born being, sustainer, person) is presented as a list of things the practitioner may be "confused about" as realities. A reader with no background will not know that this is a list of different ways of conceptualizing a person (different philosophical positions, not different kinds of beings). The term "formations" (*samskara*? or the five skandhas generally?) is also unclear here — what are "individual characteristics of formations"?

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote explaining that these six terms are classical Indian philosophical designations for a personal self-entity, and that "confusion about formations" refers to misunderstanding the aggregates as a unitary self.

---

**C-9. Passage requiring guesswork: "delighting in penetrating and being liberated from the transitory collection everywhere" (line 378)**

> "If delighting in penetrating and being liberated from the transitory collection everywhere, they should settle the mind upon the four truths."

"The transitory collection" is technical jargon for the five skandhas considered as a mistakenly-grasped self. A reader will have no idea what this means. "Penetrating and being liberated from" and "everywhere" also read as awkward constructions.

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote or inline gloss: "the transitory collection (the five skandhas mistakenly grasped as a unified self)." Rephrase: "If delighting in seeing through and being freed from the transitory collection [Translator's note: the five aggregates taken as a self] in all its aspects..."

### MODERATE ISSUES

---

**M-1. Awkward English: "the limit of things" as a section title (line 340)**

"The limit of things" is not natural English for what the text means. The section explains both the full extent of what exists and the true nature of things — neither of which is naturally called "a limit." This creates an early wrong impression.

**Suggestion:** Consider "The Full Scope and True Nature of Things" or "Things in Their Full Extent and True Nature." If the term must remain as is (for doctrinal precision), add a parenthetical clarification: "The Limit of Things (the complete range and true nature of phenomena)."

---

**M-2. Awkward English and jargon: "the full extent of what exists (*yavad-bhavikata*) and the nature of things as they are (*yathavad-bhavikata*)" (line 342)**

Two Sanskrit terms appear back-to-back without being separately defined before their definitions are given. The footnote `[^limit_twofold]` is helpful, but the body text attempts a definition that is partially redundant with the footnote, creating a slightly confusing overlap.

**Suggestion:** Move the Sanskrit terms to the footnote. Body text could read: "It is knowing the full extent of what exists and knowing the nature of things as they are with respect to the object." The footnote can then supply the Sanskrit with the technical gloss.

---

**M-3. Awkward English: "all conditioned phenomena are encompassed by the five dharmas [i.e., the five skandhas]" (line 344)**

The editorial insertion "[i.e., the five skandhas]" appears inside a sentence that has just listed the five skandhas by name. This is redundant and slightly condescending — the reader has just seen the five skandhas enumerated. It would be cleaner to remove the bracketed gloss here.

**Suggestion:** Remove "[i.e., the five skandhas]" from this sentence, as the five skandhas were listed immediately before.

---

**M-4. Logical flow break: "This is called the full extent of what exists" (line 344) — abrupt close**

The paragraph enumerating skandhas, elements, sense bases, and four truths concludes with this summary sentence, but the reader may not understand *why* this constitutes "the full extent of what exists." The logic — that these classifications exhaustively cover all phenomena, leaving nothing outside their scope — is implied but not stated.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief connective: "In this way, these classifications together cover all that exists without remainder. This is called the full extent of what exists."

---

**M-5. Unexplained jargon: "four types of reasoning" listed without preparation (line 346)**

> "possessing the character of being established through four types of reasoning: reasoning from dependence, reasoning from function, reasoning from proof, and reasoning from the nature of things"

The list is followed by the footnote `[^four_reasonings]`, which adequately explains the terms. However, the phrase "possessing the character of being established through four types of reasoning" is awkward. Why does "the nature of things as they are" possess the character of being established *by* reasoning? This suggests that "the nature of things" is something reasoned into, not simply observed — a subtle philosophical claim that deserves at least a passing explanation.

**Suggestion:** Rephrase: "It is the true reality and suchness of the object, which can be verified through four types of reasoning..." This makes the relationship clearer.

---

**M-6. Awkward English: "brings to completion all attention directed toward the images of the objects" (line 352)**

"Brings to completion all attention directed toward the images of the objects" is a noun-heavy phrase that is difficult to parse naturally. What is being "brought to completion"? The attention itself? The cultivation?

**Suggestion:** Rephrase: "brings to fruition all the attentive work directed toward the meditation images." Or: "completes the entire process of attending to the images of the objects."

---

**M-7. Footnote placement problem: `[^transformation]` footnote should be at line 352, not line 440**

The term "transformation of the basis" is first used at line 352 in the body text, and the footnote superscript appears at line 352. However, the term is then used again at line 354 in close proximity, which is fine. The issue: the actual footnote text appears at line 440 — very far from first use. In a markdown rendering, the footnote link would work (it jumps to the footnote), but the proximity of the explanation matters in print contexts.

This is a moderate structural issue rather than a critical one.

**Suggestion:** No change needed for digital reading; flag for print layout review. The footnote content is adequate.

---

**M-8. Awkward English: "they settle their minds upon a matching object... a similar object... the object without inversion" — unexplained contrast between "matching" and "similar" (line 372)**

> "they settle their minds upon a matching object. They settle their minds upon a similar object. They settle their minds upon the object without inversion."

"Matching" and "similar" appear to be near-synonyms to a plain English reader. The distinction between them is a crucial organizational point of the whole dialogue section (corresponding to the image-with-discernment / image-without-discernment distinction), but this is not visible until later. As first encountered, the three-part formulation reads as redundant.

**Suggestion:** A brief anticipatory note: "[Translator's note: 'matching object' corresponds to choosing the right meditation subject; 'similar object' corresponds to working with the mental image of that subject; 'without inversion' refers to knowing the object accurately. These three correspond to the first three pervasive objects described above.]" Alternatively, add a footnote at "matching object" (line 372) previewing the structural correspondence.

---

**M-9. Unexplained jargon: "outflows" (line 372)**

> "delight in liberating the mind from all outflows"

"Outflows" (*asrava*) is a technical term for mental afflictions that "leak" into and perpetuate samsara. A reader with no Abhidharma background will read "outflows" as a strange metaphor without understanding it as a specific technical category.

**Suggestion:** Add a parenthetical or footnote: "all outflows (the afflictions that perpetuate cyclic existence)." Or rephrase: "delight in liberating the mind from all afflictive outflows (*asrava* — the habitual mental tendencies that perpetuate suffering)."

**M-10. Sentence over 50 words (line 378 — first paragraph of the matching-objects section)**

> "Revata, furthermore, if the bhikshu is confused about the individual characteristics of formations — confused about the reality of self, sentient being, life-force, born being, sustainer, and person — they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the skandhas. If confused about causes, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the elements. If confused about conditions, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in the sense bases. If confused about impermanence, suffering, emptiness, and non-self, they should settle the mind upon skillfulness in dependent origination and in the possible and impossible. If delighting in freedom from desire for the desire realm, they should settle the mind upon the coarseness of desires and the peacefulness of forms. If delighting in freedom from desire for the form realm, they should settle the mind upon the coarseness of forms and the peacefulness of the formless. If delighting in penetrating and being liberated from the transitory collection everywhere, they should settle the mind upon the truth of suffering, the truth of the origin, the truth of cessation, and the truth of the path."

This is one paragraph containing at least 7 conditional clauses in sequence. Word count: approximately 162 words. Even though each individual conditional is under 50 words, the cumulative density makes this passage very difficult to absorb.

**Suggestion:** Break this into a formatted list with a short explanatory header: "A practitioner who is... [condition] → settles upon [object]." A table format would serve readability here, matching the list-format already used in lines 304–307.

---

**M-11. Awkward English: "the coarseness of desires and the peacefulness of forms" / "the coarseness of forms and the peacefulness of the formless" (line 378)**

These phrases are structurally parallel but will be opaque without context. "Coarseness" and "peacefulness" are comparative terms referring to the quality of experience in different cosmological realms, but the text does not signal this. A reader will not know whether "forms" refers to physical objects, formal structures, or a technical cosmological tier.

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote on first occurrence of "coarseness of desires": "The three cosmological realms — desire, form, and formless — are experienced as progressively more refined and peaceful. 'Coarseness' refers to the relative heaviness and turbulence of a lower realm; 'peacefulness' to the relative clarity and ease of a higher one. This contemplation is used to develop dispassion toward the lower realm."

**M-12. Logical flow break: transition from dialogue back to analytical framework (line 382)**

> "In order to investigate, thoroughly investigate, comprehensively examine, and comprehensively analyze the various knowable objects, the bhikshu — with respect to what was previously seen, heard, sensed, or known, and through the predominant force of seeing, hearing, sensing, and knowing — thinks and discriminates with the attention of the ground of meditative equipoise and gives rise to resolute confidence."

Word count: 65 words. This sentence re-introduces "the attention of the ground of meditative equipoise" and "resolute confidence" — terms that were defined much earlier in the image-with-discernment section — but in the context of the dialogue with Revata, the connection back to that earlier technical material is not flagged. The reader is now in a different register (a Sutra-style dialogue) and the re-use of Abhidharma technical vocabulary creates a seam.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief connector: "[This corresponds to the image with discernment described above.] The bhikshu..." Or add a footnote noting the correspondence.

---

**M-13. Awkward English: "consisting only of cognition, only of seeing, only of correct recollection" (line 382)**

> "With respect to that object, the image is similar to it — consisting only of cognition, only of seeing, only of correct recollection."

The triple phrase "consisting only of cognition, only of seeing, only of correct recollection" reads as a list but the three items appear near-synonymous. The reader cannot determine whether this is a meaningful threefold distinction or stylistic variation. No footnote clarifies this.

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote: "These three phrases — cognition, seeing, recollection — reflect the Chinese construction and may be read as a single emphatic claim: the image is purely a mental event, not a physical perception." Or resolve the apparent redundancy in the translation.

**M-14. Logical flow break: "from time to time calms the mind, and from time to time diligently practices the vipashyana of the superior wisdom-dharma" (line 384)**

> "Furthermore, that bhikshu, from time to time calms the mind, and from time to time diligently practices the vipashyana of the superior wisdom-dharma."

The phrase "vipashyana of the superior wisdom-dharma" appears without explanation. "Superior wisdom-dharma" is a technical term, but what makes it "superior"? Superior to what? "Wisdom-dharma" is itself a compound not previously defined. The phrase "from time to time" (used twice) does not have an obvious antecedent — from time to time relative to what schedule or condition?

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote on "superior wisdom-dharma." Rephrase "from time to time" to clarify the intended meaning: "alternating between calming the mind [shamatha] and engaging in analytical insight [vipashyana of the higher wisdom teaching]."

---

**M-15. Unexplained jargon: "correct recollection" (line 382)**

"Correct recollection" is a technical term (likely *samyak-smriti*, right mindfulness) but appears here without any gloss or footnote, in an already dense sentence.

**Suggestion:** Either footnote the term or rephrase to avoid ambiguity: "the image is purely mental — a matter of attention, clear seeing, and mindful awareness."

---

**M-16. Awkward English: "they come to understand the knowable domain according to its nature. They can thoroughly know it truly, without inversion." (line 388)**

"Without inversion" (*aviparyasta*) is a technical term meaning "without the four inversions" (taking the impermanent as permanent, suffering as pleasure, non-self as self, impure as pure). A plain English reader will read "without inversion" as simply "correctly" — which is the gist, but the specific technical content is lost and the phrase sounds peculiar.

**Suggestion:** Rephrase: "they come to understand the knowable domain as it actually is — clearly, truly, without any distortion or confusion." Add a footnote: "Without inversion (*aviparyasta*): free from the four inversions — the mistaken views of permanence, pleasure, selfhood, and purity that distort perception of conditioned phenomena."

---

**M-17. Awkward English: "they engage in uninterrupted application and earnest application" (line 392)**

"Uninterrupted application and earnest application" reads as a hendiadys (two words for one concept) but the text treats it as two distinct practices. Without a footnote, the reader cannot tell whether these are two separate things or a single emphatic phrase.

**Suggestion:** Add a footnote or parenthetical: "uninterrupted application (sustained, gap-free effort) and earnest application (vigorous, effortful engagement) — corresponding to the two classical descriptions of correct practice in this context."

---

**M-18. Logical flow break: "Through cultivation, habituation, and repeated practice, all heaviness is entirely extinguished" (line 394)**

The section heading is "(d) Not abandoning meditative absorption," and the preceding sentence (line 392) describes the signs of calming, uplifting, and equanimity. Then line 394 jumps directly to the final fruits (four purifications). The intermediate process — how continued practice of the three signs leads to the elimination of heaviness — is not narrated. The reader is left with a gap: what actually happened between "practicing the three signs" and "all heaviness is entirely extinguished"?

**Suggestion:** Add a transitional phrase: "Through such cultivation — habituation and repeated practice of these three signs — all heaviness is progressively extinguished." Or add a footnote noting that the intermediate stages are detailed in fascicle 30.

---

**M-19. Awkward English: "They progressively realize and contact the purification of the basis" (line 394)**

"Realize and contact" is an awkward compound — "contact" is not the usual English collocate for "purification." The phrase appears four times in this sentence. It may be a calque from the Chinese (實現 + 觸) but reads oddly.

**Suggestion:** Consider: "they attain, stage by stage, the purification of the basis" or "they progressively come to realize and directly experience the purification of the basis." The repeated "realize and contact" across four clauses also creates cumulative awkwardness; a parallel construction using "attain" throughout would be smoother.

---

**M-20. Verse: "knowing all true meaning" — unclear antecedent (line 403)**

> "knowing all true meaning"

In the verse, "knowing all true meaning" is a participial phrase that lacks a clear subject. The commentary explains it refers to "the limit of things," but standing alone in the verse, it floats. A reader encountering the verse before the commentary will not know who is knowing, or what "true meaning" is.

**Suggestion:** Consider making the subject explicit in the verse translation: "knowing the full scope of things" or "knowing all things as they are." Alternatively, the verse is fine as verse (impressionistic) as long as the commentary explains it — which it does. This is a minor issue if the reader reads straight through.

---

**M-21. Second verse: "able to receive the taste of seclusion" — unclear (line 416)**

> "able to receive the taste of seclusion"

"Taste of seclusion" is a somewhat unusual English phrase. "Seclusion" in Buddhist context typically refers to physical or mental withdrawal from distraction, but "tasting" seclusion is a non-standard idiom. The commentary explains this as "delighting in abandonment and delighting in cultivation" — which does not map intuitively onto "taste of seclusion."

**Suggestion:** Consider "savoring the joy of seclusion" or "delighting in the calm of withdrawal." If the Chinese supports a more literal "taste," a footnote explaining the idiom would help.

---

**M-22. Sentence over 50 words (line 422 — commentary on second verse)**

> "Here, 'thoroughly knowing the mind's signs' means: the image with discernment and the image without discernment are designated by the name 'the mind's signs,' and the limit of things is designated by the name 'thorough knowing.' 'Able to receive the taste of seclusion' refers to the practitioner who practices correctly with respect to the object, delighting in abandonment and delighting in cultivation. 'In meditative absorption with constant, earnest mindfulness' refers to constantly and diligently cultivating shamatha and vipashyana, practicing with earnest training. 'Receiving joy and pleasure, free from attachment' refers to the accomplishment of the purpose."

Word count: 94 words. This is the commentary on the second verse, rendered as a single dense paragraph. The prose structure (four successive "X refers to Y" clauses in one paragraph) makes it harder to follow than the first verse commentary, which uses the same structure but feels clearer because the terms are simpler.

**Suggestion:** No structural change needed — this is a stylistic issue. Could add line breaks between the four commentary clauses for visual breathing room.
