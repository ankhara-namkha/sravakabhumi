# Readability Review — Fascicle 31, Sections 31.1–31.4

**Agent R (Readability) — 80% practitioner / 20% scholar**
**Register standard:** Nanamoli, Bodhi, Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee

---

## Overall Assessment

Sections 31.1–31.4 are largely solid. The structural framework (six-aspect vipashyana applied to three objects) is clear, and the translation mostly avoids the worst translated-ese. The main issues are: (1) a few passages where qualifications bury the main point, (2) formula monotony in the "Reasoning from proof/nature" closings, (3) one mid-length sentence in 31.1 that requires parsing effort, and (4) a handful of moments where the prose reads as taxonomy rather than practice instruction. None are severe. All are fixable with targeted edits.

---

## Findings by Category

### (1) Translated-ese

**31.1 — "Things" subsection (line 27):**
> "One further discerns the twelve links (*dvadasha-nidana*), internal and external, generating conviction."

The phrase "generating conviction" is a dangling participle that sounds imported from Classical Chinese verb-final structure rather than written in English. In context, "conviction" is an odd word — the practitioner is meant to understand or become clear about the twelve links, not simply be convinced of them. The brevity also feels truncated relative to the same subsection's handling in 31.2 and 31.3.

**Suggested fix:**
> "One then examines the twelve links (*dvadasha-nidana*) as they appear internally and externally, coming to understand each with clarity and confidence."

---

**31.1 — "Own-Characteristic" subsection (line 29):**
> "One further discerns the own-characteristics of each link, beginning with ignorance — ignorance regarding the past, ignorance regarding the future — as broadly explained in the previous analysis of the links of dependent origination."

The em-dash insertion "— ignorance regarding the past, ignorance regarding the future —" interrupts the sentence just as the reader is following a clean structure. The cross-reference at the end ("as broadly explained in the previous analysis") is fine as a scholarly pointer but lands flatly for a practitioner.

**Suggested fix:**
> "One discerns the characteristic nature of each link individually, beginning with ignorance: ignorance regarding the past, ignorance regarding the future — each as explained in the earlier detailed analysis of dependent origination."

Minor improvement; not critical.

---

### (2) Formula Monotony

**31.1, 31.2, 31.3 — "Reasoning from proof" and "Reasoning from the nature of things" closings:**

All three sections use nearly identical phrasing for these two reasoning types:

- 31.1: "This is confirmed by scripture, by direct inner realization, and by inference." / "The established nature of dharmas likewise confirms it."
- 31.2: "This is confirmed by scripture, by inner direct cognition, and by inference." / "The established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, and their abiding nature also confirm it."
- 31.3: "This is confirmed by scripture, by inner direct cognition, and by inference." / "The established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, and their abiding nature confirm it."

The "Reasoning from proof" line appears three times in nearly identical form across three consecutive sections. The "Reasoning from the nature of things" line appears twice in identical form (31.2 and 31.3) and once in abbreviated form (31.1).

This repetition is doctrinally faithful — the Chinese text is formulaic here — but it creates a numbing effect for the reader by the third section. The practitioner registers "boilerplate" and skips it.

**Suggested approach:** Since the content is genuinely parallel and the text is explicitly cross-referencing a fixed schema, acceptable solutions include: (a) vary the phrasing at least in the third instance; (b) use a bracketed note indicating parallelism ("Following the same pattern as above."); or (c) compress 31.3's closing into a single sentence acknowledging the parallel. Option (a) is cleanest.

**Suggested fix for 31.3 "Reasoning from proof":**
> "The same three witnesses apply here: scripture, direct inner experience, and inference."

**Suggested fix for 31.3 "Reasoning from the nature of things":**
> "The nature of dharmas — fixed, inconceivable, and abiding — confirms this as well. It calls not for analysis but for faith and understanding."

(Note: 31.3's existing text already includes the "faith and understanding" gloss, which is good. The issue is only the repeated framing.)

### (3) Nested Qualification

**31.1 — "Reasoning from dependence" (lines 39–40):**
> "These are merely conventional designations established upon dharmas. Within the chain from ignorance conditioning formations through to birth conditioning aging-and-death, we merely posit conventional labels, speaking of a 'doer' and an 'experiencer': one who has such-and-such a name, clan, and lineage; who eats such food, experiences such-and-such suffering or happiness, lives so long, endures so long, reaches such a limit of lifespan."

The colon followed by three semi-colon-linked qualifications ("one who has... who eats... lives so long...") creates a catalog that buries what the sentence is actually saying: conventional identity-descriptions have no ultimate reference. The reader loses the thread between "doer and experiencer" and the enumeration that follows. This is quoted speech within the dependent-origination reasoning, so the register should feel more like a realized practitioner's first-person reflection, not a grammatical taxonomy.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "These are merely conventional designations resting on dharmas. Through the whole chain from ignorance conditioning formations to birth conditioning aging-and-death, we do nothing more than attach conventional labels — speaking of a 'doer' and an 'experiencer.' Such designations describe one who bears a name, belongs to a clan and lineage, eats a certain food, tastes such-and-such suffering and happiness, lives for a span, endures for a period, and reaches the end of a lifespan. Nothing more than that."

---

**31.1 — Drawing cause paragraph (line 45):**
> "Regarding these two fruits, ignorance first arises. With ignorance as the forerunner, meritorious, non-meritorious, and imperturbable formations are generated. These formations appropriate the consciousness of future existence, causing the sprout of existence to arise — for by appropriating the seed of consciousness, they in turn appropriate the seeds of name-and-form, six sense-bases, contact, and feeling for the next existence."

This is a 59-word sentence (the clause beginning "for by appropriating...") nested inside a longer construction. The explanatory clause introduced by "for" compounds the difficulty. The reader must hold three levels simultaneously: (a) what formations do (appropriate consciousness), (b) why (causing the sprout to arise), (c) how that works (by appropriating the seed, which in turn appropriates further seeds). For a practitioner trying to see how karma operates, this reads like a doctrinal proof rather than illumination.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Regarding these two fruits, ignorance arises first. With ignorance as the forerunner, meritorious, non-meritorious, and imperturbable formations are generated. These formations appropriate the seed of consciousness for a future existence — and through that, they in turn appropriate the seeds of name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, and feeling. The sprout of existence arises because each of these seeds has been appropriated in advance, ready to unfold in sequence in the next life. All of this constitutes the drawing cause."

### (4) Register Drift

**31.1 — "Time" subsection (lines 35–36):**
> "The self-continuity obtained in past lives had no true permanence — yet so it abided. The self-continuity obtained in the present has no true permanence — yet so it now abides. The self-continuity to be obtained in future lives will have no true permanence — yet so it will abide."

This is faithful to the Chinese three-time structure, but the phrasing "yet so it abided / yet so it now abides / yet so it will abide" is awkward English and does not communicate what is meant to a practitioner. The point is that continuity appears to persist even though no lasting substance underlies it — a profound observation. The current rendering conveys this as taxonomy, not as something that should land.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The stream of continuity arising in past lives lacked any true permanence — and yet it flowed. The stream arising in the present life lacks any true permanence — and yet it flows. The stream to arise in future lives will lack any true permanence — and yet it will flow. Conditioned continuity is real without being enduring."

The final added sentence is not in the source. If the translator prefers strict fidelity, omit it — but the three repetitions need to build to something, or a practitioner hears the same claim three times without insight accruing.

---

**31.2 — "Time" subsection (line 79):**
> "One discerns that across past, present, and future, the six elements serve as the conditions for entering the mother's womb."

This sentence is technically correct but reads as a doctrinal note rather than a practitioner's recognition. "Serve as the conditions for entering the mother's womb" is functional language; the significance — that what we take to be a unified body is assembled from elemental conditions across lifetimes — does not emerge.

**Suggested fix:**
> "One recognizes that in past, present, and future, it is through these very six elements that the continuity of life enters into rebirth."

Minor; adjust if the doctrinal precision of "mother's womb" is important to preserve.

---

**31.4 — The closing section (lines 129–131):**

> "Having investigated the purifying-conduct objects through the six aspects in this way, one repeatedly stills the mind internally, and repeatedly investigates what has been discerned through the superior observation of vipashyana. Because shamatha serves as the support, vipashyana is swiftly purified. Because vipashyana serves as the support, shamatha grows vast and expansive."

This is the only moment in sections 31.1–31.4 where the text speaks directly about the practice experience — shamatha and vipashyana in dynamic relationship. It deserves to land with some presence. "Repeatedly stills... repeatedly investigates" is accurate but mechanical. "Grows vast and expansive" is good. The passage is close; it just needs one revision to open up.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Having investigated the purifying-conduct objects through the six aspects, one alternates: stilling the mind inwardly through shamatha, then turning that stillness toward what has been discerned through the superior observation of vipashyana. Supported by shamatha, vipashyana is swiftly refined. Supported in turn by vipashyana, shamatha grows vast and expansive."

The word "alternates" adds a sense of living practice that "repeatedly... repeatedly" does not carry.

### (5) Sentences Over 50 Words

**31.1 — Reasoning from dependence, drawing cause (line 45):**

> "These formations appropriate the consciousness of future existence, causing the sprout of existence to arise — for by appropriating the seed of consciousness, they in turn appropriate the seeds of name-and-form, six sense-bases, contact, and feeling for the next existence. So that consciousness, name-and-form, the six sense-bases, contact, and feeling may arise in their proper sequence in a future life, one first appropriates the seeds of those dharmas."

Both sentences exceed 50 words (first: ~54 words; second: ~36 words). The first is the more problematic (see issue 3 above). The second is borderline but manageable.

**31.1 — Reasoning from dependence, opening paragraph (line 39):**

> "'Just as grass, wood, and other materials are assembled around empty space and are conventionally called a 'house,' so too sinew, bone, blood, and flesh — the assembled conditions surrounding empty space — are by conventional designation called a 'body.' Furthermore, this body arises from past karma and afflictions together with one's own seeds as causes and conditions.'"

Wait — this is from 31.2, not 31.1. Flagging it here for completeness. The house-simile sentence runs ~55 words. The em-dash interruption "— the assembled conditions surrounding empty space —" is embedded qualification that makes the sentence harder to parse. Minor restructuring would help:

**Suggested fix:**
> "Just as grass, wood, and other materials assembled around empty space are conventionally called a 'house,' so too sinew, bone, blood, and flesh — assembled around empty space — are conventionally called a 'body.' And this body arises through the causes and conditions of past karma, afflictions, and one's own seeds."

### (6) Unexplained Jargon

**31.1 — "imperturbable formations" (line 45):**
> "meritorious, non-meritorious, and imperturbable formations"

"Imperturbable formations" (*aneñjya-samskara*) is specialized Abhidharma terminology referring to formations leading to the form and formless realms. A practitioner without background will not know what makes a formation "imperturbable" or how it differs from meritorious or non-meritorious. The term appears without gloss.

**Suggested fix:** Add a brief parenthetical on first occurrence:
> "meritorious, non-meritorious, and imperturbable formations (those leading to the form and formless realms)"

Or add a footnote.

---

**31.3 — "anapanasati" (line 101):**
> "this is the meaning of anapanasati"

The section heading uses the Sanskrit "anapanasmriti" correctly. But the body text reverts to the Pali form "anapanasati" midway through the "Meaning" subsection. Per the terminology watchlist, Pali forms are deprecated in this translation. This is a terminology issue (better flagged by Agent T), but the Pali form in running text also disrupts register for a practitioner expecting Sanskrit.

**Suggested fix:** Replace "anapanasati" with "anapanasmriti" or simply "mindfulness of breathing."

---

**31.1 — "affliction-purifying objects" (line 131):**
> "the affliction-purifying objects"

This is a technical category term. A practitioner reading section 31.4's closing summary may not recall what these are (the purifying-conduct objects were just reviewed; the skillful objects and affliction-purifying objects are forward-referenced). The sentence works as a structural note for the translator but reads as a bookmark rather than a living statement.

**Suggested fix:** No change needed; the cross-reference to "their proper place later" is sufficient. But if footnote space allows, a brief note identifying the three object categories would help orient the reader.

---

### (7) Broken Logic

No instances of broken logic found in sections 31.1–31.4. The six-aspect framework is applied coherently to each of the three contemplation objects. The internal logic of the dependent origination analysis (two causes, two fruits) is particularly clear and well-organized.

## Verdict

The translation is in good shape — significantly better than the mid-fascicle 29 drift that was previously diagnosed. The six-aspect framework provides clear organization that the English respects. Priority fixes are: (1) the drawing-cause paragraph in 31.1 (nested qualification obscures what karma actually does), (2) the "Time" subsection in 31.1 (three parallel claims that go nowhere for a practitioner), and (3) glossing "imperturbable formations." Formula monotony in the parallel structure closings is the only systemic issue, and it is mild — inherent in the source's repetitive structure — but should be varied at least once.
