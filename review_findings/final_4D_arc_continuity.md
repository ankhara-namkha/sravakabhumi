# Arc Continuity Review: The Sravakabhumi as a Single Work

Reviewer: Arc Reader (Doctrinal continuity across all 8 fascicles)

---

## 1. Twenty-Eight Person Types (F26.2 -> F29.13-14)

### Names

F26.2 defines twenty-eight person types by numbered list (1-28). F29.13 revisits a subset as "behavioral types" using a related but distinct vocabulary:

- F26.2 lists types 3-7 as "Those dominated by greed/aversion/delusion/pride/discursive thought."
- F26.3 immediately re-designates them as persons of "greed-conduct," "aversion-conduct," etc.
- F29.13 uses the "conduct" terminology consistently: "greed, aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive-thought conduct."

**Assessment: CONSISTENT.** The shift from "dominated by X" (type designation in the enumeration) to "X-conduct" (behavioral characterization) is explicitly bridged in section 26.3: "A person dominated by greed is called a person of greed-conduct." This mapping is maintained throughout.

F29.13 adds two further types: "equal-part conduct" and "thin-dust conduct." These correspond to F26.2 types 8 ("Those of balanced disposition") and 9 ("Those of thin-dust nature"). The mapping is preserved but the vocabulary differs slightly:

- F26.2: "balanced disposition" -> F26.3: "balanced-conduct" -> F29.13: "equal-part conduct"
- F26.2: "thin-dust nature" -> F26.3: "thin-dust-conduct" -> F29.13: "thin-dust conduct"

**Minor issue: "balanced disposition" vs "equal-part conduct."** F26.3 says "A person of balanced disposition is called a person of balanced-conduct." F29.13 introduces "equal-part conduct" without cross-reference. The underlying Chinese is likely the same (等分行), but the English renderings differ: "balanced-conduct" (F26.3) vs "equal-part conduct" (F29.13). This is a terminology inconsistency.

### Cross-references

F29.13 footnote [^thin_dust_qualities] says: "Cf. the 28 person types in fascicle 26 (sections 26.1-26.2)." This provides the backward link.

F26.3 footnote [^carita] defines the seven conduct types.

**Missing cross-reference:** F29.14 ("Six Categories of Persons") introduces an entirely distinct person typology (shramana, brahmin, etc.) that is NOT derived from the 28 types. No footnote clarifies the relationship between the two typologies. A reader might wonder whether the six categories are a subset, a reclassification, or a separate system.

### F29.14 and F26.2 Connection

F29.14's six categories (shramana, brahmin, pure-conduct practitioner, bhikshu, the diligent, renunciant) are alternative designations for "the person" (pudgala), not a reclassification of the 28. This is stated at F29.14 line 434: "all of these are alternative designations for the person (*pudgala*)." The distinction is clear in the text but **a footnote connecting this to the 28-type system in 26.2 would help the reader see that these are two orthogonal classification systems -- the 28 types classify practitioners by spiritual capacity and behavioral disposition, while the six categories classify by social/institutional role.**

## 2. Five Purifying-Conduct Objects (F26.4/26.6-27.3 -> F30.15-31.3 -> F32.12-15)

### Names and Order

Three canonical statements of the five objects:

| Location | Names | Order |
|----------|-------|-------|
| F26.4 (26.6-27.3) | impurity; loving-kindness and compassion; dependent origination; element differentiation; mindfulness of breathing | 1-2-3-4-5 |
| F30.15-31.3 (vipashyana applied) | impurity; loving-kindness (F30.16); dependent origination (F31.1); element differentiation (F31.2); mindfulness of breathing (F31.3) | 1-2-3-4-5 |
| F32.12-15 (beginner practice) | impurity (32.1-11); loving-kindness (32.12); dependent origination (32.13); element differentiation (32.14); mindfulness of breathing (32.15) | 1-2-3-4-5 |

**Assessment: FULLY CONSISTENT.** Names and order are identical across all three major presentations. The five-to-five mapping with behavioral types (greed -> impurity, aversion -> loving-kindness, delusion -> dependent origination, pride -> element differentiation, discursive thought -> mindfulness of breathing) is stated in F26.5 (the Revata dialogue), restated in F32.12 footnote [^five_objects], and echoed in F32.17 footnote [^five_conduct_echo].

### Cross-references

F32.12 footnote [^five_objects] explicitly says: "The five objects were first defined in section 26.4 and treated in detail in sections 26.5-27.3." This is the necessary backward link.

F30.15 footnote [^body_parts] references section 26.6 for the impurity contemplation details.

**Assessment: WELL CROSS-REFERENCED.** The three-layer treatment (definition -> vipashyana analysis -> beginner practice) is clearly linked.

## 3. Thirty-Seven Factors of Awakening (F28.10 -> F29.1-29.11)

### Terminology

F28.10 enumerates all seven groups:
- Four foundations of mindfulness (*smrityupasthana*)
- Four right efforts (*samyak-pradhana*)
- Four bases of supernatural power (*riddhipada*)
- Five faculties (*indriya*)
- Five powers (*bala*)
- Seven factors of awakening (*bodhyanga*)
- Eightfold noble path (*arya-ashtangika-marga*)

F29 elaborates each group: 29.1 (right efforts), 29.2-29.4 (samadhis and riddhipada), 29.5-29.6 (faculties and powers), 29.7 (correlation), 29.8 (wholesome roots), 29.9 (seven bodhyangas), 29.10 (eightfold path), 29.11 (summary).

**Assessment: FULLY CONSISTENT.** The Sanskrit terms, English renderings, and group names are identical across F28 and F29. The enumeration order is maintained.

Individual member names checked:
- Seven factors of awakening: "mindfulness, investigation-of-dharmas, diligence, joy, ease, concentration, equanimity" -- identical in F28.10 (line 494) and F29.9 (lines 242-244).
- Five faculties: "faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, prajna" -- identical in F28.10 (line 490) and F29.5.
- Eightfold path: "right view, right thought, right speech, right action, right livelihood, right effort, right mindfulness, right concentration" -- identical in F28.10 (line 496) and F29.10.

**Note:** F28.11 also elaborates the four foundations of mindfulness in detail, and F29.1 picks up the four right efforts "without interruption" from the mindfulness practice. The narrative flow is maintained.

### Cross-references

F29 footnote [^eight_factors_correlation] explicitly references "Fascicle 28" for the four-factor yoga. F29 footnote [^shramana_fruits] provides doctrinal context. The summary at 29.11 ties the entire sequence together.

**Assessment: WELL LINKED.**

## 4. Nine Stages of Mental Abiding (F26.5 -> F30.10 -> F31.6 -> F32)

### Names

Three distinct presentations:

| Location | Names |
|----------|-------|
| F26.5 (abbreviated) | "internally abide, continuously abide, re-settle, closely abide, be tamed, be pacified, be completely pacified, be one-pointed, and settle in equipoise" |
| F26.5 footnote | "(1) internal abiding, (2) continuous abiding, (3) re-settling, (4) close abiding, (5) taming, (6) pacifying, (7) completely pacifying, (8) one-pointed, (9) settling in equipoise" |
| F30.10 (full treatment) | "(1) Inward abiding, (2) Settled abiding, (3) Re-established abiding, (4) Close abiding, (5) Taming, (6) Pacifying, (7) Fully pacifying, (8) One-pointed attention, (9) Equipoise" |

**Terminology discrepancies detected:**

| Stage | F26.5 (footnote) | F30.10 | Discrepancy |
|-------|------------------|--------|-------------|
| 1 | internal abiding | Inward abiding | "internal" vs "inward" |
| 2 | continuous abiding | Settled abiding | "continuous" vs "settled" |
| 3 | re-settling | Re-established abiding | "re-settling" vs "re-established abiding" |
| 7 | completely pacifying | Fully pacifying | "completely" vs "fully" |

These are minor rendering differences, not doctrinal errors. However, for a work meant to be read as a single text, the reader encountering "continuous abiding" in F26 and "settled abiding" in F30 will not immediately recognize these as the same stage.

**F31.6** does not list the nine stage names, only refers to "the nine stages of mental abiding" and provides a footnote [^nine_ref] that says: "These nine stages of mental abiding were defined in section 30.10. They range from initial placement (*sthapana*) through to balanced settling (*samadhi*)." This uses yet another set of English glosses ("initial placement" / "balanced settling") that correspond to Sanskrit but differ from both F26 and F30 English renderings.

**F32** footnote [^nine_stages_ref] references the nine stages through "section 30.10" and uses the F30 terminology: "inward abiding (*citta-sthapana*), settled abiding (*samsthapana*), and re-established abiding (*avasthapana*)."

**Recommendation (MODERATE):** Harmonize the nine-stage names between F26.5 and F30.10. The F30.10 names are authoritative (that is where the stages are formally defined). The F26.5 footnote should use the same English terms as F30.10, with a note that the full treatment appears there.

## 5. Shamatha-Vipashyana Union (F31.9 -> F32 -> F33)

### Theoretical Definition (F31.9)

F31.9 defines the "path of paired operation" (*yugananddha-marga*):
- Requires the ninth stage of mental abiding (meditative equipoise) as foundation
- From that perfected samadhi, one cultivates higher wisdom through dharma-investigation
- The path "flows spontaneously, operating without effort"
- "Vipashyana becomes pure and luminous, pliantly gathered within shamatha's embrace, flowing as if on shamatha's own path"
- Footnote [^paired] cites Tsongkhapa on this passage

### Practical Application (F32)

F32 does NOT use the term "paired operation" or "yugananddha." Instead, it describes the alternation of shamatha and vipashyana in practical terms:
- Section 32.7: "Alternating Shamatha and Vipashyana"
- Section 32.16: mutual reinforcement of pliancy and one-pointedness leading to the "possessed of attention" status

**The connection is implicit, not explicit.** F32 describes the practical process that culminates in what F31.9 theoretically defined, but no footnote in F32 says "this is the practical realization of the paired operation described in section 31.9."

### Application in F33

F33 does NOT reference the "paired operation" concept. The seven attentions framework (F33.4-33.7) is an entirely new structure. The shamatha-vipashyana union is assumed to have been attained (as the "possessed of attention" status) but is not revisited doctrinally.

**Assessment: The theoretical definition in F31.9 and the practical application in F32-33 are doctrinally compatible but not explicitly linked.** The reader can reconstruct the connection: F31.9 defines what "union" means, F32.16-17 describes the practitioner attaining the prerequisite (ninth stage -> pliancy -> possessed of attention), and F33 moves forward from that attainment. But a footnote in F32.7 or F32.16 connecting to the F31.9 definition would strengthen the arc.

**Recommendation (MINOR):** Add a footnote in section 32.7 or 32.16 referencing the paired-operation definition of section 31.9 to make the theoretical-practical connection explicit.

## 6. "Possessed of Attention" Handoff (F32.17 -> F33.1-2)

### Transition Quality

F32.17 establishes the status "possessed of attention" (*samanaskara*):
- Technical criterion: first attaining form-realm concentration
- Characteristics: pliancy arises swiftly, hindrances subdued, five afflictive encounters manageable
- Footnote [^possessed_attention] explicitly says: "It is the starting point for the Fourth Yoga Place (fascicle 33)"

F33 opens with:
- Section 33.1: Uddana verse for the Fourth Yoga Place
- Section 33.2: "Two Paths After Attaining 'Possessed of Attention'"
- The exact terminology is carried across: "The yogins who have attained the status of 'possessed of attention'"
- Footnote [^two_paths] explicitly says: "Having attained the initial shamatha ('possessed of attention,' *samanaskara*) at the end of fascicle 32"

**Assessment: EXCELLENT HANDOFF.** This is the strongest fascicle-to-fascicle transition in the entire work. The terminology is identical. Both sides of the transition include footnotes pointing to the other. The reader cannot miss the connection.

F33.2 assumes knowledge from F32 (the characteristics of "possessed of attention" status, the meaning of form-realm concentration, the role of pliancy) but does not re-explain them. This is appropriate -- the text flows as a continuous exposition.

## 7. Overall Doctrinal Arc

### The Progressive Path

The translation does convey a coherent progressive path:

**WHO (F26):** Person types (28 types + behavioral characterization) establish that practitioners differ and require different approaches. The meditation object framework (four types) establishes the complete apparatus. The purifying-conduct objects match specific antidotes to specific behavioral types.

**WHAT (F26-27):** The five purifying-conduct objects are defined and elaborated. The skillful objects and affliction-purifying objects complete the framework. All meditation objects are thoroughly analyzed.

**GROUND RULES (F28):** The three trainings, gates of liberation, yoga types, and types of cultivation provide the procedural framework. The thirty-seven factors of awakening enumerate the path-factors.

**HOW - FACTORS (F29):** Each group within the 37 factors is elaborated in practice terms. The four shramana fruits establish the soteriological goal. The person typology is revisited with practice-specific detail. The text returns to behavioral types to show how they interact with the factors.

**HOW - INSTRUCTION (F30):** The teacher-student relationship is established. The nine stages of mental abiding are formally defined. The six powers and four attentions provide the mechanism. The four types of vipashyana are defined. Vipashyana is applied to each purifying-conduct object (impurity, loving-kindness).

**HOW - CONTINUED (F31):** Vipashyana applied to remaining objects (dependent origination, element differentiation, mindfulness of breathing). The nine practice approaches. Shamatha-vipashyana union defined. Cultivation of attention.

**HOW - BEGINNER'S PRACTICE (F32):** The five signs framework for the beginner. Each purifying-conduct object is now practiced concretely (impurity, loving-kindness, dependent origination, element differentiation, mindfulness of breathing). Shamatha and pliancy attainment. Culmination: "possessed of attention."

**WHAT HAPPENS (F33):** Two paths diverge (worldly and supramundane). The seven attentions for abandoning desire. The four dhyanas. The formless attainments. Superknowledges. Rebirth destinations. Characteristics of the person free from desire.

### Arc Coherence

**Assessment: STRONG.** The arc moves logically from classification (who are the practitioners?) through theory (what do they practice?) through method (how do they practice?) through attainment (what happens when they succeed?). Each fascicle builds on the previous.

Key structural strengths:
1. The five purifying-conduct objects appear in three layers (definition, analysis, practice) -- the reader encounters them with increasing depth.
2. The nine stages of mental abiding are introduced in F26, defined in F30, referenced in F31, and assumed in F32 -- a consistent thread.
3. The 28 person types in F26 are not abandoned but revisited in F29 with additional practical detail.
4. The "possessed of attention" handoff at the F32-F33 boundary is seamless.

### Where the Arc is Weakest

**The F28 transition.** F28 introduces the three trainings, yoga types, and factors of awakening, which are necessary for F29's elaboration. But the transition from the meditation-object framework of F26-27 to the training-framework of F28 is abrupt. The text does not explicitly say "having established what to practice (objects), we now establish the framework for how to practice (trainings and factors)." The uddana verse at 28.1 provides structural orientation, but a reader might experience F28 as a change of subject rather than a deepening of the same subject.

**The F29-F30 transition.** Similarly, F29 ends with person typology (29.13-29.17) and F30 opens with the teacher-student relationship (30.1-30.3). The shift from theoretical classification to practical instruction is doctrinally logical but not narrated. A reader might benefit from a footnote at F30.1 noting that the Third Yoga Place now transitions from "what to cultivate" to "how to cultivate it under a teacher's guidance."

## Summary

**Overall verdict: The translation functions well as a single, coherent work.** The progressive doctrinal arc is clear and well-structured. The major continuity threads -- person types, purifying-conduct objects, 37 factors, nine stages, shamatha-vipashyana -- are maintained with high consistency. The F32-F33 handoff is particularly strong.

**Issues requiring attention:**
1. **(MODERATE)** Nine-stage names differ between F26.5 footnote and F30.10 canonical definitions. Harmonize.
2. **(MODERATE)** "Balanced-conduct" (F26.3) vs "equal-part conduct" (F29.13) -- same type, different English rendering.
3. **(MINOR)** Five missing cross-reference footnotes identified above (section 8).
4. **(MINOR)** F31.9 -> F32 shamatha-vipashyana union link is implicit rather than explicit.

No critical arc-continuity failures were found.
