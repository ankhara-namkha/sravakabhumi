## Findings

### 1. [m] Footnote on acharya/upadhyaya: English glosses potentially misleading

**Location:** Footnote [^teacher_types], section 30.2

**Text:** "The preceptor (*acharya*, 軌範師) is the teacher who models monastic conduct and discipline. The instructor (*upadhyaya*, 親教師) is the personal ordination teacher."

**Issue:** The English glosses "preceptor" and "instructor" are reversed relative to the most common usage in English-language Vinaya scholarship. In standard usage:
- *upadhyaya* (Pali: *upajjhaya*) = the **preceptor**, i.e., the ordination master who confers ordination
- *acharya* = the **instructor** or **teacher**, i.e., the one who teaches monastic conduct, recitation, etc.

Xuanzang's own translations confirm this: 親教師 (qinjiaoshi, "personal teaching master") = upadhyaya, the ordination preceptor; 軌範師 (guifanshi, "model/standard master") = acharya, the conduct instructor. The Chinese-Sanskrit correspondences in the footnote are correct (acharya = 軌範師, upadhyaya = 親教師), but the English words "preceptor" for acharya and "instructor" for upadhyaya may confuse readers familiar with Vinaya scholarship where "preceptor" conventionally designates the upadhyaya.

The running text itself ("their preceptor (*acharya*), their instructor (*upadhyaya*)") carries the same potential confusion.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- The Sanskrit and Chinese are correctly matched; the issue is only with the English gloss convention. However, a scholar cross-referencing with Vinaya literature could be momentarily confused.

**Recommendation:** Either (a) swap the English glosses so that upadhyaya = "preceptor" and acharya = "instructor/discipline master," which matches the dominant convention in Horner, Prebish, and other Vinaya scholars; or (b) keep the current glosses but add a brief note acknowledging the terminological variation ("The English terms 'preceptor' and 'instructor' are used here to render the functional roles described in the Chinese; note that some Vinaya scholarship uses these terms in the reverse assignment").

### 2. [m] "Person of self-regard" -- rendering of 自愛補特伽羅

**Location:** Section 30.2, line 38

**Text:** "When a person of self-regard who is a beginner..."

**Issue:** The Chinese 自愛補特伽羅 (zi'ai putejialuo) is a standard Yogacara term meaning "a person who loves/cares for themselves" in the positive Buddhist sense -- someone who genuinely seeks their own spiritual welfare. The translation "person of self-regard" is defensible but carries a slightly ambiguous connotation in modern English, where "self-regard" can shade toward vanity or self-esteem rather than the intended meaning of genuine self-care directed toward liberation.

The canonical parallel is AN 4.97 (Khippanisanti Sutta), where the Buddha speaks of one who "loves oneself" (attakamarupena) in the sense of seeking one's own true welfare through practice. Bodhi renders the concept as "one who loves himself" in the context of pursuing what is truly beneficial.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- The meaning is recoverable from context ("seeking to attain their own true benefit" immediately follows), but a reader unfamiliar with the Buddhist usage might briefly misread "self-regard" as narcissism.

**Recommendation:** Consider "a person who genuinely cares for their own welfare" or "a self-caring person" -- or retain "self-regard" but add a brief parenthetical: "a person of self-regard (one who genuinely seeks their own spiritual benefit)."

### 3. [m] Four floods footnote -- standard enumeration confirmed, minor phrasing note

**Location:** Footnote [^four_floods], section 30.3

**Text:** "The four floods (*catur-ogha*, 四瀑流) are desire, existence, views, and ignorance"

**Issue:** The enumeration is doctrinally correct. The four oghas (floods/torrents) in both Pali and Sanskrit Abhidharma are: (1) kama-ogha (flood of desire), (2) bhava-ogha (flood of existence/becoming), (3) drishti-ogha (flood of views), and (4) avidya-ogha (flood of ignorance). This matches SN 45.171 and AN 4.10 as cited.

One minor note: the footnote uses "desire" for kama-ogha, which is acceptable but could be made more precise as "sensual desire" to distinguish from the broader concept of desire/craving (trishna). The bhava-ogha is rendered "existence," which is standard. No doctrinal error.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- Technically correct; "sensual desire" would be more precise than bare "desire" for the first ogha.

**Recommendation:** Consider "sensual desire, existence, views, and ignorance" for greater precision.

### 4. [m] "Pure conduct" for 梵行 -- acceptable but worth noting

**Location:** Section 30.4, line 94

**Text:** "From the very beginning of your pure conduct..."

**Issue:** 梵行 (fanxing) = brahmacarya, the "holy life" or "pure conduct" of a renunciant. The translation "pure conduct" is a reasonable rendering. More common scholarly translations include "the holy life" (Bodhi), "the spiritual life" (some translators), or "the brahmacharya life." "Pure conduct" hews closer to the Chinese literal meaning (梵 = pure/Brahma, 行 = conduct) and is not doctrinally wrong. However, the term carries strong Vinaya overtones of celibacy and complete monastic discipline that "pure conduct" somewhat understates.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- Not an error; a matter of register preference.

**Recommendation:** No change required. If the translator wishes to signal the full weight of the term, "holy life" (Bodhi's standard) or "brahmacarya" with a first-use gloss would work, but "pure conduct" is defensible and consistent with the NTC approach of using English where possible.

### 5. [C] No critical doctrinal errors found

The four inquiry points in section 30.4 are doctrinally sound and match the Chinese source precisely:

1. **Exclusive refuge in the Three Jewels** -- correctly framed as refuge in Buddha, Dharma, and Sangha "and not in non-Buddhist teachers, their wrong teachings, or their assemblies of disciples." This matches the standard formula for going for refuge (saranagamana) with the additional Yogacara emphasis on exclusivity (一向歸). The phrase "non-Buddhist teachers" for 外道師 is appropriate.

2. **Purified shila and right view from the beginning** -- correctly captures 淨修梵行善淨尸羅正直見. The pairing of shila (moral discipline) and right view (samyag-drishti) as foundational requirements for a student is standard in all Buddhist traditions. Tsongkhapa's Lamrim Chenmo similarly lists pure moral discipline as the first quality of a proper student.

3. **Heard and retained the noble truths** -- correctly captures 諸聖諦法若少若多聞受持 ("whether in brief or in detail"). The emphasis on both hearing (shruta) and retention (dharana) of the four noble truths is standard Yogacara pedagogy.

4. **Deep faith in nirvana and going forth for cessation** -- correctly captures 於涅槃深心信解為證寂滅而出家. The pairing of faith (adhimoksha) in nirvana with the motivation for going forth (pravrajya) is doctrinally standard for the shravaka path.

**Severity:** This is not a finding but a confirmation -- no critical doctrinal issues in the four inquiry points.

### 6. [m] Encouragement section -- "Elder" as address term for 賢首

**Location:** Section 30.3, line 58 ("Well done, well done, noble one!") and line 78 ("Elder, know this...")

**Issue:** The Chinese uses two different address terms: 賢首 (xianshou, "noble one / wise one") in the initial greeting and 長老 (zhanglao, "elder / sthavira") later. The translation correctly renders 賢首 as "noble one" initially. However, it then renders 長老 as "Elder" on line 78, which is correct. This is consistent and doctrinally fine. No issue here on closer inspection.

One small note: 賢首 is literally "worthy head/chief" and is a polite form of address for a practitioner, not necessarily implying seniority. The rendering "noble one" captures this well.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- Confirmed correct. No change needed.

### 7. [m] "Gods, humans, asuras, and all the rest" -- scope of beings

**Location:** Section 30.3, line 80

**Text:** "out of compassion for the world -- for gods, humans, asuras, and all the rest -- so that they may attain benefit and happiness"

**Issue:** The Chinese 哀愍世間及諸天人阿素洛等 uses the standard formula "devas, humans, asuras, etc." (天人阿素洛等). This is a stock formula from sutras (cf. the epithets of the Buddha: "teacher of gods and humans"). The translation is doctrinally correct. The 等 (etc.) is appropriately rendered as "and all the rest" to indicate the formula encompasses other classes of beings.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- Confirmed correct. Merely noting for completeness that this is a stock formula faithfully rendered.

### 8. [m] Uddana footnote -- structural claim about the ten topics

**Location:** Footnote [^uddana_30]

**Text:** "This *uddana* outlines the ten topics of the Third Yoga Place. The first four (approaching the teacher, encouragement, inquiry, investigation) describe the preliminary encounter between student and teacher. The remaining six describe the five domains..."

**Issue:** The footnote describes "the remaining six" as describing "five domains," which requires clarification. The uddana verse lists ten items: (1) approaching, (2) encouragement, (3) inquiry, (4) investigation, (5) establishment, (6) guarding the resources, (7) seclusion, (8) one-pointed mind, (9) purification of obstacles, (10) cultivating attention. The footnote says "establishment" is the "encompassing framework" and the other five (6-10) are the five domains. This structural interpretation is reasonable and matches the Chinese text's subsequent treatment, where 安立 (establishment) is the overarching category containing five sub-topics (護養定資糧, 遠離, 心一境性, 障清淨, 修作意), as confirmed at T30.0449c15-17. However, the phrasing "The remaining six describe the five domains... with 'establishment' as the encompassing framework" could be clearer -- one might miscount since "remaining six" = items 5-10, and "establishment" (item 5) + "five domains" (items 6-10) = six items total, not five domains within six items. The actual structure is: establishment IS the framework that CONTAINS five sub-areas.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- The note is not wrong but could be clearer in distinguishing the framework term from its five contents.

**Recommendation:** Rephrase to something like: "Items 5 through 10 constitute a single unit: 'establishment' (item 5) is the overarching framework, comprising five specific domains: guarding samadhi resources, seclusion, one-pointed mind, purification of obstacles, and cultivating attention."

**Overall assessment:** Sections 30.1-30.4 are doctrinally sound. The translation accurately conveys the Yogacara pedagogical framework for the teacher-student encounter: the student's proper mindset, the protocol for approaching, the teacher's encouragement, and the four prerequisite inquiries. No doctrinal errors, contradictions, anachronisms, or category errors were found. The minor findings are matters of terminological precision and footnote clarity rather than doctrinal substance. The four inquiry points correctly capture the standard Yogacara prerequisites for a shravaka student: exclusive refuge, pure discipline with right view, knowledge of the noble truths, and faith in nirvana as the goal of going forth. These align with both the Pali canonical requirements and the Yogacara-Sarvastivada elaboration in the Sravakabhumi.
