## Section 26.2: The Twenty-Eight Types of Persons

### Issue 1 — FM (Formula Monotony): Dominated-person definitions
**Severity: Moderate**

Passages (3)–(7) follow an identical five-clause template with no variation:

> "What is a person dominated by X? It is a person who in previous lives has cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly cultivated the affliction of X. Due to this cause and condition, in this present life, with regard to objects of X, they have fierce X and enduring X. This is called a person dominated by X."

This pattern repeats verbatim five times in succession (greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive thought). Nanamoli would have varied sentence rhythm after the second iteration. By the fourth, a practitioner's attention has switched to pattern-recognition rather than meaning.

**Suggested revision approach:** The content of each entry is genuinely parallel — compression is appropriate here. Consider collapsing entries (3)–(7) into a single paragraph:

> "Persons dominated by greed, aversion, delusion, pride, or discursive thought share a common structure: in previous lives they cultivated, habituated, and repeatedly reinforced the corresponding affliction. The residue of that habituation carries forward: in this life, with respect to the corresponding objects, both fierce and enduring forms of that affliction arise readily."

Then handle any entry that genuinely requires individual treatment (there are none here) separately. This is a legitimate compression the Chinese structure invites (the repetition is mechanical, not doctrinal).

**Note:** The spark-similes for persons (20)–(23) — the intermediate-state subtypes — are excellent. These are exactly the kind of passage that should be held up as a model: vivid, concrete, alive.

### Issue 2 — TE (Translated-ese): Person 8, "balanced disposition"
**Severity: Low**

> "Due to this cause and condition, with regard to objects of desire, hatred, confusion, pride, and discursive thought, they have no fierce greed and no enduring greed — yet greed still manifests in accordance with those objects. The same applies to aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought."

The phrase "in accordance with those objects" is a literal transfer of a Chinese locative that has no natural English equivalent in this position. A reader must pause to decode it. The following clause "the same applies to..." while not wrong, feels like a translator stepping out from behind the text.

**Suggested revision:**

> "...they have neither fierce nor enduring greed — yet some greed still arises when encountering its objects. The same pattern holds for aversion, delusion, pride, and discursive thought."

---

### Issue 3 — NQ (Nested Qualification): Person 25, "liberated by occasion"
**Severity: Moderate**

> "That is to say, by the force of preventing regression, they either merely maintain their present level of wholesome qualities, or through successive days, nights, moments, eye-blinks, and instants, they strive and progress — yet they have not attained the most supremely acute [realization]."

The main verb (they maintain / they strive) is buried under the lead qualification ("by the force of preventing regression"), and then the sentence is interrupted by the em-dash before landing on a negative conclusion. The temporal series ("days, nights, moments, eye-blinks, and instants") creates an additional layer between subject and predicate. Over 50 words.

**Suggested revision:**

> "To prevent regression they may simply hold their present attainment steady; or they press forward through each day, night, and moment — yet the most supremely acute realization eludes them still."

**Severity: Low**

The two verse commentaries follow the same template: quote phrase from verse, gloss it with a term or reference, move to the next phrase. This is appropriate for a commentary passage and the Chinese almost certainly mandates it. At two iterations it is not a problem; it would be if there were four or five. Flag only for awareness, not for revision.

### Issue 5 — LG (Long Sentence): Image passage
**Severity: Low**

> "Although the knowable objects are not actually present through direct encounter, nor has some unrelated substitute appeared, nevertheless — through the resolute confidence of the ground of meditative equipoise, through an apprehension by attention that is similar to the actual objects — the knowable objects appear in a similar fashion."

Word count: approximately 54 words. The nested prepositional sequence ("through... through...") after the dash creates a slight stumble. Readable but could breathe.

**Suggested revision:**

> "The knowable objects are not directly present, nor has some unrelated substitute appeared. Yet through the resolute confidence of meditative equipoise — through attention that apprehends them in a form similar to the actual objects — they appear in similar fashion."

### Issue 6 — UJ (Unexplained Jargon): "transformation of the basis"
**Severity: Low**

> "they attain the transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravritti*)."

The footnote [^transformation] is referenced but I cannot see its content in the excerpt. If the footnote explains this term, the flag is resolved. If the footnote is absent or minimal, this is a significant omission — "transformation of the basis" is not self-evident even for a practitioner-reader, and *ashraya-paravritti* is a Yogacara technical term that requires at minimum a one-sentence gloss on first occurrence.

**Action:** Verify footnote [^transformation] exists and is explanatory. If not, add: "[Translator's note: The transformation of the basis (*ashraya-paravritti*) — the fundamental shift in the practitioner's cognitive ground (*ashraya*) — marks the passage from mediation through images to direct, non-conceptual perception of the object.]"

## Section 27.3: Purifying-Conduct Objects: Mindfulness of Breathing

### Issue 7 — FM (Formula Monotony): Sixteen victory practices, training formulas
**Severity: High**

The sixteen victory practices present thirty-two iterations of the formula "I now train in X while breathing in. I now train in X while breathing out." This is repeated without variation across all sixteen practices and their commentary. The Chinese (*我學...* repeated) mandates the repetition, but the English effect is numbing.

Passages:

> "(1) 'I now train in being mindful of the inbreath. I now train in being mindful of the outbreath.' (2) 'I now train in knowing whether the breath is long or short while breathing in and breathing out.' (3) 'I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing in. I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing out.' (4) 'I now train in calming body formations while breathing in. I now train in calming body formations while breathing out.'"

...and the same pattern continues across all four tetrads. Then the commentary section repeats the same formulas verbatim after explaining each practice:

> "Therefore one resolves: 'I now train in being mindful of the inbreath. I now train in being mindful of the outbreath.'"

This phrase ("one resolves: 'I now train in...'") appears approximately twelve times in the commentary alone.

**Note on source fidelity tension:** This is genuinely difficult. The Chinese text intends the repetition — these are formulas the practitioner is meant to recite, and their ritualized quality is part of the teaching. Bodhi handles this in his Majjhima Nikaya translation by preserving the Pali formula structure but using varied connective tissue around it. Nanamoli similarly.

**Suggested approach (does not alter the formulas themselves):**

1. In the initial listing of all sixteen, consolidate the in-breath/out-breath pairs onto a single line: "(1) 'I train in being mindful of the breath, in and out.'" This is not a mistranslation — the paired structure can be preserved in a note explaining that the Chinese presents each direction separately.

2. In the commentary, where one resolves the formula, alternate between: "one resolves..." (first use per tetrad) and "one takes up this training" or simply end the sentence with the practice name after first introduction. The reader already knows the formula structure.

3. Alternatively, present the full formulas once in the enumeration, then in the commentary refer back by number rather than requoting: "Therefore one resolves the first practice [as above]."

### Issue 8 — NQ (Nested Qualification): Commentary on "knowing the entire body"
**Severity: High**

> "When one takes as object the fine channels within the body through which the inbreath and outbreath pervade and enter through all the pores, and generates resolute confidence (*adhimoksha*) regarding this, one resolves: 'I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing in and breathing out.'"

This is a single sentence with four embedded clauses before the main predicate ("one resolves"). The relative clause ("through which the inbreath and outbreath pervade and enter through all the pores") inside the noun phrase ("the fine channels within the body") inside the object phrase ("taking as object") creates a three-level nesting. A practitioner reading this must hold the syntactic structure in working memory while trying to understand the meditation instruction.

**Suggested revision:**

> "The fine channels throughout the body are the pathways through which the breath enters and pervades, reaching even the pores. Holding these channels as one's object and generating resolute confidence regarding them, one resolves: 'I now train in knowing the entire body while breathing in and breathing out.'"

---

### Issue 9 — TE (Translated-ese): Commentary on calming body formations
**Severity: Moderate**

> "When at times the inbreath and intermediate inbreath have ceased and the outbreath and intermediate outbreath have not yet arisen — taking as object the empty, absent interval between inbreath and outbreath, the position where the breath is absent — or when the outbreath and intermediate outbreath have ceased and the inbreath and intermediate inbreath have not yet arisen — taking as object the empty, absent interval between outbreath and inbreath — at that time one resolves..."

Word count: approximately 80 words. The sentence is structured as a Chinese pivot-construction: state condition A / describe action / or state condition B / describe action / then conclude. This maps Chinese clause-chaining directly into English, producing a sentence that requires two full readings to parse.

**Suggested revision:**

> "Between every outbreath and inbreath, and between every inbreath and outbreath, there is an empty interval where the breath is momentarily absent. Taking that gap as object — the still point where breath has ceased and not yet turned — one resolves: 'I now train in calming body formations while breathing in and breathing out.'"

This preserves the doctrinal content (the four intervals are collapsed into a general description, which is accurate since both are the same object) and dramatically improves readability.

---

### Issue 10 — TE (Translated-ese): Comprehending dependent origination passage
**Severity: Low**

> "Having investigated, one truly comprehends: the inbreath and outbreath depend on the body and are conditioned by the body; they depend on the mind and are conditioned by the mind."

The pair "depend on X and are conditioned by X" is a Chinese redundancy (依...緣...) that in English reads as tautology. Nanamoli resolves this by choosing one: "conditioned by body and mind." Bodhi similarly. The doubling adds nothing for the English reader.

**Suggested revision:** "...one truly comprehends: breath depends on body and on mind, each as its condition."

---

### Issue 11 — TE (Translated-ese): "Abiding in it extensively" formula
**Severity: Low**

The phrase "abiding extensively in this" or "abiding in it extensively" appears three times across the section as a closing formula for each of the first four cultivations:

- "When the practitioner thus abides extensively within the skandhas, this is called having comprehended them."
- "When one has abided extensively in this comprehension, one attains direct realization..."
- "When one has thus thoroughly cultivated comprehension within the noble truths..."

The third instance varies the formula slightly; the first two are nearly identical. The phrase "abides extensively" is a Chinese temporal-quantifier idiom meaning something like "dwells in this practice fully" or "has thoroughly established this." "Extensively" in English suggests spatial or categorical breadth, not depth of practice.

**Suggested revision:** "When the practitioner has thus established this comprehension and dwells fully within it, this is called having comprehended the skandhas." (And similar for the other two instances.)

## Standout Passages (Model Quality)

For the record, these passages exemplify what the translation is at its best:

**26.2 — The intermediate-state spark similes (persons 20–23):** Concrete, graduated, memorable. The three simile-types map cleanly onto the three sub-types without clutter. A practitioner can hold these images.

**26.5 — The Revata dialogue:** The back-and-forth is natural. The Buddha's "Excellent, excellent!" lands. The categories (matching, similar, non-inverted) are introduced clearly, and the exposition follows without hedging.

**27.3 — The counting cultivation (section i–vii):** The progression from counting one-as-one through transcending the counting stage entirely is well-managed. The move from technique to insight — "at this point one has transcended the stage of counting and should no longer count" — is clean and instructive. The pliancy-arising paragraph at the close of counting cultivation is the correct length.
