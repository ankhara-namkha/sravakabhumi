## Section 28.1: The Three Superior Trainings

---

**FLAG R28-001**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Line 67

> "A noble disciple who has not yet attained the root dhyanas (*maula-dhyana*) may first enter the path through seeing. Afterward, in order to progressively sever all afflictions to be abandoned on the path of cultivation (*bhavana-marga*), they engage diligently in preparatory practice, cultivating the mindfulness factor of awakening (*smriti-sambodhyanga*) through to the equanimity factor of awakening (*upeksha-sambodhyanga*)."

The second sentence is ~55 words with the main verb ("engage") buried after a long purpose clause ("in order to progressively sever all afflictions to be abandoned on the path of cultivation"). The three Sanskrit parentheticals break the reading rhythm at every clause boundary. A practitioner reading aloud would stumble three times.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"A noble disciple who has not yet attained the root dhyanas may first enter the path through seeing. Afterward, to sever the remaining afflictions progressively, they engage in diligent preparatory practice — cultivating each of the seven factors of awakening, from mindfulness through to equanimity."

---

**FLAG R28-002**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Line 71

> "Furthermore, one may possess the superior training in discipline without the superior training in mind or prajna. Or one may possess the superior training in discipline together with the superior training in mind, yet lack the superior training in prajna. However, it is impossible to possess the superior training in prajna without also possessing the superior training in discipline and the superior training in mind."

"The superior training in X" is repeated nine times in three sentences. The pattern — "X without Y or Z; X with Y but not Z; impossible to have Z without X and Y" — is logically crisp. But the repetition of the full phrase each time is heavy Chinese-influenced sentence-building, where each branch of a logical tree is stated in full. In English, a reader would profit from some compression after the first iteration.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"One may possess the training in discipline alone, or the trainings in discipline and mind together without that in prajna. But it is impossible to possess the training in prajna without also having the other two. When the third training is present, all three are necessarily complete."

---

**FLAG R28-003**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (UJ)
**Location:** Line 29

> "...achieve the certainty of correctness — definitive entry onto the noble path (*samyaktva-niyama*)."

The inline gloss "definitive entry onto the noble path" is good. But "certainty of correctness" as the primary rendering still requires unpacking. What is being correct about? The footnote [^samyaktva_niyama] is excellent. In the body text, the phrase reads as an abstract property rather than as a decisive turning point on the path.

**Suggested rewrite:** "...achieve the certainty of correct direction — the point at which one has definitively entered the noble path and cannot turn back."

## Section 28.2: The Three Faculties

---

**FLAG R28-004**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Register drift (RD) / Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Lines 99–103

> "For one who has not yet directly realized the truths and is diligently engaged in preparatory practice for the direct realization of the truths — based on this, the faculty of 'I shall come to know the unknown' is established.
>
> For one who has already attained the direct realization of the truths and abides as a learner (*shaiksha*) — based on this, the faculty of full knowledge is established.
>
> For an arhat whose task is accomplished, abiding in the position of a non-learner (*ashaiksha*) — based on this, the faculty of one who possesses full knowledge is established."

The triple repetition of "based on this, the faculty of X is established" is a mechanical Chinese formula (以此立X根). It reads as institutional text rather than living teaching. The structure also front-loads conditions in a way that delays the payoff — the reader plows through a qualifying clause before getting the name of the faculty.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"The faculty of 'I shall come to know the unknown' belongs to one who has not yet directly realized the truths but is diligently preparing to do so. The faculty of full knowledge belongs to one who has already realized the truths and abides as a learner still traversing the path. The faculty of one who possesses full knowledge belongs to the arhat whose task is complete, abiding beyond training."

## Section 28.3: The Three Gates of Liberation

---

**FLAG R28-005**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Line 119

> "Among conditioned things, this refers to the five skandhas (aggregates) bound to the three realms. Among unconditioned things, this refers to nirvana. These two — conditioned and unconditioned — taken together are called 'the existent.' As for what is spoken of as a self, or as a sentient being, a life-force, a being born, and so forth — this is called 'the non-existent.'"

The sentence "As for what is spoken of as a self, or as a sentient being, a life-force, a being born, and so forth" piles up five terms in apposition before landing on the predicate. The em-dash delays the resolution. A reader learns what the "non-existent" is only after four stacked qualifiers. This is recognizable Chinese clause structure (至於所謂自我...).

**Suggested rewrite:**
"Whatever is conventionally spoken of as a self, a sentient being, a life-force, or a born entity — none of this actually exists. This is called 'the non-existent.'"

**FLAG R28-007**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Line 149

> "(6) Generating desire, attachment, and craving for the various worldly idle elaborations (*prapanca*) — of many different kinds and categories — that one ponders."

The em-dash insertion "— of many different kinds and categories —" is a parenthetical modifier inside a sentence that is already a nominalized clause. The main logical content is: "desire for idle elaborations that one ponders." The "of many different kinds" is just emphasis. The em-dashes make the sentence feel cluttered.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"(6) Generating desire, attachment, and craving for the many varieties of worldly idle elaboration (*prapanca*) on which the mind dwells."

**FLAG R28-008**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (UJ)
**Location:** Line 155

> "(iii) For one who is lax and slack in cultivating shamatha and vipashyana: attending to drawbacks, torpor and drowsiness overwhelming the mind, causing extreme mental contraction."

The grammatical subject of "attending to drawbacks" is unclear. The clause can be read as (a) the lax practitioner deliberately attending to drawbacks (a meditation technique), or (b) the drawbacks themselves overwhelming the mind. The intended reading is (b), but the syntax does not make this evident.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"(iii) For one who is lax and slack in cultivating shamatha and vipashyana: the mind drifts toward negative signs, torpor and drowsiness overwhelm it, and extreme mental contraction results."

**FLAG R28-009**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM)
**Location:** Lines 243–245 (Repeated observation, section 28.4)

> "Based on ethical discipline, one repeatedly observes whether one refrains from wrongdoing and whether one performs what is good. Regarding wrongdoing not committed, one does not relapse. Regarding wholesome deeds not yet performed, one does not retreat from them. Regarding wrongdoing committed, one abandons it. Regarding wholesome deeds performed, one does not abandon them."

Five consecutive sentences each beginning "Regarding X, one does Y." The Chinese parallelism here (就...就...) is preserved mechanically. A practitioner-reader recognizes the fourfold pattern but the "Regarding X..." construction repeated four times creates bureaucratic monotony. The same content could be delivered with more natural sentence flow.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"Based on ethical discipline, one repeatedly examines one's conduct. Wrongdoing not yet committed, one does not let arise. Wrongdoing already committed, one abandons. Wholesome deeds not yet done, one does not shrink from. Wholesome deeds already established, one does not relinquish."

*Or in a more flowing register:*
"Based on ethical discipline, one keeps watch: preventing uncommitted wrongs from arising, abandoning wrongs already committed, not shrinking from wholesome deeds yet to be done, and not abandoning those already accomplished."

## Section 28.5: Four Types of Yoga Failure

---

**FLAG R28-010**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Sentences >50 words (LG)
**Location:** Line 282

> "Furthermore, regarding their devoted disciples — whether monastic or lay followers — they become attached, nurturing affection and following them along in order to attract more. They then reflect: 'These monastic and lay disciples have faith in me and all consider me an arhat. Should they, relying on yogic attention and shamatha-vipashyana practice, come to question me, and I am unable to respond, they will lose their faith in me and no longer consider me an arhat. This will result in the loss of offerings and respect.'"

The quoted interior monologue is ~65 words in a single sentence, delivered through a conditional with three embedded clauses ("should they...come to question me, and I am unable to respond, they will lose faith..."). The reader must track two nested conditions before reaching the imagined consequence. The content is psychologically vivid, but the sentence structure collapses it.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"They then reflect: 'My disciples — whether monastic or lay — have faith in me and regard me as an arhat. But what if they apply themselves to yogic attention and shamatha-vipashyana and come to ask me questions? If I cannot answer, they will lose faith. They will no longer regard me as an arhat, and the offerings and respect will cease.'"

**FLAG R28-011**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Sentences >50 words (LG)
**Location:** Line 286

> "If there are bhikshus well-versed in the three baskets (*tripitaka*), the person conceals their yoga and does not wish to reveal it to them. But to their own lay and monastic disciples, they privately and secretly impart this yoga, keeping it from becoming public. Why? They fear that should someone well-versed in the three baskets hear of their yoga, they would examine it against the sutras and find it does not accord, illuminate it with the vinaya and find it does not reflect the vinaya, and investigate it through dharma and find it contradicts the nature of dharma."

The final sentence is ~60 words with three parallel "they would X it and find Y" clauses chained by commas. The three-clause tricolon is rhetorically powerful here — it builds like a legal indictment. But the sentence-length makes it hard to deliver the full rhetorical impact. Consider breaking after "keeping it from becoming public." then beginning a new sentence with the three-part exposure formula.

**Suggested rewrite (last sentence only):**
"Why? They fear exposure: were someone learned in the three baskets to hear of this yoga, they would examine it against the sutras and find it discordant, illuminate it with the vinaya and find it in violation, investigate it through dharma and find it contrary to the nature of dharma."

## Section 28.6: The Four Yogas

---

**FLAG R28-012**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Broken logic / unclear structure (BL)
**Location:** Lines 316–326 (The treatment of "method")

> "**Diligent method** (*upaya*) is of four kinds. Through the power of the restraint of ethical discipline, one well guards one's mindfulness. Because one well guards mindfulness, one is able to be non-negligent — protecting the mind and cultivating wholesome dharmas. Because of non-negligence, the mind is directed inward to cultivate shamatha and the superior wisdom-dharma of vipashyana."

The text announces "four kinds" but presents a causal chain of four steps without numbering them. Faith, aspiration, and diligence each received an explicit numbered list of four sub-types. Method does not, despite the claim "of four kinds." A reader counting the steps in the passage would get three causal links, not four. The sub-types of method are never distinguished from each other.

Additionally, "diligent method" (精進方便) appears to merge two yogas — method and diligence — which were already listed separately. This creates a logical tension: is this the fourth yoga (method) or a continuation of the third (diligence)?

**Suggested rewrite:**
Retain the causal chain, but explicitly number the four stages as "(1) guarding ethical discipline, (2) guarding mindfulness, (3) practicing non-negligence, and (4) cultivating shamatha and vipashyana," parallel to the sub-enumeration given for the other three yogas.

**FLAG R28-013**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Line 332

> "In this regard, one should know: First, through faith, one develops deep conviction regarding what should be attained. Having developed conviction, one generates aspiration for wholesome dharmas. Because of aspiration, one encourages oneself day and night, abiding in diligence with firm courage. Having generated diligence, one takes up the method and can attain what has not been attained, contact what has not been contacted, and realize what has not been realized."

"Attain what has not been attained, contact what has not been contacted, and realize what has not been realized" is a formulaic Chinese threefold expression (未得令得...未觸令觸...未證令證) repeated verbatim here from its earlier occurrence (line 308). The parallel construction is traditional, but a practitioner-reader encounters the same phrase twice in close proximity without recognizing it as a citation or formula. The phrase "contact what has not been contacted" is also the most opaque of the three — what is being contacted?

**Suggested rewrite (for this second occurrence):**
"...having generated diligence, one takes up the right method — and thereby achieves the as-yet-unachieved, touches the direct experience of what has remained out of reach, and realizes liberation."

## Section 28.7: Types of Attention

---

**FLAG R28-014**
**Severity:** High
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (UJ) / Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Lines 381–382 (The condition aspect)

> "**The condition aspect:** The prerequisites for samadhi must be accumulated: (1) being guided by the teaching; (2) accompanied by practice; (3) having keen aspiration; (4) generating deep disenchantment regarding what should be abandoned; (5) being able to discerningly and thoroughly know disturbance from non-disturbance; and (6) being untroubled by others, whether caused by humans or non-humans, by sounds or by activity. When vipashyana takes the lead, one internally concentrates the mind and, after extremely vigorous observation, attends to the condition aspect. When shamatha takes the lead, having aroused excellent observation, one calms the mind with extreme vigor and attends to the condition aspect."

Multiple problems converge here.

(a) "Being able to discerningly and thoroughly know disturbance from non-disturbance" is dense adverbial stacking ("discerningly and thoroughly know") without payoff. What is "disturbance from non-disturbance"? Disturbance of what? The meditation object? The mind?

(b) The final two sentences both end with "attends to the condition aspect" — the very thing being defined. This is circular. The reader learns that when either vipashyana or shamatha leads, one attends to the condition aspect — but not what the condition aspect actually *is* in practice.

(c) "After extremely vigorous observation" and "with extreme vigor" applied to calming are near-paradoxical to a reader without the technical context. Extreme vigor in calming? The text needs one orienting phrase.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"**The condition aspect** concerns the conditions that support samadhi: (1) being guided by a teacher; (2) having a consistent practice; (3) having keen aspiration; (4) generating genuine disenchantment with what should be abandoned; (5) being able to distinguish mental disturbance from mental stillness; and (6) being free from disruption by other people, sounds, or activity.

When vipashyana leads, one concentrates the mind internally, applies vigorous observation, and in this way attends to what conditions the samadhi. When shamatha leads, one first arouses discernment, then calms with equal vigor, attending to the same conditioned ground."

---

**FLAG R28-015**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM) / Register drift (RD)
**Location:** Lines 394–404 (Nine types of resolute confidence)

> "**Resolute confidence with luminosity:** Regarding the sign of light, one clarifies the mind, skillfully apprehends it, and has resolute confidence accompanied by luminosity. **Without luminosity:** One cannot skillfully apprehend the sign of light and has resolute confidence accompanied by dimness. **Dull:** The resolute confidence found in a person of dull faculties. **Quick:** The resolute confidence found in a person of sharp faculties. **Narrow:** Resolute confidence accompanied by narrow faith and aspiration, and resolute confidence directed at a narrow object of mental comprehension — because the attention is narrow and the object is narrow, it is called narrow. **Vast:** Resolute confidence accompanied by vast faith and aspiration, and directed at a vast object — because the attention is vast and the object is vast, it is called vast. **Immeasurable:** Resolute confidence accompanied by boundless and limitless faith and aspiration, directed at a boundless and limitless object — because the attention is immeasurable and the object is immeasurable, it is called immeasurable. **Pure:** Resolute confidence accompanied by what has been well cultivated, fully matured, and brought to completion. **Impure:** Resolute confidence accompanied by what has not been well cultivated, not fully matured, and not brought to completion."

This 150-word block delivers nine definitions in undifferentiated prose. The three middle definitions (Narrow/Vast/Immeasurable) each use the formula "because the attention is X and the object is X, it is called X" — three times verbatim. "Resolute confidence" appears fourteen times. The definitions for Pure and Impure use triple negative patterning ("not well cultivated, not fully matured, and not brought to completion") that is accurate but enervating.

A practitioner reading for penetration will either skip this section or go numb. The content — a ninefold taxonomy of meditation quality — is actually rich and practically useful. The format defeats the content.

**Suggested rewrite (structural recommendation):**
Present as a two-column table or as nine short numbered paragraphs, each beginning with the quality name (bold), followed by one compact definition sentence. The three middle items (Narrow/Vast/Immeasurable) can be compressed together: "Narrow, vast, and immeasurable resolute confidence correspond to faith and aspiration — and their objects — of those respective scopes."

**FLAG R28-016**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Line 349

> "After having attained the attention being cultivated, as one gradually advances through the worldly and supramundane paths, the attention to characteristics (*lakshana-pratisanvedana-manaskara*) is interspersed with samadhi and with reflection — one is not yet able to practice exclusively and purely."

## Section 28.8: Yogic Activities and Three Types of Yogin

---

**FLAG R28-017**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Sentences >50 words (LG) / Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Line 446

> "Furthermore, beginning from the time one first generates the aspiration to cultivate wholesome dharmas until one has not yet given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration (*nirvedha-bhagiya-kushala-mula*) — during that entire period, one is called a beginner. If one has already given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration — namely warmth (*ushmanas*), summit (*murdhan*), patience concordant with the truths (*satya-anulomika-kshanti*), and the supreme worldly dharma (*laukikagra-dharma*) — one is called practiced. If one has already entered the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*) and attained the direct realization of the truths, and is no longer dependent on others regarding the Buddha's noble teaching, nor can be led astray by other conditions — at that point, one is called one who has transcended attention."

Three sentences, ~130 words, six Sanskrit parentheticals. Each sentence is built as a Chinese-style conditional front-load ("Beginning from X until Y — during that period, one is called Z"). This is recognizable translated-ese. In English, a practitioner would expect the definition before the conditions: "A beginner is one who has not yet given rise to..." or "The practiced yogin has already..." The Chinese conditional structure inverts this.

**Suggested rewrite (structural):**
Break into three short paragraphs, each starting with the label:
"**The beginner** is anyone who has not yet given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration — that is, someone who has aspired to cultivate wholesome dharmas but has not yet reached the threshold stages of the path.
**The practiced yogin** has already achieved the four threshold stages: warmth, summit, patience, and the supreme worldly dharma.
**One who has transcended attention** has entered the certainty of correctness, directly realized the truths, and no longer depends on others regarding the noble teaching."

**FLAG R28-018**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Lines 416–418 (yogic activities)

> "Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object are primary to the cessation and transformation of the basis — through the power of thorough knowledge and delight, the basis ceases and transforms. Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object take the purification of the basis as primary — through the power of the purified basis, thorough knowledge of the object becomes well purified, and delight in the object becomes well purified, operating at the time of accomplishing the purpose (*krtyanushthana*)."

"Thorough knowledge and delight" repeated four times. "The basis ceases and transforms" / "the purified basis" / "well purified" appearing five times in two sentences, alternating as cause and effect. The relationship being described — that knowledge can purify the basis, and the purified basis in turn deepens knowledge — is a key insight about the bidirectional relationship between wisdom and concentration. But the repetition of the same nouns while the causal arrow reverses is confusing rather than illuminating.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"The relationship works in both directions. Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight come first: the practitioner's insight into the object is what loosens the heavy basis and transforms it toward pliancy. Sometimes the purified basis comes first: when the body-mind is already lightened through practice, knowledge and delight flow correspondingly into their perfected form, operating at the level of final accomplishment."

## Section 28.10: The Thirty-Seven Factors of Awakening

---

**FLAG R28-021**
**Severity:** High
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM)
**Location:** Lines 492–498 (The four right efforts)

> "**(1)** For the purpose of abandoning already-arisen evil and unwholesome dharmas, one generates aspiration, exerts oneself, arouses energetic effort, urges the mind, and sustains the mind. This is right effort.
>
> **(2)** For the purpose of preventing the arising of evil and unwholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen, one generates aspiration, exerts oneself, arouses energetic effort, urges the mind, and sustains the mind. This is right effort.
>
> **(3)** For the purpose of giving rise to wholesome dharmas that have not yet arisen, one generates aspiration, exerts oneself, arouses energetic effort, urges the mind, and sustains the mind. This is right effort.
>
> **(4)** For the purpose of causing already-arisen wholesome dharmas to abide, not to be forgotten, to be cultivated to perfection, to be extensively developed, to increase, and to expand, one generates aspiration, exerts oneself, arouses energetic effort, urges the mind, and sustains the mind. This is right effort."

This is the most severe formula monotony in the fascicle. The identical five-verb sequence — "generates aspiration, exerts oneself, arouses energetic effort, urges the mind, and sustains the mind" — is repeated verbatim four times in four consecutive paragraphs, each ending with the identical sentence "This is right effort." The first three purposes are also parallel in structure: "For the purpose of [verb phrase], one [identical action sequence]."

This pattern is authentic to the Chinese source (欲、勤、精進、發心、攝心), which legitimately repeats the formula for each of the four efforts. But the project's prose register guidelines explicitly prohibits this: "Formula monotony: same phrasing repeated verbatim 3+ times without variation. Never repeat the same formula translation 3+ times in succession."

The content is important — the four right efforts are a core practice instruction. But delivered as four identical paragraphs, the text communicates by volume rather than by meaning. A practitioner's eye glazes at the third repetition. The fourth is invisible.

**Suggested rewrite (NTC-style compression):**
"**The four right efforts** direct this same pattern of energy — generating aspiration, applying oneself, arousing energetic effort, urging and sustaining the mind — toward four distinct aims:

1. Abandoning evil and unwholesome dharmas already arisen.
2. Preventing unarisen evil and unwholesome dharmas from arising.
3. Giving rise to wholesome dharmas not yet present.
4. Sustaining and bringing to completion wholesome dharmas already established — maintaining them, developing them, and allowing them to increase and expand.

Each of these, carried out with that full exertion, is right effort."

**FLAG R28-022**
**Severity:** High
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM)
**Location:** Lines 502–505 (The four bases of supernatural power)

> "1. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of aspiration (*chanda*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 2. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of diligence (*virya*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 3. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of mind (*citta*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 4. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of investigation (*mimamsa*), endowed with formations of exertion"

"The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of X, endowed with formations of exertion" repeated four times verbatim. Each item is 20 words, distinguished only by the Sanskrit term for the varying element. "Endowed with formations of exertion" is also unexplained — "formations" (行) here is a technical Abhidharma term for the volitional formations that accompany the samadhi. "Formations of exertion" will be opaque to any reader who does not know this.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"**The four bases of supernatural power** are four types of samadhi, each distinguished by its animating force — aspiration (*chanda*), diligence (*virya*), mind (*citta*), and investigation (*mimamsa*) — and each accompanied by the volitional formations of sustained effort."

*Or, following the NTC compression model from design-invariants.md:*
"These four bases take their names from the force that predominates: the samadhi of aspiration, the samadhi of diligence, the samadhi of mind, and the samadhi of investigation — each endowed with the formations of sustained effort."

**FLAG R28-023**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM)
**Location:** Lines 507–513 (five faculties, five powers, seven factors, eightfold path)

> "**The five faculties:** (1) the faculty of faith, (2) the faculty of diligence, (3) the faculty of mindfulness, (4) the faculty of concentration, and (5) the faculty of prajna.
>
> **The five powers:** (1) the power of faith, (2) the power of diligence, (3) the power of mindfulness, (4) the power of concentration, and (5) the power of prajna."

The five faculties and five powers share identical members (faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, prajna), differing only in the label "faculty" vs. "power." Presenting them as two separate identical lists in immediate succession — with no note on their relationship — is more disorienting than informative. A reader naturally wonders: why are they listed twice? Are they different? The text gives no indication.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"**The five faculties and five powers** share the same five members: faith, diligence, mindfulness, concentration, and prajna. As faculties, these are the capacities that govern the stream of practice; as powers, they are these same qualities brought to the strength that makes them unshakeable."

*(This compression borrows from the pattern the text itself uses in fascicle 29's fuller treatment; here the distinction needs at least one bridging sentence.)*

**FLAG R28-024**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (UJ)
**Location:** Line 484 (thirty-seven factors heading)

> "What are the thirty-seven factors conducive to awakening? They are: the four foundations of mindfulness (*smrityupasthana*), the four right efforts (*samyak-pradhana*), the four bases of supernatural power (*riddhipada*), the five faculties (*indriya*), the five powers (*bala*), the seven factors of awakening (*bodhyanga*), and the eightfold noble path (*arya-ashtangika-marga*)."

"Bases of supernatural power" (*riddhipada*) is introduced with no gloss or orienting phrase. "Supernatural power" (*riddhi*) evokes psychic abilities and levitation. In this context it refers to the concentrated power of mind that "accomplishes" what practice is aimed at. Without this clarification, a practitioner unfamiliar with the term may spend cognitive effort wondering why magical powers are in the thirty-seven factors.

**Suggested rewrite:** Add "(the power of concentrated accomplishment)" after *riddhipada*, or add a footnote.

**FLAG R28-025**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Register drift (RD)
**Location:** Section 28.10 as a whole

Section 28.10 (lines 478–513) presents the complete thirty-seven factors of awakening as a bare enumeration: names only, no descriptions, no connective tissue, no indication of why these thirty-seven and not some other number. **Suggested rewrite (structural):**
Add a transitional sentence at the end of 28.10, before 28.11, signaling what happens next: "Of these seven groups, the four foundations of mindfulness will now be examined in detail, as they are the ground in which all the remaining factors are cultivated."

**FLAG R28-027**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Formula monotony (FM) / Sentences >50 words (LG)
**Location:** Lines 559–563 (hindrances and factors of awakening)

> "Furthermore, when hindrances are present internally, one is able to know for oneself: 'I have hindrances.' When hindrances are absent internally, one is able to know for oneself: 'I am free from hindrances.' When hindrances that had not yet arisen come to arise, one is also able to know this. When hindrances that had arisen come to be dispelled and cease, one is also able to know this."

The formula "one is able to know for oneself: [quoted speech]" is used four times in one paragraph, then the pattern repeats for fetters (line 561 — four more instances), then again for the seven factors of awakening (line 563). Across lines 559–563, the formula "one is able to know for oneself" or "one is also able to know this" appears at least twelve times. This is the most formulaic passage in the entire fascicle.

The Satipatthana Sutta itself uses this repetition deliberately as a meditation instruction — the repetition in the original Pali/Chinese is meant to be internalized as practice, not merely read. But in this translation register, the mechanical repetition overwhelms the reader before the teaching lands.

**Structural suggestion:**
Compress the fourfold formula (present/absent/arising/ceasing) to an introductory sentence, then cite it by reference for subsequent applications:

"When hindrances are present internally, the practitioner knows: 'I have hindrances.' When absent: 'I am free from hindrances.' And when they arise or cease, the practitioner knows this too. One applies this same fourfold awareness to fetters — of the eye, ear, nose, tongue, body, and mental faculty. One applies it also to each of the seven factors of awakening: present or absent, arising or ceasing, developed to completeness or not — in each case, one knows."

**FLAG R28-028**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Nested qualification (NQ)
**Location:** Lines 573–579 (third through sixth interpretations)

*Third interpretation (line 577):*
> "When one takes as object one's own internal form in the concentrated state, accompanied by pliancy — this is contemplation of the internal body. When one takes as object one's own internal form in the unconcentrated state, accompanied by coarseness — this is contemplation of the external body. When one takes as object the form of others, whether accompanied by pliancy or coarseness — this is contemplation of the internal-and-external body."

Each clause of each interpretation is structured as: "When one takes as object [noun phrase with multiple qualifiers] — this is [technical label]." This "When X — this is Y" structure is used eighteen times in the six interpretations (three clauses per interpretation × six interpretations). Applied six times consecutively, with only the qualifying phrase changing, the structure becomes entirely mechanical. By the fourth interpretation, the practitioner's mind has found a groove to slide through without absorbing the distinctions.

Furthermore, the extension "Similarly, for the feeling, mind, and dharmas that arise dependent on these three kinds of form, one should understand the three contemplations accordingly" appears five times (after interpretations 1–5), near-verbatim. Five repetitions of this closing sentence.

**Structural suggestion:**
Introduce the six interpretations with one framing sentence, then present each as a compact three-element entry (internal = X / external = Y / internal-and-external = Z). Consolidate the extension clause once at the very end: "In each case, the same three-fold structure extends to the feeling, mind, and dharmas arising from these forms."

**FLAG R28-029**
**Severity:** Moderate
**Category:** Register drift (RD)
**Location:** Lines 601–605 (four types of self-delusion and four dimensions of karma)

> "**The four types of ignorance regarding the self.** There is another rationale. Worldly beings, regarding the skandhas that are merely skandha-nature, merely dharma-nature, do not know them as they truly are. They falsely conceive a self that depends upon the body. Because this supposed self depends on the body, it experiences suffering and pleasure. The one who experiences suffering and pleasure becomes defiled and pure through dharmas and non-dharmas. In order to remove the delusion regarding what the self supposedly depends upon, the foundation of mindfulness of the body was established. In order to remove the delusion regarding what the self supposedly experiences, the foundation of mindfulness of feeling was established..."

This paragraph uses "the supposed self" and "the self supposedly X" four times, each time echoing the same "the delusion regarding what the self supposedly [verb phrase]" formula. This is appropriate for one occurrence (establishing the framing), but four consecutive instances in a paragraph make the teaching feel circular. The logic is: the four foundations counteract four types of misapprehension of self. That structure is clear. But the execution buries the simple clarity under repetitive qualification.

**Suggested rewrite (beginning only):**
"**The four types of self-delusion.** There is another rationale. Worldly beings, not knowing the skandhas as they truly are, construct a false self in four ways: they imagine the self depends on the body, that it experiences pleasure and pain, that it is itself a knowing mind, and that it becomes defiled or purified through dharmas. Against each of these four delusions, one foundation of mindfulness was established."

**FLAG R28-030**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Line 537 (feeling enumeration)

> "**What is feeling (*vedana*)?** It is: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling. Pleasant bodily feeling, painful bodily feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant bodily feeling. As with bodily feeling, so too with mental feeling. Pleasant feeling with relish, painful feeling with relish, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling with relish. Feeling without relish likewise. Feeling based on indulgence likewise. Pleasant feeling based on renunciation, painful feeling based on renunciation, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling based on renunciation. Thus there are altogether twenty-one kinds of feeling, or nine kinds of feeling."

"Feeling without relish likewise. Feeling based on indulgence likewise." The two "likewise" sentences are compressed ellipses that a reader unfamiliar with the system cannot reconstruct. "Pleasant feeling without relish, painful feeling without relish, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling without relish" — that is three more types, but "Feeling without relish likewise" reduces them to nothing. And "Feeling based on indulgence likewise" presupposes the reader can mentally expand the full triplicate. This compression prioritizes brevity over comprehension.

**Suggested rewrite:**
"It is: pleasant feeling, painful feeling, and neither-painful-nor-pleasant feeling — three basic types. These three occur as bodily feeling (associated with the five sense-consciousnesses) or mental feeling (associated with the mental consciousness), giving six. Each of these six occurs either with relish (belonging to the realms of conditioned existence) or without relish (according with nirvana), giving twelve. Those without relish may be based on renunciation or on indulgence, and so on — altogether twenty-one kinds of feeling, which may also be grouped as nine."

**FLAG R28-031**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (UJ)
**Location:** Line 545

> "...pleasant feeling is feeling arising from contact conducive to pleasant feeling, included in the category of balanced feeling."

"Balanced feeling" / "unbalanced feeling" / "neither-balanced-nor-unbalanced feeling" — these are technical renderings of Abhidharma categories that do not have intuitive English equivalents. "Balanced" in ordinary English means proportionate or even. A practitioner would not understand why pleasant feeling is "balanced" or why painful feeling is "unbalanced." ## Cross-Sectional Issues

---

**FLAG R28-CROSS-01**
**Severity:** High
**Category:** Register drift (RD)
**Location:** Section 28.11 as a whole

Section 28.11 occupies roughly half the fascicle. Its content — the four foundations of mindfulness — is the most practice-relevant material in fascicle 28 and among the most important teachings in the entire Sravakabhumi. A practitioner coming to this section hoping to understand how to practice mindfulness of body, feeling, mind, and dharmas will encounter:

- A 35-item taxonomy of "body" (line 521)
- Threefold classification of contemplation (line 523)
- Threefold function of mindfulness (lines 527–533)
- 21 kinds of feeling (line 537)
- 20 kinds of mind (line 539)
- 20 kinds of dharmas (line 541)
- Extended analysis of each feeling and mind type (lines 545–555)
- Fourfold awareness formula repeated for hindrances, fetters, and seven factors (lines 559–563)
- Six interpretive frameworks for internal/external/internal-and-external (lines 573–583)
- Four inversions (lines 589–597)
- Four types of self-delusion (lines 601)
- Four dimensions of karma (lines 603)
- Four dimensions of defilement/purification (lines 605)
- Three meanings of "establishment of mindfulness" (lines 611–619)

This is an extraordinary amount of taxonomic material with virtually no practice instruction. The Yogacara-Abhidharma character of the Sravakabhumi dictates this approach — the text is a philosophical analysis, not a meditation manual. But a reader expecting dharma teaching will feel they have been handed a filing system.

The register is consistently that of Abhidharma scholasticism — precise, thorough, analytical — without the warm practical voice found elsewhere in the fascicle (e.g., the four inversions passage, which is excellent). The section as a whole reads as a "scholarly catalog" (the RD flag criterion) rather than "living text a practitioner could sit with."

**Structural recommendation:**
Add a brief contextualizing paragraph at the top of 28.11, before the body analysis, acknowledging the nature of what follows: "What follows is the Sravakabhumi's characteristically analytical treatment: rather than giving instructions, it maps the territory — defining precisely what 'body,' 'feeling,' 'mind,' and 'dharmas' comprise, and what 'mindfulness' and 'establishment of mindfulness' actually mean. The practitioner is given a map before the journey." This framing will not change the content but will orient the reader and prevent the glazing that comes from receiving taxonomy without context.

**FLAG R28-CROSS-02**
**Severity:** Low
**Category:** Translated-ese (TE)
**Location:** Throughout (repeated phrase)

"One should know" appears approximately fourteen times across the fascicle. In the Chinese this is the ubiquitous formula 當知 (literally "one should know"). Each instance is faithful, but accumulated across a fascicle, the phrase becomes a verbal tic that a practitioner begins to skip. Some instances genuinely signal a key structural observation (e.g., line 555: "These fourteen kinds of mind should all be understood as arising during the meditative state"). Others are merely transitional filler.

**Recommendation:** Review each instance. Where it precedes a genuinely important structural observation, retain it. Where it is purely formulaic filler before a sentence that stands on its own, delete it.
