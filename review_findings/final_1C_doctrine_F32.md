## 1. Five Signs Framework (32.1-32.4)

### 32.1 Framework Introduction

The five signs (五種相) are correctly identified: (1) disenchantment (厭離相), (2) delight (欣樂相), (3) danger (過患相), (4) luminosity (光明相), (5) discerning the object (了別事相). The numbering, naming, and enumeration match the Chinese exactly.

The footnote [^five_signs] states: "The first two signs -- disenchantment and delight -- correspond to the disciplining and moistening attentions of section 31.11." This is **doctrinally accurate and well-observed**. Section 31.11 defines four types of attention: (1) disciplining (調練, turning the mind from the disagreeable), (2) moistening (滋潤, delighting it with the admirable), (3) pliancy-generating, and (4) wisdom-purifying. The mapping of sign 1 to attention 1 and sign 2 to attention 2 is explicitly confirmed at T30.0460b01: 厭離相故調練其心...欣樂相故滋潤其心.

The three modes of apprehension -- seeing (見), hearing (聞), inference (心比度) -- are correctly rendered.

**No issues.**

### 32.2 Sign of Disenchantment

The six types of decline (衰損) are correctly enumerated in the footnote: health, life, kin, wealth, discipline, right view. The Chinese text at T30.0459c01 lists: 病衰損, 壽命衰損, 眷屬衰損, 財寶衰損, 淨戒衰損, 正見衰損. The translation correctly states the last two are gravest because they "lead to evil destinies" -- confirmed by the Chinese: 於現法中住諸苦惱於當來世往諸惡趣.

The teaching on impermanence of prosperity is doctrinally sound: prosperity is subject to decline, and decline makes renewed prosperity difficult. This matches standard Buddhist soteriology (e.g., the reflection on the rarity and preciousness of human birth).

The closing aspiration at T30.0460a01 is rendered faithfully: the practitioner resolves that through recognizing the unreliability of samsaric conditions, they will sincerely delight in abandonment. The practitioner test passes -- a meditator following this instruction would correctly generate disenchantment as the first stage of practice.

**No issues.**

### 32.3 Sign of Delight

The progressive sequence pure discipline -> non-regret -> gladness -> joy is correctly presented and the footnote [^non_regret_sequence] accurately identifies the Pali parallels (AN 10.1, 11.1). The Yogacara rendering distinguishes:

- 歡悅 (gladness, *pramodya*) -- from clean conscience
- 喜悅 (joy, *priti*) -- from confidence in capacity
- 悅意 (delight) -- the general category encompassing both

This is **correctly handled** in both translation and footnote. The distinction between gladness-from-conscience and joy-from-confidence is a genuinely subtle doctrinal point and the translation preserves it clearly.

The examination of one's own discipline (*shila*) and the instruction to resolve never again to violate the training rules -- followed by the "by the very nature of things" (法爾) causation -- is correct Yogacara phenomenology. The Chinese 然其法爾 is rendered "by the very nature of things," which accurately captures the *dharmata* principle: these mental states arise not through deliberate intent but through the natural causal order.

The closing summary ("Two qualities of pleasant mind emerge here...") correctly synthesizes the Chinese 如是行相諸適悅意先名歡悅今名喜悅總名悅意.

**No issues.**

### 32.4 Sign of Danger and Entry into Shamatha

**Finding D1 (m -- minor).** The footnote [^three_disturbances] states: "secondary afflictions (隨煩惱, *upaklesha*) to the five beginning with sensual desire (*kamacchanda*) -- i.e., the five hindrances." The identification of the five 隨煩惱 beginning with 貪欲 as the five hindrances (*nivarana*) is **standard and correct**. The five hindrances in standard Sarvastivada/Yogacara enumeration are: (1) kamacchanda (sensual desire), (2) vyapada (ill will), (3) styana-middha (sloth-torpor), (4) auddhatya-kaukritya (restlessness-remorse), (5) vicikitsa (doubt). The Chinese 貪欲等五 (five beginning with greed-desire) matches. However, the footnote uses the Pali term *kamacchanda* rather than the Sanskrit *kama-cchanda* or simply *kamacchanda* in its Sanskritized form. This is a **style inconsistency** rather than a doctrinal error -- the project convention (per terminology.md) requires Sanskrit forms. The standard Sanskrit is identical in spelling here (*kamacchanda*), so this is barely notable, but worth flagging for consistency since the project watchlist explicitly flags Pali forms.

**The six perceptions (六想/六種行).** The three pairs are correctly identified: (1) signlessness + non-activity, (2) non-discrimination + freedom from longing/agitation, (3) quiescence + peaceful bliss. This matches the Chinese exactly (T30.0460b01): 一無相想, 二於無相中無作用想, 三無分別想, 四於無分別中無所思慕無躁擾想, 五寂靜想, 六於寂靜中離諸燒惱寂滅樂想. The pairing structure (each "outer" perception deepening into its "inner" quality) is correctly represented.

**The nine stages allusion.** Footnote [^nine_stages_ref] identifies 令心住內住等住 as alluding to the first three of the nine stages of mental abiding. This is **correct**: 住 = *sthapana* (placing), 內住 = *samsthapana* (continued placing/settling), 等住 = *avasthapana* (re-placing/making even). The cross-reference to section 30.10 is appropriate.

**No doctrinal errors in 32.4.**

## 2. The Oil-Bowl Simile (32.6)

The simile is faithfully rendered. Cross-checking against the Chinese (T30.0461a01):

- 平滿鉢油 = "bowl brimming with oil" -- correct
- 魁膾露拔利劍 = "executioner... with a drawn sword" -- correct
- 眾善 = "fine crowd" -- the translation renders this as "a fine crowd" with the explanation "the prime and beautiful forms." The Chinese 盛壯美色 means "flourishing, vigorous, beautiful appearances," which the translation captures well.
- 大等生等 = "great beings" -- this is a compressed reference. The Chinese 大等生 is somewhat opaque but refers to prominent persons in the crowd. The translation's "great beings" is adequate.

The footnote [^oil_bowl] correctly identifies the Pali parallel at SN 47.20. The doctrinal mapping -- the bowl as the settled mind, the executioner as recognition of danger -- is sound.

**Finding D2 (m -- minor).** The footnote states the simile "appears in the Pali canon at SN 47.20 (the *Sedaka Sutta* group)." The oil-bowl simile actually appears at SN 47.20, which is called the *Sedaka Sutta* in some editions, but the simile itself is sometimes identified as the *Janapada-kalyani Sutta* within the Sedaka vagga. This is a **very minor imprecision** in the footnote's parenthetical -- the sutta reference number SN 47.20 is correct, but calling it "the Sedaka Sutta group" rather than naming it more precisely is slightly loose. Not a doctrinal error, just a citation refinement.

**Practitioner test: PASS.** The simile is correctly decoded and would not mislead a practitioner. The point -- that genuine understanding of danger concentrates attention naturally -- is well communicated.

## 3. Alternating Shamatha and Vipashyana (32.7)

### Simile Decoding

The point-by-point mapping at 32.7 is **comprehensive and correct**:

| Simile Element | Decoded Meaning | Chinese | Correct? |
|---|---|---|---|
| Fine crowd | Conditions fueling desire-entanglement | 能隨順貪欲纏等隨煩惱法 | Yes |
| Singing/dancing | Conditions fueling discursive thoughts | 能隨順尋思戲論躁擾處法 | Yes |
| Great beings | Ten signs beginning with form | 色相等十種相法 | Yes |
| Wise man | Yoga teacher | 瑜伽師 | Yes |
| Bowl of oil | Mind settled in shamatha | 奢摩他所安住心 | Yes |
| Executioner | Sign of danger | 先所取...過患相 | Yes |
| Guarding against a drop | Shamatha path with discernment | 審諦周遍了知亂不亂相之所攝受奢摩他道 | Yes |

The translation adds that the bowl represents the mind "generating physical and mental pliancy and moistness" (能令身心輕安潤澤). This is present in the Chinese and doctrinally significant -- the settled shamatha mind is not merely concentrated but produces the somatic and mental qualities of pliancy (*prashrabdhi*).

### The Alternation Instruction

**Finding D3 (O -- observation, no error).** The instruction to alternate between shamatha and vipashyana when shamatha becomes "forced and strained" (極大艱辛勵力策發) is a **critical practical teaching** and is rendered correctly. The Chinese specifies: 還應速疾出無分別所緣境相於有分別所緣境相繫念在前 -- "quickly emerge from the non-discriminating object and establish mindfulness on a formed, discriminating object." The translation captures this accurately.

The cross-reference to section 31.11 (four types of attention, the goldsmith simile) is **doctrinally sound**. Section 31.11 describes the practitioner alternating between disciplining/moistening attentions and between shamatha/vipashyana, and the goldsmith simile illustrates this alternation directly. Section 32.7 applies this principle specifically to the beginner's practice.

The footnote [^alternation] correctly identifies the sign-following mode of vipashyana (隨相行毘鉢舍那) as the first of four vipashyana modes defined in 31.7.

**Practitioner test: PASS.** The instruction is clear: if formless shamatha becomes forced, return to formed-object vipashyana (contemplating impurity signs). This matches standard Yogacara pedagogy and would not lead to incorrect practice.

## 4. Graduated Contemplation (32.8)

### Progression from One to Limitless

The graduated expansion is correctly rendered:
- Begin with a single livid corpse
- When practice grows "clear and bright" (明淨), expand numerically: 2, 3, 4, 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 100, 1000
- Expand spatially: all cardinal and intermediate directions
- Fill completely: "not even a space the size of a staff-tip" (一杖端處)

This matches the Chinese exactly and is standard Yogacara *adhimoksha-manaskara* methodology.

### Transition to Real Contemplation

**Finding D4 (m -- minor).** The footnote [^two_attentions] explains imaginative attention (*adhimoksha-manaskara*) and real contemplation (*zhenshi zuoyi*, 真實作意) as "the two main phases of meditation in Yogacara practice." This is **slightly overstated**. The Yogacara system actually recognizes seven types of attention (七作意), not just two. However, these two are indeed the two most prominent in the beginner's practice as presented in this section. A more precise statement would be "two primary modes of attention in the beginner's contemplative practice" rather than "the two main phases of meditation in Yogacara practice." This is a **minor imprecision** that could mislead a scholar but would not affect a practitioner.

### Alternation within Graduated Practice

The instruction to alternate shamatha and vipashyana at every level -- "from each individual asubha stage and each level of spatial expansion" -- is correctly rendered. The five qualities marking natural ease are listed: joyful (喜樂), clear (明), bright (淨), undisturbed (無諸擾惱), effortless (不強勵力). The footnote [^alternation_detail] correctly identifies these five.

### Quiescence Closing

**Finding D5 (O -- observation).** The closing instruction at the end of 32.8 is doctrinally precise and the translation handles it well: "withdraw the manifold signs but do not fully abandon the formed object" (方便除遣安置眾相不顯現中不全棄捨有分別相). The footnote [^quiescence_close] correctly notes this intermediate state -- neither pure formless shamatha nor active vipashyana. This matches the Yogacara understanding of a transitional state between the two modes, and the footnote's cross-reference to the six perceptions of 32.4 is apt.

**The Mount Vipula reference.** Footnote [^vipula] correctly identifies 脅山 as Mount Vipula (*Vipula*) near Rajagriha and notes the SN 15.10 parallel. The footnote accurately distinguishes the Sravakabhumi's application (all corpses collectively) from the SN passage (a single person's bones). **No error.**

**Practitioner test: PASS.** The graduated contemplation is clearly described and the alternation principle is well communicated. A practitioner following this instruction would correctly (a) begin with a single object, (b) expand gradually, (c) transition between imaginative and real contemplation, and (d) alternate shamatha and vipashyana throughout.

## 5. Light-Perception (32.9)

**This is the section requiring most careful scrutiny.**

### The Four Luminous Qualities

The translation lists: (1) accompanied by luminosity (光明俱心), (2) accompanied by illumination (照了俱心), (3) accompanied by clarity and brightness (明淨俱心), (4) free from darkness (無闇俱心). The footnote [^luminosity_applied] states these "describe aspects of the same quality rather than stages."

**Finding D6 (m -- minor).** The claim that these four terms are "aspects of the same quality rather than stages" is a **reasonable interpretation but could be debated**. The Visuddhimagga's treatment of the *aloka-kasina* and *obhasa* (luminosity) distinguishes between the kasina practice as a concentration device and the *aloka-sanna* (perception of light) as a factor that brightens the meditation object. The Sravakabhumi's treatment here is closer to the latter -- luminosity as a quality pervading practice rather than a separate kasina. The footnote's interpretation is **defensible and probably correct for this text**, but the parenthetical "rather than stages" makes a stronger claim than the Chinese text warrants. The Chinese simply lists four terms in sequence. One could read them as a progressive intensification: luminosity -> illumination -> clarity-brightness -> freedom from darkness. Neither reading produces a doctrinal error, but the footnote's firm "rather than stages" might overclaim.

### Clarity of Initial Apprehension

The practical principle -- that a clear initial apprehension propagates clarity while a murky one propagates murkiness -- is **doctrinally important and correctly rendered**. The Chinese at T30.0462a01 is unambiguous: 若有最初於所緣境多不分明...後所修習所有勝解亦不分明; 若有最初於所緣境多分分明...後所修習轉復分明.

This matches Kamalashila's teaching in *Stages of Meditation* Ch. 8 that the initial sign (*nimitta*) must be clearly apprehended for subsequent practice to develop well. The Visuddhimagga (IV.31) similarly emphasizes that the learning sign (*uggaha-nimitta*) must be clearly grasped before the counterpart sign (*patibhaga-nimitta*) can arise. The principle is universal across traditions.

**Practitioner test: PASS.** The instruction is clear and practically correct -- the meditator is advised to invest effort in the initial clarity of the sign. This is sound advice and matches all major meditation manuals.

### The Five-Sign Recapitulation

**Finding D7 (M -- moderate).** Footnote [^five_recap] discusses the recapitulation of the five signs, noting that the original five (disenchantment, delight, danger, luminosity, discerning the object) are here restated as (disenchantment, delight, shamatha, vipashyana, luminosity). The footnote states: "The text appears to reconceptualize the five signs within the shamatha-vipashyana framework now operative in the beginner's practice, rather than offering a strict one-to-one substitution between the two lists."

Checking the Chinese at T30.0462a01: 善取如是厭離相故善取如是欣樂相故善取如是奢摩他相故善取如是毘鉢舍那相故善取如是光明相故. This lists: disenchantment, delight, shamatha, vipashyana, luminosity -- five items.

The footnote's observation is **doctrinally important but needs more precision**. The original signs 3, 4, 5 were: danger (過患相), luminosity (光明相), discerning the object (了別事相). The recapitulation replaces them with: shamatha (奢摩他相), vipashyana (毘鉢舍那相), luminosity (光明相). The mapping is:

- Danger (過患相) -> this was already described as the entry point for shamatha in 32.4, so "shamatha sign" subsumes and transforms it
- Discerning the object (了別事相) -> this corresponds to vipashyana's discriminating activity
- Luminosity (光明相) remains but moves from 4th to 5th position

The footnote's comment that this is "a reconceptualization rather than a strict substitution" is **correct** but could be more explicit about the doctrinal logic: danger leads to shamatha, discernment leads to vipashyana, and luminosity pervades both. This transformation from "preparatory" signs (danger, luminosity, discernment) to "operative" signs (shamatha, vipashyana, luminosity) represents the beginner's progression from the instructional framework to actual practice. The footnote could state this more explicitly to prevent reader confusion.

## 6. Four Foundations via Asubha (32.10)

### Body Contemplation -- Threefold

The threefold body contemplation is correctly rendered:
1. **Internal** (於內身): own 36 bodily substances (from 32.9)
2. **External** (於外身): asubha stages of others' corpses -- limited, extensive, limitless
3. **Internal-external** (於內外身): imagining one's own body through death and decomposition

The footnote [^body_threefold] correctly identifies the third mode as "striking" -- the practitioner imagines their own body being carried to the charnel ground. This is **doctrinally accurate** and matches the Chinese exactly: 於自所愛汝當發起如是勝解復於死已出送塚間.

### Feeling, Mind, and Dharmas -- The Three Divisions

**Finding D8 (M -- moderate).** The mapping of the three divisions to the three satipatthana modes is a crucial interpretive choice. The translation and footnote [^formless_aggregates] state:

- Shamatha division = internal contemplation
- Non-distraction division = external contemplation
- Vipashyana division = internal-external contemplation

Checking against the Chinese at T30.0462b01-b15:
- 於奢摩他品...如是名為於內受心法修循受心法觀 -- shamatha = internal. **Correct.**
- 於無散亂品...如是名為於外受心法修循受心法觀 -- non-distraction = external. **Correct.**
- 於毘鉢舍那品...如是名為於內外受心法修循受心法觀 -- vipashyana = internal-external. **Correct.**

The mapping is faithful to the source. However, the **doctrinal explanation of why "external" refers to past disturbed states rather than others' mental states** requires careful attention. The footnote [^external_formless] provides this explanation and is **substantially correct**:

> "External" refers not to others' mental states but to one's own past disturbed states... These are "external" because they belong to the unfixed state outside the practitioner's current meditative abiding.

This is the correct Yogacara interpretation. The "non-distraction division" (無散亂品) explicitly deals with states that arose when the practitioner "apprehended various objects and fell into the unfixed state" (取諸境界緣諸境界墮不定地). These are "external" to the current meditative state, not external in the sense of "belonging to another person."

The footnote correctly notes this diverges from the Pali commentarial understanding (which takes "external" as another person's phenomena). This comparative note is **accurate and valuable for scholars**.

**Finding D9 (m -- minor).** The translation of 誑幻所作 as "the work of deception and illusion" is adequate but could be slightly more precise. 誑 = deception/delusion, 幻 = illusion/magical creation. The phrase suggests these disturbed states are like a magician's illusion -- vivid but without substance. The translation captures the meaning; the footnote helpfully adds "vivid but insubstantial." No doctrinal error, just a note that "the work of deception and illusion" might be more effectively rendered as "wrought by deception and illusion" or "products of magical illusion" to capture the *maya*-like quality more precisely.

### Moment-by-Moment Impermanence

In both the shamatha and vipashyana divisions, the contemplation of the four formless skandhas includes the reflection: "moment by moment continuously different -- only new, never the old, flowing in succession" (剎那剎那展轉別異唯是新新而非故故相續流轉).

This is **doctrinally correct** -- it represents the standard Abhidharma/Yogacara teaching of momentary arising and ceasing (*ksanika-vada*). The practitioner contemplates the radical impermanence of their own mental states within meditation. This is sound and matches the Visuddhimagga's treatment of impermanence contemplation (Ch. XX) where the meditator sees formations arising and passing away moment by moment.

**Practitioner test: PASS.** The instruction is clear: within meditation, observe the impermanence of one's own mental states -- whether settled (shamatha) or disturbed (external) or investigative (vipashyana). The three-fold framework gives the practitioner a complete map of mental states to contemplate.

## Overall Assessment

The translation of sections 32.1-32.10 is **doctrinally sound**. No critical errors were found. The five-signs framework is correctly identified and coherently presented. The oil-bowl simile is faithfully decoded. The shamatha-vipashyana alternation matches the theoretical framework established in fascicle 31. The graduated contemplation progression is clear and complete. The light-perception teaching is handled carefully and the practical instruction about initial clarity is valuable. The four foundations via asubha correctly applies the satipatthana framework through the Yogacara lens.

The two moderate findings (D7, D8) concern footnotes that would benefit from fuller doctrinal explanation -- the five-sign recapitulation logic and the "external" reinterpretation for formless skandhas. Neither affects the translation text itself, only the scholarly apparatus.

**Every passage giving meditation instruction would lead to correct practice if followed as written.** The practitioner test passes across all ten sections.
