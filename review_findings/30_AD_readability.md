# Readability Review — Fascicle 30, Sections 30.1–30.4
**Agent R (Readability) | 2026-03-01**
**Register standard: Nanamoli/Bodhi/Lamrim Chenmo TC**

---

## Overall Assessment

Sections 30.1–30.4 are short but represent three distinct register challenges: a transitional framing passage (30.1), a procedural description (30.2), a rhetorical teacher's speech (30.3), and a catechetical inquiry section (30.4). The teacher's speech (30.3) is the strongest passage — the parallel rhetoric works and carries emotional charge. The procedural passages (30.2, 30.4) have significant translated-ese and formula issues that flatten the text.

**Severity counts: H×3, M×5, L×3**

---

## Issue Log

---

### [30.1] Transition and Summary Verse

---

**[R-01] | M | Translated-ese — awkward subordinate chain**

> "Thus has been explained the establishment of person types, the objects of meditation and so forth, up through fruitful and fruitless practice."

"And so forth, up through" is a phrase assembled from Chinese connective structure, not English. "The objects of meditation and so forth" is vague and a little clumsy as a participial summary. The sentence reads as a list of topics being ticked off, not as a genuine transition.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The preceding section has covered person types, the objects of meditation, and the range of practice — from its fruits down to its failures."

Or more cleanly:
> "The preceding chapters have established the person types, the objects of meditation, and the full range of practice — fruitful and fruitless alike."

---

**[R-02] | L | Unexplained jargon — "practitioner"**

> "How to properly establish the practitioner in these — I shall now explain."

"The practitioner" appears without antecedent. Up to this point in the sentence, the reader has only encountered abstractions (person types, objects). Who is being established? The student who has just been described? A new figure? The sentence introduces a topic shift but leaves the subject undefined.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "How the teacher properly establishes the student in all of this — that I will now explain."

(This also aligns better with the content of 30.2–30.4, which is about the student-teacher relationship.)

---

### [30.2] Approaching the Teacher

---

**[R-03] | H | Nested qualification / translated-ese — opening sentence**

> "When a person of self-regard who is a beginner first undertakes practice, seeking to attain their own true benefit, they should first establish mindfulness in four areas and then approach a teacher skilled in yoga — whether their preceptor (*acharya*), their instructor (*upadhyaya*), or another venerable or semi-venerable one."

This is a 55-word sentence with the main verb ("should approach") buried under two opening participial phrases, a mid-sentence qualification dash, and a tripartite appositive. It reads as if each Chinese qualifier has been attached in sequence rather than absorbed and rewritten.

"A person of self-regard who is a beginner" is a particularly awkward phrase — "self-regard" is not a natural English collocation here; it sounds slightly pejorative. The intended meaning is probably "one who is concerned with their own liberation" or "one who seeks their own welfare."

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "A beginner who seeks their own genuine benefit — someone newly undertaking practice — should first settle the mind in four areas of mindfulness before approaching a teacher skilled in yoga. That teacher may be their preceptor (*acharya*), their instructor (*upadhyaya*), or another respected senior."

---

**[R-04] | M | Register drift — "The Four Areas of Mindfulness" subheading**

The subheading "The Four Areas of Mindfulness" is not in the Chinese (by this project's design conventions). Here it creates a documentary/academic break that interrupts the flow of a procedural passage. The four items are introduced immediately with "What are the four areas?" — the Q&A format is already doing the structural work. The subheading is redundant and drains energy from the passage.

**Suggested rewrite:** Remove the subheading. Let "What are the four areas?" open the paragraph directly.

---

**[R-05] | H | Formula monotony — the four mindfulness areas**

> "(1) Seeking solely to understand, without any intention to challenge or confront. (2) Generating deep respect, without any mind of arrogance. (3) Seeking only what is excellent and wholesome, not displaying one's own abilities. (4) Established purely in the wish to cultivate roots of virtue for oneself and others, not seeking material support, honor, or fame."

All four items follow the same two-clause structure: positive aim + negative qualifier. This is mechanically rendered from the Chinese. In NTC prose, a list like this would vary the syntax — perhaps one item as a gerund, one as a noun phrase, one as a full clause — so the parallel structure emphasizes the content rather than the Chinese template.

Additionally, items (1) and (3) are semantically very close ("seeking to understand / not challenging" and "seeking what is wholesome / not displaying abilities") and would benefit from either compression or differentiation.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> (1) Coming only to understand, with no intention to dispute or contradict. (2) Approaching with deep respect and without arrogance. (3) Keeping attention on what is excellent and wholesome, not on demonstrating one's own capacities. (4) Motivated purely by the wish to cultivate virtue for oneself and others — not by desire for support, honor, or renown.

*(This varies gerund → prepositional phrase → participial phrase → noun clause, which breaks the mechanical uniformity.)*

---

**[R-06] | M | Register drift / translated-ese — protocol paragraph**

> "Having arrived at the teacher's place with this mindfulness, one first requests permission and asks about the appropriate time. Then, in a composed manner, one personally presents one's request. At the moment of asking, one bares the left shoulder and places the right knee on the ground — or takes a lower seat and sits with body bowed. Joining the palms in respect, with deep humility and reverence, lowered gaze and gentle voice, one requests instruction in yoga..."

The procedural detail here is fine and the content is vivid. But "one personally presents one's request" is weak and redundant after "one first requests permission." "One personally presents" is translated-ese — in English one would just say "makes one's request" or "presents oneself." Also, "sits with body bowed" is odd; "sits with a bow" or "bows in one's seat" is cleaner.

The final clause — "one requests instruction in yoga: 'I wish to receive training...'" — is a little curt. The transition into the quoted speech could be smoother.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> Having arrived at the teacher's dwelling with this mindfulness, one first asks permission and inquires about a suitable time. Then, composedly, one makes one's request. At the moment of asking, one bares the left shoulder, places the right knee on the ground — or takes a lower seat and bows — and, joining the palms with deep reverence, gaze lowered and voice gentle, one speaks: "I wish to receive training in this yogic practice. Out of compassion, please explain it to me."

---

**[R-07] | L | Register drift — "The Protocol for Requesting Instruction" subheading**

Same issue as R-04. The subheading is editorial scaffolding that interrupts a flowing procedural description. The paragraph manages itself. Remove it.

### [30.3] The Teacher's Encouragement

The teacher's speech is the strongest passage in these sections. The parallel anaphora ("Among beings who... you alone...") is well handled and emotionally alive. The following are minor issues.

---

**[R-08] | M | Sentence over 50 words — the "Elder, know this" passage**

> "Elder, know this: if you can truly be so diligent in yogic practice, then you will deserve the name of one who does not waste the faithful offerings of the people; who genuinely carries out the Tathagata's holy teaching without abandoning it; who accomplishes dhyana and grows in superior insight; who delights in dwelling in solitude — a true companion in the Dharma, diligent in studying the yoga of one's own benefit, bringing no disgrace upon the wise companions in the pure life."

Word count: approximately 89 words. The relative-clause chain ("who does not... who genuinely... who accomplishes... who delights...") is mechanically accumulated and begins to feel like a bureaucratic list rather than a climactic affirmation. The final appositive (after the em-dash) is tangled.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Elder, know this: if you practice with such diligence, you will deserve to be called one who honors the faithful offerings of the people, who carries out the Tathagata's teaching without abandoning it, who accomplishes dhyana and deepens insight, who delights in solitude — a true companion in the Dharma who brings no disgrace upon those who share the pure life."

*(Shortened to ~66 words. The list still has rhythm but is compressed.)*

---

**[R-09] | H | Nested qualification / translated-ese — closing teacher speech paragraph**

> "You have come to ask because you wish to diligently practice the right conduct that benefits both yourself and others, to bring benefit and happiness to immeasurable beings, and out of compassion for the world — for gods, humans, asuras, and all the rest — so that they may attain benefit and happiness."

The phrase "out of compassion for the world — for gods, humans, asuras, and all the rest — so that they may attain benefit and happiness" is added inside an em-dash interruption after two infinitive phrases have already closed the sentence. The result is a run-on that loses coherence. The main verb is "you have come to ask because you wish to practice," but the sentence keeps adding qualifications until the closing phrase "so that they may attain benefit and happiness" feels disconnected from the opening.

Additionally, "diligently practice the right conduct that benefits both yourself and others" is translated-ese for what should be more naturally expressed.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "You have come to ask out of a wish to practice rightly — for your own benefit and for others'. You come with compassion for the world: for gods, humans, asuras, and all beings. May they all attain happiness and be free from harm."

*(Breaking this into three sentences clarifies the three distinct motivations and gives the close appropriate weight.)*

### [30.4] The Four Inquiry Points

---

**[R-10] | M | Unexplained jargon — "right view" without context**

> "From the very beginning of your pure conduct, have you purified your moral discipline (*shila*) and established right view?"

"Right view" (*samyag-drsti*) appears without gloss or context. In other Buddhist texts aimed at practitioners, this is generally self-explanatory. However, in this passage, the question immediately following it ("Have you heard and retained... the dharma of the noble truths...") effectively defines what right view means here — hearing and retaining the four noble truths. The sequence is slightly inverted for a reader who doesn't already know the term.

This is a low-stakes issue in context, but consider either:
- Adding a light parenthetical: "established right view (a correct understanding of the path)"
- Or reordering so the noble-truths question precedes this one.

**Suggested note:** L severity; doctrinal ordering is for Agent D to assess.

---

**[R-11] | L | Translated-ese — "heard and retained"**

> "Have you heard and retained, whether in brief or in detail, the dharma of the noble truths — their general presentation and their detailed analysis?"

"Heard and retained" is a calque of 聞持 (hearing and holding/maintaining). It is a recognizable Dharma term but "retained" is awkward in English — one retains a lawyer, not a teaching. "Heard and kept in mind" or "learned and remembered" or simply "received and studied" would be more natural English.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "Have you received and studied the dharma of the noble truths — whether in brief summary or in detailed analysis?"

---

**[R-12] | M | Formula monotony / register drift — the four questions**

All four questions in 30.4 follow the same syntactic pattern: "Have you [done X]?" with a qualification appended. This is appropriate for a catechism, and the parallel form is actually defensible here — it should feel like a formal inquiry. However, the fourth question is slightly tangled:

> "Do you possess deep faith and understanding regarding nirvana — have you gone forth for the sake of realizing cessation?"

This is actually *two questions* joined by an em-dash, which breaks the consistent four-question format and reads as an editing artifact. Either merge them into one question or separate them properly.

**Suggested rewrite (merged):**
> "Do you hold deep faith in nirvana and have you gone forth with the aim of realizing cessation?"

Or (kept as formal parallel with the others):
> "Have you gone forth with genuine faith in nirvana — with the aim of realizing cessation?"
