### MAJOR (Severity 2)

**F-2.1: Perception skandha -- subtle rendering choice for 無諸所有無所有處想**
- **Chinese:** 無量想無諸所有無所有處想
- **English:** "immeasurable perception and the perception of the base of nothingness"
- **Analysis:** The Chinese 無諸所有無所有處想 is rendered as "the perception of the base of nothingness." The Chinese literally says "the perception of nothingness / the base of nothingness" (無所有處想), preceded by 無諸所有, which could be parsed as an intensifier or as part of the compound. The English rendering captures the essential meaning -- this is the perception associated with the sphere of nothingness (akincanyayatana). However, the Chinese wording 無諸所有 ("without any existence whatsoever") is somewhat compressed in the English. This is an acceptable translation choice following standard Abhidharma commentary, not a mistranslation.
- **Severity:** 2 (notable but defensible rendering)
- **Recommendation:** No change required. The rendering follows standard Abhidharma identification. A footnote noting the Chinese phrasing could be added but is not essential.

**F-2.2: Sense base section -- Chinese sentence structure compression**
- **Chinese:** 色為所緣緣等無間滅。意為等無間緣。
- **English:** "form serves as the object condition; the just-ceased preceding mind serves as the immediately preceding condition."
- **Analysis:** The Chinese 等無間滅意 (the just-ceased-without-interval mind) is a compressed phrase. The English expands this as "the just-ceased preceding mind," which accurately conveys the Abhidharma meaning (samanantara-niruddha-manas). The phrase 等無間滅 modifies 意 -- it is the mind that has just ceased without interval. The English correctly interprets this. However, the Chinese sentence structure places 等無間滅 between 所緣緣 and 意, making it syntactically ambiguous whether it modifies what precedes or what follows. The translation correctly identifies it as modifying 意.
- **Severity:** 2 (technical parsing issue, correctly resolved)
- **Recommendation:** No change required. The translation is correct.

### MINOR (Severity 3)

**F-3.1: Dependent origination -- 愁悴悲嘆憂苦惱 expansion**
- **Chinese:** 愁悴悲嘆憂苦惱法
- **English:** "grief, withering, sorrow, lamentation, anguish, suffering, and distress"
- **Analysis:** The Chinese lists 7 terms (愁/悴/悲/嘆/憂/苦/惱). The English renders all 7 as separate items. This is faithful. The rendering of 悴 as "withering" is slightly unusual -- most translations render the compound 愁悴 as a single term (e.g., "grief and despondency"). However, since the Chinese text here lists them separately with individual characters, the translation's decision to render each separately is defensible.
- **Severity:** 3 (minor lexical choice, no meaning loss)

**F-3.2: Suffering of change -- 母邑 rendering as "woman"**
- **Chinese:** 入變壞心執母邑手
- **English:** "With a mind subject to change, one grasps a woman's hand"
- **Analysis:** 母邑 (matrika/matrgrama) is an archaic Chinese Buddhist term for "woman/women." The English "woman" is correct. The passage is a Buddha quotation about how afflictions cause the mind to deteriorate. The rendering is accurate.
- **Severity:** 3 (correct, noted for completeness)

**F-3.3: "Teaching-based instruction" -- 軌範/親教 rendering**
- **Chinese:** 達解瑜伽軌範親教
- **English:** "one who understands yoga, its rules, and personal instruction"
- **Analysis:** 軌範 (acarya, preceptor/teacher of rules) and 親教 (upadhyaya, personal instructor) are technically titles for specific types of Buddhist teachers, not generic descriptions. The English "its rules, and personal instruction" treats these as descriptions of what the teacher understands rather than as titles. A more literal rendering would be "one who understands yoga -- whether a preceptor (acarya) or personal instructor (upadhyaya)." However, the current rendering communicates the essential sense without being wrong.
- **Severity:** 3 (slight interpretive shift, core meaning preserved)

**F-3.4: Mind-reading display -- 心行差別 rendering**
- **Chinese:** 能尋求他心行差別
- **English:** "one can investigate the differences in others' mental states and tendencies"
- **Analysis:** 心行 literally means "mental activities/movements." The English "mental states and tendencies" is a reasonable expansion. "Tendencies" goes slightly beyond the literal Chinese but captures the pragmatic function of mind-reading -- understanding not just present states but dispositional patterns. Minor expansion.
- **Severity:** 3 (slight expansion, not distortive)
