# Practice Usability Review: Fascicles 30.8-30.16, 31, 32

**Reviewer role:** Practice Advisor on a Nalanda Translation Committee -- experienced meditation teacher evaluating whether practice instructions are actually usable by a practitioner.

**Core test:** "Could I meditate with this?"

---

## 1. INSTRUCTION COMPLETENESS

### 1A. Issues: "One should do X" without clear HOW

**Issue 1: Counter-mindfulness (30.8) -- what does a practitioner actually do?**

> "Mindfulness conjoined with wise attention (*yonisho-manaskara*) is called counter-mindfulness (beinianl) because it opposes and dispels all unwholesome qualities. Mindfulness that takes the sign of samadhi as its object is likewise called counter-mindfulness because it opposes and dispels all objects of the non-concentrated state."

A practitioner reading this knows the *definition* of counter-mindfulness but not *how to establish it*. What is "the sign of samadhi"? How does one take it as an object? The term appears at a pivotal transition point -- the practitioner has just sat down in posture and is told to "establish counter-mindfulness." Without knowing what to actually *do*, this instruction stalls.

**Suggested footnote:** "In practice, 'establishing counter-mindfulness' means deliberately calling to mind the quality of settled attention -- its stillness, its clarity, its distinction from the scattered state -- and holding this quality as the reference point for one's session. It functions as a threshold: the practitioner crosses from ordinary awareness into meditative awareness by recollecting what samadhi feels like and inclining toward it."

**Issue 2: The five types of clearing (31.7) -- operational meaning unclear**

> "*Non-attending attention* means: from the very beginning, one turns away from all signs and abides without disturbance."

How does one "turn away from all signs"? A meditator who has never encountered this instruction will not know whether this means to stop looking at things, to suppress mental images, or something subtler. The other four types are similarly defined by their effects rather than their methods.

**Suggested footnote for non-attending attention:** "This is not suppression of mental content but a deliberate withdrawal of the mind's engagement with the content. When an image or thought arises, one neither follows it nor fights it -- one simply does not attend to it, the way one might be aware of a sound in the distance without turning to listen. The sign 'turns away' not through effort but through non-engagement."

**Issue 3: "Generate imaginative attention" (32.8) -- the operational core is underspecified**

The phrase "generate imaginative attention" (*adhimoksha-manaskara*) is the single most important instruction in the entire graduated contemplation (32.8-32.15), appearing dozens of times. Yet the translation relies entirely on the footnote to explain what it means:

> "In the first, the practitioner works with mentally constructed images, expanding and intensifying the contemplated object through conviction."

This is adequate for a scholar but not for someone sitting down to practice. The practitioner needs to know: Am I trying to *visualize* a corpse? Am I *thinking about* impurity? Am I generating a *felt sense* of revulsion? The difference matters enormously for the quality of the meditation.

**Suggested addition to the first occurrence (32.8):** After "generate imaginative attention regarding a single livid corpse," add a footnote: "Imaginative attention (*adhimoksha-manaskara*) is the deliberate generation of a vivid mental image accompanied by deep conviction in its reality. The practitioner does not merely think about a corpse but actively constructs the image in the mind's eye -- its color, its form, its location -- and then intensifies the image by resolving 'This is truly how it is.' The image should be vivid enough to produce the emotional and somatic response appropriate to the contemplation: revulsion for impurity, warmth for loving-kindness, etc."

**Issue 4: Transition from imaginative to real contemplation (32.8)**

> "Relying on this imaginative attention, you should then enter real contemplation."

When? How does the practitioner know the imaginative phase is complete? The text says to make a transition, then provides the content of the reflection, but never specifies the experiential criterion for readiness. A practitioner might spend weeks in imaginative attention wondering when to shift.

**Suggested clarification footnote:** "The transition to real contemplation occurs when imaginative attention has become stable and vivid -- when the constructed images arise clearly without great effort. At that point, one turns from the constructed scenario to reflect on actual reality: the real corpses one has discarded across lifetimes, the real impermanence of this present body. The shift is from 'I am generating this image' to 'This is actually true of my situation.'"

**Issue 5: Six perceptions (32.4) -- compressed beyond usability**

> "First, the perception of signlessness -- and within signlessness, the perception of non-activity. Second, the perception of non-discrimination -- and within non-discrimination, the perception of being free from longing and agitation. Third, the perception of quiescence -- and within quiescence, the perception of peaceful bliss free from all torment."

These six perceptions are central to the shamatha instructions throughout fascicle 32. They are listed but never unpacked as practice. What does "the perception of signlessness" feel like? How does one cultivate it? The footnote clarifies the structure (three pairs), but not the experience. A practitioner sitting on the cushion with this passage would have no idea what to do next.

**Suggested footnote expansion:** "The six perceptions describe the positive qualities of shamatha, experienced as three deepening pairs. *Signlessness* means the mind has ceased grasping at specific sense-objects -- when the sign of a sound or sight arises, the mind does not take it up. Within that, *non-activity* means the mind is free from the busyness of doing anything with what arises. *Non-discrimination* means the mind has stopped sorting experience into categories (pleasant/unpleasant, self/other). Within that, *freedom from longing and agitation* is the felt absence of desire and restlessness. *Quiescence* is the deep settling of the mind into stillness. Within that, *peaceful bliss free from torment* is the positive felt quality of the settled state -- not neutral emptiness but active peace. In practice, these are not generated sequentially but recognized as aspects of the state one is already cultivating."

### 1B. Instructions that ARE complete and usable

**The nine stages of mental abiding (30.10)** -- Excellent. Each stage names what the practitioner does AND what the mind is doing at that point. The progression from "withdraws the mind from all external objects" through "one does not tolerate them for even a moment but immediately cuts them off" to "no longer requiring effort or exertion" is clear and trackable. A practitioner can locate themselves on this map.

**The four types of attention mapped to stages (30.12)** -- Very usable. Forceful attention for stages 1-2, interrupted for 3-7, uninterrupted for 8, effortless for 9. A practitioner can self-diagnose: "My attention keeps breaking -- I'm in the interrupted range, stages 3-7."

**The four inquiry points (30.4) and four-fold investigation (30.5)** -- These teacher-student interaction instructions are remarkably specific and usable. The behavioral signs of each disposition type (tears streaming for the greed type, scattered reception for the discursive type) would genuinely help a teacher identify a student's needs.

## 2. SEQUENCE CLARITY

### 2A. The Nine Stages (30.10) -- STRONG

The nine stages present an unmistakable progression. Each builds on the prior. The logic is clear: bind the mind (1-2), recover it when it wanders (3-4), prevent it from wandering in the first place (5-7), sustain without interruption (8), flow effortlessly (9). The six powers mapping (30.11) reinforces this by showing *what capacity* drives each stage-pair. A practitioner can follow this step by step.

One gap: the text does not explicitly say how long each stage takes or what signals readiness to move on. The Dalai Lama's commentary on Kamalashila addresses this for each stage; the Sravakabhumi does not. This is a genre difference, not a translation defect -- but a footnote mentioning that "the stages are not traversed deliberately; one does not 'decide' to move from stage 3 to stage 4 but recognizes the transition by the changing quality of one's experience" would help prevent misuse of the map.

### 2B. The Five Signs (32.1-32.11) -- MOSTLY STRONG, one sequence gap

The five signs form a beautiful instructional sequence: disenchantment (turning from samsara), delight (turning toward practice), danger (recognizing what disrupts meditation), luminosity (the quality of a clear mind), discerning the object (actual contemplation). This is a genuine practice curriculum.

**Sequence gap:** Between the sign of luminosity (32.5) and the graduated contemplation (32.8), the practitioner is told to "go to the charnel ground and apprehend the sign of the livid," then return to their dwelling and sit. But the transition from apprehending external signs to generating internal imaginative attention is compressed into a single sentence: "Then, within that, apply attention using the six perceptions." A practitioner would benefit from a sentence acknowledging the shift: "Having established the external sign through direct perception or memory, one now generates the same sign internally through imaginative attention, building from a single instance."

### 2C. Graduated Contemplation (32.8) -- EXCELLENT

The instruction to expand from one corpse to two, three... to limitless is one of the clearest graduated instructions in the text. The principle is explicit: stabilize at each level before expanding. The five quality-checks (joyful, clear, bright, undisturbed, effortless) provide the practitioner with a self-assessment tool: "The moment effort is required, return inward to cultivate stillness." This is exceptionally usable.

### 2D. Shamatha-Vipashyana Alternation -- STRONG but needs emphasis

The alternation principle is stated clearly at the end of 32.7: if formless shamatha becomes "forced and strained," return to formed-object vipashyana. This is repeated throughout 32.8. The principle is crucial and well-articulated. However, it appears as instruction embedded in long paragraphs rather than as a highlighted principle. Given its importance -- it prevents the single most common beginner error (forcing concentration) -- it deserves more prominence.

## 3. SIMILE EFFECTIVENESS

### 3A. The Oil-Bowl Simile (32.6-32.7) -- EXCELLENT

This simile works brilliantly. The image is immediately graspable: a bowl of oil, an executioner, a crowd of distractions. The point-by-point decoding in 32.7 ensures nothing is left to guesswork. Most importantly, the footnote captures the essential insight: "The point is not that practice requires terror, but that genuine understanding of what is at stake naturally concentrates attention." This prevents the common misreading that meditation requires fear-based motivation. A practitioner can sit with this simile and feel its meaning directly.

### 3B. The Goldsmith Simile (31.11) -- STRONG

> "It is like a skilled goldsmith -- or the goldsmith's apprentice -- who at intervals fires and refines gold and silver, causing them to shed all impurities, and at intervals plunges them in cool water, rendering them workable, pliant, and suited for the craft."

The mapping is precise: firing = disenchantment/discipline, cooling = delight/moistening, crafting = shamatha-vipashyana applied. The simile illuminates the *alternation* that is central to the four attentions. A practitioner reading this understands viscerally why the mind needs both heat and cooling -- why pure renunciation without joy becomes brittle, and why pure joy without renunciation lacks direction. The footnote makes the mapping explicit without being heavy-handed.

### 3C. The Fire-Drill Simile (32.11) -- ADEQUATE but underexplained in this location

The fire-drill appears as a brief allusion: "like the worldly method of drilling for fire, applying uninterrupted effort and earnest application." The footnote directs the reader to the full treatment in section 29.8. At this location in the practice instructions, the simile functions as a reminder rather than a teaching. It is effective for someone who has read 29.8; for a reader who has not (or who is using fascicle 32 as a standalone practice manual), the reference is too compressed.

**Suggestion:** Expand the footnote slightly: "The fire-drill simile emphasizes continuity: just as pausing mid-drill allows the wood to cool and lose all accumulated heat, pausing mid-practice causes the momentum of concentration to dissipate entirely. The practitioner must maintain unbroken effort -- 'constant and thorough' (常常修習, 安住修習) -- or risk starting over each time."

### 3D. The Painter Simile (31.7) -- STRONG

> "The apprentice studies the model, observes it carefully, and draws the form. Having drawn it, one erases it. Having erased it, one draws again. Each time one erases and redraws, the successive forms become clearer, more refined, and more fully realized."

This is one of the most usable similes in the text. It makes the counterintuitive point that *releasing* the meditation object periodically *improves* its clarity -- directly contrary to the beginner's instinct to hold on tighter. The closing line -- "But if one never erases, instead painting layer upon layer over the same form, the image will never become clear and refined" -- is vivid and immediately applicable. A practitioner who tends to force concentration can hear this and relax.

### 3E. The House Simile (31.2, element differentiation) -- ADEQUATE

> "Just as grass, wood, and other materials are assembled around empty space and are conventionally called a 'house,' so too sinew, bone, blood, and flesh -- the assembled conditions surrounding empty space -- are by conventional designation called a 'body.'"

Functionally clear. Does its job. Not as alive as the oil-bowl or the painter -- it explains rather than illuminates. A practitioner can use it but is unlikely to feel something shift.

## 4. WARNING SIGNS: Are they recognizable?

### 4A. The Five Signs (32.1-32.4) -- STRONG

The five signs framework is well-structured as a diagnostic tool. The sign of disenchantment (32.2) gives concrete experiential markers: hearing of others' misfortune, witnessing decline in one's own settlement, reflecting on past pleasures that faded. The six types of decline (health, life, kin, wealth, discipline, right view) are specific enough that a practitioner can check them off.

The sign of delight (32.3) provides an excellent self-diagnostic: "When you examine yourself thus and know that the aggregate of your discipline is pure -- then, without even intending 'I shall generate non-regret,' by the very nature of things, pure discipline gives rise to non-regret." This tells the practitioner exactly what to look for: not a manufactured feeling but a natural consequence of clean conduct. If the feeling is absent, the problem is in one's conduct, not one's meditation technique. This is both precise and practical.

### 4B. Obstacle Purification (31.8) -- MOSTLY STRONG, one gap

The four obstacle types are clearly described:

- Timidity obstacle: "afflicted longing, displeasure, anxious hope, and distress surface" -- recognizable.
- Covering obstacle: the five hindrances -- well-known, recognizable.
- Discursive-thought obstacle: afflicted discursive thoughts "beginning with thoughts of sensual desire" -- recognizable.
- Self-elevation obstacle: "one exalts oneself, thinking 'I can attain this; others cannot'" -- recognizable.

The six causes of timidity are excellent: bodily weakness, excessive effort, failure to practice, being a beginner, intense afflictions, unfamiliarity with seclusion. A practitioner experiencing timidity can scan this list and diagnose the cause. The antidotes are matched to causes, which is genuinely useful.

**Gap:** The antidotes for the covering/discursive/self-elevation obstacles are compressed into a single sentence: "the antidote is wise attention -- the direct opposite of the improper attention that caused them." A practitioner experiencing the covering obstacle needs more than "apply wise attention." What does wise attention look like when sensual desire is present? The five improper-attention examples in the prior paragraph provide the pairings (impurity for desire, loving-kindness for aversion, etc.), but the connection between the obstacle description and its specific antidote needs to be more explicit for a practitioner in the moment.

**Suggested footnote:** "In practice: when sensual desire arises, turn attention to impurity. When anger arises, turn to loving-kindness. When torpor arises, attend to the sign of brightness. When discursive thought arises, attend to shamatha. When self-view arises, contemplate dependent origination. Each antidote directly reverses the improper attention feeding the obstacle."

### 4C. Precursor Sign of Pliancy (32.16) -- EXCELLENT

> "A precursor sign appears: on the crown of the head, something like heaviness arises, but this is not a harmful sensation."

This is exactly what a practitioner needs: a specific physical marker, with an immediate clarification that it is benign. "Not a harmful sensation" prevents the meditator from interpreting the sign as something going wrong. The subsequent description of the sequence -- mental heaviness eliminated, mental pliancy arising, bodily pliancy following, initial elation, then subsiding -- is one of the most practically valuable passages in the entire text. A practitioner experiencing these signs can locate themselves with confidence.

### 4D. Signs of Disturbance in Practice (32.5)

> "Yet even when the mind has settled, it may lose mindfulness through habitual familiarity with signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions. Then images of the meditation object arise repeatedly, like reflections of a face in a mirror."

The mirror simile is effective: it captures the surprising quality of these images -- vivid but insubstantial. The remedy ("apply non-attending attention... dispel and dissolve those images") is clear. The follow-up acknowledgment is well-placed: "Worthy One, know this: such objects are extremely subtle and difficult to penetrate. You should arouse fervent aspiration and apply diligent effort to penetrate them." This normalizes the difficulty without minimizing it.

## 5. CONTEMPLATIVE QUALITY

### 5A. Best Passages -- Instruction You Could Sit With

**Passage 1 (best in entire review): The arising of pliancy, 32.16**

> "When this first arises, the mind leaps with joy and delight, accompanied by rapture and happiness. The nature of the meditative object appears vividly in the mind. After this initial surge, the force of pliancy gradually softens and eases. A subtle pliancy moves with the body, operating throughout it. The leaping exuberance of the mind gradually subsides, and -- held by shamatha -- the mind moves serenely upon its object."

This passage has genuine contemplative power. The arc -- from leaping exuberance to serene movement -- is described from the inside. The phrase "held by shamatha" is beautiful: the mind does not hold itself but is held. A practitioner reading this would feel recognized if they had experienced it, and would know exactly what to aspire toward if they had not. This is translation at the register of Nanamoli or the Lamrim Chenmo Translation Committee.

**Passage 2: The urgency countdown, 31.5**

> "'If my life should last seven more days -- six, five, four, three, two, one day -- one hour, half an hour, a moment, half a moment, the time of a single meal, a single in-breath to out-breath, a single out-breath to in-breath -- for however long I remain alive, I will attend to the Buddha's sacred teaching with diligent effort, cultivating yoga. In whatever time remains, I will surely accomplish much.'"

This is one of those passages that changes the quality of a practice session. The countdown from seven days to a single breath generates genuine urgency without panic. The footnote rightly identifies it as "one of the most vivid expressions of impermanence-practice in the Sravakabhumi." A practitioner could read this before sitting and it would sharpen the entire session.

**Passage 3: The teacher's encouragement, 30.3**

> "Among beings who have entered the perilous wilderness paths of samsara -- you alone seek passage beyond this wilderness."

The entire encouragement passage (30.3) has a quality of warmth and gravity that reads like genuine instruction from a teacher to a student. The alternation between the world's predicament and the student's rare aspiration creates emotional resonance without sentimentality. This is the voice of a teacher who has seen something and is welcoming someone else to the seeing.

**Passage 4: The non-regret sequence, 32.3**

> "When you examine yourself thus and know that the aggregate of your discipline is pure -- then, without even intending 'I shall generate non-regret,' by the very nature of things, pure discipline gives rise to non-regret. And when non-regret arises, without even intending 'I shall generate gladness,' by the very nature of things, one who is free of remorse naturally generates gladness."

The repetition of "without even intending" is powerful. It teaches the practitioner that these qualities are not manufactured but naturally emergent -- which is itself a form of instruction. If gladness is not arising, the problem is upstream (in one's discipline), not in the meditation technique. This passage could genuinely change how a practitioner relates to the prerequisites of meditation.

**Passage 5: Alternation principle, 32.8**

> "Sustain the contemplation only so long as the object appears joyful, clear, bright, free from disturbance, and without strained effort. The moment effort is required, return inward to cultivate stillness."

This single instruction, if followed, would prevent more beginner errors than any other in the text. It is concise, clear, and immediately actionable.

### 5B. Worst Passages -- Catalog You Could File Away

**Passage 1 (most catalog-like): Six-aspect vipashyana for dependent origination, 31.1**

> "**Things.** One then examines the twelve links (*dvadasha-nidana*) as they appear internally and externally, coming to understand each with clarity and confidence."
>
> "**Own-Characteristic.** One further discerns the own-characteristics of each link, beginning with ignorance -- ignorance regarding the past, ignorance regarding the future -- as broadly explained in the previous analysis of the links of dependent origination."

The six-aspect vipashyana sections (31.1-31.3) follow an identical template for each contemplation object: Meaning, Things, Own-Characteristic, Common Characteristic, Categories, Time, Reasoning. While this structure is faithful to the source, the repetition across three consecutive sections produces a filing-cabinet quality. A practitioner reading 31.1 through 31.3 in sequence would experience diminishing engagement. The instruction *to do* the contemplation is technically present, but it reads as a checklist of cognitive tasks rather than as practice guidance one could sit with.

This is not a translation defect -- the source text IS structured this way. But it is worth noting from a practice-usability standpoint that these sections function more as reference material (consult when working with a specific object) than as sequential reading.

**Passage 2: The four reasonings applied to each object**

The four-reasoning structure (dependence, function, proof, nature of things) repeats identically across impurity (30.15), loving-kindness (30.16), dependent origination (31.1), elements (31.2), and breathing (31.3). By the third iteration, the pattern is:

> "*Reasoning from proof.* This is confirmed by scripture, by inner direct cognition, and by inference."
>
> "*Reasoning from the nature of things.* The established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, and their abiding nature also confirm it."

These compressed repetitions serve as placeholders in the source text. They are correctly translated but essentially dead from a practice standpoint -- no practitioner will meditate with "the established nature of dharmas confirms it." The translation handles these as economically as possible, which is appropriate. No rewrite would help; this is inherent to the genre.

**Passage 3: Two causes and two fruits in dependent origination, 31.1**

> "The two fruits: (1) *The fruit of self-continuity* -- the maturation-born six sense-bases and related dharmas in the present life. (2) *The fruit of experiential objects* -- the feelings born from the six contacts..."

This is Abhidharma analysis, not practice instruction. It is doctrinally important and well-translated, but a practitioner reading for meditation guidance would skim past it. This is fine -- not everything in a root text needs to be practice-facing.

## 6. JARGON GATES

### 6A. Critical jargon gates (terms a practitioner would need to stop and look up at a moment when stopping would break the practice flow)

**"Pliancy" (*prashrabdhi*)** -- This term is pivotal in 32.16 and used throughout. The translation glosses it as "ease and pliancy" on first appearance and the footnote in 32.16 provides the full technical picture. Adequate, but the word "pliancy" itself is unusual English. A practitioner encountering it cold might not connect it to the felt quality described. A one-time gloss such as "pliancy -- the quality of physical and mental suppleness and lightness that marks genuine concentration" would anchor it.

**"Imaginative attention" (*adhimoksha-manaskara*)** -- Already flagged above under Issue 3. This term carries the weight of the entire graduated contemplation. The footnote explains it technically, but the term "imaginative" in English suggests "made up" or "unreal," which is the opposite of the intended meaning (deep conviction in the reality of the image). This is a potential stumbling block. The practitioner may unconsciously treat the practice as fantasy rather than as the generation of vivid experiential conviction. Consider whether "conviction-based attention" or "resolute attention" might serve better, or at minimum ensure a footnote on first use clarifying that "imaginative" here means "generated through the force of conviction" rather than "fictional."

**"Discursive thought" (*vitarka*)** -- Used throughout, especially in the obstacles section (31.8). A practitioner familiar with the NTC register will know this, but someone new to Buddhist meditation may confuse it with "thinking about the dharma" (which is encouraged). A footnote clarifying that *vitarka* here refers specifically to afflicted, wandering thought -- not deliberate analytical thinking -- would help at its first use in these fascicles.

**"Formations" (*samskara*)** -- Appears in 31.1 and throughout. This is one of the most notoriously opaque terms in Buddhist translation. The translation does not re-gloss it in these fascicles. A practitioner encountering "all these conditioned formations are without exception: previously nonexistent, presently existent, and once having existed, they disintegrate" needs to know what "formations" are to make the contemplation function. The existing glossary entry should suffice if the practitioner has read earlier fascicles, but if fascicle 31 is read standalone, this is a jargon gate.

**"Homogeneous object-field" (30.9)** -- The definition of samadhi includes this phrase. It is explained in the next paragraph ("belonging to the same class of knowable things -- sharing a similar nature"), which is adequate. But the initial phrase is dense enough to slow a practitioner's reading.

### 6B. Terms that are well-glossed and do NOT create jargon gates

- **Shamatha and vipashyana** -- Consistently glossed as "calm abiding" and "insight" where needed. Well-handled throughout.
- **The five hindrances** -- Identified as "sensual desire and the rest" with footnotes providing the full list. Standard NTC usage.
- **The nine stages** -- Sanskrit names provided in footnote, English operational names used in running text. Excellent.
- **Skandhas** -- Consistently handled per glossary conventions.

## 7. PARTICULARLY STRONG PASSAGES

These passages genuinely open something for a practitioner:

### 7A. The alternation principle as lived instruction (32.8)

> "Sustain the contemplation only so long as the object appears joyful, clear, bright, free from disturbance, and without strained effort. The moment effort is required, return inward to cultivate stillness. Apply this same method through every stage -- from the festering through to the skeleton -- and through every level of expansion to the limitless."

This encodes the entire beginner's relationship to effort. It says: meditation is not forcing. It says: the criterion for when to push and when to rest is *felt quality*, not clock time. It says: this principle applies at every level -- micro (within a single contemplation) and macro (across the graduated expansion). This one paragraph, if followed faithfully, could sustain years of practice.

### 7B. The goldsmith simile as complete integration (31.11)

The entire goldsmith passage -- from the simile through to the four attentions and their integration -- is the most practically complete instruction in these fascicles. It tells the practitioner: (1) you will need to fire the mind with disenchantment, (2) you will need to cool it with delight, (3) these two prepare it for (4) shamatha and (5) vipashyana. The five movements are mapped to the simile, mapped to the four attentions, and then synthesized in a closing paragraph that says how they work together "day and night, moment by moment." A practitioner could structure an entire retreat around this passage.

### 7C. Characteristics of "one possessed of attention" (32.17)

> "Sitting quietly in a secluded room and briefly holding the mind, body-mind pliancy arises swiftly. One is not greatly oppressed by bodily heaviness. The hindrances do not arise with great frequency. Thoughts bound up with longing, displeasure, and worry do not excessively arise. Even when rising from samadhi and going out for walking meditation, a residual force of pliancy continues to operate in body and mind."

This passage is remarkable because it describes attainment in modest, recognizable terms. It does not promise transcendence. It says: pliancy comes quickly, heaviness does not oppress, hindrances are less frequent, pliancy persists between sessions. A practitioner who has experienced even a week of sustained practice in retreat would recognize this description. Its very ordinariness makes it trustworthy.

### 7D. The "sign of danger" instruction (32.4)

The three-fold classification of mental disturbance -- signs make the mind busy, discursive thoughts make it long and agitated, secondary afflictions keep it perpetually unsettled -- is experientially precise. A practitioner can sit with this taxonomy and identify which of the three is operating in a given moment. This is the kind of instruction that becomes a companion on the cushion.

### 7E. The mirror image of habitual patterns (32.5)

> "Yet even when the mind has settled, it may lose mindfulness through habitual familiarity with signs, discursive thoughts, and secondary afflictions. Then images of the meditation object arise repeatedly, like reflections of a face in a mirror."

The mirror simile is both beautiful and diagnostically precise. It describes a very specific experience: the moment when the mind has genuinely settled but habitual patterns project images into the stillness -- vivid, seductive, but insubstantial. The instruction to "dispel and dissolve those images so they no longer appear before you" is matched by the honest acknowledgment that "such objects are extremely subtle and difficult to penetrate." This combination of precision and honesty is the mark of authentic instruction.

## SUMMARY ASSESSMENT

**Overall practice usability: STRONG.** The translation successfully conveys practice instruction that a meditator could work with. The strongest sections -- the nine stages (30.10), the five signs framework (32.1-32.5), the graduated contemplation (32.8), the pliancy attainment (32.16), and the goldsmith simile (31.11) -- are among the best practice-facing passages in the entire Sravakabhumi. The alternation principle ("the moment effort is required, return inward") is the single most important practical teaching in these fascicles and is rendered clearly.

**Primary gap:** The operational meaning of "imaginative attention" (*adhimoksha-manaskara*) needs fuller explanation at its first occurrence. This term carries the entire graduated contemplation and a practitioner who misunderstands it will misunderstand the practice.

**Secondary gaps:** Counter-mindfulness (30.8), the five clearing types (31.7), and the six perceptions (32.4) each need a practice-oriented footnote explaining what the practitioner actually *does* rather than what the term *means*.

**Genre note:** The six-aspect vipashyana sections (31.1-31.3) and the repeated four-reasoning structure across all five contemplation objects are inherently catalog-like. This is not a translation defect but a feature of the source text. These sections serve as reference material, not sequential practice instruction. The translation handles them as economically as possible.

**Strongest quality:** The translation's handling of similes. The oil-bowl, goldsmith, painter, fire-drill, and mirror similes are all rendered in language that illuminates practice rather than merely explaining a point. The footnotes consistently draw out the practical implications without being heavy-handed.

**Critical issue count:** 5 instruction-completeness issues (flagged above with suggested footnotes), 4 jargon gates (2 significant, 2 moderate), 0 sequence-breaking problems, 0 misleading instructions. The text is safe and usable; the issues are matters of further clarity, not of error.
