# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 26, Sections 26.1-26.2

**Agent:** D (Doctrinal Accuracy)

**Scope:** Twenty-eight types of persons (pudgala), sections 26.1-26.2
**Cross-references checked:**

- Buddhaghosa/Nanamoli, *Visuddhimagga* (Ch. XXIII, para. 55-58)

## Summary

The translation is doctrinally sound. The twenty-eight person types are accurately enumerated and described. The four shramana fruits, the three fetters, the faith-follower/dharma-follower distinction, the arhat classifications, and the non-returner subtypes all correspond to standard Abhidharma categories as found in the reference literature. A few minor issues and one moderate-severity item are noted below.

---

## Findings

### 1. The Twenty-Eight Person Types: Enumeration

**Status:** CORRECT

The twenty-eight types listed in the translation match the Chinese source (T30.0424a19) exactly in number and order. The Yogacarabhumi's twenty-eight-fold typology is a Sarvastivada/Yogacara expansion of the standard seven-person typology found in the Pali Nikayas (MN 70, the Kitagiri Sutta: both-ways-liberated, wisdom-liberated, body-witness, view-attainer, faith-liberated, dharma-follower, faith-follower). The Sravakabhumi adds the dispositional types (1-9), the path/fruit categories (10-11), the stream-entry subtypes (17-19), the non-returner subtypes (20-24), and the regression/non-regression pair (25-26) to produce a more comprehensive schema. This expanded classification is characteristic of Sarvastivada-Yogacara Abhidharma and is doctrinally accurate.

No issues found.

### 2. The Four Shramana Fruits

**Status:** CORRECT

The four fruits are correctly ordered (stream-entry, once-returning, non-returning, arhatship) in both person type 10 (path-practitioners) and type 11 (fruit-abiders). This matches MN 22 (Alagaddupama Sutta, Bodhi pp. 140-142) and the universal Abhidharma ordering.

No issues found.

### 3. The Three Fetters at Stream-Entry (Person Type 17)

**Status:** CORRECT

The three fetters severed at stream-entry are correctly identified as:
1. View of the transitory collection (satkaya-drishti / sakkaya-ditthi)
2. Attachment to rules and observances (shila-vrata-paramarsha / silabbata-paramasa)
3. Doubt (vicikitsa / vicikiccha)

This matches the Chinese (薩迦耶見戒禁取疑三種結) and the standard Nikaya/Abhidharma list. Bhikkhu Bodhi (p. 375) confirms: "The stream-enterer abandons the first three fetters: identity view (sakkayaditthi)... doubt (vicikiccha)... and the wrong grasp of rules and observances (silabbataparamasa)."

The translation also correctly states the stream-enterer "has become one of the nature of non-falling, and is certainly destined for awakening" (成無墮法定趣菩提), matching the Nikaya formula "no longer bound to the lower world, fixed in destiny, with enlightenment as his destination" (SN 25:1; Bodhi p. 701).

**Note on fetter ordering:** The Chinese text lists satkaya-drishti, shila-vrata-paramarsha, and vicikitsa in that order. The Pali canonical ordering in the ten-fetter scheme is: sakkaya-ditthi, vicikiccha, silabbata-paramasa (i.e., doubt second, rules-and-observances third). The Sravakabhumi's ordering (putting rules-and-observances second) follows the Chinese exactly and is an acceptable variant that reflects the Sarvastivada tradition. This is not an error -- it is a legitimate doctrinal difference between traditions, and the translation correctly follows the source.

No issues found.

### 4. Faith-Follower / Dharma-Follower and Faith-Resolved / View-Attainer (Types 12-15)

**Status:** CORRECT, with one minor note

The translation accurately captures the key distinction:
- Faith-follower (shraddhanusarin): practices by relying on others' guidance through faith
- Dharma-follower (dharmanusarin): practices through own capacity, following what they have heard, investigated, and examined

This matches MN 70 (Bodhi p. 671): the faith-follower has "sufficient faith in and love for the Tathagata," while the dharma-follower is one for whom "those teachings proclaimed by the Tathagata are accepted after being pondered to a sufficient degree with wisdom."

The progression from faith-follower to faith-resolved (shraddhadhimukta) and from dharma-follower to view-attainer (drishtiprapta) upon "touching and realizing one of the shramana fruits" is doctrinally correct. This matches the Nikaya scheme where the faith-follower and dharma-follower are on the path, and upon attaining the fruit they become "one liberated by faith" (saddhavimutta) and "one attained to view" (ditthippatta) respectively (see Bodhi's note, p. 375, and Kitagiri Sutta discussion).

**Minor note:** The Sravakabhumi's terminology differs slightly from the Pali. In the Pali scheme, the faith-follower upon fruition becomes saddhavimutta ("one liberated by faith"), while the Sravakabhumi calls this person shraddhadhimukta ("one resolved through faith"). Similarly, the Pali ditthippatta ("one attained to view") corresponds to the Sanskrit drishtiprapta ("view-attainer"). These are equivalent terms across traditions and the translation handles them correctly.

No issues found.

### 5. Body-Witness (Type 16)

**Status:** CORRECT

The translation states the body-witness "enters and emerges from the eight liberations in forward and reverse order, bears witness through the body, and abides in them extensively, but has not yet attained the complete exhaustion of the outflows."

This matches the Chinese (於八解脫順逆入出。身作證多安住。而未能得諸漏永盡) and the Nikaya definition in MN 70 (Bodhi p. 663): "Here some person contacts with the body and dwells in those liberations that are peaceful and formless, transcending forms, and some of his taints are destroyed by his seeing with wisdom."

The Pali commentary (Bodhi note 15, p. 885) specifies: "This includes all those from persons on the path to arahantship down to stream-enterers who attain the formless attainments." The Sravakabhumi's definition is consistent with this understanding.

No issues found.

### 6. Three Subtypes of Intermediate-State Parinirvana (Type 20)

**Status:** CORRECT

The three subtypes and their similes are accurately rendered:

1. **Immediate extinction** -- like a tiny spark (小札火微星) that barely rises before extinguishing. Chinese: 如小札火微星纔舉即便謝滅.
2. **Short-duration extinction** -- like a spark streaming from a blazing iron ingot being hammered (鐵摶鋌炎熾赫然鎚鍛星流), extinguishing before it falls. Chinese: 如鐵摶鋌炎熾赫然鎚鍛星流未下便滅.
3. **In-transit extinction** -- like a spark flying from hammered hot iron (熱鐵椎鍛星流), falling but not reaching the ground before extinguishing. Chinese: 如彼熱鐵椎鍛星流下未至地即便謝滅.

The three subtypes represent a distinctive Sarvastivada elaboration. In the Pali tradition (Visuddhimagga XXIII.56-57), the intermediate-state parinirvana is typically presented as a single category ("one who attains Nibbana early in his next existence"), not subdivided into three. The Sravakabhumi's threefold subdivision, with its graduated similes showing progressively longer durations in the intermediate state, is a well-known feature of Sarvastivada Abhidharma. The translation accurately represents this doctrinal position.

**Doctrinal note:** The concept of antarabhava (intermediate existence) itself is a point of sectarian divergence. The Theravada tradition denies the intermediate state, while the Sarvastivada and Yogacara affirm it. As a Yogacara text, the Sravakabhumi's use of antarabhava is expected and correct.

No issues found.

### 7. The One-Interval Person (Type 19)

**Status:** CORRECT

The translation says: "It is precisely a once-returner who, having practiced toward the fruit of non-returning, has permanently severed the upper and middling grades of desire-realm afflictions, with only the lower grade remaining."

This matches the Chinese (即一來補特伽羅。行不還果向已。能永斷欲界煩惱上品中品。唯餘下品) and correctly identifies the one-interval person (ekavicika) as a subcategory of the once-returner who has advanced toward non-return but retains only the lower grade of desire-realm afflictions. This is consistent with Sarvastivada Abhidharma.

No issues found.

### 8. Clan-to-Clan Practitioners (Type 18)

**Status:** CORRECT

The translation correctly identifies two subtypes (celestial and human clan-to-clan) and states "both of these are stream-enterer persons." This matches the Chinese (當知此二俱是預流補特伽羅). The Visuddhimagga (XXIII.55) describes this type as one who "with two or three rebirths in noble families... makes an end of suffering," classifying them as a subtype under the first path (stream-entry). The accounts are consistent.

No issues found.

### 9. Upstream-Goer (Type 24)

**Status:** CORRECT, minor observation

The translation states: "It is a non-returner who, after being born upward into the first dhyana, abides there without attaining parinirvana. After passing away from there, they are successively reborn upward through the various planes of rebirth, reaching as far as the Akanishtha heaven or the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception."

The Chinese (從此上生初靜慮已。住於彼處不般涅槃。從彼沒已展轉上生諸所生處。乃至或到色究竟天。或到非想非非想處) is accurately rendered. The Visuddhimagga (XXIII.57) describes this type as "one who is going upstream bound for the Highest Gods," who "passes on upwards from wherever he is reborn [in the Pure Abodes] to the Highest Gods' becoming."

**Observation:** The Sravakabhumi notably includes the possibility that the upstream-goer reaches "the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception" (非想非非想處), i.e., the formless realm. The Pali Visuddhimagga's upstream-goer goes only to the Akanitttha (Highest Gods) within the Pure Abodes of the form realm. The Sravakabhumi's broader formulation, including the formless realm as a possible destination, reflects the Sarvastivada/Yogacara understanding and is faithfully translated. This is a genuine doctrinal difference between traditions, not a translation error.

No issues found.

### 10. Samayavimukta -- "Those Liberated by Occasion" (Type 25)

**Severity:** MINOR -- terminology choice merits a note

The Chinese term is 時解脫 (literally "time-liberation" or "occasion-liberation"). The translation renders this as "those liberated by occasion" with the Sanskrit samayavimukta. The Visuddhimagga does not use this exact term but discusses the same concept under the heading of regression (退) vs. non-regression from arhatship. Bhikkhu Bodhi does not translate this term, as it is not prominent in the Pali Nikayas.

The Chinese source (in the eleven criteria section at T30.0425b-0427a) explicitly connects this category with regression: 由退故建立時解脫阿羅漢。彼於現法樂住容有退失 ("By reason of regression, one establishes the samayavimukta arhat. Such a person, with respect to the pleasant abidings in the present life, is liable to lose them"). The translation correctly captures this: "a person of dull-faculty lineage who, with respect to the pleasant abidings in the present life, is liable to regress."

The rendering "liberated by occasion" is defensible but somewhat opaque to English readers. The term samayavimukta means "liberated at a [particular] time/occasion" -- i.e., their liberation is conditional on favorable circumstances and can be lost. Some translators render this as "one liberated on occasion" or "occasionally liberated." The current rendering is acceptable, but a brief parenthetical or footnote clarifying that "occasion" (samaya) means their liberation is temporally conditioned and not permanent would strengthen comprehension.

**Suggested enhancement:** Consider adding a brief note similar to the existing footnotes, clarifying that samayavimukta means their liberation from the dhyanic attainments is contingent on conditions (time, occasion) and therefore subject to regression, as opposed to the akopyadharma person whose liberation is immovable. The current footnote [^samaya] explains "pleasant abidings" but does not explain the "occasion" in the person-type name itself.

### 11. Prajna-Liberated vs. Both-Parts-Liberated (Types 27-28)

**Status:** CORRECT

The translation accurately captures the key distinction:
- **Prajna-liberated** (prajnavimukta / 慧解脫): has attained exhaustion of outflows but has NOT borne witness to the eight liberations through the body.
- **Both-parts-liberated** (ubhayatobhagavimukta / 俱分解脫): has attained exhaustion of outflows AND has borne witness to the eight liberations through the body.

This matches the Chinese exactly and corresponds to MN 70 (Bodhi pp. 659-661):
- "One liberated-by-wisdom" (pannavimutta): "does not contact with the body and dwell in those liberations that are peaceful and formless, transcending forms, but his taints are destroyed by his seeing with wisdom."
- "One liberated-in-both-ways" (ubhatobhagavimutta): "contacts with the body and dwells in those liberations that are peaceful and formless, transcending forms, and his taints are destroyed by his seeing with wisdom."

Bhikkhu Bodhi's commentary (note 13, p. 881) explains: "He is liberated in both ways because he is liberated from the form body by the formless attainments and from the mental body by the path of arahantship."

The footnote [^ubhaya] in the translation correctly explains that the "two parts" are the affliction-obstruction (煩惱障分, kleshaavarana) and the liberation-obstruction (解脫障分, i.e., samadhiavarana/concentration-obstruction). This is confirmed by the Chinese source's later section (T30.0425b-0427a): 慧解脫阿羅漢者。謂已解脫煩惱障未解脫定障。俱分解脫阿羅漢者。謂已解脫煩惱障及已解脫定障 -- "The prajna-liberated arhat has been freed from the affliction-obstruction but not from the concentration-obstruction. The both-parts-liberated arhat has been freed from both."

**Note on the term "liberation-obstruction":** The definition section (type 28) uses 解脫障分 ("liberation-obstruction portion"), while the eleven-criteria section uses 定障 ("concentration-obstruction"). These are effectively synonymous in this context: the "liberation-obstruction" is the obstruction to full mastery of the eight liberations, which is a concentration-based attainment. The footnote correctly explains this equivalence.

No issues found.

### 12. The Five Non-Returner Subtypes (Types 20-24) as a Set

**Status:** CORRECT

The five subtypes of non-returner in the Sravakabhumi are:
1. Intermediate-state parinirvana (中般涅槃, antaraparinirvana)
2. Rebirth parinirvana (生般涅槃, upapadyaparinirvana)
3. Without-exertion parinirvana (無行般涅槃, anabhisamskara-parinirvana)
4. With-exertion parinirvana (有行般涅槃, sabhisamskara-parinirvana)
5. Upstream-goer (上流, urdhvasrotas)

This matches the standard fivefold division found in the Visuddhimagga (XXIII.56): antaraparinibbayI, upahaccaparinibbayI, asankhara-parinibbayI, sasankhara-parinibbayI, and uddhaIsota. The terminology differs between traditions (e.g., "without exertion" vs. "without prompting"; "with exertion" vs. "with prompting") but the concepts are doctrinally equivalent. The Visuddhimagga (XXIII.57) explains the with/without prompting distinction as whether the practitioner generates the highest path "with effort" or "without effort" -- exactly matching the Sravakabhumi's formulation.

No issues found.

### 13. Discursive Thought (vitarka) as a Dominant Characteristic (Type 7)

**Status:** CORRECT, minor observation

The Sravakabhumi lists "those dominated by discursive thought" (尋思增上者) as one of the seven dispositional types. The standard Pali scheme in the Visuddhimagga (III.74ff.) lists six character types (carita): greed-character (ragacarita), hate-character (dosacarita), delusion-character (mohacarita), faith-character (saddhacarita), intelligent-character (buddhicarita), and discursive-character (vitakkacarita). The Sravakabhumi's list differs by including pride (慢) as a separate type and having "balanced disposition" (得平等) and "thin-dust nature" (薄塵性) instead of faith-character and intelligent-character.

This reflects the Sarvastivada/Yogacara classification system rather than the Theravada scheme. The translation correctly follows the source, and this is a legitimate inter-traditional difference, not an error.

No issues found.
