# Register Audit — Fascicles 30 and 31
## Agent 4B: Senior Reader, Nalanda Translation Committee
## Date: 2026-03-02

---

## Summary Assessment

Fascicle 30 is the stronger of the two. The teacher-student encounter sections (30.2–30.4) succeed as living practice instruction — the teacher's praise speech in 30.3 achieves genuine warmth. The nine stages of mental abiding (30.10) are clear and precise. The two major vipashyana sections (30.15 impurity, 30.16 loving-kindness) are technically well-executed but structurally too close to Chinese formula-repetition: each of the six aspects (Meaning/Things/Own-Characteristic/Common Characteristic/Categories/Time/Reasoning) arrives in identical cadence, producing a cataloging effect that deadens what should be living inquiry.

Fascicle 31 contains the project's best single extended passage (the painter simile in 31.7) and its best footnote (fn. goldsmith, 31.11). The nine practice approaches (31.5–31.7) feel genuinely instructional — a reader could sit with them. The vipashyana sections (31.1–31.3) suffer the same formula-monotony problem as F30's contemplation sections: three applications of the identical six-aspect scaffold, in identical sequence, in identical cadence, with minimal prose variation.

The structural shift into the Third Yoga Place is marked but not dramatized. The teacher-student material is the most narratively engaging section in either fascicle, but misses an opportunity to signal that the text has pivoted from taxonomy to practice guidance.

Footnote coverage is reasonably handled; sparsity creates stranding in a few key technical passages.

## Criterion 1: Translated-Ese
*(Prose sounds assembled from Chinese rather than written in English)*

### F30 Instances

**F30-TE-1 [30.7, moderate]**
> "Whatever wholesome dharmas of this kind have already been obtained, one guards carefully against regression. For wholesome dharmas not yet attained, one generates aspiration and zealous enthusiasm, dwelling with constant, vigorous diligence to bring them to swift and complete fulfillment — practicing in even accord with what has been taught, neither adding to nor diminishing from it."

The phrase "practicing in even accord with what has been taught, neither adding to nor diminishing from it" carries the shape of a Chinese formula (如說而行, 不增不減). Natural English would subordinate this as a participial: "practicing precisely as taught, without addition or omission." The entire sentence is clause-stacked in a way that follows the Chinese original's linear accumulation rather than organizing around an English main idea.

**F30-TE-2 [30.14, minor]**
> "Why are these six aspects established? Because they map onto three distinct modes of understanding. Investigating meaning opens up comprehension of what words convey. Investigating things and their own-characteristics reveals the limits of things as they are. Investigating common characteristics, categories, time, and reasoning brings one to an understanding of reality itself."

"Opens up comprehension" is slightly off — too abstract. "Reveals what words convey" is cleaner. "Brings one to an understanding of reality itself" is a weak landing for what the source text probably intends as a stronger claim. Compare Nanamoli: "that is how it is in reality."

**F30-TE-3 [30.15, minor]**
> "From this point, the teacher should investigate lineage, faculties, and disposition through discourse." (30.5)

The word "discourse" (言說) in context means "through conversation / sustained explanation" — not the formal register "discourse" implies. This is a translation that imported the Chinese word's formal weight when a simpler rendering would serve.

**F30-TE-4 [30.16, minor]**
> "One further discerns these three categories. Those with a beneficial bearing are called dear ones, those with a harmful bearing are called enemies, and those whose bearing is neither are called neutral."

"Bearing" (行相) here means "orientation / disposition toward one" — the rendering is technically defensible but sounds slightly off in English ("bearing" carries a physical/posture sense). "Those whose orientation is beneficial are called dear ones" is more natural.

### F31 Instances

**F31-TE-1 [31.1, moderate]**
> "Based on the correct teachings concerning conditioned arising — through the dominant force of hearing and retaining them — one comes to understand: 'Because such-and-such dharmas arise, such-and-such dharmas arise; because such-and-such dharmas cease, such-and-such dharmas cease.'"

The phrase "such-and-such dharmas" (諸法) repeated four times in a single sentence is a Chinese structural artifact. English would condense: "one comes to understand the basic principle: particular conditions arising gives rise to particular effects; their cessation brings cessation of those effects." The quoted speech form preserves the Chinese's use of reported internal understanding, but "such-and-such" used four times in sequence is mechanical.

**F31-TE-2 [31.8, minor]**
> "One thoroughly understands: because these obstacles exist, what has not been attained cannot be attained, and what has been attained is lost."

"What has not been attained cannot be attained, and what has been attained is lost" — the parallelism is mechanical (Chinese 未得不得, 已得退失). Natural English would vary the structure: "what has not yet been attained remains beyond reach, and whatever has been attained slips away."

**F31-TE-3 [31.9, moderate]**
> "As the practitioner's vipashyana grows clearer and more powerful through sustained cultivation, it generates ever-greater physical and mental pliancy. The shamatha aspect grows and expands accordingly. As pliancy increases, one-pointed attention toward the object grows in turn; as mental one-pointedness grows, pliancy increases yet further. Mental one-pointedness and physical-mental pliancy depend upon each other, each sustaining the other."

Four sentences in succession follow a chain-dependent structure (A → B → C → A → B) that mirrors the Chinese's circular description. In English this chain-repetition feels like an argument going in circles rather than a process being described. A single well-constructed paragraph could capture the mutuality without the chiastic loop.

## Criterion 2: Formula Monotony
*(Same phrasing repeated verbatim 3+ times without variation)*

This is the most significant register problem across both fascicles, and it manifests at two scales: within individual sections, and across the vipashyana application sections.

### F30 — Six-Aspect Template Monotony

Sections 30.15 (impurity) and 30.16 (loving-kindness) each present the six-aspect vipashyana using identical bold-header structure and near-identical transitional language:

**Meaning. Having understood the meaning / Based on the correct teachings concerning X... Things. Having understood the meaning, one observes... Own-Characteristic... Common Characteristic... Categories... Time... Reasoning.**

The "Reasoning" subsection of each contemplation follows the identical four-part structure, with near-verbatim language in three of the four sub-items. By the fourth occurrence of *"This is confirmed by scripture, by direct experience in meditation, and by logical inference"* (which appears in this exact form in 30.15 and again in 30.16, and again in 31.1–31.3), the reader has stopped reading and started skimming.

Specific repetitions to flag:

- *"Reasoning from proof. This is confirmed by scripture, by direct experience in meditation, and by inference."* — appears in near-identical form in 30.15, 30.16, 31.1, 31.2, 31.3.
- *"Reasoning from the nature of things. That cultivating X eradicates Y — this is the established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, their abiding nature. This should not be further analyzed or elaborated upon, but met with resolute confidence/faith."* — essentially word-for-word in 30.15 and 30.16; slightly varied in 31.1–31.3.
- *"This is the six-aspect vipashyana for one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of X."* — closing sentence, verbatim, in 30.15, 30.16, 31.1, 31.2, 31.3.

**F30-FM-1 [Critical: affects entire contemplation method sections]**
Five consecutive six-aspect vipashyana presentations use the same structural formula with near-verbatim language at the sub-section level. The design invariants explicitly flag this: "Never repeat the same formula translation 3+ times in succession." This applies here not to a connector word but to entire subsections.

**Recommended approach:** The first full presentation (30.15, impurity) can retain the full explicit scaffold. Later presentations (30.16, 31.1, 31.2, 31.3) should explicitly reference the template while varying the prose. "The six-aspect investigation follows the same sequence as above. Here the distinctive content is: [compressed presentation of what differs]." This is not omission; it is structural honesty that the Chinese's repetition was designed for oral teaching, not literary reading.

### F30 — Smaller Formula Repetitions

**F30-FM-2 [30.16, minor]**
> "This is the established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, their abiding nature."

The triple appositional "their X nature, their Y nature, their Z nature" is a Chinese formula (法爾, 不思議, 住性). On first use (30.15) it is vivid. On second and third use (30.16, then repeatedly in F31) it has become wallpaper. By the fifth use (31.3) it requires variation.

### F31 — Six-Aspect Template Applied Three More Times

Sections 31.1 (dependent origination), 31.2 (element differentiation), 31.3 (mindfulness of breathing) add three more applications of the identical template. The cumulative effect across F30 and F31 is five full vipashyana-method presentations in identical format.

**F31-FM-1 [Critical: same as F30-FM-1, continued]**
The closer examination of F31.1–31.3 reveals that even within each section, the sub-item language is varied slightly more than in F30 — but not enough. The Categories sub-item in 31.2 differs meaningfully; the Time sub-item in 31.3 is genuinely fresh. But the Reasoning from Proof and Reasoning from Nature sub-items are still near-verbatim.

**F31-FM-2 [31.5–31.7, minor]**
Within the nine practice approaches, the phrase "as broadly explained above" (*如前廣說*) appears twice (31.8 has "as broadly explained above" once). This is within acceptable limits; not a critical issue.

## Criterion 3: Nested Qualification
*(Main verb buried under 2+ layers of prepositional phrases or em-dashes)*

### F30 Instances

**F30-NQ-1 [30.9, moderate]**
> "Repeated mindfulness of a homogeneous object-field, flowing without fault, conjoined with ease, causing the mind to continue — this is called samadhi, and it is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness."

The head noun ("mindfulness") is delayed by three participial qualifications before arriving at the main clause — a delayed-subject construction that English tolerates better than Chinese, but barely. The em-dash reversal is the standard maneuver for Chinese-style predicate delay. Better: "Samadhi — what is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness — is the mind's sustained, unbroken, ease-conjoined attention within a homogeneous field."

**F30-NQ-2 [30.14, minor]**
> "Vipashyana following appearances works as follows: regarding the teachings one has heard and retained, or the instructions and guidance received, one enters meditative equipoise with wise attention and briefly contemplates — not yet thinking through, evaluating, analyzing, or examining."

"Not yet thinking through, evaluating, analyzing, or examining" is four-item negative enumeration attached as an afterthought. Cleaner: "one enters meditative equipoise and briefly rests with the teaching — without yet analyzing, probing, or drawing conclusions."

**F30-NQ-3 [30.15, moderate]**
> "If I do not truly know the impure nature underlying seemingly clean forms, and generate desire for clean forms internal and external, this is inversion (*viparyasa*) — belonging to the dark category: conflicted, painful, harmful, laden with calamity and burning distress."

Two em-dashes and a colon create three layers of apposition. The information is all present but the stacking obscures the main proposition. Better: "Generating desire toward what appears clean, without recognizing its impure nature, is inversion — the dark category. It is conflicted, painful, harmful: a source of calamity and burning distress."

**F30-NQ-4 [30.16, moderate]**
> "Moreover, those formations arise from karma, afflictions, and other dependent conditions." [In the Reasoning from Dependence subsection]

Not nested qualification, but the sudden compression here — after an extended philosophical argument — creates a bathetic non-landing. The paragraph builds, and then ends with a clause that sounds like a bibliographic note rather than a conclusion.

### F31 Instances

**F31-NQ-1 [31.1, moderate]**
> "Within the chain from ignorance conditioning formations through to birth conditioning aging-and-death, we merely posit conventional labels, speaking of a 'doer' and an 'experiencer': one who has such-and-such a name, clan, and lineage; who eats such food, experiences such-and-such suffering or happiness, lives so long, endures so long, reaches such a limit of lifespan."

This sentence has a main clause, a colon, and then three relative clauses each with embedded enumeration. The content is essential — this is the twelve-links analysis — but the sentence is unsittable. Recommend breaking into two: "We posit conventional labels — a 'doer' and an 'experiencer.' This being has a particular name, clan, and lineage; eats such food; experiences such happiness or suffering; lives for a certain time."

**F31-NQ-2 [31.8, minor]**
> "Bodily weakness, excessive effort, and beginner inexperience call for balanced, thorough application of effort. Failure to practice calls for respectful listening and diligent questioning."

This passage (in the antidotes for timidity) presents five antidote prescriptions in five sentences, each following Subject + calls for + Object pattern. The parallelism is correct and functional, but the fifth prescription is notably longer and breaks the cadence without signaling why. Minor, but the paragraph loses its shape at the end.

## Criterion 4: Register Drift
*(Passage reads as scholarly catalog rather than living text a practitioner could sit with)*

### F30

**F30-RD-1 [30.5, moderate]** The investigation of lineage, faculties, and disposition through the four methods (30.5) is the fascicle's most scholastic passage. The description of how each conduct-type responds to dharma (greed-conduct body-hair standing on end, tears streaming; aversion-conduct the opposite; etc.) is engaging as individual portraits. But the transition into the "Investigating Through Discourse" subsection loses energy — it reads like a protocol for detecting character-type rather than a living encounter between teacher and student.

**F30-RD-2 [30.15, moderate]** The impurity contemplation body-parts list passage: "One knows that within the body there are hair, body-hair, and so forth through to brain-membrane and urine." The shorthand "and so forth" (*乃至*) compression at the body-parts list is acceptable given that the full list appears in fascicle 26. But the surrounding context is clinical rather than evocative. The contemplation of the corpse stages (lividity through bone-chain) are handled well — they have a concrete physicality that serves the practice instruction. The philosophical frame around them is more catalog-like.

**F30-RD-3 [30.7, minor]** The guarding of samadhi resources section (30.7) is competent but inert. The passage covers moral training, sense restraint, moderation in eating, and wakefulness — each handled in one or two lines. This is the foundational preparation section and deserves at least one sentence that conveys what it feels like to live within this framework, not only what it prescribes.

### F31

**F31-RD-1 [31.2, moderate]** The element differentiation vipashyana (31.2) is the weakest of the three six-aspect presentations. The house simile is vivid and does genuine work. But the surrounding material — particularly the Categories and Time subsections — are very thin: "In brief, all these elements are impermanent, and likewise suffering, empty, and without self." That is one sentence for Common Characteristic. "One discerns that across past, present, and future, the six elements serve as the conditions for entering the mother's womb." One sentence for Time. These were expanded carefully in 30.15 (impurity) and 31.1 (dependent origination). The thinness here is jarring — it signals that the translator was compressing by this point.

**F31-RD-2 [31.9, moderate]** The "paired operation" passage (31.9): "Vipashyana becomes pure and luminous, pliantly gathered within shamatha's embrace, flowing as if on shamatha's own path." This one sentence rises to contemplative register and is the best prose moment in 31.9. But it is sandwiched between more clinical framework description, and the section overall does not build to it — the reader encounters it and moves on rather than pausing.

## Criterion 5: Contemplative Quality
*(Could a practitioner sit with a paragraph and find it opening something?)*

### Successes

**30.3 (Teacher's encouragement speech) — HIGH.** This is the most successful single extended passage in both fascicles. The anaphoric structure ("Among beings who... you alone...") builds genuine momentum, and the sustained second-person address gives the reader the position of the student. The closing paragraph — "you alone seek the great light of wisdom" — lands well. This is NTC register. The speech as a whole has warmth and a forward momentum that the taxonomic sections lack.

**30.10 (Nine stages of mental abiding) — HIGH.** The nine-stages passage is the best technical description in F30. Each stage is defined concisely, with a concrete sense of what shifts from stage to stage. The language is precise without being mechanical. The closing — "No longer requiring effort or exertion, the mind's samadhi continues of its own accord, unbroken and free from distraction" — has quiet authority.

**31.7 (Painter simile) — HIGH.** The painter simile is the best extended metaphor in the two fascicles. It carries the weight of the pedagogical insight it illustrates: the necessity of alternating conviction and dissolution, generation and clearing. The sentence "Each time one erases and redraws, the successive forms become clearer, more refined, and more fully realized" has genuine instructional power. A practitioner reading this passage will understand something real about the inner rhythm of contemplative training.

**31.11 (Goldsmith simile) — HIGH.** The goldsmith passage is warm and practice-grounded. The integration section before it — "Day and night, moment by moment, the practice deepens" — is among the best closing sentences in either fascicle. The footnote mapping the simile's parts to the four attentions is particularly well-executed scholarship.

**31.4 (Shamatha and vipashyana in mutual support) — GOOD.** Brief but effective. "Supported by shamatha, vipashyana is swiftly purified. Supported by vipashyana, shamatha grows vast and expansive." Simple, balanced, alive.

### Failures

**30.15 and 30.16 (six-aspect vipashyana) — LOW on practice register.** The philosophical content is rich, but the rigid six-aspect scaffold with identical transitions produces what Nanamoli's Visuddhimagga avoids through paragraph variation: a taxonomy that does not open into practice. A practitioner reading 30.15 can learn what impurity contemplation involves intellectually; they cannot feel how to sit with it.

**31.1–31.3 (three more six-aspect presentations) — LOW to MEDIUM.** Slightly better than F30 because F31's versions have stronger individual moments (especially the "no agent" passage in 31.1 and the house simile in 31.2). But the cumulative effect of five identical templates (two in F30, three in F31) produces fatigue.

## Criterion 6: Reading Arc
*(Does the fascicle build, vary, breathe?)*

### F30 Reading Arc

F30 has a naturally strong arc built into its structure: the teacher-student encounter (30.2–30.4) is the warmest section; the investigation protocols (30.5) are analytical; the establishment framework (30.6–30.8) is prescriptive; the meditation theory sections (30.9–30.14) are definition-dense but organized; the two vipashyana applications (30.15–30.16) are formula-heavy.

The arc problem: the fascicle ends on its weakest register — the loving-kindness vipashyana (30.16), which is the second identical template application. The expanded argument for equal loving-kindness (the eight reasons section within Common Characteristic) breaks the template and is genuinely good — but it is too much a parenthesis in the scaffold rather than the heart of the section. The fascicle ends on "This is the six-aspect vipashyana for one who diligently cultivates the contemplation of loving-kindness" — a bureaucratic closing for a fascicle that opened with real warmth.

Recommendation: If the closing sentence cannot be changed (it is faithful to the source), at least ensure the paragraph before the closing has some energy. Currently the Reasoning from Nature of Things subsection — the last substantive passage — is almost word-for-word identical to 30.15's closing. This is where the variation is most needed.

### F31 Reading Arc

F31 has a stronger arc than F30. It moves: three vipashyana applications (31.1–31.3, template-heavy but decreasing in detail in a way that implies mastery) → brief bridge passage (31.4) → nine practice approaches (31.5–31.7, genuinely instructional, building in complexity, culminating in the painter simile) → purification of obstacles (31.8, analytical but grounded) → paired operation (31.9, a doctrinal passage with moments of genuine altitude) → summary verse (31.10, competent) → four attentions with goldsmith simile (31.11, the warmest close).

The arc works: the fascicle ends higher than it begins, and the goldsmith simile is a proper ending — practice-grounded, vivid, and complete. This is structurally superior to F30.

The weak point is the front: 31.1–31.3 are the most formula-heavy sections, and they open the fascicle. A reader picking up F31 cold encounters three applications of the same template before reaching the more engaging practice material.

## Criterion 7: Five Worst Passages Per Fascicle With Rewrites

### F30 — Five Worst

**F30-WORST-1 [30.15, Reasoning from Nature — repeated throughout]**
> "That cultivating impurity meditation serves as the antidote that eradicates sensual desire — this is the established nature of dharmas, their inconceivable nature, their abiding nature. This should not be further analyzed or elaborated upon, but only accepted with faith."

*Problems:* Triple appositional formula; word-for-word in 30.16 and throughout 31.1–31.3; "only accepted with faith" sounds like a shut-down rather than an invitation.

*Rewrite (for 30.15, as first use — set a varied template for later uses):*
> "That impurity practice eradicates sensual desire is simply the nature of dharmas — established, inconceivable, not subject to further proof. Here analysis has reached its edge. What remains is confident acceptance."

---

**F30-WORST-2 [30.9, samadhi definition opening]**
> "Repeated mindfulness of a homogeneous object-field, flowing without fault, conjoined with ease, causing the mind to continue — this is called samadhi, and it is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness."

*Problems:* Subject-delay construction; Chinese noun-string as English subject; em-dash reversal is overused. The definition is important (Tsongkhapa cites it) and deserves cleaner English.

*Rewrite:*
> "Samadhi is also called wholesome mental one-pointedness. Its essential nature: the mind sustained within a consistent field of knowable objects — mindfulness returning again and again to that same field, flowing continuously, free from affliction, conjoined with ease."

---

**F30-WORST-3 [30.15, Categories subsection]**
> "If I do not truly know the impure nature underlying seemingly clean forms, and generate desire for clean forms internal and external, this is inversion (*viparyasa*) — belonging to the dark category: conflicted, painful, harmful, laden with calamity and burning distress. Through this, future birth, aging, sickness, death, grief, lamentation, and every sort of disturbance arise."

*Problems:* Double-layer apposition; colon followed by four-item enumeration followed by another sentence with an eight-item enumeration. The passage is dense but not by necessity.

*Rewrite:*
> "Not truly recognizing the impure nature beneath apparently clean forms, and generating desire toward them — this is inversion (*viparyasa*). It belongs to the dark category: conflicted, painful, laden with suffering. From it arise future birth, aging, illness, death, grief, and all the rest of samsaric disturbance."

---

**F30-WORST-4 [30.16, Common Characteristic — mid-paragraph]**
> "Because in reality there is no insulter and no one insulted, no one angry and no object of anger, no mocker and no one mocked, no striker and no one struck. There are only sounds and names."

*Problems:* Not bad in itself — the triple negation has rhetorical force. But it arrives abruptly in a long argument without a connective that shows how it follows from the previous sentence. And "There are only sounds and names" is an anti-climactic landing for a potentially powerful insight.

*Rewrite:*
> "When examined closely, there is no insulter and no one insulted, no angry one and no object of anger, no striker and no one struck — nothing but sounds and designations arising within a stream of conditions. The category 'enemy' is constructed, not given."

---

**F30-WORST-5 [30.5, Investigating Through Discourse — greed-conduct portrait]**
> "Those of greed-conduct (*raga-carita*), when they hear teachings of what is pure and beautiful, generate the utmost pure faith and loving delight, entering into its meaning. Their body-hair stands on end, tears stream from their eyes. Outwardly their body takes on a moistened, supple appearance; inwardly their mind becomes soft and joyful."

*Problems:* This is actually reasonably good as a paragraph — the physical description is vivid. The weakness is the transition: "Those of aversion-conduct (*dvesha-carita*) respond in all respects opposite to the above." After a paragraph that shows rather than tells, this sentence is a dismissive shorthand. Two lines should show the aversion-conduct response, even briefly.

*Rewrite:*
> "Those of aversion-conduct respond in the opposite way: where the greed-conduct type opens and softens, they stiffen and withdraw. Praise and beauty provoke suspicion; they hold themselves apart."

---

### F31 — Five Worst

**F31-WORST-1 [31.1, internal-quote passage]**
> "Because such-and-such dharmas arise, such-and-such dharmas arise; because such-and-such dharmas cease, such-and-such dharmas cease."

*Problems:* "Such-and-such" repeated four times; mechanical Chinese conditional reduplication imported verbatim. The quote is meant to capture the practitioner's internal understanding of dependent origination's pattern, but reads as a template rather than an insight.

*Rewrite:*
> "Because these conditions arise, these effects arise; because those conditions cease, those effects cease — and in none of this is there a maker, an owner, or a self."

---

**F31-WORST-2 [31.2, Common Characteristic and Time subsections]**
> "**Common Characteristic.** In brief, all these elements are impermanent, and likewise suffering, empty, and without self.
> **Time.** One discerns that across past, present, and future, the six elements serve as the conditions for entering the mother's womb."

*Problems:* Two consecutive subsections that are each one sentence — by far the thinnest in any of the five vipashyana templates. The Common Characteristic in 30.15 ran four substantive paragraphs. Here it is nine words after "in brief." This creates a jarring asymmetry that reads as hasty drafting.

*Rewrite (Common Characteristic):*
> "The elements one observes externally — in grass, wood, stone, water, the warmth of fire, the movement of wind — share the same nature as the elements composing one's own body. All are impermanent. All are, by that impermanence, a basis for suffering. None contain a self. The differentiated body one inhabits and the differentiated world one perceives are equally, completely without exception subject to these three marks."

*Rewrite (Time):*
> "In past lives, the six elements were assembled in this same way — arising from karma and afflictions — and are now spent. In the present, they are assembled again. In future lives, they will be assembled yet again — the recurring conditions for womb-entry and embodiment."

---

**F31-WORST-3 [31.9, shamatha-vipashyana mutual growth chain]**
> "As the practitioner's vipashyana grows clearer and more powerful through sustained cultivation, it generates ever-greater physical and mental pliancy. The shamatha aspect grows and expands accordingly. As pliancy increases, one-pointed attention toward the object grows in turn; as mental one-pointedness grows, pliancy increases yet further. Mental one-pointedness and physical-mental pliancy depend upon each other, each sustaining the other."

*Problems:* Circular argument-structure that mirrors the Chinese's chain description: A → B → C → A → B → C, "each sustaining the other." The last sentence is a conclusion that the paragraph has already stated by its midpoint.

*Rewrite:*
> "As vipashyana is sustained and refined, it generates increasing physical and mental pliancy — and that pliancy, in turn, deepens mental one-pointedness. Shamatha and vipashyana thus feed each other: the stillness sharpens the seeing; the seeing opens the stillness wider. This mutual nourishment continues until it culminates in transformation of the basis."

---

**F31-WORST-4 [31.5, nine approaches introduction]**
> "Furthermore, there are nine approaches belonging to the bright category. Their opposites, one should know, are nine approaches belonging to the dark category."

*Problems:* "Furthermore" to open a new major section (the nine approaches) is weak — this is a significant structural marker, not a minor addendum. "One should know" is a translation-ese filler (*當知*). The section heading "Introduction and Approaches 1–4" already signals the transition; the prose should pick up that signal rather than offering a tentative "furthermore."

*Rewrite:*
> "Nine approaches characterize practice belonging to the bright category; their nine opposites characterize the dark. Through the bright nine, samadhi is swiftly attained and caused to advance; through them, whatever ground should be reached is reached without delay. The dark nine work in the opposite direction."

---

**F31-WORST-5 [31.7, correct practice — "only two" landing]**
> "Although these five types of clearing apply across all objects, the primary methods intended here are just two: internally collecting the mind and non-attending attention."

*Problems:* After three dense paragraphs explaining the painter simile and the principle of alternating generation and clearing, this sentence attempts a summary that lands flat. "Just two" sounds like a footnote. The reader has just processed the painter simile, which is the passage's best moment — this sentence pulls the energy back down.

*Rewrite:*
> "Of the five types of clearing, two are foundational: internally collecting the mind, and non-attending attention. The others arise from specific circumstances. These two are the root — the alternating rhythm of inward gathering and release that the painter simile describes."

## Criterion 8: F30 Third Yoga Place Opening

### Does the Translation Signal the Major Structural Shift?

The shift from the Second Yoga Place (taxonomic: person types, objects of meditation, fruitful/fruitless practices) to the Third Yoga Place (relational/practical: the teacher-student encounter, practice guidance) is a significant structural turn in the Sravakabhumi. It is the moment the text moves from "what practice is" to "how the teacher establishes the student in it."

**Assessment: Partially successful, not fully realized.**

The heading "Basic Section: Shravaka Ground, Third Yoga Place, Part One" signals the structural shift formally. The uddana footnote (fn. uddana_30) provides excellent orientation — the reader learns immediately that the first four topics describe the teacher-student encounter. This is well done.

What is missing: a bridging paragraph or even a sentence that conveys the interpretive import of the shift. Nalamoli's Visuddhimagga handles such structural transitions with explicit framing that helps the reader understand what kind of text they are now in. Here, the opening sentence — "The preceding chapters have covered the person types, the objects of meditation, and the full range of practice — fruitful and fruitless alike. How the teacher properly establishes the student in all of this, I shall now explain" — is accurate and functional, but understated for what it is announcing.

The phrase "properly establishes" (善安立) in that opening sentence is doing heavy interpretive lifting. "Establishes" is correct but might be enhanced: "brings the student into alignment with all of this" or "gives the student secure footing in all of this" would convey the relational, practice-establishment sense more vividly.

### Is the Teacher-Student Encounter (30.1–30.4) Engaging or Another Enumeration?

**Assessment: Genuinely engaging. The project's best sustained practice-register passage.**

30.2 (Approaching the Teacher) works well. The four areas of mindfulness before approaching are crisp and practice-grounded. The physical description of the approach — baring the right shoulder, placing the right knee, joining the palms — has physical specificity that grounds the instruction in real practice. The request itself ("I wish to receive training in this yogic practice. Out of compassion, please explain it to me") is a natural sentence that could be said aloud.

30.3 (Teacher's Encouragement) is the fascicle's finest passage, as noted above. The anaphoric praise structure is vivid and warm. The teacher's voice is present and human.

30.4 (Four Inquiry Points) is the weakest of the teacher-student sections — it is a brief list of four yes/no questions with minimal framing. The questions themselves are important (refuge, shila, knowledge of noble truths, genuine aspiration for nirvana) but the transition out is abrupt. The fascicle moves from the teacher's warm speech directly into a four-question interrogation, and the shift in register is jarring. A brief sentence acknowledging that the encouragement has prepared the student for this examination would ease the transition.

## Criterion 9: F31 Practice Register

### Nine Approaches (31.5–31.7): Instruction or Classification?

**Assessment: Genuinely instructional. A practitioner could sit with this.**

The nine approaches section succeeds where the six-aspect vipashyana sections struggle. The difference is in the quality of the prose at the level of individual approaches.

Approach 4 (Unslackened practice, 31.5) is the strongest individual description — the countdown meditation from seven days to a single out-breath has an immediacy that is rare in either fascicle. The specific threats (wind, heat, phlegm; snakes, scorpions, centipedes; humans and non-humans) are concrete and grounding. The countdown structure is one of the project's most effective rendering of a Chinese rhetorical device.

Approach 3 (Non-inverted practice, 31.5) is well-rendered. "Free from conceit, not clinging to one's own views, free from wrong and biased attachments, never disdaining the venerable teacher's instruction" — each quality named is a practice in itself.

Approach 9 (Correct practice, 31.7) is the most technically demanding and the translation handles the complexity well. The painter simile carries the intellectual weight. The five types of clearing are perhaps too compressed — especially "non-attending attention" and "signless-realm attention," which are each given one sentence and could benefit from even one more sentence each.

**Minor criticism:** Approach 8 (Not-abandoning-the-yoke, 31.7) contains a surprising detail — "Even upon seeing a young woman of attractive appearance, one does not grasp at signs or attend to secondary features" — which is addressed to a male monastic audience. A translator's note acknowledging the gendered address and its context in the monastic system would serve both the scholarly and practitioner reader.

### Goldsmith Simile (31.11): Instruction or Classification?

**Assessment: Instruction. The translation handles this exceptionally.**

The goldsmith simile, as noted above, is the project's best ending to any fascicle. The three-part structure (firing → plunging → working) maps cleanly to the four attentions without requiring the mapping to be stated within the prose (the footnote handles it). The simile lands as a teaching rather than an illustration: "Whatever purpose one sets the mind toward, one accomplishes in accord with one's resolute confidence" is a genuine conclusion, not a summary.

## Criterion 10: Footnote Sparsity Impact

### F30 (14 footnotes)

**Stranding points:**

1. **30.9 (samadhi definition):** The footnote correctly identifies the Tsongkhapa citation and five key elements. Adequate.

2. **30.10 (nine stages):** The Sanskrit names footnote is essential and present.

3. **30.14 (three understandings):** The three Chinese terms (語義覺, 事邊際覺, 如實覺) are given in the footnote. Adequate.

4. **30.15 (body-parts list):** The text says "hair, body-hair, and so forth through to brain-membrane and urine" with a footnote pointing back to fascicle 26. This is technically acceptable but risks losing a reader who has not read fascicle 26 recently or at all. Since the full list occupies less than twenty words, inserting it parenthetically would cost nothing and avoid the forward-reference problem.

5. **30.15 (reasoning from dependence):** The passage on impurity (dependent on karma, afflictions, parents' substances, food) is philosophically significant. The footnote handles it competently, connecting to Yogacara epistemology.

6. **Stranding point not addressed:** The five conduct-types system (greed, aversion, delusion, pride, discursive-thought conduct) is introduced in 30.5 and used extensively through both fascicles. It is a central Sravakabhumi framework. There is no omnibus footnote introducing the system as a system — only scattered usage references. A new reader encountering "greed-conduct type" in F30 section 30.5 would benefit from a footnote noting that this is a five-fold classification introduced in the Second Yoga Place and that its application here is to determine the appropriate meditation object.

### F31 (17 footnotes)

**Stranding points:**

1. **31.1 (two causes, two fruits analysis):** The footnote is one of the best in either fascicle — it maps the Yogacara-distinctive analysis to the twelve links and cites the Lamrim Chenmo chapter precisely.

2. **31.5 (approach 4, urgency — countdown meditation):** The footnote notes the Pali parallel (AN 6.19–20) but does not note that this countdown practice has a separate name in the Tibetan tradition (*nyams len gyi nges 'byung*) or that it represents one of the most distinctive features of the Sravakabhumi's urgency cultivation. A brief additional note would increase the scholarly utility.

3. **31.7 (correct practice — five types of clearing):** The five types of clearing are listed with brief one-line descriptions, but no footnote. "Non-attending attention" and "signless-realm attention" are technical terms that would benefit from at least a brief orienting note — especially signless-realm attention, which points toward the formless absorptions or cessation, depending on interpretation.

4. **31.7 (correct practice — "images of material dharmas appear as coarse, transformation-like forms"):** No footnote. This is one of the more opaque sentences in either fascicle. "Transformation-like" (*變化似*) is a technical Yogacara usage that deserves at least a parenthetical: "that is, arising from mental seed-impressions rather than from external objects directly."

5. **31.8 (covering obstacle — five examples of improper attention):** Footnote fn. improper_five correctly maps the five examples to the five conduct types and five purifying-conduct objects. Well-executed. The parallel structure is explicitly drawn, which saves the reader effort.

6. **31.9 (transformation of basis — ashraya-paravrtti):** Footnote fn. transformation is competent. The identification of the term as central to Yogacara soteriology is correct. A note connecting this specifically to the *Three Natures* doctrine or the *Five-Path* structure would add scholarly depth, but is not required for practice-register reading.
