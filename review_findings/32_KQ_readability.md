# Readability Review — Fascicle 32, Sections 32.11–32.17
**Agent R (Readability) | Model: Automated Review Agent**
**Date: 2026-03-02**

Register standard: Nanamoli/Visuddhimagga, Bodhi anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo TC.
Audience: 80% practitioner, 20% scholar.

---

## Summary Assessment

Sections 32.11–32.17 are largely excellent — the practitioner-voice register is well-maintained throughout much of the teaching sections, and the key doctrinal passages (especially 32.16 on pliancy and 32.17 on attainment) read with clarity and alive presence. The major issues are concentrated in three areas: (a) the dependent origination and element differentiation sections drift into list-assembly prose where sentences become enumerative catalogs rather than living instruction; (b) several long sentences exceed 50 words and bury their main verbs; (c) one key term ("imaginative attention") is used so frequently and mechanically that it starts to feel like unexplained jargon despite having been introduced earlier. Overall quality: B+. Fixable without major surgery.

### 32.11 — Conduct Between Sessions; The Fire-Drill Simile

**Quality: Good.** This section reads well as a teacher-to-student address. The fire-drill simile and the aspiration paragraph are genuinely alive. A few issues:

**Issue R-01 | Type 3 (Nested Qualification) | Line: "Where sense objects should not engage the faculties..."**

> "Where sense objects should not engage the faculties, do not exert yourself toward them — simply guard the senses well. Where they do require engaging the faculties, exert yourself properly, dwell in right mindfulness, and prevent afflictions from manifesting. Guard your body, guard your senses, dwell in right mindfulness, attend with proper attention, and know the proper measure in taking food."

The five-item stacked list at the end ("Guard your body, guard your senses, dwell in right mindfulness, attend with proper attention, and know the proper measure in taking food") is fine as a list but follows immediately on three preceding parallel constructions ("Guard your body well" / "guard the senses well" / "dwell in right mindfulness"). The word "guard" appears three times in five lines, and "dwell in right mindfulness" appears twice within the same paragraph. This creates formula monotony (Type 2) at a small scale.

**Suggested rewrite (targeted):**
> "Where objects are unwholesome, turn the senses away and let them pass. Where engagement is required, bring mindfulness to bear so that afflictions do not take hold. Between sessions: protect the body from harm, keep the senses restrained, maintain clear awareness throughout, attend carefully, and eat in moderation."

---

**Issue R-02 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | The Jambudvipa aspiration paragraph:**

> "Suppose the combined lifespans of every person in Jambudvipa were gathered into my single life. Even with such a vast span, I would devote every moment without pause to diligent practice of superior shamatha and vipashyana for the sake of abandonment. For I rightly understand that this practice yields great fruit and great benefit. How much more so this brief life — this short span of existence, at its longest barely a hundred years, which when reckoned carefully is but a moment."

Word count on the key sentence: 52 words ("Even with such a vast span..."). Technically over the limit but only barely, and this sentence has genuine rhetorical drive. The real issue is the phrase "superior shamatha and vipashyana" — this is the third or fourth occurrence of this exact phrase in the sections reviewed. It's accurate terminology but the repetition creates a slightly mechanical feel.

**Targeted fix:** No structural change needed; "superior" can be dropped here — the context makes clear this is advanced practice: "I would devote every moment to deepening my practice of shamatha and vipashyana."

### 32.12 — The Remaining Four Objects; Loving-Kindness Contemplation

**Quality: Good overall, with one significant structural issue.**

**Issue R-03 | Type 1 (Translated-ese) + Type 2 (Formula Monotony) | "Contemplating the body within..." passages:**

> "This is called contemplating the body within the internal body."
> "This is called contemplating the body within the external body."
> "This is called contemplating the body within the internal-and-external body."

Three consecutive sentences with the identical structure "This is called contemplating the body within the X body" — this is formula monotony. Additionally, the phrase "contemplating the body within the internal body" is Chinese technical syntax that reads oddly in English. The formula repeats again in 32.15 with the same wording. By the time we reach 32.15, a practitioner has seen this structure five or six times across the fascicle.

**Suggested paragraph-level rewrite:**
Rather than three separate sentence declarations, group the three modes and vary the phrasing:

> "These three modes of attention correspond to the three foundations of mindfulness. Attending to one's own wish for happiness establishes the internal foundation. Recognizing the same wish in others establishes the external. Seeing the equality of all beings — that each seeks happiness just as I do — establishes the internal-and-external foundation."

---

**Issue R-04 | Type 3 (Nested Qualification) | The merit comparison sentence:**

> "The merit and virtue nourished in the real contemplation of the loving-kindness abiding, compared to the merit accumulated through imaginative attention — the latter does not reach even a hundredth part of the former, nor a thousandth, nor any countable, calculable, or *upanishad* fraction."

The subject-object inversion and the dangling comparative structure are Chinese-sentence artifacts. The reader must hold the comparison open across a long phrase before learning its direction. The main verb ("does not reach") is buried after a 17-word dependent phrase.

**Suggested rewrite:**
> "The merit generated through real contemplation of loving-kindness dwarfs that accumulated through imaginative attention: the latter does not reach even a hundredth, a thousandth, or any *upanishad* fraction of the former."

### 32.13 — Dependent Origination Contemplation

**Quality: Moderate. The opening paragraph is the strongest; the concluding internal/external/internal-and-external mapping is notably dry.**

**Issue R-05 | Type 4 (Register Drift) + Type 1 (Translated-ese) | The four inversions opening:**

> "All sentient beings, through various forms of ignorance and delusion, see what is impermanent and wrongly conceive it as permanent; see what is impure and wrongly conceive it as pure; see what is suffering and wrongly conceive it as happiness; see what is without self and wrongly conceive it as self."

This is a four-fold parallel structure rendered mechanically ("see what is X and wrongly conceive it as Y" four times). It reads as a Chinese list translated into parallel English. For a practitioner reading this, the accumulative power of the four inversions is lost — it reads as a catalog rather than a recognition.

**Suggested paragraph-level rewrite:**
> "All sentient beings are caught in a web of ignorance and confusion. They take what is impermanent for something lasting, what is defiled for something pure, what is suffering for happiness, and what has no self for a self. These four reversals of seeing are not mere philosophical errors — they are the very ground from which craving grows."

---

**Issue R-06 | Type 2 (Formula Monotony) | "as previously explained" repetitions:**

In section 32.12: "The rest is as previously explained." (end of loving-kindness section)
In section 32.13: "The rest is as previously explained." (end of dependent origination section)

Two consecutive sections both end with the identical phrase. This is formula monotony. In 32.13 it follows almost immediately after the statement "there is no separate phase of imaginative attention" — and "the rest is as previously explained" then points back to something the reader may not easily locate. For a practitioner, this repeated gesture of deferral breaks the sense that they are receiving actual instruction.

**Targeted fix for 32.13:** Since this section is notably brief and ends abruptly, rather than deferring, either name what is being deferred ("The three-fold mapping into foundations of mindfulness follows the same pattern as sections 32.12 above") or absorb the content. The sentence at the end of 32.13 — "The rest is as previously explained" — should be cut or replaced with: "The remainder of the practice follows the same sequence: internal reflection on one's own present skandhas, external reflection on others', and internal-and-external reflection on past and future — as described above."

---

**Issue R-07 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Section 32.13, reflection passage:**

> "Having well apprehended these signs, one generates this conviction within oneself: 'My own present great mass of suffering arose in just this way. My successive selves — boundless and without limit, unknowable from the beginningless past — have also arisen in this way. All sentient beings, past, future, and present — their entire selves are encompassed by the mass of suffering. They have arisen thus and will arise thus.'"

The internal monologue works. The sentence "My successive selves — boundless and without limit, unknowable from the beginningless past — have also arisen in this way" is 20 words with a 7-word interrupting appositive — the em-dash construction buries the point slightly. Minor, but "unknowable from the beginningless past" is slightly incoherent: what is "unknowable" — the count of rebirths? The selves themselves? The past? A tiny logic wobble (Type 7).

**Targeted fix:** "My successive selves — more than I could ever count, stretching back without beginning — have also arisen in exactly this way."

### 32.14 — Element Differentiation Contemplation

**Quality: Weakest section in the batch. Most structural issues are concentrated here.**

**Issue R-08 | Type 1 (Translated-ese) + Type 4 (Register Drift) | Element survey paragraphs:**

> "Next apprehend the great external signs of water — rivers and streams, reservoirs and lakes, ponds and marshes, wells and the like — and generate imaginative attention regarding the moisture within. Next, the signs of fire — the scorching sun of the hot season, blazing conflagrations, wildfires in mountains and marshlands, fires in kilns and furnaces — generating attention regarding the warmth within. Next, the signs of wind — winds from east, west, south, north, and every direction, up to the cosmic wind-wheel — generating attention regarding the wind within."

This is three sentences with identical structure: "Next [element] — [list of external examples] — generating attention regarding the [quality] within." The repetition is not just a content list — it is the same grammatical template three times. The accumulated effect is a translation inventory, not meditation instruction. A practitioner is given a list of things to look at, not a sense of how to move from seeing water to feeling moisture. The word "Next" beginning three consecutive paragraphs is formula monotony.

**Suggested paragraph-level rewrite:**
> "Turn next to water: rivers and streams, reservoirs, ponds and wells. Having brought these to mind, attend to the moisture within your own body. Then fire: the scorching sun at the height of summer, blazing wildfires, the flames of kilns and furnaces — and then the warmth in your own body. Then wind: the breath of all four directions, the great winds of the outer world — and the wind that moves through you. In each case, apprehend the outer sign first, then let it illuminate the inner element."

---

**Issue R-09 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Consciousness element paragraph:**

> "When the internal eye faculty is unimpaired, external forms are present, and the generative attention properly arises, eye consciousness is produced; if any condition is absent, it cannot arise. The same principle extends through to the mental faculty, dharmas, and mental consciousness. Having apprehended these signs, generate imaginative attention — knowing that within this body of four great elements there resides the seed-realm of all consciousnesses: their lineage and intrinsic nature."

The last sentence runs 38 words and contains two em-dash interruptions: "generate imaginative attention — knowing that within this body of four great elements there resides the seed-realm of all consciousnesses: their lineage and intrinsic nature." The double interruption (em-dash + colon) buries the main instruction inside metadata about what the practitioner should "know." The structure means the practitioner hears "generate imaginative attention — [qualification] — [further qualification]" rather than a clear directive.

**Targeted fix:**
> "Having apprehended these signs, generate imaginative attention with the understanding that this body holds the seed of every consciousness — their lineage and nature."

---

**Issue R-10 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Cosmic samsara reflection passage:**

> "From the death of parents, brothers, sisters, and relatives, from the separation and loss of friends, wealth, rank, and position — the tears I have shed. The mother's milk I have drunk. From being a thief — pressing in and plundering, breaking through walls and locks — suffering countless times having hands severed, feet cut off, head chopped, nose removed, body and limbs variously dismembered — immeasurable blood has flowed."

This is not too long, but the fragment sentence structure ("The tears I have shed. The mother's milk I have drunk.") is choppy in a way that feels like translated fragments rather than a deliberate rhetorical choice. The passage has genuine power but it needs more breath. The phrase "From being a thief — pressing in and plundering, breaking through walls and locks — suffering countless times having hands severed, feet cut off, head chopped, nose removed, body and limbs variously dismembered" is 31 words with three separate em-dash interruptions. The main verb ("blood has flowed") arrives so late that the sentence loses its impact.

**Suggested paragraph-level rewrite:**
> "Think of the grief: parents, brothers, sisters, relatives dying; friends, wealth, rank, and honor lost. I have wept an ocean. I have drunk rivers of mother's milk. I have been a thief — broken through walls, seized what was not mine — and suffered for it: hands severed, feet cut off, head struck from my shoulders, body dismembered in ways beyond counting. The blood I have shed would fill another ocean."

---

**Issue R-11 | Type 6 (Unexplained Jargon) | "imaginative attention" overuse:**

In section 32.14 alone, "generate imaginative attention" or "imaginative attention" appears nine times. Across 32.11–32.17, it appears approximately 18 times. The term was likely introduced and glossed earlier in the fascicle (sections 32.1-32.10), but by this point it has become a verbal reflex — a technical formula that a practitioner reading section 32.14 in isolation would find opaque, and even a reader who has read from 32.1 will begin to lose the sense of what "imaginative" means as distinct from "real" contemplation.

The distinction between imaginative attention (*adhimoksha*, 勝解作意) and real contemplation (真實作意) is important and substantive — it is the distinction between simulated visualization and direct insight. But the 18-fold repetition of "generate imaginative attention" without ever pausing to remind the reader why this distinction matters drains the term of meaning.

**Targeted fix:** At the opening of section 32.14, add one sentence of reminder: "As with the other contemplations, one begins with imaginative attention — holding the signs vividly in mind rather than perceiving them directly — before transitioning to real contemplation." This would anchor the term for any reader whose memory of the gloss has faded across the long intervening sections.

### 32.15 — Mindfulness of Breathing Contemplation

**Quality: Good, with one structural issue.**

**Issue R-12 | Type 2 (Formula Monotony) | "This is called contemplating the body within the X body" repeated:**

This section repeats the same three-fold internal/external/internal-and-external framework with the same sentence structure as 32.12 and implied in 32.13 and 32.14. By this fifth occurrence across the fascicle, the formula is fully exhausted for the reader. The practitioner has already understood the structure; seeing it for the fifth time in identical wording signals mechanical translation rather than teaching.

> "If one attends to one's own in-breath and out-breath flowing ceaselessly, this is called contemplating the body within the internal body. If one attends to others' dead corpses at the livid stage and beyond, where breathing has ceased, this is called contemplating the body within the external body. If one generates imaginative attention regarding oneself approaching death — or regarding the cessation of breathing after death, or regarding the cessation of breathing even before death, by the natural order of things — this is called contemplating the body within the internal-and-external body."

**Suggested paragraph-level rewrite:**
> "These three modes of breath contemplation correspond to the three foundations of mindfulness. Attending to one's own continuous breath establishes the internal foundation. Contemplating the breath's cessation in the corpse at the livid stage establishes the external foundation. Imagining one's own approach to death — the breath growing faint, eventually ceasing — establishes the internal-and-external foundation."

### 32.16 — Universal Shamatha Practice and Pliancy Attainment

**Quality: Excellent. This is the best section in the batch.** The description of pliancy arising reads as living instruction. The causal chain is clear, the register is warm and precise, and the prose breathes. Minor observations only.

**Issue R-13 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Pliancy sequence paragraph:**

> "When beginners receive correct instruction and cultivate correct practice, they abide with ardor, clearly knowing and mindful, having subdued all worldly craving and grief."

Fine as written — this is intentionally formulaic (it is the satipatthana formula). No change needed.

**Issue R-14 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Mental one-pointedness paragraph:**

> "But through cultivating superior shamatha and vipashyana, as body and mind become clear, supple, and pliant, the previously faint mental one-pointedness and pliancy gradually increase, growing strong enough to become easily perceptible. Through the power of these causes mutually producing and drawing forth one another, one will before long give rise to strong, easily perceptible body-mind pliancy and mental one-pointedness."

Second sentence: 30 words, manageable. But the phrase "the power of these causes mutually producing and drawing forth one another" is translated-ese (Type 1) — it tries to render a Chinese compound (互相滋長?) into English and ends up with a slightly awkward English noun phrase.

**Targeted fix:** "Through the mutual reinforcement of these causes — each nourishing the other — strong and easily perceptible pliancy and mental one-pointedness will before long arise."

**Issue R-15 | Type 3 (Nested Qualification) | Wind element paragraph:**

> "Because this sign arises internally, the mental heaviness associated with the affliction category — which obstructs the joy of abandonment — is entirely eliminated. Its antidote arises: mental suppleness and mental pliancy. Because mental pliancy arises, bodily pliancy follows accordingly. The wind element increases predominantly, and the great elements come to enter the body. Because of this, the bodily heaviness associated with the affliction category is entirely removed. Its antidote — bodily suppleness and bodily pliancy — pervades the entire body as if filling it to overflowing."

This is close to excellent — the parallel structure (mental heaviness → mental pliancy → wind element → bodily heaviness → bodily pliancy) is clear and logical. The one small weakness is "the great elements come to enter the body" — this is vague and slightly translated-ese. What enters? The pure elements, as opposed to the afflictive heaviness? A slight clarification here would help.

**Targeted fix:** "The wind element intensifies, carrying the pure great elements into the body" or, if that overclaims the Chinese, "The wind element increases and the great elements pervade the body."

### 32.17 — Attainment of the Status "Possessed of Attention"

**Quality: Very good. Closes the fascicle well.**

**Issue R-16 | Type 3 (Nested Qualification) | Characteristics paragraph:**

> "One has obtained some degree of concentrated mind belonging to the form realm, and some degree of body-mind pliancy and mental one-pointedness. One has the strength and capacity to properly cultivate the practice of purifying afflictions through its object, causing the mind to flow with continuity and suppleness. Protected and sustained by shamatha, one is able to purify conduct."

The phrase "the practice of purifying afflictions through its object" is awkward — "through its object" is unclear as to what "its" refers to (the practice? the purification?). This is a small translated-ese issue (Type 1).

**Targeted fix:** "One has the strength and capacity to cultivate the practice of purifying afflictions through its meditation object" or, more naturally: "One has the strength to work through the meditation object, purifying afflictions with continuity and suppleness of mind."

---

**Issue R-17 | Type 5 (Long Sentence) | Secluded room paragraph:**

> "Sitting quietly in a secluded room and briefly holding the mind, body-mind pliancy arises swiftly. One is not greatly oppressed by bodily heaviness. The hindrances do not arise with great frequency. Thoughts and attention accompanied by longing, displeasure, and worry do not excessively manifest. Even when rising from samadhi and going out for walking meditation, a residual force of pliancy continues to operate in body and mind."

Each individual sentence here is short, which is good. But "Thoughts and attention accompanied by longing, displeasure, and worry do not excessively manifest" is translated-ese — "manifest" is being used intransitively in a slightly awkward way, and "attention accompanied by longing, displeasure, and worry" is an unwieldy phrase. Also "do not excessively manifest" has a slightly academic tone ("manifest" instead of "arise" or "appear").

**Targeted fix:** "Thoughts bound up with longing, irritation, or worry do not excessively arise." Or: "Covetous, aversive, or anxious thoughts no longer press insistently."
