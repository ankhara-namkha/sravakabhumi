# Readability Review: Fascicle 26, Sections 26.6–26.7

**Reviewer perspective:** Educated English-speaking practitioner. No Sanskrit, Chinese, or Abhidharma background. Reading as printed, including footnotes at point of reference.

**Scope:** Section 26.6 "Purifying-Conduct Objects: Impurity" (line 438) through the end of section 26.7 "Purifying-Conduct Objects: Loving-Kindness and Compassion" (line 531).

**Date:** 2026-03-01

## Issue Catalog

### CRITICAL ISSUES

---

**C-1. Passage requiring guesswork: "the transitory collection (*satkaya*)" — first use without explanation (line 467)**

> "everything up to the summit of existence appears impure when compared to the peace of nirvana, which is the cessation of the transitory collection (*satkaya*)"

"The transitory collection" is the first appearance of this term in section 26.6 (it appeared in earlier sections, but a reader arriving at 26.6 in a sitting or as a reference section has no definition at hand). The phrase is deeply opaque: what is being collected, and why is it transitory? The Sanskrit (*satkaya*) provides no help to this reader. The phrase is then used again at line 481 ("desire and craving for the transitory collection") and line 506 ("desire and craving for the transitory collection — from the desire realm up through the summit of existence") without further clarification. The footnote `[^satkaya]` exists earlier in the document (line 568) but is not referenced at any point in section 26.6.

A reader must guess that "the transitory collection" is something one can have desire for, something that exists across cosmic realms, and something whose cessation equals nirvana. Without more, they cannot determine what it actually refers to.

**Suggestion:** At line 467, add a footnote reference to `[^satkaya]` or add an inline gloss: "the transitory collection (the five aggregates mistakenly grasped as a self)." This term recurs three times in sections 26.6-26.7 and needs to be accessible at first use within this section.

---

**C-2. Sentence over 50 words and requiring guesswork (line 514)**

> "Whether toward those in the category of friends, the category of enemies, or the category of the neutral, one dwells with an equally established intention of benefit, giving rise to resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi that can bring about lower, middle, and upper grades of happiness."

Word count: 48 words. Marginally under the threshold, but the sentence packs in multiple undefined concepts simultaneously: "equally established intention of benefit," "resolute confidence," "ground of samadhi," and "lower, middle, and upper grades of happiness." The reader encounters all four at once with no prior orientation. "Resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi" is particularly opaque — is this confidence about samadhi, confidence that occurs within samadhi, or confidence that constitutes samadhi? "Ground" here is ambiguous (a foundation? a level? the "ground" of the text's title?). The phrase "that can bring about lower, middle, and upper grades of happiness" forces the reader to hold in mind three grades whose content is unspecified until line 522 — eight lines later.

The opening definition of the section's core meditation object should be immediately comprehensible. As written, it requires prior Abhidharma training to parse.

**Suggestion:** Break into two sentences and provide brief glosses: "Whether toward friends, enemies, or those who are neither, one cultivates an equal intention to benefit all of them. This intention gives rise to deep meditative confidence — a settled assurance, within the state of samadhi, that one can bring about happiness at three ascending levels." The technical apparatus ("resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi") can then be unpacked in the sentence that follows (line 516), which already does this to some extent.

**C-3. Passage requiring guesswork: "resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi" — repeated without resolution (lines 514, 516, 524, 526)**

This phrase appears four times in section 26.7:
- Line 514: "giving rise to resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi"
- Line 516: "the resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi, are the subjective aspect"
- Line 524: "the resolute confidence in the ground of samadhi that can bring about happiness"
- Line 526: "this resolute confidence is the resolute confidence and attention that accompany the supreme intention"

By the fourth occurrence the reader has encountered this phrase enough times to feel it is important, but still cannot determine its meaning. "Resolute confidence" (*adhimoksha*) was introduced earlier in section 26.5, but its technical meaning — a specific mental factor that makes the meditation image vivid and stable — is not recapped here. "The ground of samadhi" is never unpacked in this section. The repetition without clarification compounds the problem rather than resolving it.

**Suggestion:** At the first occurrence (line 514), add a brief parenthetical or footnote: "resolute confidence (a settled, vivid conviction arising within meditative concentration)." Consider whether "the ground of samadhi" can be rendered more transparently — perhaps "the basis of meditative concentration" or "the state of samadhi" — depending on what the Chinese actually conveys.

**C-4. Logical flow break: the relationship between loving-kindness, compassion, and sympathetic joy is introduced abruptly (line 528)**

> "With respect to the three categories of friends, enemies, and the neutral — whether beings are without suffering and without happiness: wishing equally to give them happiness is loving-kindness (*maitri*). With respect to beings who have suffering or beings who have happiness among the three categories: wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion (*karuna*); wishing to rejoice in their happiness is sympathetic joy (*mudita*)."

Up to this point, the section has discussed only "loving-kindness and compassion" as a unified meditation object. Sympathetic joy (*mudita*) appears here for the first time with no preparation. The reader has been tracking two qualities (loving-kindness and compassion); suddenly there are three, with a threefold logic: (a) beings without suffering or happiness receive loving-kindness, (b) beings with suffering receive compassion, (c) beings with happiness receive sympathetic joy. This threefold framework is powerful and clear once grasped, but its sudden appearance without any transitional sentence ("This unified practice actually encompasses three distinct attitudes...") creates a logical break.

Additionally, the footnote `[^maitri_object]` at line 514 mentions four brahmaviharas including equanimity (*upeksha*), but equanimity never appears in the body text. A reader who reads the footnote will expect four qualities; the body text delivers three. This mismatch adds confusion.

**Suggestion:** Add a transitional sentence before line 528: "Within this unified practice, three distinct attitudes are distinguished according to the condition of the beings one contemplates:" Then present the three as a brief list. If equanimity is implied but not stated, a translator's note would prevent the reader from searching for the missing fourth.

### MODERATE ISSUES

---

**M-1. Unexplained jargon on first use: "purifying-conduct objects" (line 442)**

> "What are the purifying-conduct objects of meditation?"

The section title and opening sentence use "purifying-conduct objects" without explanation. The term was introduced in section 26.5, but "purifying-conduct" is not self-explanatory. What conduct is being purified? Whose conduct? A reader arriving at 26.6 (which is a natural entry point as a topic section) may not have the earlier framework in mind.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief orienting sentence: "The purifying-conduct objects are meditation subjects matched to the practitioner's dominant behavioral pattern — each one designed to counteract a specific affliction." Alternatively, add a parenthetical at first use: "purifying-conduct objects (meditation subjects that counteract specific habitual tendencies)."

---

**M-2. Unexplained jargon: "element differentiation" (line 442)**

> "the nature of conditions and dependent origination, element differentiation, and mindfulness of breathing"

"Element differentiation" appears in a list of five purifying-conduct objects. "Elements" in English usually means chemical elements or basic components; "differentiation" could mean distinguishing them or breaking them down. A reader cannot determine which "elements" are meant (the Buddhist *dhatus*? earth, water, fire, wind? the eighteen *dhatus*?). No footnote is provided at this list.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief parenthetical: "element differentiation (analysis of the body and experience into their component elements)." The full explanation belongs in the section treating this object, but the reader needs enough context to hold the term as they read through section 26.6.

---

**M-3. Sentence over 50 words (line 457)**

> "It is the impurities within one's own body: hair of the head, hair of the body, nails, teeth, dust and grime, skin, flesh, bones, sinews, veins, heart, gallbladder, liver, lungs, large intestine, small intestine, the upper stomach, the lower stomach, belly, spleen, kidneys, pus, blood, hot phlegm, fat, grease, muscle, marrow, brain membrane, mucus, saliva, tears, sweat, excrement, and urine."

Word count: approximately 76 words. This is a list, and lists are inherently acceptable at greater length. However, the single-sentence format makes it visually dense and hard to scan.

**Suggestion:** This is a Minor issue bordering on editorial preference. The list could be broken into categories (solid structures, organs, fluids, waste products) for visual clarity, but this would deviate from the source structure. Acceptable as is, but a line break after the colon and a bulleted sub-list would improve scannability. Severity is low because the content is self-explanatory even at length.

---

**M-4. Awkward English: "unequal feeling" (line 463)**

> "the unpleasant and unequal feeling — whether of body or mind"

"Unequal feeling" is not natural English. A feeling can be unpleasant, painful, or disturbing, but "unequal" does not collocate with "feeling" in standard usage. It may be translating a Chinese term meaning "uneven" or "disturbed," but the result is awkward.

**Suggestion:** Rephrase: "the unpleasant and disturbing feeling" or "the painful and unsettled feeling." If the Chinese term has a specific technical meaning (e.g., feeling that is not balanced or equanimous), a footnote would clarify.

---

**M-5. Awkward English: triple intensifier "more extremely low, more extremely inferior, or more extremely base" (line 465)**

> "there is no realm more extremely low, more extremely inferior, or more extremely base"

Three near-synonymous phrases with "more extremely" before each reads as over-emphatic and slightly clumsy. The repetition may faithfully render a Chinese rhetorical pattern, but in English it reads as thesaurus-hunting rather than intensification.

**Suggestion:** Reduce to two terms or rephrase the triple: "there is no realm lower, more inferior, or more debased than this." Alternatively: "no realm surpasses it in baseness." If the triple is deliberate rhetorical emphasis in the source, a translator's note could flag that.

---

**M-6. Unexplained jargon: "the summit of existence" (lines 467, 506)**

> "everything up to the summit of existence appears impure"
> "from the desire realm up through the summit of existence"

"The summit of existence" is a technical term for the highest level of the formless realm (the sphere of neither-perception-nor-non-perception). A reader with no cosmological background will not know what this refers to. The phrase appears twice without a footnote or gloss.

**Suggestion:** Add a parenthetical at first use: "the summit of existence (the highest cosmological plane, at the top of the formless realm)." Alternatively, add a footnote.

---

**M-7. Unexplained jargon: "fetters, bonds, latent tendencies, secondary afflictions, and entanglements" (line 469)**

> "All the fetters, bonds, latent tendencies, secondary afflictions, and entanglements throughout the three realms"

Five technical terms appear in a single phrase, none explained. A reader can infer the general sense (these are all bad mental qualities), but cannot distinguish between them. Are these five distinct categories? Overlapping synonyms? A graduated sequence? "Latent tendencies" is somewhat transparent; "fetters" and "bonds" are near-synonyms in English; "entanglements" is vague; "secondary afflictions" implies there are primary ones but those are not mentioned.

**Suggestion:** Either reduce to a representative pair with a footnote listing the full five ("all afflictions — including fetters, bonds, latent tendencies, secondary afflictions, and entanglements [see footnote] — throughout the three realms"), or add a footnote briefly distinguishing them: "These five terms represent standard Abhidharma classifications of mental afflictions, from the grossest (fetters, bonds) to the subtlest (latent tendencies)."

---

**M-8. Unexplained jargon: "the five appropriated skandhas (*pancopadana-skandha*)" (line 471)**

> "The five appropriated skandhas (*pancopadana-skandha*) are impermanent..."

"Skandhas" was introduced earlier in the document, but "appropriated skandhas" is a new term. The footnote `[^upadana_skandha]` explains the distinction (the skandhas as objects of grasping), but the body text gives no hint why "appropriated" has been added. A reader who has just learned what skandhas are must now wonder how "appropriated" modifies them.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief inline gloss: "The five appropriated skandhas — the aggregates as they are grasped by attachment — are impermanent..." This primes the reader before they reach the footnote.

---

**M-9. Unexplained jargon: "forms" as a category of desire (line 480)**

> "Desire and craving for forms"

In the five types of desire, items 1-3 and 5 are comprehensible: one's own body, others' bodies, objects, and the transitory collection. But "forms" at item 4 is ambiguous. Does it mean physical forms (shapes, appearances)? The form realm (a cosmological category)? Visual objects? The footnote `[^five_desires]` clarifies this as "attachment to the form realm," but the body text at line 480 gives no signal. Since "forms" has multiple possible referents, a reader will likely default to "physical shapes" — which is incorrect.

**Suggestion:** Rephrase: "Desire and craving for the form realm" or "Desire and craving for [the refined pleasures of] forms." The footnote can then elaborate.

**M-10. Sentence over 50 words (line 496)**

> "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such a corpse — dead for one day, two days, or seven days — being devoured by crows, magpies, hungry dogs, vultures, foxes, jackals, and various beasts. One then takes up this sign and applies it to one's own body: "This body too is of such a nature, of such a type, and cannot transcend this condition"' — this indicates the stages from the livid through the gnawed."

Word count: approximately 79 words. The sentence is a sutra quotation wrapped in an explanatory frame. The quotation itself is vivid and effective. The issue is structural: the frame ("When it is said... this indicates...") wraps the entire quotation in a single grammatical unit, making it syntactically heavy.

**Suggestion:** Set the sutra quotation as a block quote, then follow it with the analytical gloss: "The passage above indicates the stages from the livid through the gnawed." This separates the quoted material from the commentary and reduces the syntactic burden.

**M-11. Sentence over 50 words (line 502)**

> "When it is said, 'By the charnel ground path, one sees such-and-such bones — hand bones in one place, foot bones in another, hip bones, knee bones, arm bones, elbow bones, spine, shoulder bones, rib bones, jawbone, teeth, and skull, each scattered in different places; or after one, two, three, or up to seven years, white as conch shell or dove-colored; or bones mixed with dust and earth' — this indicates the scattered."

Word count: approximately 76 words. Same structural issue as M-10 — a long sutra quotation embedded in a single sentence.

**Suggestion:** Same as M-10: set as block quote, then gloss separately.

**M-12. Logical flow break: sudden shift from impurity-desire mapping to charnel ground passages (line 496)**

The text moves from a clean analytical framework (six impurities mapped to five desires, lines 483-494) directly into four charnel ground sutra quotations (lines 496-502) without a transitional sentence explaining the purpose of these quotations. Line 496 opens "For this reason, all the various external impurities of putrefaction that the Blessed One taught are established in terms of four charnel ground paths" — but "for this reason" does not clearly connect to what precedes it (line 494: "These four together constitute the purification from sensual craving"). The reader must infer that the text is now providing scriptural authority for the fourfold classification just established.

**Suggestion:** Strengthen the transition: "Having established these four correspondences, the text now cites the Blessed One's teaching to show their scriptural basis. All the external impurities of putrefaction are established in terms of four charnel ground paths."

---

**M-13. Awkward English: "the ground of samadhi" (line 514, 516, 524)**

"Ground" in English most naturally means "earth," "basis," or "reason." In the context of this translation project, "ground" is used in the title ("The Hearer's Ground") to render *bhumi* (a stage or level). Using "ground" here for what appears to be the meditative state of samadhi creates confusion with the title usage. Is the "ground of samadhi" a level of attainment? A basis for samadhi? The state of samadhi itself?

**Suggestion:** Clarify with a footnote at first use, or consider rendering as "the basis of samadhi" or "the state of samadhi" if the Chinese supports it.

---

**M-14. Awkward English: "wishing equally to give them happiness is loving-kindness... wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion" (line 528)**

> "wishing equally to give them happiness is loving-kindness (*maitri*). With respect to beings who have suffering or beings who have happiness among the three categories: wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion (*karuna*); wishing to rejoice in their happiness is sympathetic joy (*mudita*)."

The construction "wishing X is Y" (where Y is the name of a quality) reads as a definition format, which is appropriate. However, the middle clause — "With respect to beings who have suffering or beings who have happiness among the three categories" — is syntactically dense. The reader must hold "beings who have suffering OR beings who have happiness" and then apply the first group to compassion and the second to sympathetic joy. The sentence does not clearly signal the split.

**Suggestion:** Break into explicit clauses: "With respect to beings who have suffering: wishing equally to remove their suffering is compassion (*karuna*). With respect to beings who have happiness: wishing to rejoice in their happiness is sympathetic joy (*mudita*)."

---

**M-15. Unexplained jargon: "aversion-conduct" (line 530)**

> "If a person of aversion-conduct practices loving-kindness..."

"Aversion-conduct" was defined in section 26.2 as one of the 28 person types (type 4: "those dominated by aversion"). However, a reader arriving at section 26.7 may not recall or have read section 26.2. The term "aversion-conduct" is not self-explanatory — it sounds like conduct characterized by aversion, but could also mean conduct aimed at overcoming aversion.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief parenthetical: "a person of aversion-conduct (one whose dominant tendency is irritability and ill will)." This costs five words and eliminates the ambiguity.
