## Findings

### Issue 1 — Translated-ese / Register Drift: 32.5 Six-Perception Enumeration (STRUCTURAL)

**Location:** Line 119, second paragraph of 32.5.

**Problem text:**
> "...apply attention using the six perceptions: signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, and peaceful bliss free from torment."

This is a Chinese list rendered directly as a list. In isolation, the items would not communicate anything to a practitioner — "non-activity," "freedom from longing and agitation," and "peaceful bliss free from torment" feel like category labels rather than descriptions that open understanding. The reader has no foothold. The list sits in the middle of an otherwise warm, embodied passage (washing feet, sitting cross-legged) and suddenly reads like a taxonomy entry.

Additionally, the entire sentence in which this list sits is a 66-word compound that the reader has to parse before arriving at any meaning:

> "Then, within that, apply attention using the six perceptions: signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, and peaceful bliss free from torment."

**What is broken:** A practitioner reading this passage has just been given very concrete instructions (wash feet, sit, settle body) and is then handed an enumeration that gives no sense of how these six perceptions are applied or what it feels like to apply them. Register drops sharply from embodied to technical.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph-level):**

The current paragraph reads:
> "Having apprehended these signs, return to your dwelling — whether a forest retreat, the base of a tree, or an empty room. On a large couch, a small rope-seat, or a seat of grass and leaves, first wash your feet, then sit cross-legged, body upright, attention settled, and establish mindfulness. First, on a single object, keep the mind from scattering and tether mindfulness before you. Then, within that, apply attention using the six perceptions: signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, and peaceful bliss free from torment."

Proposed revision:
> "Having apprehended these signs, return to your dwelling — whether a forest retreat, the base of a tree, or an empty room. On a couch, a rope-seat, or a seat of grass and leaves, wash your feet and sit cross-legged, body upright, attention gathered. Tether mindfulness to a single object and prevent the mind from scattering. Within that stillness, apply attention through six modes of perception: recognizing the object as signless and non-discriminating; resting in quiescence and non-activity; releasing longing and agitation; abiding in peaceful bliss free from torment."

The revision does three things: (a) breaks the 66-word compound into shorter units, (b) introduces the list as "six modes of perception" with a colon that signals a structured explanation rather than a bare enumeration, and (c) uses participial phrases to give each item a sense of action rather than category-label stasis.

**Note:** If the "six perceptions" are a technical category with a name (a defined set), a footnote gloss would be appropriate here, as the footnotes elsewhere in these sections are well-used. Check whether 六相 or equivalent appears in the glossary.

### Issue 2 — >50-word sentence: 32.5, Line 119 (MECHANICAL)

**Location:** Line 119, first compound instruction sentence.

**Problem text (full sentence):**
> "On a large couch, a small rope-seat, or a seat of grass and leaves, first wash your feet, then sit cross-legged, body upright, attention settled, and establish mindfulness."

Word count: 38 words. This one is borderline and acceptable — the participial tail "body upright, attention settled" is a graceful construction. Not a flag.

**Actual flag — Line 119, second compound sentence:**
> "First, on a single object, keep the mind from scattering and tether mindfulness before you. Then, within that, apply attention using the six perceptions: signlessness, non-discrimination, quiescence, non-activity, freedom from longing and agitation, and peaceful bliss free from torment."

The second sentence above runs 44 words — also borderline. The structural rewrite in Issue 1 handles this.

**Actual flag — Line 171 (32.7, alternation instruction):**
> "But if, due to habitual faults, you cannot generate deep joy and happiness in it, and the practice appears only with great effort and strain, then you should quickly emerge from the formless, non-discriminating meditation object and establish mindfulness on a formed, discriminating object — such as the signs of impurity you previously apprehended."

Word count: 71 words. This sentence has three nested conditions and two em-dashes. It qualifies, qualifies again, and only delivers its main instruction ("emerge... and establish mindfulness") near the end. A practitioner trying to receive this as guidance will have to read it twice.

**Targeted fix:**

> "But if, due to habitual faults, deep joy and happiness do not come — if the practice feels forced and strained — you should quickly leave the formless, non-discriminating object. Return instead to a formed, discriminating object: the impurity signs you previously apprehended."

The fix splits at the em-dash, converts the nested conditional into a shorter parallel clause, and lands the instruction cleanly.

### Issue 3 — >50-word sentence: 32.7, Line 171 (MECHANICAL)

This is the same as Issue 2's second flag (the alternation passage). For tracking purposes, noting separately.

**Full sentence word count:** The entire instruction block on line 171 from "if you diligently practice..." to "...the bone-chain" is approximately 130 words as a single quotation block. Even read as a direct quotation (and thus perhaps intentionally long), two distinct instructions are fused into one: (a) if practice is going well, this is the well-cultivated path; (b) if it is not going well, do this instead. These should be separated.

**Suggested rewrite (paragraph-level):**

Current:
> "Worthy One, if you diligently practice the shamatha path in this way — with such skillful means, accompanied by right mindfulness and clear knowing, with a joyful and happy mind — this is called the well-cultivated shamatha path. But if, due to habitual faults, you cannot generate deep joy and happiness in it, and the practice appears only with great effort and strain, then you should quickly emerge from the formless, non-discriminating meditation object and establish mindfulness on a formed, discriminating object — such as the signs of impurity you previously apprehended. Apply the sign-following mode of vipashyana: contemplate the blue-black corpse, the festering, and so on through to the bones and the bone-chain."

Proposed:
> "Worthy One, if you practice the shamatha path diligently — with skillful means, right mindfulness, clear knowing, and a joyful and happy mind — this is what is called the well-cultivated shamatha path.

> "But habitual faults may arise. If deep joy and happiness do not come, and the practice feels forced and strained, then quickly emerge from the formless, non-discriminating object. Establish mindfulness instead on a formed, discriminating object — such as the impurity signs you previously apprehended. Apply the sign-following mode of vipashyana: contemplate the blue-black corpse, the festering, and so on through to the bones and the bone-chain."

This breaks a 130-word monolith into three shorter paragraphs, separates the positive and negative cases, and makes the conditional instruction ("But habitual faults may arise") land with more weight.

### Issue 4 — Formula Monotony, Minor: 32.5, "Having apprehended" (STRUCTURAL, LOW SEVERITY)

**Location:** Lines 115–117, 32.5.

**Problem:**
> "Having apprehended the sign of danger, you should then repeatedly apprehend the sign of luminosity..."
> "Having apprehended the sign of luminosity, go to the charnel ground and apprehend the sign of the blue-black corpse..."
> "Having apprehended these signs, return to your dwelling..."

Three successive paragraphs begin "Having apprehended." This is borderline — it may be intentional structural echo (step 1 → step 2 → step 3). However, the third occurrence in particular ("Having apprehended these signs, return...") could be absorbed into the preceding paragraph without loss, breaking the formula:

**Targeted fix:**

Rather than starting the third paragraph with "Having apprehended these signs," it could read:

> "From these signs, return to your dwelling..."

Or the preceding paragraph could end:

> "...apprehend these signs from paintings or from sculptures of wood and stone. Then return to your dwelling..."

This avoids starting three consecutive paragraphs with the same participial construction. Low severity — the formula does carry sequential meaning — but the variation is easy and improves the flow.
