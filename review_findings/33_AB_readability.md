# Agent R Readability Review — Fascicle 33, Sections 33.4–33.5

**Scope:** Lines 70–138 (`translation/fascicle_33_en.md`)
**Reviewer role:** 80% practitioner / 20% scholar
**Register standard:** Nanamoli/Visuddhimagga, Bodhi anthologies, Lamrim Chenmo TC

---

## Summary Assessment

Sections 33.4–33.5 are mostly solid. The prose is clear and well-organized, and the eight similes passage (line 96–104) and the medicine simile (line 112–113) are genuinely alive. However, several passages collapse into taxonomic catalog mode — especially the own-characteristic paragraph (line 92) and the reasoning paragraph (line 108) — and there are two long sentences that need surgery. The footnote apparatus is excellent and does real work; no issues there.

---

## Findings

---

### R-01 | NESTED QUALIFICATION — line 92 (own-characteristic paragraph) | H

**Category:** Nested qualification + register drift

The paragraph analyzing the own-characteristic of desires is a catalog that reads as if someone assembled clauses from a schema. The structure is: define the two types → list three bases → for each base, list its associated afflictions → conclude. This is mechanically faithful to the schema but the practitioner does not feel the texture of how desire actually works — the passage becomes an Abhidharma grid rather than a guide to seeing.

Specific structural problem: "What conduces to pleasant feeling serves as the basis for craving and for the inversions of perception and mind. What conduces to painful feeling serves as the basis for anger and resentment. What conduces to neither-pleasant-nor-painful feeling serves as the basis for ignorance, for concealment, vexation, deceit, flattery, shamelessness, and recklessness, and for the inversion of views." — three parallel sentences with identical frames, making the reader sense the grid behind the text. The neutral-feeling clause is also an overwhelming compound: eight items strung with "for...for...and for" that buries the reader before they reach the conclusion.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> *How does one investigate the characteristic of desires?* One investigates the two basic forms: the affliction of desire itself — the inner craving (*klesha-kama*) — and the objects that occasion it (*vastu-kama*).
>
> Each class of sense-experience then becomes a breeding ground for specific afflictions. Pleasant experience sustains craving and distorts both perception and mind. Painful experience gives rise to anger and resentment. Neutral experience, more subtly, sustains ignorance — and from that root: concealment, vexation, deceit, flattery, shamelessness, recklessness, and distorted views. Whatever the feeling-tone, desire ensures that affliction follows close behind. This is the characteristic of desires.

This restructuring: (a) reduces three parallel skeleton sentences to flowing prose; (b) front-loads the topic (affliction follows sense-experience regardless of valence); (c) preserves all content; (d) moves Sanskrit glosses into the text inline rather than leaving bare technical terms.

### R-03 | LONG SENTENCE — line 94 | M

**Category:** >50-word sentence

"Those who indulge desires are driven and oppressed even when their desires are fully satisfied, for they remain unfreed from birth and the rest." — 30 words, within limit. But the sentence before it is: "One investigates that all desires are attended and bound by the suffering of birth, the suffering of aging, and so on through the suffering of not obtaining what one seeks." — 37 words, slightly below 50. Together they form a clunky block. The phrase "and so on through the suffering of not obtaining what one seeks" is translated-ese: the "through" construction signals a compressed list in Classical Chinese but sounds awkward in English. A practitioner would wonder: what are all the sufferings in between?

**Targeted fix:**

> One investigates that all desires are attended and bound by suffering — the suffering of birth, aging, sickness, death, separation from what is loved, and the failure to obtain what one seeks. Those who indulge desires are driven and oppressed even when apparently satisfied, because they remain unfreed from birth and from all that follows it. However excellent the pleasures, they last only a moment.

### R-04 | LONG SENTENCE — line 100 | H

**Category:** >50-word sentence + nested qualification

The fifth fault in the World-Honored One's citation is a remarkable sentence:

> "Fifth, when one habitually engages them — I say there is no evil or unwholesome action that one will not commit."

This is actually fine — the em-dash pause preserves the scriptural voice. No change needed here.

BUT the fourth fault sentence is awkward: "Fourth, they cause fetters to accumulate and increase." — this is fine at 9 words.

The real problem is the opening sentence of the paragraph: "As the World-Honored One has said, there are five faults in habitually engaging desires." This is a perfectly clear framing. No issue.

The actual long-sentence problem is the third fault: "Third, they are constantly reproached and condemned through innumerable teachings by the Buddhas and their disciples, by the virtuous, the rightly practicing, and the genuinely good." — 35 words, just under limit, but "the virtuous, the rightly practicing, and the genuinely good" is vague translated-ese. What does "the rightly practicing" mean to a practitioner? A lay reader will not know.

**Targeted fix:**

> "Third, they are constantly reproached and condemned in the teachings of the Buddhas and their disciples, and by all those who are virtuous and practicing rightly."

### R-05 | LONG SENTENCE + NESTED QUALIFICATION — line 108 | H

**Category:** >50-word sentence + nested qualification + register drift

This is the most problematic passage in the section. Count: "One investigates that desires can be acquired, produced, and increased only through great resources, great pursuit, great exertion, and innumerable kinds of skilled occupations. Moreover, even when well established and increased, desires mostly serve external dependents — parents, spouse, children, servants, workers, friends, and relatives — or else remedy the needs of one's own body, composed of the coarse and heavy four great elements: a body requiring gruel and rice for nourishment, constant covering, bathing, and massage; a body subject by its nature to deterioration, breakage, dispersal, and destruction, and giving rise to various kinds of suffering."

The second sentence is 82 words. It consists of: main clause → appositive "or else" branch → relative clause "composed of..." → further relative clause "requiring..." → another appositive clause "subject by its nature to..." → participial "giving rise to...". The reader has completely lost the thread by the time the sentence ends.

Additionally "a body requiring gruel and rice for nourishment" reads as translated-ese — "gruel and rice" signals a Classical Chinese idiom (粥飯) for food in general, but to an English reader it evokes a prison.

**Suggested rewrite:**

> *How does one investigate the reasoning about desires?* One investigates that acquiring, sustaining, and increasing desires demands enormous resources — great effort, great exertion, and mastery of countless trades and skills. And even when desires are fully established, what do they actually serve? Mostly they support others — parents, spouse, children, servants, friends, relatives — and whatever remains is spent maintaining one's own body: this heavy vessel of the four great elements, which requires constant feeding, clothing, bathing, and care. The body is inherently subject to decay, injury, dissolution, and death, and it is the source of suffering without end.

This rewrite: (a) breaks the 82-word sentence into four manageable units; (b) inserts a rhetorical question that creates the same effect as the Chinese rhetorical structure; (c) replaces "gruel and rice" with "feeding" (preserving the sense without the cultural specificity that will mislead); (d) preserves all content; (e) lets the practitioner feel the argument rather than merely track it.

### R-06 | TRANSLATED-ESE — line 106 | L

**Category:** Translated-ese

"One investigates that desires — past, future, and present — are constantly and perpetually full of faults, injuries, afflictions, and calamities."

"Constantly and perpetually" is a hendiadys that signals compressed Classical Chinese (恒常 or similar). In English these are near-synonyms and the doubling sounds redundant.

**Targeted fix:** "One investigates that desires — past, present, and future — are faults and injuries and calamities through and through, without exception and without end." OR: "One investigates that desires, across all three times — past, present, and future — are unrelievedly full of faults, injury, affliction, and calamity."

### R-07 | FORMULA MONOTONY — line 88–116 | M

**Category:** Formula monotony

The six investigative sub-sections all begin with the same structure: "*How does one investigate the coarse [X] of desires?* One investigates that..." This is unavoidable given the Q&A structure of the text, and the Chinese surely repeats the pattern exactly. However, there are six instances, and by the fifth the reader feels the mechanical grid.

Possible variation approaches (not a full rewrite recommendation, since structure fidelity is required):
- Drop "coarse" from the later headers where it appears but was already established (the text says "coarse category," "coarse time-aspect," "coarse reasoning" — once the pattern is set, the word "coarse" can be dropped from the question without losing content)
- Vary the sentence following the question: not every sub-section need begin "One investigates that..." — some could begin with the substantive point directly

This is a structural constraint issue rather than a fixable prose problem. Flag for translator's awareness. L/M priority.

### R-08 | UNEXPLAINED JARGON — line 114 | M

**Category:** Unexplained jargon

"Their coarse aspects are known through one's own direct insight and perception." — This is fine.

BUT the phrase "inconceivable nature of things (*dharmata*)" appears with a Sanskrit gloss in parentheses but no English explanation of what "dharmata" means. The footnote (note 34) explains it well, but a practitioner reading the text body alone may wonder: what does it mean that something's coarseness is established by "the inconceivable nature of things"? The argument here is: desires are simply impure by nature from beginningless time, and this is not further reducible to conceptual explanation.

**Targeted fix (in-text):** "...and desires have been coarse and impure by nature since beginningless time — this simply is how things are (*dharmata*), beyond conceptual elaboration." OR add a brief parenthetical: "(*dharmata* — the basic nature of phenomena, not further reducible to analysis)."

---

### R-09 | REGISTER STRENGTH — line 118 | L

**Category:** Register drift (minor)

"One further discerns the peaceful aspects of the first dhyana: all the coarseness of the desire realm is entirely absent in the first dhyana. Because it is free from these coarse qualities, the first dhyana is said to possess the quality of peacefulness."

This is clear and accurate but the repetition of "coarse/coarseness" three times in two sentences is slightly monotonous. The paragraph's job is to create a felt contrast — the practitioner should feel the relief of the dhyana in contrast to the heaviness just enumerated. The current phrasing is taxonomic.

**Minor suggestion:** "One further discerns the peaceful quality of the first dhyana: everything enumerated above — the faults, injuries, afflictions, and entanglements of the desire realm — is entirely absent there. Free from all that weight, the first dhyana has the quality of peace."

This is L priority — the existing version works.
