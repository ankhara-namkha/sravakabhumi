# Doctrinal Review: Fascicle 32, Sections 32.8-32.10

**Reviewer:** Agent D (Doctrinal)
**Sections:** 32.8 (Graduated Contemplation), 32.9 (Light-Perception; Entering Foundations of Mindfulness), 32.10 (Four Foundations via Asubha)
**Source range:** T30.0461c01-0462b28

---

## Summary

The translation is doctrinally sound on the major points. The graduated expansion method, the two-phase attention distinction, the shamatha-vipashyana alternation, the threefold body contemplation, and the mapping of formless aggregates to the three remaining foundations are all faithful to established Yogacara doctrine as presented in the Sravakabhumi. Five findings below, none critical.

---

## Findings

### 1. [m] Mount Vipula reference cited as SN 15.10 -- reference may be imprecise

**Location:** Footnote [^vipula], line 203

**Issue:** The footnote states: "Mount Vipula (脅山) is one of the five peaks surrounding Rajagriha, used in the Pali canon (SN 15.10) as a measure of cosmic scale." The reference SN 15.10 is to the Puggala Sutta in the Anamataggasamyutta, which discusses the heap of bones from a single person's rebirths in a single kalpa equaling Mount Vepulla (Vipula). However, several points need clarification:

(a) The Pali equivalent is Vepulla, not Vipula (the Sanskrit form). The sutta itself (SN 15:10, Puggala Sutta) says: "the bones, the skeleton, the bone-heap of a single person roaming and wandering on through this long course would make a cairn, a pile, a heap as great as this Mount Vepulla." This is about the bones of a **single person** across a single kalpa, not corpses collectively. The Chinese text (脅山, literally "rib/flank mountain") renders the name differently. This is a minor precision issue -- the footnote could note that the Chinese 脅山 is Xuanzang's rendering of Vipula/Vepulla, and the SN passage concerns one person's bones over a kalpa, whereas the Sravakabhumi text says the corpses from a single kalpa would equal the mountain. The Sravakabhumi is adapting the simile, not quoting it verbatim. This should be noted to avoid the impression of exact quotation.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- the reference is close enough, but the adaptation from the Pali context could be flagged more precisely.

**Recommendation:** Adjust the footnote to note: (1) the Pali form is Vepulla, (2) the SN 15:10 passage concerns a single person's bones, (3) the Sravakabhumi adapts this to corpses collectively across samsara, which is a Yogacara expansion of the original simile. This prevents a scholar from checking the reference and finding a mismatch.

### 2. [m] Thirty-six bodily substances: doctrinal note could clarify divergence from Pali thirty-one/thirty-two

**Location:** Footnote [^thirtysix], line 231

**Issue:** The footnote states the "thirty-six bodily substances (三十六物) is a standard list for contemplation of the body." The Pali Satipatthana Sutta lists thirty-one parts (or thirty-two in some recensions that add the brain). The Visuddhimagga works with thirty-two. The Chinese Yogacarabhumi tradition uses thirty-six, which includes additional items not in the Pali list. This is a well-known divergence between the Sarvastivada/Yogacara and Theravada enumerations.

The footnote says the full list was presented in fascicle 28.11 and does not flag this divergence. While this is not a doctrinal error in the translation itself (the Chinese does say 三十六物), a scholarly reader may wonder why 36 rather than 31/32. A brief note acknowledging this as a Sarvastivada enumeration distinct from the Pali thirty-one/thirty-two would strengthen the scholarly apparatus.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- the translation correctly follows the source; the issue is one of scholarly completeness in the notes.

**Recommendation:** Add a brief parenthetical: "The Sarvastivada/Yogacara tradition enumerates thirty-six; the Pali tradition lists thirty-one (or thirty-two with the brain). The additional items reflect a more detailed anatomical analysis in the northern traditions."

### 3. [m] "External" formless contemplation -- counterintuitive mapping deserves stronger doctrinal grounding

**Location:** Section 32.10 paragraph (2) and footnote [^external_formless], lines 247, 255

**Issue:** The translation and footnote correctly flag the counterintuitive nature of "external" contemplation of feeling, mind, and dharmas: it refers not to others' mental states but to one's own past disturbed states. The footnote says these are "'external' because they belong to the unfixed state outside the practitioner's current meditative abiding."

This is a distinctively Yogacara reinterpretation of the standard satipatthana formula. In the Pali commentarial tradition (Analayo, pp. 897-939 of Satipatthana), "internal" and "external" in the satipatthana refrain are understood as one's own vs. another person's phenomena, following the Abhidhamma and commentary. The Sravakabhumi radically redefines "external" for the formless aggregates as the practitioner's own past disturbed mental states -- a move consistent with Yogacara's internalist epistemology but quite different from the mainstream interpretation.

The footnote explains the mapping but does not explicitly note that this is a distinctive Yogacara interpretation that diverges from the standard commentarial understanding. A reader familiar with the Pali tradition might be confused or think the translation is wrong.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- the translation faithfully renders the Chinese, and the footnote correctly explains what is happening. The issue is whether to flag the sectarian divergence explicitly.

**Recommendation:** Add one sentence to the footnote: "This reinterpretation of 'external' as one's own past disturbed states rather than as another person's mental states (the Pali commentarial interpretation) is distinctive to the Yogacara tradition and reflects its emphasis on internal mental processes as the primary domain of contemplation."

### 4. [m] Three divisions mapping to three modes -- doctrinal logic could be made more explicit

**Location:** Footnote [^formless_aggregates], line 253

**Issue:** The footnote states: "The three divisions (shamatha, non-distraction, vipashyana) map to the three modes: internal, external, and internal-external." This mapping is:
- Shamatha division = internal contemplation of feeling, mind, dharmas
- Non-distraction division = external contemplation of feeling, mind, dharmas
- Vipashyana division = internal-and-external contemplation of feeling, mind, dharmas

The doctrinal logic of this mapping is not obvious and deserves brief explanation:
- **Shamatha = internal** because shamatha is the mind withdrawn inward, observing its own quiet, undisturbed formless aggregates in present meditative absorption.
- **Non-distraction = external** because one contemplates the formless aggregates that arose during past scattered states (outside current meditative abiding), seeing them as deceptive and impermanent.
- **Vipashyana = internal-and-external** because vipashyana actively investigates with signs and discrimination, which involves both the internal process of investigation and the formless aggregates that arise in connection with the investigated object -- combining features of both modes.

The Chinese source explicitly makes these assignments (如是名為於內受心法修循受心法觀, etc.), so the translation is doctrinally correct. But the footnote's bare statement that the three divisions "map to" the three modes could leave a reader puzzled about *why* this mapping holds.

**Severity:** m (minor) -- a matter of explanatory completeness, not doctrinal error.

**Recommendation:** Expand the footnote to briefly explain the rationale for each mapping, as sketched above.

### 5. [M] Five signs recap: the replacement of "danger" and "discerning the object" with "shamatha" and "vipashyana"

**Location:** Footnote [^five_recap], line 229

**Issue:** The footnote states that the five signs are recapped with functional names, and that "danger" (過患相) and "discerning the object" (了別事相) are "replaced by" shamatha (奢摩他相) and vipashyana (毘鉢舍那相). It then offers an explanation: "the sign of danger supports shamatha by revealing what disturbs the mind, while discerning the object supports vipashyana by providing the material for insight."

This explanation is reasonable but doctrinally debatable. Looking at the Chinese source at T30.0462a01:

善取如是厭離相故。善取如是欣樂相故。善取如是奢摩他相故。善取如是毘鉢舍那相故。善取如是光明相故。

The five items listed are: (1) disenchantment, (2) delight, (3) shamatha, (4) vipashyana, (5) luminosity. In the original list from section 32.1 (T30.0459b25), they are: (1) disenchantment (厭離相), (2) delight (欣樂相), (3) danger (過患相), (4) luminosity (光明相), (5) discerning the object (了別事相).

The ordering differs: in section 32.1, luminosity is fourth and discerning the object is fifth. Here in 32.9, shamatha is third, vipashyana is fourth, and luminosity is fifth. The footnote's claim that "danger" corresponds to shamatha and "discerning the object" corresponds to vipashyana is one possible reading, but another interpretation is that the text is not performing a one-to-one functional substitution but rather listing the same underlying practice categories with different names appropriate to this stage of instruction. The sign of danger (過患相) relates more directly to the vipashyana function (seeing the faults of distraction) while shamatha relates to the sign of the six perceptions (which was part of the original fourth sign in its fuller exposition).

The mapping proposed in the footnote (danger -> shamatha, discerning-the-object -> vipashyana) is plausible but not the only reading. The more cautious position is that at this point in the instruction, the text names the five apprehensions using the language of shamatha and vipashyana because the practitioner is now working explicitly within that framework, and the earlier functional labels (danger, discerning) have been subsumed into shamatha and vipashyana practice more broadly.

**Severity:** M (major) -- the footnote presents one interpretation as though it were definitive. A scholar could reasonably dispute the specific one-to-one mapping proposed.

**Recommendation:** Soften the footnote's claim. Instead of the definitive statement about which sign "supports" which, note that the text appears to reconceptualize the five signs in terms of the shamatha-vipashyana framework now operative, and that the precise correspondence between the two lists is a matter of interpretation. The shift in ordering (luminosity moves from 4th to 5th) further suggests the text is recasting rather than substituting.

## Items Checked Without Findings (No Issues)

1. **Graduated expansion method (32.8):** The progression from one corpse to limitless, then from imaginative attention to real contemplation, accurately reflects the standard Yogacara meditation method as described in the Sravakabhumi. The expansion sequence (1, 2, 3, 4, 5, 10, 20, 30, 40, 50, 100, 1000, all directions) matches the Chinese. No anachronism.

2. **Imaginative attention vs. real contemplation (32.8):** The two-phase structure (勝解作意 -> 真實作意) is correctly rendered and the footnote accurately describes the doctrinal distinction. The transition from mentally constructed images to reflection on actual reality (the infinite corpses of beginningless samsara) is standard Yogacara meditation theory. Cross-checked against glossary entry for adhimoksha-manaskara.

3. **Shamatha-vipashyana alternation (32.8):** The five qualities marking natural ease (joyful, clear, bright, undisturbed, effortless) are correctly identified. The instruction to return to shamatha when effort is required is standard and doctrinally sound. The closing quiescence instruction (withdraw signs without fully abandoning the formed object) correctly renders the Chinese 不全棄捨有分別相 and is consistent with Yogacara meditation theory.

4. **Threefold body contemplation (32.9-32.10):** Internal (own 36 substances), external (others' corpses in asubha stages), internal-external (imagining one's own body going through asubha stages). This threefold schema is consistent with the Yogacara interpretation of the satipatthana refrain and the Chinese source. The standard Pali interpretation (own/others/alternating) is related but not identical; the Sravakabhumi's specific method of assigning content to each mode is its own contribution.

5. **Four formless aggregates covering three foundations (32.10):** The logic that body contemplation covers the form aggregate, so the remaining three foundations (feeling, mind, dharmas) are addressed through the four formless aggregates (vedana, samjna, samskara, vijnana), is doctrinally sound and standard Abhidharma. The mapping is: vedana -> feeling contemplation, vijnana -> mind contemplation, samjna + samskara -> dharma contemplation. The text treats all four formless aggregates together under each of the three divisions rather than separately assigning them, which is consistent with the Chinese 受等四無色蘊 ("feeling and the rest of the four formless aggregates").

6. **"Work of deception and illusion" (32.10):** The phrase 誑幻所作 applied to past disturbed mental states is doctrinally appropriate within the Yogacara framework. It reflects the tradition's view that mental constructions during scattered states are not ultimately real -- they are "conjured" by the afflicted mind. This is not an anachronistic Madhyamaka import; the Yogacara tradition independently uses similar language about the illusory nature of afflicted mental processes.

7. **Moment-by-moment impermanence (32.10):** The phrase "moment by moment continuously different -- only new, never the old, flowing in succession" (剎那剎那展轉別異。唯是新新而非故故相續流轉) reflects the standard Abhidharma doctrine of ksanika-vada (momentariness). No doctrinal issue.
