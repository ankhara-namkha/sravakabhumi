## Positive Calibration: Passages That Work Well

Before the flags, seven passages that a practitioner-reader would find clear, compelling, and well-crafted:

**POSITIVE P-28.1-01**
**Location:** Lines 47-51 (the graduated chain from discipline to nirvana)

> "First, because ethical discipline is well purified, one is free from worry and remorse. Being free from worry and remorse, one experiences gladness and ease. Because there is ease, the mind attains right concentration..."

This causal chain is beautifully rendered. Each step flows naturally into the next. A practitioner can immediately see the logic of the path. The rhythm mimics the progressive quality of the path itself. Excellent.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.4-01**
**Location:** Lines 151-173 (eleven drawbacks of yogic attention)

The enumeration of meditation obstacles -- doubt, heaviness, too-intense or too-weak effort, restlessness, terror of nirvana, argumentativeness, mental scattering, brooding -- reads like a practical diagnostic list. A meditator would recognize many of these from personal experience. The blockquote formatting sets them apart clearly. This section is one of the most practice-relevant in the fascicle.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.5-01**
**Location:** Lines 270-282 (the counterfeit dharma passage)

The extended narrative about the corrupt teacher who fabricates a private "yoga" to maintain offerings and respect is vivid, psychologically acute, and reads like living advice. The three diagnostic criteria (sutras, vinaya, dharma-nature) are clear and memorable. This passage would resonate strongly with any practitioner concerned about authentic teaching.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.6-01**
**Location:** Lines 306-314 (the four diligences)

The four types of diligence (learning, reflection, meditation, purifying obstructions) are concise, concrete, and practically applicable. A practitioner reading this would immediately know what to do. The parallelism is well controlled without becoming monotonous.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.9-01**
**Location:** Lines 456-464 (the standing/sitting/lying down metaphor)

The reinterpretation of bodily postures as temporal positions (standing = present, sitting = future, lying down = past) is handled with remarkable clarity. The systematic unpacking of each metaphor -- why "standing" represents present awareness, why "lying down" represents what has already ceased -- is a model of making a counterintuitive teaching intelligible. The footnote [^before_after] reinforces this beautifully.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.11-01**
**Location:** Lines 581-589 (the four inversions and four foundations of mindfulness)

The one-to-one mapping between the four inversions and the four foundations is clear and practically useful. Each paragraph follows the same structure (inversion --> foundation --> practice --> elimination), making the whole section easy to absorb. A practitioner would walk away knowing why each foundation exists.

---

**POSITIVE P-28.11-02**
**Location:** Lines 593-597 (the four dimensions of karma and defilement/purification)

These two rationales for why there are exactly four foundations are crisp and intellectually satisfying. The parallel structures (body = what one depends upon, feeling = what one acts for the sake of, mind = the agent, dharmas = the means) are immediately graspable. No jargon obscures the point.

## Detailed Findings

### Section 28.1: The Three Superior Trainings

---

**FLAG R-28.1-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 23

> "What are the trainings? They are the three superior trainings (*tri-shiksha*): (1) the superior training in ethical discipline (*adhishila-shiksha*), (2) the superior training in mind (*adhicitta-shiksha*), and (3) the superior training in prajna (*adhiprajna-shiksha*)."

This opening sentence contains four Sanskrit parentheticals in a single sentence. For a reader without Sanskrit, the parenthetical terms break the flow without adding comprehension. The English is clear on its own -- "the superior training in ethical discipline" is perfectly intelligible -- but the Sanskrit clutter makes the sentence feel forbidding. This is the very first sentence of the fascicle, and first impressions matter.

**Suggestion:** The footnote [^three_trainings] provides excellent orientation. Consider reducing the inline Sanskrit to just the collective term: "the three superior trainings (*tri-shiksha*)" and moving the individual Sanskrit names to the footnote. The reader who needs them will find them; the reader who does not will not be intimidated.

---

**FLAG R-28.1-02**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 27

> "...possessing examination (*vitarka*) and analysis (*vicara*), entering and fully abiding in the first dhyana with its joy and ease born of separation..."

"Examination" and "analysis" are presented as dhyana factors, but a practitioner-reader encountering these for the first time in this fascicle will not know what is being examined or analyzed. The terms are technically correct but contextually bare. "Joy and ease born of separation" is good -- it tells the reader what the experience is like. But "examination and analysis" are abstract.

**Suggestion:** A brief parenthetical after the pair: "possessing examination and analysis (the mind's initial and sustained engagement with its object)" would ground the terms.

---

**FLAG R-28.1-03**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 29

> "However, it is by relying on the dhyanas that one can first enter the direct realization of the noble truths (*satya-abhisamaya*) and achieve the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*)."

"The certainty of correctness" is a crucial concept here -- it marks the decisive entry onto the noble path -- but the English rendering is abstract. "Certainty of correctness" could mean anything: certainty that one's view is correct, certainty about correct behavior, etc. The footnote [^samyaktva_niyama] is superb and fully explains the concept. But a reader who skips footnotes will be left guessing what "correctness" refers to.

**Suggestion:** Add a brief inline gloss: "the certainty of correctness -- definitive entry onto the noble path."

---

**FLAG R-28.1-04**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Sentence length
**Location:** Line 67

> "A noble disciple who has not yet attained the root dhyanas (*maula-dhyana*) may first enter the path through seeing. Afterward, in order to progressively sever all afflictions to be abandoned on the path of cultivation (*bhavana-marga*), they engage diligently in preparatory practice, cultivating the mindfulness factor of awakening (*smriti-sambodhyanga*) through to the equanimity factor of awakening (*upeksha-sambodhyanga*)."

The second sentence is approximately 55 words with three Sanskrit parentheticals. The grammatical structure is sound, but the density of parenthetical terms makes it hard to read aloud without stumbling. The content is important (this is the "dry-insight" path described in the footnote), but the sentence asks the reader to hold too many terms simultaneously.

**Suggestion:** Break after "preparatory practice": "...they engage diligently in preparatory practice. Specifically, they cultivate the mindfulness factor of awakening through to the equanimity factor of awakening." The Sanskrit can remain in the footnote.

---

**FLAG R-28.1-05**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Logical flow break
**Location:** Lines 65-73

The passage at lines 65-73 covers three important but distinct points in rapid succession: (a) the mutual dependence of training in mind and training in prajna (lines 65-69), (b) the logical entailment among the three trainings (lines 71-72), and (c) the exhortation to train diligently (line 73). These are separated only by single blank lines and no section headers or transitional signals. A reader finishing the Q&A sections above (lines 35-61) has been following clearly demarcated questions and answers. Suddenly the format shifts to a series of "Furthermore" paragraphs with no guiding structure. The reader may lose track of what is being argued.

**Suggestion:** Consider a brief introductory phrase such as "Several further points regarding the three trainings:" before line 65, or use sub-bolded headings (as is done elsewhere in the fascicle) to organize these addenda.

### Section 28.2: The Three Faculties

---

**FLAG R-28.2-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Lines 93-95

> 1. The faculty of "I shall come to know the unknown" (*anajnatam-ajnasyami-indriya*)
> 2. The faculty of full knowledge (*ajna-indriya*)
> 3. The faculty of one who possesses full knowledge (*ajnatavi-indriya*)

These three faculty names are extremely long and heavily Sanskritized. The first -- "the faculty of 'I shall come to know the unknown'" -- is essentially a first-person vow embedded in a noun phrase. A practitioner encountering this for the first time will struggle to parse what it means as a "faculty." The footnote [^three_faculties] is excellent, but the body text gives no hint that these three map neatly onto three stages of the path (entering, traversing, completing).

**Suggestion:** After the numbered list, add one sentence: "These three faculties correspond to three stages: the faculty active when first entering the noble path, the faculty active while traversing it, and the faculty present when the task is complete."

### Section 28.3: The Three Gates of Liberation

---

**FLAG R-28.3-01**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 123

> "Because one has no yearning for conditioned things, one gives rise to deep yearning for nirvana -- perceiving it as supremely peaceful, supremely subtle, and as permanent emergence."

"Permanent emergence" is an unusual English phrase. "Emergence" typically implies a process (emerging from something), but "permanent emergence" suggests a completed state that is also somehow still an emergence. The footnote [^signlessness_nirvana] explains the concept clearly (freedom from the marks of conditioned existence). But in the body text, "permanent emergence" reads oddly.

**Suggestion:** Consider "permanent emergence from conditioned existence" to complete the phrase and make clear what is being emerged from. The two additional words resolve the ambiguity.

---

**FLAG R-28.3-02**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Awkward English
**Location:** Line 125

> "Regarding the non-existent -- within that which does not exist -- there is neither yearning nor absence of yearning. Just as it is non-existent, so it is known as non-existent and seen as non-existent."

The phrase "within that which does not exist -- there is neither yearning nor absence of yearning" is philosophically precise but reads like a tongue-twister. The double dash after "non-existent" creates a parenthetical within a parenthetical. A practitioner would need to read this two or three times.

**Suggestion:** Simplify slightly: "As for the non-existent -- that is, what has no real existence at all, such as the supposed self -- one neither yearns for it nor refrains from yearning. One simply knows it as non-existent." The added example (the self) provides a concrete referent.

### Section 28.4: Factors Opposing and According with Training

---

**FLAG R-28.4-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon (first use)
**Location:** Line 141

> "(2) Generating attachment to the conditioned processes comprised within the transitory collection (*satkaya*)."

"The transitory collection" is an important and recurring term in this fascicle (it appears at lines 141, 145, 167), but it is never defined in the body text of this fascicle. The term refers to the five skandhas as the basis for the mistaken notion of self -- but "transitory collection" is opaque to a reader without Abhidharma background. "Transitory" could describe anything impermanent; "collection" suggests a physical grouping. Why is one generating attachment to a "collection"?

**Suggestion:** On first use (line 141), add a brief gloss: "the transitory collection (*satkaya*) -- the ever-changing aggregate of body and mind that is mistaken for a self." Subsequent uses can stand without gloss.

---

**FLAG R-28.4-02**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon
**Location:** Line 147

> "(5) Attachment to fine flavors, relying on material nourishment (*kavadikara-ahara*)."

"Material nourishment" followed by a Sanskrit parenthetical is oddly specific for what is essentially "food." The Abhidharma distinction between types of nourishment (material, contact, volition, consciousness) is not in play here -- the text is simply talking about attachment to delicious food. The Sanskrit feels like unnecessary technical apparatus.

**Suggestion:** Simplify to "Attachment to fine flavors -- relying on food (*kavadikara-ahara*)" or just "Attachment to fine flavors and food."

---

**FLAG R-28.4-03**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Awkward English
**Location:** Line 157

> "(iii) For one who is lax and slack in cultivating shamatha and vipashyana: attending to drawbacks, torpor and drowsiness overwhelming the mind, causing extreme mental contraction."

The grammatical subject shifts midstream. "For one who is lax and slack" sets up a person, but "attending to drawbacks" has no clear agent -- is the lax person attending to drawbacks, or is this something that happens to them? "Torpor and drowsiness overwhelming the mind" is a second clause that could be either parallel to "attending to drawbacks" or a consequence of it. The reader cannot easily parse the sentence structure.

**Suggestion:** Restructure: "For one who is lax and slack in cultivating shamatha and vipashyana: the mind turns toward drawbacks, and torpor and drowsiness overwhelm it, causing extreme mental contraction."

---

**FLAG R-28.4-04**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 233

> "When someone, due to physical toil or exhaustion, generates physical and mental heaviness (*dausthulya*), this can be stilled by changing to a relaxed posture. Or when excessive examination (*vitarka*) and analysis (*vicara*) generate physical and mental heaviness, this can be stilled through the method of internal mental calming. Or when the mind becomes contracted and feeble, entangled by torpor and drowsiness, generating physical and mental heaviness, this can be stilled through the superior wisdom-dharma, vipashyana, and purifying attention. Or when physical and mental heaviness cannot yet be abandoned because afflictions have not been severed, this can be stilled through continuous diligent cultivation of the correct path."

This is a single paragraph of approximately 120 words containing four "Or when..." clauses. Each clause is individually clear, but the four stacked together without any paragraph break or numbering create a wall of text. The content is excellent and practically useful -- it gives four specific remedies for heaviness. But the formatting does not serve the content.

**Suggestion:** Number the four remedies as (1)-(4), each on its own line. This matches the numbered-list formatting used elsewhere in the fascicle and makes the remedies scannable.

---

**FLAG R-28.4-05**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Register inconsistency
**Location:** Line 239

> "...timidity arises, and the mind becomes weary and depleted."

"Weary and depleted" is vivid and slightly informal compared to the surrounding register. Elsewhere the translation uses more measured language for mental states ("contracted," "overwhelmed," "sunken"). "Depleted" works well, but "weary" has a colloquial overtone. This is extremely minor.

### Section 28.5: Four Types of Yoga Failure

---

**FLAG R-28.5-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 260

> "Permanent destruction of yoga refers to a person without lineage (*gotra*). Why? Because within their being there is no dharma capable of progressing toward nirvana; their supramundane yoga is permanently destroyed."

"A person without lineage" is a startling claim -- that some people permanently lack the capacity for liberation. The footnote [^agotra] explains the doctrinal controversy well. But in the body text, "without lineage" could be misread as "without a Buddhist lineage" (i.e., a teacher-student lineage), which is a very different meaning. The intended sense is "lacking the innate spiritual capacity" -- but "lineage" in contemporary dharma English almost always means "teaching tradition."

**Suggestion:** On first use, gloss: "a person without [spiritual] lineage (*gotra*) -- that is, one said to lack the innate capacity for nirvana." The brackets and dash prevent the misreading.

---

**FLAG R-28.5-02**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 278

> "If there are bhikshus well-versed in the three baskets (*tripitaka*), the person conceals their yoga and does not wish to reveal it to them. But to their own lay and monastic disciples, they privately and secretly impart this yoga, keeping it from becoming public. Why? They fear that should someone well-versed in the three baskets hear of their yoga, they would examine it against the sutras and find it does not accord, illuminate it with the vinaya and find it does not reflect the vinaya, and investigate it through dharma and find it contradicts the nature of dharma."

This paragraph is approximately 105 words. The final sentence alone is about 55 words. The three-part examination (sutras, vinaya, dharma) is rhetorical and powerful, but the sentence structure (three parallel "they would X it against Y and find Z" clauses chained together) makes the sentence very long. The content is among the best in the fascicle (see positive note P-28.5-01 above), so the issue is purely structural.

**Suggestion:** Break after "becoming public." Then: "Why the concealment? They fear that someone well-versed in the three baskets would examine their yoga against the sutras and find it does not accord; illuminate it with the vinaya and find it does not reflect the vinaya; investigate it through dharma and find it contradicts the nature of dharma."

### Section 28.6: The Four Yogas

---

**FLAG R-28.6-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon / passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 294

> "The two bases are: (1) the basis of examining the principles of dharmas, and (2) the basis of placing faith in the supernormal powers of [holy] persons."

"The basis of examining the principles of dharmas" is abstract. What principles? What dharmas? A reader without Abhidharma training does not know whether "dharmas" means "teachings" or "phenomena" here. The phrase "principles of dharmas" compounds the ambiguity: principles about teachings? Or inherent rules of phenomena?

The second basis, "placing faith in the supernormal powers of [holy] persons," is clearer but raises a different question: what are "supernormal powers"? The brackets around "[holy]" signal editorial insertion, which may confuse a reader unfamiliar with translation conventions.

**Suggestion:** Gloss: "(1) the basis of rational examination -- investigating the principles of the dharma's teachings to verify their truth, and (2) the basis of trust -- placing faith in the verified attainments of realized practitioners."

---

**FLAG R-28.6-02**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Logical flow break / missing context
**Location:** Lines 316-318

> "**Method** (*upaya*) is of four kinds. Through the power of the restraint of ethical discipline, one well guards one's mindfulness..."
>
> "These four yogas have sixteen types in total."

The treatment of "method" is anomalous. Faith gets two aspects and two bases (= 4 sub-items). Aspiration gets four types. Diligence gets four types. But method, which is announced as "of four kinds," does not present a numbered list. Instead, it describes a causal chain (discipline --> mindfulness --> non-negligence --> shamatha/vipashyana) that implicitly contains four steps, but the reader must count them. Then line 318 says "These four yogas have sixteen types in total" -- the reader must verify: 4 (faith) + 4 (aspiration) + 4 (diligence) + 4 (method) = 16. But the four sub-types of method were never explicitly enumerated.

**Suggestion:** Make the four methods explicit. Something like: "**(1) Guarding ethical discipline.** Through the restraint of ethical discipline... **(2) Guarding mindfulness.** Because one guards mindfulness... **(3) Non-negligence.** ... **(4) Cultivating shamatha and vipashyana.** ..." This parallels the treatment of the other three yogas and makes the "sixteen total" claim verifiable.

---

**FLAG R-28.6-03**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Awkward English
**Location:** Line 300

> "...one goes to the sangha-arama and approaches those among one's fellow practitioners of the holy life who possess knowledge and have accomplished the excellent wisdom of yoga..."

"The excellent wisdom of yoga" is a slightly awkward compound. "Yoga" here means meditative practice, but "wisdom of yoga" sounds like a book title. "Fellow practitioners of the holy life" is a good, dignified rendering. But the sentence tries to pack too many qualifying phrases around the person being approached.

**Suggestion:** Simplify: "...one goes to the monastery and approaches fellow practitioners who possess knowledge and have accomplished the wisdom gained through practice."

### Section 28.7: Types of Attention

---

**FLAG R-28.7-01**
**Severity:** C (Critical)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 374

> "**The condition aspect:** The accumulation of the provisions of samadhi -- being guided by the teaching, accompanied by practice, having keen aspiration, generating deep disenchantment regarding what should be abandoned, being able to discerningly and thoroughly know disturbance from non-disturbance, and being untroubled by others -- whether caused by humans or non-humans, by sounds or by activity. When vipashyana takes the lead, one internally concentrates the mind, and after extremely vigorous observation, the condition aspect [is attended to]. When shamatha takes the lead, having aroused excellent observation, after extremely vigorous calming, the condition aspect [is attended to]."

This is the most difficult passage in the fascicle for a practitioner-reader. Multiple problems converge:

(a) "The accumulation of the provisions of samadhi" is never explained. What are "provisions"? This is a technical rendering of *sambhara* (equipment, prerequisites), but the English "provisions" suggests food supplies.

(b) The long list of conditions ("being guided by the teaching, accompanied by practice, having keen aspiration...") is presented as a single run-on modifier of "provisions of samadhi." It is unclear whether these are the provisions themselves or circumstances that accompany them.

(c) "Being untroubled by others -- whether caused by humans or non-humans, by sounds or by activity" -- the dash creates ambiguity. Is "whether caused by..." modifying "untroubled" or "others"?

(d) The final two sentences ("[is attended to]") use brackets to supply a missing verb, which signals that the translation itself found the source text elliptical. The reader sees brackets and wonders if the text is corrupt or incomplete.

**Suggestion:** Break the passage into numbered conditions and supply the ellipsis without brackets:
"The condition aspect requires that the prerequisites for samadhi be in place: (1) being guided by the teaching; (2) having a regular practice; (3) having keen aspiration; (4) generating deep disenchantment with what should be abandoned; (5) being able to distinguish disturbance from non-disturbance; and (6) being free from disruption by others -- whether human or non-human, by sounds or by activity.
When vipashyana takes the lead, one concentrates the mind internally and, after extremely vigorous observation, attends to the condition aspect. When shamatha takes the lead, having aroused excellent observation, one calms the mind extremely vigorously and attends to the condition aspect."

---

**FLAG R-28.7-02**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 396

> "**Narrow:** Resolute confidence accompanied by narrow faith and aspiration, and resolute confidence directed at a narrow object of mental comprehension -- because the attention is narrow and the object is narrow, it is called narrow. **Vast:** Resolute confidence accompanied by vast faith and aspiration, and directed at a vast object -- because the attention is vast and the object is vast, it is called vast. **Immeasurable:** Resolute confidence accompanied by boundless and limitless faith and aspiration, directed at a boundless and limitless object -- because the attention is immeasurable and the object is immeasurable, it is called immeasurable."

This entire paragraph of nine definitions (lines 396) is a single block. Each definition uses nearly identical phrasing, creating a mechanical quality. The first two (luminosity/no luminosity) and the next two (dull/quick) are brief and clear. But the middle three (narrow/vast/immeasurable) become repetitive, with phrases like "because the attention is X and the object is X, it is called X" repeated three times. The final two (pure/impure) are concise but by that point the reader may have glazed over.

**Suggestion:** Format each of the nine as a separate short paragraph or use a definition-list format. This would also help the reader cross-reference with the numbered list above (lines 386-394).

---

**FLAG R-28.7-03**
**Severity:** m (minor)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon
**Location:** Line 341

> "...the attention to characteristics (*lakshana-pratisanvedana-manaskara*) is interspersed with samadhi and with reflection..."

"The attention to characteristics" is the first of seven attentions in a system that structures the entire meditative path in the Sravakabhumi. It appears here for the first time in fascicle 28 and is referenced multiple times afterward (lines 343, 428, 430). The footnote [^attention_to_characteristics] explains the system well. But in the body text, "attention to characteristics" is opaque. What characteristics? Whose? Of what? The technical name is doing no work for the reader who does not already know the seven-attention system.

**Suggestion:** On first use, gloss briefly: "the attention to characteristics -- the initial analytical attention that examines the coarse and peaceful features of the meditation object."

### Section 28.8: Yogic Activities and Three Types of Yogin

---

**FLAG R-28.8-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 410

> "Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object are primary to the cessation and transformation of the basis -- through the power of thorough knowledge and delight, the basis ceases and transforms. Sometimes thorough knowledge and delight in the object take the purification of the basis as primary -- through the power of the purified basis, thorough knowledge of the object becomes well purified, and delight in the object becomes well purified, operating at the time of accomplishing the purpose (*krtyanushthana*)."

This sentence describes a circular causal relationship: sometimes knowledge causes the basis to transform; sometimes the transformed basis improves knowledge. This is an important point about the bidirectional relationship between insight and purification. But the phrasing is dense, with "thorough knowledge and delight" repeated four times in two sentences, and "ceases and transforms" / "well purified" used for both sides of the equation. The reader loses track of which is cause and which is effect.

**Suggestion:** Restructure with clear labels: "The relationship between knowledge and the basis works in two directions. Sometimes knowledge leads: through the power of thorough knowledge and delight in the object, the practitioner's basis ceases its heaviness and transforms toward pliancy. Sometimes the basis leads: through the power of a purified basis, one's knowledge of the object and delight in it become correspondingly purified, operating at the level of final accomplishment."

---

**FLAG R-28.8-02**
**Severity:** C (Critical)
**Category:** Unexplained jargon / incomprehensible without prior knowledge
**Location:** Lines 406-408

> "(1) Cessation of the basis, (2) transformation of the basis, (3) thorough knowledge of the object, and (4) delight in the object."
>
> "...the basis accompanied by heaviness (*dausthulya*) gradually ceases, and the basis accompanied by pliancy (*prashrabdhi*) gradually transforms."

"The basis" (appearing 8 times in lines 406-410) is never defined in this section. What is "the basis"? In the Yogacara context, this is the practitioner's psychophysical foundation -- the body-mind complex as conditioned by afflictions or purified by practice. But a reader encountering "cessation of the basis" and "transformation of the basis" will have no idea what is ceasing or transforming. "The basis accompanied by heaviness ceases" -- does the heaviness cease, or does the basis cease? "The basis accompanied by pliancy transforms" -- transforms into what?

The concept of *ashraya-paravritti* (transformation of the basis) is one of the most important ideas in Yogacara thought, but it is introduced here without any definition or gloss. The glossary entry (line 153 of the glossary) defines it as "Fundamental shift in the practitioner's cognitive basis," but this definition does not appear in the translation.

**Suggestion:** Before line 408, add one sentence of orientation: "The 'basis' refers to the practitioner's psychophysical constitution -- the underlying body-mind foundation that either supports or hinders practice." Then the definitions at line 408 become comprehensible: the heavy basis (weighted by afflictions) ceases, and a pliant basis (conducive to practice) takes its place.

---

**FLAG R-28.8-03**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words
**Location:** Line 438

> "Furthermore, beginning from the time one first generates the aspiration to cultivate wholesome dharmas until one has not yet given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration (*nirvedha-bhagiya-kushala-mula*) -- during that entire period, one is called a beginner. If one has already given rise to the wholesome roots concordant with penetration -- namely warmth (*ushmanas*), summit (*murdhan*), patience concordant with the truths (*satya-anulomika-kshanti*), and the supreme worldly dharma (*laukikagra-dharma*) -- one is called practiced. If one has already entered the certainty of correctness (*samyaktva-niyama*) and attained the direct realization of the truths, and is no longer dependent on others regarding the Buddha's noble teaching, nor can be led astray by other conditions -- at that point, one is called one who has transcended attention."

This is a single paragraph of approximately 130 words containing three dense sentences, six Sanskrit parentheticals, and seven technical terms. It is the second framework for the three yogin types (the first being the seven-attention framework at lines 420-432). The content is important and the parallel structure is appropriate. But the density of technical terms makes it nearly impenetrable on first reading.

**Suggestion:** (1) Break into three numbered paragraphs, one per yogin type. (2) Reduce inline Sanskrit -- the footnote [^nirvedha_bhagiya] already lists the four stages with full explanations. (3) The four stages (warmth, summit, patience, supreme worldly dharma) can be listed as a sub-list rather than embedded in a sentence.

### Section 28.9: Two Types of Cultivation

---

**FLAG R-28.9-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 452

> "When cultivating the worldly path, one cultivates the perception of the dangers of lower realms. When cultivating the path to nirvana, regarding the realm of abandonment (*prahana-dhatu*), the realm of dispassion (*viraga-dhatu*), and the realm of cessation (*nirodha-dhatu*), one perceives the supreme benefit of peacefulness and cultivates the perception of abandonment, the perception of dispassion, and the perception of cessation."

Three technical terms appear in quick succession -- the realm of abandonment, the realm of dispassion, and the realm of cessation. These are three aspects of nirvana (explained in fascicle 27, glossary lines 223-225), but they are introduced here without definition. A reader who has not read fascicle 27 will encounter three "realms" that sound like cosmological locations rather than aspects of liberation. "The realm of abandonment" -- what realm? Where? Of what?

**Suggestion:** Add a brief parenthetical: "regarding the three aspects of cessation -- the realm of abandonment (afflictions abandoned through seeing), the realm of dispassion (afflictions cut off through cultivation), and the realm of cessation (complete cessation of all bases of existence)..."

**FLAG R-28.9-02**
**Severity:** m (minor)

**Location:** Line 466

### Section 28.10: The Thirty-Seven Factors of Awakening

---

**FLAG R-28.10-01**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Sentence over 50 words / awkward English
**Location:** Lines 494-497

> 1. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of aspiration (*chanda*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 2. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of diligence (*virya*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 3. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of mind (*citta*), endowed with formations of exertion
> 4. The basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of investigation (*mimamsa*), endowed with formations of exertion

Each item is approximately 20 words, but the four-fold repetition of "the basis of supernatural power consisting of the samadhi of X, endowed with formations of exertion" is extremely heavy. "Formations of exertion" is also opaque -- what are "formations" here, and how do they relate to "exertion"? This is a standard Abhidharma formula, but it reads like a machine transliteration.

The phrase "bases of supernatural power" itself is problematic for a practitioner-reader. "Supernatural power" evokes telekinesis and clairvoyance. The actual meaning is closer to "the foundations for accomplishment through concentrated practice."

**Suggestion:** Simplify each item: "The basis of supernatural power through aspiration-samadhi..." and gloss "formations of exertion" once: "endowed with formations of exertion (the effort that sustains concentration)." Also consider a footnote explaining that "supernatural power" (*riddhi*) here refers to the power of concentrated practice to accomplish what was previously impossible, not to magical displays.

These four objects of meditation were treated in detail in fascicle 27, but they appear here with different English renderings than what the glossary uses. **FLAG R-28.11-04**
**Severity:** M (Major)
**Category:** Passage requiring guesswork
**Location:** Line 537

> "Furthermore, pleasant feeling is feeling arising from contact conducive to pleasant feeling, included in the category of balanced feeling. This is called pleasant feeling."

"The category of balanced feeling" is introduced without any definition. What does "balanced" mean here? The term appears to correspond to a technical Abhidharma classification where "balanced" means the sense faculties are functioning harmoniously, producing a pleasant experience. But "balanced" in ordinary English means "even, moderate, equitable" -- which is closer to the description of neutral feeling. Similarly, "unbalanced feeling" (for painful feeling) and "neither-balanced-nor-unbalanced feeling" (for neutral) are counterintuitive labels. Why would painful feeling be "unbalanced"? The terms create confusion rather than clarity.

**Suggestion:** If these are technical categories, gloss them on first use: "included in the category of balanced feeling (feeling arising when the sense faculties are in a harmonious, undisturbed state)." Alternatively, if they are not doing essential work, consider whether the analysis can be presented without these category labels.
